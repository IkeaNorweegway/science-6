## The Particle Model — Direct Instruction

Everything you can see, touch, smell, and taste is made of **particles** — pieces of matter so small they cannot be seen with the naked eye.

The **particle model of matter** has four key ideas:

| Idea | What it means |
|---|---|
| All matter is made of particles | Every solid, liquid, and gas — particles all the way down |
| Particles are always moving | Even in a solid. They vibrate in place. |
| Particles have space between them | They are not touching each other continuously |
| Temperature affects particle speed | More heat = faster movement |

### Particles in a solid, liquid, and gas

**Solid:** Particles are close together. Attractive forces between them are strong. They vibrate but stay in place. This is why solids have a fixed shape.

**Liquid:** Particles are close but can slide past each other. Attractive forces are weaker. This is why liquids flow but still have a definite volume.

**Gas:** Particles are far apart and move quickly in all directions. Attractive forces are very weak. This is why gases expand to fill any container.

## The Most Important Misconception

> **Wrong idea:** "Particles ARE the material. A particle of water is a tiny drop of water."

This is wrong — and it matters.

Particles are not tiny versions of the material. A water particle (H₂O molecule) is not a tiny drop of water. It is a completely different kind of thing — an arrangement of atoms. The *behaviour* of enormous numbers of these particles together is what produces what we call "water."

This distinction becomes important when we study phase changes. The particle doesn't change — the arrangement and movement of particles changes.
