## Unit Overview for Teachers

**Tier 1 unit — 5–6 weeks, highest instructional priority.**

The particle model is the most abstract content in Grade 6 Science. Students cannot see particles, so everything depends on building and using a mental model. The model must be introduced carefully, checked frequently, and retrieved often.

**The key misconception to watch all unit:** particles are tiny versions of the material. This error is common, persistent, and corrosive — students who hold it will misinterpret phase changes, expansion, and contraction.

## CS Integration — Lesson 10

The particle model is an abstraction: a simplified version of something complex. In Lesson 10, students:
- Learn the definition of abstraction in the CS sense
- Discuss examples from daily life (light switches, steering wheels, apps)
- Reflect on how the particle model fits the definition
- Apply abstraction thinking to their inquiry project design

## Misconception Map

| Misconception | When it appears | How to address |
|---|---|---|
| Particles = tiny versions of material | Lesson 1, returns throughout | Water molecule diagram vs. water drop |
| Mass changes in a phase change | Lesson 3 | Controlled experiment: weigh before and after melting |
| Ice is denser than water | Lesson 7 | Ice cube in water observation + particle model explanation |
| Particles in solids don't move | Lessons 1–2 | If true, what would happen at absolute zero? |
