## Timing Notes

- **Retrieval (5–7 min):** Q3 is intentionally open-ended. Expect a range of answers — don't correct yet. Note what students say for the misconception discussion.
- **Engage (5 min):** Collect brain dump responses. Scan for the most common misconception (particles = tiny versions of material). Don't address it yet — let it surface in instruction.
- **Instruction (18–20 min):** The table is the anchor. Walk through each row with a physical demonstration where possible (shaking a tray of marbles loosely = gas; packed tightly = solid).
- **Practice (20–22 min):** Circulate. Q2 is the misconception check — listen for students who still say "the particles melt." Correct immediately in conversation.
- **Exit (5–7 min):** Collect all. Q3 confidence rating is data — if most students rate 1–2, open next class with re-teaching before retrieval warm-up.

## Socratic Question Prompts

Use these when circulating during practice or when a student gives a partial answer:

- *"If particles were tiny versions of the material, what would you expect to see when water freezes?"*
- *"You said particles move faster when heated. What does that mean for the space between them?"*
- *"Can you show me on your diagram where the attractive forces are strongest?"*
- *"What would have to be true about gas particles for them to expand to fill any container?"*

## Misconception Flags

**Primary misconception:** Particles are tiny versions of the material (e.g., a particle of water is a tiny water drop).

**How to address it:** Draw a particle of water (H₂O — two H atoms, one O atom) next to a drop of water. Ask: "Do these look the same?" The particle is a chemical arrangement — not a scaled-down version of the bulk material.

**Secondary misconception:** Particles in a solid don't move at all.

**How to address it:** "If that were true, what would happen to the temperature of a solid? Could it ever get warmer?" All matter above absolute zero has particle motion. Solids vibrate.

## Lab / Activity Notes

**Marble tray demonstration:** Use a flat tray with walls and a handful of marbles. Show:
- Packed close, barely moving = solid
- Slightly spaced, rolling past each other = liquid
- Few marbles, shaking tray vigorously = gas

This gives students a physical referent for their diagrams.
