Part A: Earth's Interior (16 marks)

Q1 — Interior layers: (8 marks — 2 per row)

Label Layer name One key property
a Crust Thin solid outermost layer; oceanic crust (basalt, thin, dense) or continental crust (granite, thick, less dense)
b Mantle Thickest layer; solid rock that flows very slowly over geological time; contains convection currents
c Outer core Liquid iron and nickel; convection in the outer core generates Earth's magnetic field
d Inner core Solid iron and nickel; hottest layer; extreme pressure keeps it solid despite very high temperature

Q2 — How scientists know Earth's interior: (4 marks)

Seismic waves (produced by earthquakes). When an earthquake occurs, seismic waves travel through Earth in all directions. P-waves (primary, compressional) travel through solids and liquids; S-waves (secondary, shear) travel only through solids. Scientists at seismograph stations worldwide detect these waves. By analyzing: (1) how fast they travel (speed varies with material density and rigidity), (2) where S-waves are absent (shadow zone reveals the liquid outer core), and (3) where waves bend/refract at layer boundaries, scientists can map the depth, composition, and state of each layer without drilling.

Q3 — Inner core solid despite high temperature: (2 marks)

Extreme pressure from the weight of all layers above prevents the iron-nickel atoms from spreading apart into a liquid. Melting requires particles to have enough energy to overcome attractive forces AND enough space to move freely; the immense pressure at Earth's centre (~360 GPa) denies that space, keeping material locked in a solid state regardless of temperature.

Q4 — Liquid outer core importance: (2 marks)

The convecting liquid iron-nickel outer core generates Earth's magnetic field (the magnetosphere). This field deflects the solar wind (high-energy charged particles from the Sun) away from Earth's surface. Without it, solar radiation would strip away the atmosphere and bombard the surface, making life as we know it impossible.

Q6 — Basalt vs. granite crystal size: (3 + 1 = 4 marks)

a. Basalt cooled rapidly at Earth's surface (lava): atoms only had a very short time to arrange into crystal lattices before the rock solidified → small or microscopic crystals. Granite cooled slowly underground over millions of years: atoms had abundant time to move and organize into orderly crystal lattices → large, visible crystals. Crystal size is a record of cooling rate: fast = small; slow = large.

b. Both are igneous rock (solidified from molten rock — magma or lava).

Q7 — Rock cycle triangle: (6 marks)

Three-point triangle diagram must show:

  • Igneous, Sedimentary, and Metamorphic at corners
  • At least five labelled processes on arrows between them:
  • Igneous → Sedimentary: weathering + erosion + deposition + compaction/cementation
  • Sedimentary → Metamorphic: heat + pressure (burial/subduction)
  • Metamorphic → Igneous: melting (magma)
  • Any rock → Metamorphic: heat + pressure
  • Any rock → Igneous: melting
  • Any rock → Sedimentary: weathering + erosion

Q8 — Fossils in rock types: (5 marks)

Fossils require: (1) rapid burial of an organism before decay destroys soft tissues; (2) low-temperature, low-pressure conditions so organic material is preserved or replaced by minerals. Igneous rock forms from molten magma (extreme heat) → any organism would be destroyed. Metamorphic rock is subjected to intense heat and pressure that would also destroy organic material and deform fossil structures beyond recognition. Sedimentary rock: sediments (sand, mud, silt) accumulate layer by layer in relatively calm conditions (river deltas, lake floors, shallow seas); organisms can be buried quickly and preserved before decay; minerals slowly replace organic material over time. Fossils are almost exclusively found in sedimentary rock.

Q10 — Three lines of evidence: (6 marks — 2 per piece of evidence)

  1. Continental fit (jigsaw puzzle): the coastlines of Africa and South America fit together like puzzle pieces; suggests they were once joined and have drifted apart over hundreds of millions of years
  2. Matching fossils across continents: identical fossils of the same extinct species (Mesosaurus, Glossopteris) found on now-separated continents (South America + Africa; Antarctica + India); these organisms could not have crossed open ocean → continents must have been connected
  3. Seafloor spreading / paleomagnetic striping: magnetic minerals in seafloor basalt record Earth's magnetic field at the time of formation; alternating normal/reversed stripes mirror each other symmetrically on either side of mid-ocean ridges → proves new crust forms at ridges and spreads outward

Q11 — Rocky Mountains: (3 marks)

Convergent plate boundary. An oceanic plate (Juan de Fuca/Farallon plate) subducted under the North American continental plate. As the oceanic plate descended, its leading edge scraped up and crumpled the continental margin; enormous compressive forces folded and thrust the rock layers upward, creating the fold-and-thrust belt we call the Rocky Mountains. The process began ~100 Ma and continued through the Cretaceous.

Q12 — Alberta's tectonic past: (2 marks)

Any two: (1) the Rocky Mountains (fold-and-thrust belt from Cretaceous convergent boundary); (2) ancient limestone beds from marine seas (Alberta was covered by ocean during Paleozoic and Cretaceous); (3) oil sands (organic material from Cretaceous marine environment, buried and transformed by heat/pressure); (4) Precambrian basement rock exposed in northern Alberta (roots of ancient mountain belt)

Part D: Earthquakes and Volcanoes (16 marks)

Q13 — What causes an earthquake: (6 marks)

Tectonic plates move continuously (driven by mantle convection); where plates meet, rock along fault lines (fractures in crust) stores elastic strain energy as the plates try to move but remain locked by friction. When accumulated stress exceeds the rock's strength at a fault → rock suddenly breaks and slips → elastic energy releases as seismic waves. The focus (hypocenter) is the underground point where rupture begins; seismic waves radiate outward from the focus in all directions. The epicenter is the point on Earth's surface directly above the focus; shaking is typically strongest there. Faults are the fracture lines along which movement occurs; most earthquakes occur at plate boundaries where fault systems are active.

Part E: Weathering, Erosion, and Alberta Landforms (16 marks)

Q16 — Weathering vs. erosion: (3 marks)

Weathering: the breaking down of rock in place (in situ) — the rock crumbles, dissolves, or fragments but stays roughly where it was. Erosion: the transport of weathered material from one location to another by a moving agent (water, wind, ice, gravity). Weathering must occur before erosion can transport material, but erosion does not cause the initial breakdown.

Q17 — Mechanical and chemical weathering: (6 marks)

Mechanical example: freeze-thaw (frost wedging). Water seeps into cracks in rock; when temperature drops below 0°C, water expands ~9% as it freezes, exerting enormous outward pressure on the crack walls; repeated freeze-thaw cycles widen the crack until the rock splits. Common in the Rocky Mountain foothills during Alberta spring/fall. Mechanical: only the shape/size of the rock changes, not its composition.

Chemical example: oxidation. Iron-bearing minerals in rock react with oxygen (and water) to form iron oxides (rust, hematite); the original mineral structure is disrupted and weakened. Visible as orange/red staining on exposed rock faces (common in Alberta Badlands). Chemical: the mineral composition of the rock actually changes.

Q19 — Geological change is mostly slow: (3 marks)

Most geological processes operate over millions of years; even "fast" erosion is imperceptibly slow on a human timescale. Calculation: 2 mm/year × 1,000,000 years = 2,000,000 mm = 2,000 m = 2 km deep. A valley 2 km deep from a river eroding at only 2 mm/year over a million years — this illustrates why the Red Deer River valley and the Grand Canyon are so impressive: immense depth from tiny incremental changes accumulated over geological time.

Part F: Fossils and Geological Time (12 marks)

Q20 — Law of superposition: (3 marks)

In undisturbed horizontal rock layers, the oldest layers are at the bottom and the youngest layers are at the top (each new layer is deposited on top of existing layers). Used for relative dating: by reading layers from bottom to top, scientists construct a relative timeline of events — which organisms lived before or after others — without knowing the absolute age. Fossils in deeper layers are older than fossils in layers above them.

Q21 — Two fossil types besides bone replacement: (4 marks)

Any two with correct formation description:

  1. Cast fossil: original organism dissolves/decays and leaves a cavity (mold) in surrounding rock; minerals precipitate in and fill the mold → creates a solid 3D replica of the organism's exterior shape
  2. Trace fossil: preserved record of an organism's activity rather than its body — footprints, burrows, feeding marks, coprolites (fossilized dung); organism moves through soft sediment which hardens around the impression
  3. Amber fossil: organism (typically insect or small organism) trapped in tree resin; resin hardens over millions of years → organism preserved in extraordinary detail in amber
  4. Carbonized impression: soft tissues preserved as thin carbon film on rock; organic matter reduced to carbon residue leaving 2D outline of organism

Q22 — Why Alberta is an important fossil location: (3 marks)

During the Late Cretaceous (~75 million years ago), Alberta was a subtropical coastal lowland bordering the Western Interior Seaway — a shallow marine sea that divided North America. This environment supported extremely diverse ecosystems: marine organisms in the sea, and large dinosaur faunas (hadrosaurs, ceratopsians, tyrannosaurs) in the surrounding wetlands and floodplains. Rapid sediment burial in river deltas and floodplains preserved these organisms. The subsequent uplift of the Rockies and erosion by the Red Deer River has now exposed these Cretaceous-age sedimentary layers in the Badlands, making fossils accessible at the surface. Alberta contains one of the richest concentrations of Late Cretaceous dinosaur fossils on Earth.

Q23 — Relative dating from layers: (2 marks)

Layer A (containing marine invertebrate fossils) is older than Layer B (containing dinosaur fossils) because Layer A is beneath Layer B — by the law of superposition, deeper = older in undisturbed sequences. The marine invertebrates in Layer A lived and were preserved before the dinosaur-age organisms in Layer B were deposited.
