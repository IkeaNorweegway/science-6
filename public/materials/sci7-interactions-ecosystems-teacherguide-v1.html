Interactions and Ecosystems — Teacher Guide

Grade 7 Science — Teacher Guide

Read alongside: `_courses/grade-7-science/interactions-ecosystems/_context.md` and the student notes package.

Unit Overview

Suggested length: 6–7 weeks (18–21 classes at 3×/week) Tier: 1 — teach with full depth, all misconceptions addressed, retrieval built throughout Unit colour: Emerald

What this unit is really about: Students arrive thinking ecology is about naming animals. It's actually about relationships — energy moving through a system, species depending on each other in ways that aren't obvious, and humans embedded in (not above) that system. The biggest conceptual move is from "list of species" thinking to "system" thinking. The energy pyramid and the wolf reintroduction scenario are the two highest-leverage moments in the unit.

Lesson Sequence

Week 1 — Ecosystems and Components
Lesson 1 — What is an ecosystem?
Show Open with a 3-minute clip of an Alberta ecosystem (boreal forest or grassland). Ask: "What did you see?" List on board. Separate into living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) — let them do this themselves.
Say "An ecosystem isn't just the animals — it's everything, including the water, the soil, the temperature, the light, and all the invisible organisms. A list of species is not an ecosystem."
Cue Pre-knowledge check (page 1 of notes) — do NOT correct. Collect observations of what students believe before instruction. These are your teaching targets.
Notes Section 1, vocabulary table and ecosystems definition.
Lesson 2 — Alberta ecosystems
Show Map of Alberta's five ecosystem types. Images of each. Ask students to predict: which abiotic factors differ between grassland and boreal forest?
Say "The abiotic factors set the rules. Everything living in an ecosystem has adapted to those specific conditions. Change the abiotic factors and the biotic community changes."
Cue Exit Ticket 1 at end of class.
Week 2 — Energy Flow
Lesson 3 — Producers, consumers, and decomposers
Show A dead mouse in the forest thought experiment: "What happens to this mouse over the next year? Name every organism involved."
Say "Decomposers are not cleanup crew. They are the nutrient recyclers — without them, nutrients lock up permanently in dead matter and producers starve." — Address decomposer misconception before moving on.
Notes Section 2, vocabulary table, guided notes on trophic levels.
Lesson 4 — Building food webs
Show Give students a set of organism cards (boreal forest species). Have them arrange and connect them with arrows. Compare webs across groups.
Say "The arrow means energy — it flows FROM what is eaten TO what eats it. The arrow is not 'who chases whom.'"
Cue "Why is the food web harder to draw than a food chain?" — discussion, not written question.
Lesson 5 — Food webs in Alberta
Show A real Alberta food web diagram (boreal forest or grassland, with 8–10 species). Ask: "What happens if we remove beavers? Trace every chain that includes them."
Say "Removing one species from a food web rarely affects only its immediate predator or prey. The effects cascade."
Notes Section 2 continued. Exit Ticket 2.
Week 3 — Energy Pyramids
Lesson 6 — The 10% rule
Show 1000 kg of grass → how much deer? → how much wolf? Walk through the calculation visually, not mathematically. A stack of 1000 paperclips at the base → 100 → 10 → 1.
Say "Most of the energy at each level is lost as heat — used for movement, body maintenance, digestion. Only ~10% passes on. This is not inefficiency — it's physics."
Cue Address misconception: "Top predators have the most energy." The pyramid inverts what students expect.
Lesson 7 — Energy pyramid and ecosystem implications
Show Why are there so few wolves compared to moose? Map onto the pyramid. Then: "What happens to the wolf population if the moose population drops by 50%?"
Say "Large territories exist because there isn't much energy at the top. A wolf doesn't have a large territory because it enjoys hiking — it needs that much area to find enough food."
Notes Section 3. Exit Ticket 3.
Week 4 — Biodiversity
Lesson 8 — What is biodiversity?
Show Monoculture field vs. wild meadow images. Ask: "Which is more resilient to a pest? Why?"
Say "Biodiversity is insurance. If 30 species eat grasshoppers, a disease that kills grasshoppers doesn't collapse the ecosystem. If only one species does, the collapse cascades."
Notes Section 4, vocabulary.
Lesson 9 — Keystone species
Show Wolf reintroduction at Yellowstone (Alberta analog works too). Tell the story from wolf removal to ecosystem collapse to reintroduction. Ask students to map each cause onto a food web.
Say "A keystone species is like a load-bearing wall. Remove it and everything else shifts. The wolf doesn't just eat elk — it changes elk behaviour, which changes vegetation, which changes beavers, which changes rivers."
Cue "What keystone species might be most important in a boreal forest? Why?" — pair-share, not collected.
Notes Section 4 continued. Exit Ticket 4.
Week 5 — Environmental Change
Lesson 10 — Natural disturbances and succession
Show Fire in a boreal forest — before and after photos (5 years post-fire). Ask: "Is this ecosystem destroyed?" Let them predict recovery timeline.
Say "Natural disturbances are part of the system. Boreal forest fires are actually necessary — jack pine cones only open and release seeds after exposure to fire. The forest evolved with fire."
Notes Section 5, first half.
Lesson 11 — Human-caused change and invasive species
Show Zebra mussel prevention video (Alberta government). Show purple loosestrife choking a wetland vs. a healthy native wetland.
Say "Invasive species are not just aggressive competitors. They succeed because they escaped the predators and parasites that controlled them in their home ecosystem. We import only the competitor, not the controls."
Cue Tipping point concept — briefly: "Some changes can't be undone. Name one example you already know about."
Notes Section 5, second half. Exit Ticket 5.
Lesson 12 — Alberta species at risk
Show Table of Alberta species at risk. Students choose one, do a 10-minute research sprint, then share one fact with the class.
Say "Being 'at risk' doesn't mean endangered everywhere — it means the specific population or subspecies in Canada/Alberta is threatened. Context matters."
Week 6 — Sustainability and Stewardship
Lesson 13 — Ecosystem services
Show "If the boreal forest disappeared tomorrow, what would it cost?" Walk through water filtration, carbon storage, biodiversity. No forest = water treatment plant.
Say "Ecosystem services are invisible until they're gone. We don't pay for the air we breathe or the water filtering that wetlands do. That doesn't mean they have no value — it means we're not charging ourselves the true cost of destroying them."
Notes Section 6, ecosystem services.
Lesson 14 — TEK and Indigenous stewardship
Show Invite a Knowledge Keeper if possible — or use video of Blackfoot burning practices, Cree land relationships. Frame TEK as a different kind of knowledge production, not a lesser kind.
Say "TEK is built from thousands of years of observation in one specific place. Western ecology has been practising for about 200 years globally. These are complementary — not competing — ways of knowing."
Cue "What does it mean to be a steward of an ecosystem rather than just a user of it?" — written reflection, not collected.
Notes Section 6 continued. Exit Ticket 6.
Week 7 — Review and Assessment
Lesson 15 — Retrieval practice day
• Use workbook Parts C and D as in-class practice. Students work independently first, then pair-check answers. Teacher circulates to address remaining gaps.
Lesson 16 — Unit synthesis
• Students complete the notes package Unit Synthesis page from memory (notes closed).
• Students return to their Pre-Knowledge Check on page 1 and write responses to the final prompts.
• Collect — read for revision targets before the test.
Lesson 17 — Test

Misconception Management

Misconception When to address Strategy
Decomposers are not important Lesson 3, before notes State the wrong idea; use "dead mouse in forest" scenario; show what happens without decomposers
Energy increases up the food chain Lesson 6, before pyramid notes Paperclip visual; invert student expectation before explaining
Nature always recovers Lesson 11, before environmental change Show tipping point examples; contrast fire (recovers) with permafrost loss or coral bleaching (doesn't)
Humans are external to ecosystems Lesson 1 opener + Lesson 13 Frame from Day 1; reinforce in ecosystem services lesson

Lab and Activity Notes

Food web card activity (Lesson 4):

  • Cards needed: aspen, willow, grass, snowshoe hare, moose, beaver, lynx, wolf, great horned owl, raven, decomposer bacteria, mushrooms.
  • Ensure cards include both plant and animal cards. Raven is important — it is an omnivore and scavenger that links many chains.

Ecosystem services calculation (Lesson 13):

  • Have students look up the cost of a municipal water treatment plant ($50–200M CAD). Contrast with a wetland that does the same job for free. Then ask: what is the wetland "worth"?

Assessment Notes

Quiz (mid-unit after Section 3): Covers ecosystems, energy flow, and energy pyramids — all of LO 7-1-01 and 7-1-02 (partially).

Test: All five LOs. Extended response must include at least one food web interpretation question, one environmental change scenario, and one claim-with-evidence question.

Exit tickets: Check before next class. If more than 25% of students miss the decomposer question on ET2, reteach at the start of Lesson 5 before moving on.

Differentiation Notes

Students who need more support:

  • Vocabulary cards with images for biotic/abiotic sorting
  • Pre-built food web with blanks (organisms given, students draw arrows only)
  • Energy pyramid with numbers pre-filled for the base; students calculate upward

Students ready for extension:

  • Lotka-Volterra predator-prey graphs (conceptual introduction only — graph reading, not equations)
  • Tipping point research: find one real ecosystem that crossed a tipping point and document the cascade
  • Design a monitoring program for one Alberta species at risk
