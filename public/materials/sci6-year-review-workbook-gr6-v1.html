About this workbook

This workbook reviews everything you learned in Grade 6 Science. The questions are mixed up on purpose. You will not know which unit a question is from until you read it. Figuring that out is part of the review.

★ Core Core questions — for everyone. Some include a sentence starter or hint. Try all Core questions first.

★★ Extended Extended questions — same ideas, harder demand. These ask you to explain, find errors, or connect ideas. Try these after finishing the Core questions.

Stretch Zone (last section) — questions that connect two or more units. Try these only after finishing all earlier questions.

Stretch Zone

Try these only after completing all previous questions. These questions ask you to connect ideas from two or more units.

S1 ★★ Matter Forces
The railway track near Lethbridge is made of sections of steel rail, each 25 m long. In summer, each section expands by about 1.5 cm. Engineers install small expansion gaps between sections to allow for this.
(a) Use the particle model to explain what causes the steel to expand.
(b) When two sections press against each other at a closed expansion gap, what type of internal force is acting at the contact point between them? How do you know?
(c) If the rails were welded solid with no gap, what would eventually happen to the track? What type of structural failure would occur? Name the internal force involved.
S2 ★★ Living Systems Climate
The boreal forest near Fort McMurray is one of the largest intact forest ecosystems on Earth.
(a) Use your knowledge of photosynthesis to explain how a healthy boreal forest slows the buildup of CO₂ in the atmosphere.
(b) When the forest burns in a wildfire, dead trees release large amounts of CO₂. At the same time, there are fewer living trees to absorb CO₂. How does this create a feedback loop that speeds up climate change?
(c) How does this connection between Living Systems and Climate make the boreal forest important beyond just Alberta?
S3 ★★ Energy Resources Scientific Methods
The Alberta town council wants to check whether switching to wind energy is scientifically sound. They hired a research team. They also invited Elders from the local Métis community to share their knowledge.
(a) Design a scientific process for testing whether wind energy is available and workable for this town's valley. What data would you collect? What would a controlled approach look like?
(b) A community Elder notes that wind patterns in this valley have shifted noticeably over the past generation — the main wind direction has changed. How could this traditional knowledge improve the research team's study in a way that instruments alone might miss?
S4 ★★ Forces Living Systems
A large spruce tree in the boreal forest bends a lot in a powerful windstorm but does not break. A smaller, dead branch on the same tree does snap.
(a) Name and describe the internal forces acting on the living tree trunk as it bends.
(b) The living trunk returns to its upright position when the wind stops. What property of the living wood makes this possible?
(c) The dead branch snapped for good. What property has been exceeded? What is different about dead wood that makes this more likely?
S5 ★★ Matter Living Systems Computer Science
Both the particle model of matter and the photosynthesis process model are simplified models. They do not show every detail of what is actually happening at the molecular level.
(a) In your Computer Science work this year, you learned a specific term for making a simplified version of something complex. What is that term?
(b) The particle model does not show how electrons interact. The photosynthesis model does not show ATP, the Calvin cycle, or electron transport chains. Why are these simplified models still useful and correct enough, even though they are incomplete? Give one reason that applies to both models.
(c) When does a simplified model become a problem? Describe one situation where using an oversimplified model could lead to an incorrect prediction.
