Answer Key — Statistics and Scatter Plots Exit Tickets

Section 1

1. Scatter plot. Reason: we have two numerical variables (hours of sleep, test score) and we want to see if there is a relationship between them. A scatter plot plots paired data to reveal patterns, trends, or correlations.

2. Scatter plots answer questions about the relationship between two numerical variables (correlation, trend, prediction). A bar graph can only show totals or comparisons of categories — it can't show how one variable changes with another. Example: "Does outside temperature affect attendance at the local swimming pool?" — you'd plot temperature (x) against attendance (y) to see the pattern.

Section 2

1. (a) Strong positive correlation — as one variable increases, the other increases consistently. (b) Strong negative correlation — as one increases, the other decreases consistently. (c) No correlation (zero correlation) — no pattern between the variables.

2. Strong correlation: points cluster closely around a line (easy to predict one variable from the other). Weak correlation: points are spread widely around a trend line (less predictable). Yes, strong negative correlation exists — example: as the number of hours spent playing video games increases, academic performance decreases (if measured, would show a negative trend with consistent scatter).

Section 3

1. slope = (14 − 4)/(5 − 0) = 10/5 = 2. y-intercept = 4. Equation: y = 2x + 4. The y-intercept is the predicted value of y when x = 0 — it represents the starting value before any change in x occurs.

2. (1) The line should pass through or near the middle of the data — roughly half the points above and half below the line. (2) The line should follow the overall direction of the data — minimizing the total vertical distance from all points to the line.

Section 4

1. (a) y = 3(5) + 2 = 17 km/day. (b) y = 3(100) + 2 = 302 km/day.

2. Extrapolation is less reliable because the relationship between variables may not continue to hold outside the range of observed data — the pattern could change, plateau, or reverse. Example: predicting a person will run 302 km/day after 100 weeks is physically impossible. Or: predicting a baby's height at age 50 by extrapolating their growth rate from infancy would give a height of several metres.
