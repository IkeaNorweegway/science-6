4. Line of best fit equation

(Answers vary by student's line. Sample calculation using points (5, 1.5) and (30, 6.5):)

m = (6.5 − 1.5)/(30 − 5) = 5/25 = 0.2

b: 1.5 = 0.2(5) + b → b = 0.5

Equation: y = 0.2x + 0.5 (accept any reasonable line)

5. Predictions using y = 0.2x + 0.5

(a) Temperature = 18°C:

y = 0.2(18) + 0.5 = 3.6 + 0.5 = 4.1 hours

Interpolation — 18°C is within the data range (5–30°C)

(b) Temperature = 40°C:

y = 0.2(40) + 0.5 = 8.5 hours

Extrapolation — 40°C is above the data range. Less reliable — we don't know if people continue to increase outdoor time at extreme heat, or if they stay indoors.

(c) Temperature = −5°C:

y = 0.2(−5) + 0.5 = −0.5 hours

Problem: negative activity hours is impossible. The linear model breaks down below freezing — the real relationship is non-linear (no one goes outside for negative hours). This is an extrapolation producing a nonsensical result.

6. Correlation and causation — ice cream vs. crime

(a)

Not valid. Correlation does not equal causation. Just because two things rise together doesn't mean one causes the other.

(b) Third variable (confounder):

Summer/hot weather. In summer, people buy more ice cream (it's hot) AND more people are outdoors, creating more opportunity for crime. Neither causes the other.

(c) To establish causation:

A controlled experiment ruling out confounders, a plausible direct mechanism, and replication across contexts.

7. y = 8x + 45 (study time vs. score)

(a) Slope (8):

For each additional hour of studying, the predicted test score increases by approximately 8 percentage points.

(b) y-intercept (45):

The predicted test score with 0 hours of studying is 45% — the baseline score without preparation.

(c) Predict at x = 0:

y = 45%. Somewhat reasonable (students can answer some questions without studying), but this is an extrapolation if the data was only collected for students who studied. The model may not accurately reflect performance with zero study time.

8. Outlier identification — exercise vs. heart rate

(a) Outlier:

(2, 90) — 2 hours exercise with 90 bpm is far above the trend (the other 2-hour person has 75 bpm).

(b) Without the outlier:

Strong negative correlation — more exercise consistently associated with lower resting heart rate.

(c) Possible explanation:

Underlying health condition (arrhythmia), illness at time of measurement, high caffeine intake, stress, or a fitness tracker measurement error.

10. Causal reasoning

Height ↔ shoe size: More reasonable to claim a causal relationship. Biological mechanism exists — taller people have larger skeletal frames including longer feet. Direct physical link.

Ice cream ↔ drowning: NOT causal. No mechanism. Both driven by summer/heat. Claiming causation here would be the correlation ≠ causation error.

Even for height/shoe size, a "cause" claim requires care — both are driven by growth/genetics, so they may both be effects of a common cause rather than one causing the other.
