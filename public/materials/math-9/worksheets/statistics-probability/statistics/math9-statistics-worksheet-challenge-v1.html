1. Error analysis — three student claims.

(a) "My line of best fit passes through 5 of the 8 data points, so it's a good fit."

Explain why passing through many points does NOT indicate a good line of best fit.

(b) "The slope of my line is 3.2. This means the data has a positive correlation."

Is this a complete description of the correlation? What else is needed?

(c) "A study found r = 0.98 between two variables. This proves one causes the other."

What is wrong with this reasoning? Provide a counter-example.

5. Limitations of extrapolation.

A line of best fit for tree height (m) vs. age (years) is: h = 0.8a + 2.

The data was collected for trees between 5 and 30 years old.

(a) Predict the height at age 50.

(b) Predict the height at age 100.

(c) Predict the height at age 200.

(d) At what point does the linear model clearly break down? What would you expect tree growth to look like over a very long time (does it stay linear forever)?

(e) Predict the height at age 0 (using the y-intercept). What does this suggest about the model at very young ages?

6. Outlier — to remove or not.

A study of fuel efficiency (L/100km) vs. engine size (L) for 10 cars includes one data point: a sports car with a very large engine but unusually high fuel efficiency (it's a hybrid).

(a) Describe how this outlier might affect the line of best fit.

(b) Make the case for removing it: when does removing an outlier make sense?

(c) Make the case for keeping it: when should an outlier stay in the data?

(d) What would you do in this case? Justify your decision.

7. Reverse reasoning.

A line of best fit has equation y = −5x + 120, and the data range for x is 4 to 18.

(a) What type of correlation does this represent?

(b) What is a real-world context that could produce this equation? (Invent one that makes sense.)

(c) What is the prediction for x = 10? Is it interpolation or extrapolation?

(d) For what value of x does y = 0? What does this mean in your context?

Reflection: What is the most dangerous misuse of statistics you've seen or can imagine — where correlation was mistaken for causation with real consequences?
