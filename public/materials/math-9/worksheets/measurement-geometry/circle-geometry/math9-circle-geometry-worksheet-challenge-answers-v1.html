1. Error analysis

(a) "Any inscribed angle = 90°":
What the student misremembered: The inscribed angle theorem states inscribed angle = ½ × central angle (for the same arc). The 90° result is a special case only.

Correct theorem: An inscribed angle equals half the central angle subtending the same arc.

When 90° applies: Only when the inscribed angle subtends a diameter (a semicircle). Central angle for a diameter = 180°, so inscribed = 180°/2 = 90°. If the arc is not a semicircle, the inscribed angle will not be 90°.
(b) Chord 20 cm in circle with radius 9 cm — negative result:
What the negative result means geometrically: A chord of 20 cm has half-length 10 cm. The perpendicular distance from centre to chord cannot exceed the radius (9 cm). A half-chord of 10 cm > radius of 9 cm is geometrically impossible — the chord would not fit inside the circle. The negative under the square root signals an impossible configuration.

Valid radius: Any radius greater than 10 cm. E.g., with radius 13 cm: OM = √(169−100) = √69 ≈ 8.3 cm ✓

2. Multi-step chord problem — radius 17 cm

(a) Chords AB = CD = 30 cm — distance from centre:
Setup: Half-chord = 15. OM² = 17²−15² = 289−225 = 64.
OM = 8 cm (both chords are 8 cm from centre — equal chords, equal distance)
(b) Chord EF = 16 cm — distance from centre, compare to AB:
Setup: Half of EF = 8. OM² = 17²−8² = 289−64 = 225.
OM = 15 cm

AB is 8 cm from centre; EF is 15 cm from centre. AB is closer to the centre.

Why this makes sense: longer chords are closer to the centre. AB (30 cm) > EF (16 cm), so AB must be closer. The diameter passes through the centre at distance 0, and as chords get shorter, they move farther from the centre.
(c) Maximum possible chord:
The maximum chord is the diameter = 34 cm (= 2 × 17). Distance from centre = 0 (it passes through the centre).

3. Inscribed angle — multi-step

Central AOB = 4x+20°; Inscribed APB = x+20°

(a) Find x:
Setup: Inscribed = ½ × central: x+20 = ½(4x+20) = 2x+10
x+20 = 2x+10 → 10 = x
x = 10
(b) Central and inscribed angles:
Central AOB = 4(10)+20 = 60°
Inscribed APB = 10+20 = 30°
Check: 30 = 60/2 ✓
(c) Inscribed angle AQB:
AQB = 30° — both AQB and APB are inscribed angles subtending the same arc AB. Every inscribed angle subtending the same arc equals ½ the same central angle — so they are equal without additional calculation.
(d) Inscribed angle on the major arc:
Major arc central = 360° − 60° = 300°
Inscribed angle on major arc = 300°/2 = 150°

4. Tangent from external point — radius 5, OP = 13

(a) Tangent lengths PA = PB:
Setup: Right angle at A (tangent ⊥ radius). Hypotenuse = OP = 13.
PA² = 13²−5² = 169−25 = 144
PA = PB = 12 cm
(b) Angle OAP:
= 90° — the tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency (A). This is the defining property of a tangent.
(c) Angles of quadrilateral OAPB:
Sum of angles in any quadrilateral = 360°
Angles at A and B = 90°+90° = 180°
Therefore: angle AOB + angle APB = 360°−180° = 180°
(d) Angle AOB given APB = 67°:
AOB = 180°−67° = 113°

5. Constructing the centre

Method:
  1. Draw any two chords on the circle (choosing non-parallel chords works best).
  2. Find the midpoint of each chord.
  3. Draw a line perpendicular to the first chord through its midpoint.
  4. Draw a line perpendicular to the second chord through its midpoint.
  5. The two perpendicular bisectors intersect at the centre of the circle.
Why this works: By the chord bisector property, the perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre. Two such perpendicular bisectors from different chords uniquely determine a single point — the centre.

6. Combined properties — chord AB = 24 cm, 5 cm from centre; chord CD 13 cm from centre

(a) Find the radius from chord AB:
Setup: Half-chord = 12. r² = 5²+12² = 25+144 = 169.
r = 13 cm
(b) Length of chord CD:
Note: CD is 13 cm from centre, but r = 13 cm — this means the distance from centre equals the radius, which is geometrically impossible for a chord (the chord would have length 0, i.e., a tangent point). This appears to be a typographical error in the worksheet.

Likely intended distance: 12 cm.
If d = 12: half-chord = √(169−144) = √25 = 5. Chord CD = 10 cm.
(c) Inscribed angle APB subtending chord AB from major arc:
Note: Finding the central angle for a specific chord requires trigonometry (sin(θ/2) = half-chord/radius = 12/13 → θ = 2arcsin(12/13) ≈ 135°). This is beyond typical Grade 9 scope without additional given information.

For a teacher-supplied central angle: if central AOB = 134°, inscribed APB on major arc = (360°−134°)/2 = 113°.

7. Why inscribed angles from same arc are equal

By the inscribed angle theorem, every inscribed angle equals exactly half the central angle subtending the same arc.

Since P and Q are both on the same arc (the arc NOT between A and B), both angles APB and AQB subtend the same arc AB. Both are equal to ½ × the central angle AOB — the same central angle, so the same value.

The position of P or Q on the arc doesn't matter — the central angle is fixed by the arc AB alone, and both inscribed angles are half of that same value.

8. Design a problem — model answer

(Accept any problem using Pythagorean theorem + inscribed angle theorem in two connected steps.)

Problem: In a circle with centre O and radius 10 cm, a chord AB = 16 cm. Point P is on the major arc. Find (a) the distance from O to the chord, and (b) the inscribed angle APB if the central angle AOB = 106°.

Solution:
(a) Half-chord = 8. OM = √(100−64) = √36 = 6 cm (Pythagorean theorem)
(b) APB = 106°/2 = 53° (inscribed angle theorem)

Connection: Part (a) confirms the chord fits inside the circle (OM < r). Part (b) uses the known arc (defined by the chord and centre) to find the angle.
