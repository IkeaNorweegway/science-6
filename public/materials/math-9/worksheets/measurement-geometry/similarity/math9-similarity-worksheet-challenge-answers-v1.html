1. Error analysis

(a) △ABC sides 3,4,5 and △DEF sides 6,8,9:
Third ratio: 5/9 ≈ 0.556 | Is it equal to 1/2 = 0.5? No
Error: Checked only two ratios. For SSS similarity, ALL three ratios must be equal.
Not similar — the third pair of sides (5 and 9) is not in proportion 1:2.
(b) Shadows measured at different times:
The sun's elevation angle changes significantly from 10 AM to 2 PM. The shadows at those times correspond to different sun angles, so the two triangles formed are NOT similar to each other. The proportion only works when both shadows are measured simultaneously (same sun angle → AA criterion applies).
(c) △ABC~△FDE — proportion AB/FD=BC/DE:
Statement △ABC~△FDE: A↔F, B↔D, C↔E.
Corresponding sides: AB↔FD and BC↔DE.
The proportion AB/FD=BC/DE is correct for this similarity statement. The student's proportion matches the correspondence.

2. DE ∥ BC, AD=5, AB=15, DE=4

(a) Prove △ADE ~ △ABC:
Angle A is shared by both triangles (common angle).
Since DE∥BC: angle ADE = angle ABC (corresponding angles with parallel lines and transversal AB). Similarly, angle AED = angle ACB.
AA criterion: two pairs of equal angles.
(b) Scale factor from △ADE to △ABC:
AD/AB = 5/15 = 1/3
(c) BC:
DE/BC = 1/3 → 4/BC = 1/3 → BC = 12
(d) DB and EC:
DB = AB − AD = 15 − 5 = 10
EC: AE/AC = 1/3, so AE = AC/3 and EC = 2AC/3. Without AC given, EC cannot be calculated numerically. If BC=12, similar proportions apply but AC is not given.

3. △RST ~ △UVW — RS=10, ST=15, RT=20, angle R=40°, angle S=65°, UV=14

(a) Scale factor: UV/RS = 14/10 = 7/5
(b) Missing sides:
VW = ST × 7/5 = 15 × 7/5 = 21
UW = RT × 7/5 = 20 × 7/5 = 28
(c) Angles:
Angle T = 180−40−65 = 75°
U=R=40° | V=S=65° | W=T=75°
(d) Verify: 40+65+75 = 180° ✓

5. Soccer field 100×70 m on paper 25×20 cm

(a) Largest usable scale:
Length constraint: 100 m fits in 25 cm → 100/25=4 m/cm → scale limit 1:400
Width constraint: 70 m fits in 20 cm → 70/20=3.5 m/cm → scale limit 1:350
The more restrictive constraint is the length (1:400).
Largest scale that fits both: 1:400
(b) Diagram dimensions at 1:400:
Length = 100/400 m = 0.25 m = 25 cm
Width = 70/400 m = 0.175 m = 17.5 cm

6. Two indirect measurement methods

(a) Method A (shadow):
Setup: h/9 = 1.8/1.2 = 3/2
Tree height = 13.5 m
(b) Method B (mirror):
Setup: h/4.5 = 1.7/1.8
Tree height ≈ 4.25 m
(c) Same answer?
⚠️ The two methods give different answers (13.5 m vs 4.25 m) — the worksheet measurements are hypothetical and were generated independently for each method, not for the same tree. In a real scenario, both methods should give the same answer (within measurement error). Small practical differences arise from: positioning the mirror not quite flat, eye height estimation, slight slopes in the ground, or parallax errors. The large difference here indicates the problem uses two separate, unconnected sets of measurements.

7. Scale factors and area — △PQR ~ △XYZ, scale factor 3

(a) Corresponding dimensions in △XYZ:
XY = 4 × 3 = 12 cm | Corresponding height = 3 × 3 = 9 cm
(b) Areas:
Area △PQR = (1/2)(4)(3) = 6 cm²
Area △XYZ = (1/2)(12)(9) = 54 cm²
(c) Area ratio:
54/6 = 9 = 3² = scale factor² ✓
(d) Why areas scale by k²:
Area = ½ × base × height. Both the base and the height scale by k (each is a linear measurement). So area = ½ × (k×base) × (k×height) = k² × (½ × base × height) = k² × original area. Linear dimensions → ×k. Area (2D) → ×k².

8. Banff Springs Hotel, scale 1:200

(a) Model height:
60 m = 6000 cm. Model = 6000/200 = 30 cm
(b) Real room dimensions:
1.2 cm × 200 = 240 cm = 2.4 m
0.8 cm × 200 = 160 cm = 1.6 m
(c) Surface area scale factor:
Lengths scale by 200; areas scale by 200² = 40,000
To get real building SA: multiply model SA by 40,000
(d) Number of windows:
Windows are countable items, not lengths or areas — they do not scale by 200 or 200². The model has the same number of windows as the real building: 10,000. (Each window in the model represents one real window.)
