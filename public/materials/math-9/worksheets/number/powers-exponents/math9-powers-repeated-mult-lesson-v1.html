Did you write 12 for 34? Most people do at first, and it makes sense — the "4" looks like something you'd multiply by. The rest of this lesson explains exactly why that instinct is wrong, and gives you three different ways to see what exponents actually mean.

Explanation 1 — The Operations Staircase

Here is something most people have never been shown directly:

Multiplication is shorthand for repeated addition.

3 × 4 means "add 3, four times":

3 × 4 = 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12

Exponentiation is shorthand for repeated multiplication.

34 means "multiply 3, four times":

34 = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3 = 81

Look at the staircase:

LevelOperationWrittenWhat it means
1Repeated addition3 + 3 + 3 + 3Add 3, four times
2 (shorthand)Multiplication3 × 4Shorthand for level 1
3Repeated multiplication3 × 3 × 3 × 3Multiply 3, four times
4 (shorthand)Exponentiation34Shorthand for level 3

The key insight: The 4 in both 3 × 4 and 34 means the same thing — "four repetitions." What changes is what you are repeating. In 3 × 4 you repeat addition. In 34 you repeat multiplication. Same count, different operation.

Explanation 2 — The Growth Machine

This explanation gives you a reliable step-by-step procedure. Use it any time you need to evaluate a power and you lose track.

The rule:

  1. Start at 1.
  2. The base is the multiplier. The exponent is how many times you apply it.
  3. Multiply step by step, keeping a running total.

Example: Evaluate 34

StepRunning totalWhat happened
Start1Neutral starting point
Apply ×3 once3This is 31
Apply ×3 again9This is 32
Apply ×3 again27This is 33
Apply ×3 again81This is 34

Why start at 1? Because 1 is the identity element for multiplication — it doesn't change anything. Starting at 1 and multiplying zero times gives you 1. That is not a coincidence: it is why a0 = 1 for any nonzero base.

Explanation 3 — Count the Factors

Here is the source of the confusion: the word "times." In everyday language, "3, four times" means 3 + 3 + 3 + 3. In exponents, we need a different word.

Replace "times" with "factors."

A factor is a number being multiplied. When you see:

3 · 3 · 3 · 3

...you have four factors of three. The exponent counts the factors, not the additions.

ExpressionSay thisNot this
34Three, as a factor, four timesThree times four
25Two, as a factor, five timesTwo times five

Why this matters for the error: 3 × 4 = 12 uses three as an addend four times (3+3+3+3). But 34 = 81 uses three as a factor four times (3·3·3·3). The 4 has the same meaning in both — but the question is: factor of what kind of operation?
