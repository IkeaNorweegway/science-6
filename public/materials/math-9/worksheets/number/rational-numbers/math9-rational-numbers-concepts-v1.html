Rational Numbers

Concept Reference

Key Vocabulary

TermMeaningExample
Rational numberAny number written as a/b where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0−3/4, 5, 0.25
PrincipalThe positive version (used with square roots and fractions)
LCDLeast common denominator — the smallest shared denominatorLCD of 1/4 and 1/6 is 12
ReciprocalFlip numerator and denominatorReciprocal of 2/3 is 3/2
BEDMASOrder of operations: Brackets, Exponents, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction

Key Rules

Comparing Negatives

On the number line, the number closer to zero is greater.

−1/4 > −3/4   (−1/4 is closer to zero)

Adding / Subtracting Fractions

Find LCD first. Only add/subtract numerators.

1/2 + 1/3 = 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6 ✓    NOT 2/5 ✗

Multiplying Fractions

Multiply numerators, multiply denominators. Sign rules apply.

(−3/4) × (2/5) = −6/20 = −3/10

Dividing Fractions

Multiply by the reciprocal of the divisor.

(3/4) ÷ (2/5) = (3/4) × (5/2) = 15/8

Sign Rules
  • Negative × Negative = Positive
  • Negative × Positive = Negative
  • Count negative factors: odd count → negative result

Worked Example 1 — Adding with unlike denominators

Find: −3/4 + 5/6

Step 1: LCD of 4 and 6 = 12

Step 2: Rewrite: −3/4 = −9/12    5/6 = 10/12

Step 3: −9/12 + 10/12 = 1/12

Why do we find the LCD? Denominators name the size of pieces. You cannot add pieces of different sizes until they're the same size.

Worked Example 2 — Dividing rational numbers

Find: (−5/6) ÷ (3/4)

Step 1: Reciprocal of 3/4 is 4/3

Step 2: (−5/6) × (4/3) = −20/18 = −10/9

Step 3: Sign: negative × positive = negative ✓

Why does dividing by a fraction less than 1 give a larger result? Dividing by 3/4 asks "how many 3/4-pieces fit in this number?" More fit, so the result is larger.

Worked Example 3 — BEDMAS with rationals

Evaluate: (−1/2 + 1/4)2 ÷ (−3/4)

B: −1/2 + 1/4 = −2/4 + 1/4 = −1/4

E: (−1/4)2 = 1/16

D: (1/16) ÷ (−3/4) = (1/16) × (−4/3) = −4/48 = −1/12
