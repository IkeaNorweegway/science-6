3. Estimating square roots

(a) √28

52 = 25 < 28 < 36 = 62, so 5 < √28 < 6
28 is closer to 25 (distance 3) than to 36 (distance 8)
√28 ≈ 5.3

(b) √63

72 = 49 < 63 < 64 = 82, so 7 < √63 < 8
63 is very close to 64 (distance 1)
√63 ≈ 7.9

(c) √95

92 = 81 < 95 < 100 = 102, so 9 < √95 < 10
95 is closer to 100 (distance 5) than to 81 (distance 14)
√95 ≈ 9.7

Number line: √28 ≈ 5.3 | √63 ≈ 7.9 | √95 ≈ 9.7

6. Rational or irrational?

(a) √100

Rational — because √100 = 10, and 10 is an integer (expressible as 10/1)

(b) √2

Irrational — because 2 is not a perfect square; its decimal expansion (1.41421...) never terminates or repeats

(c) √(1/4)

Rational — because √(1/4) = 1/2, which is already a ratio of integers

7. √(9+16) ≠ √9 + √16

Left side: √(9+16) = √25 = 5

Right side: √9 + √16 = 3 + 4 = 7

Equal? No — 5 ≠ 7

Disproves: the rule √(a+b) = √a + √b — radicals do NOT distribute over addition

Common Mistake

This error is especially tempting in Pythagorean contexts. √(a2 + b2) ≠ a + b.

8. Alberta context — Lethbridge park path

(a) Diagonal length

d = √(602 + 802)
= √(3600 + 6400)
= √10000
= 100 m

Common Mistake

Students sometimes add 60 + 80 = 140 without squaring. The diagonal is shorter than the perimeter path (100 < 140) — this makes physical sense.

(b) Rational or irrational?

Rational — √10000 = 100, a whole number (60-80-100 is a 3-4-5 triple × 20)

9. Connecting to exponents

(a) 251/2 = √25 = 5

(b) 641/2 = √64 = 8

(c) (1/9)1/2 = √(1/9) = 1/3

(d) (√7)2 = 7

Explanation: squaring and square-rooting are inverse operations. (√7)2 = (71/2)2 = 71 = 7.

10. Ladder problems

(a) Ladder 15 m, base 9 m from wall

h2 + 92 = 152
h2 + 81 = 225
h2 = 144
h = 12 m

(b) Base moved to 12 m

h2 + 122 = 152
h2 + 144 = 225
h2 = 81
h = 9 m

Note: moving the base further out lowers the height — trade-off between horizontal reach and vertical reach.
