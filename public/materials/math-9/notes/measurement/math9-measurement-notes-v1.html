Guided Notes

Surface area = total area of all outer faces.

Strategy: draw the net (unfold the 3D shape into 2D).

Right Prism — general formula:

SA = 2 × (area of base) + × (perimeter of base)

The lateral surface area = of the base × height of prism

(Think: unroll the sides into a rectangle with width = height and length = perimeter of base)

Worked Example 1 — Rectangular prism:

Dimensions: l = 5 cm, w = 3 cm, h = 4 cm

Net: three pairs of faces

Two l × w faces: 2 × (5 × 3) = cm2

Two l × h faces: 2 × (5 × 4) = cm2

Two w × h faces: 2 × (3 × 4) = cm2

SA = + + = cm2

Explain: Why does a rectangular prism have 6 faces? Why do they come in pairs?

Misconception

Surface area and volume are the same measurement

Wrong idea: "A bigger box has more surface area AND more volume, so they're basically the same thing."

Why it fails: Surface area measures the total outer area in cm2 (or m2). Volume measures the space inside in cm3 (or m3). A flat sheet of metal can have a huge surface area but almost zero volume. A thick cube can have the same surface area as a flat box but much more volume. They measure fundamentally different things.

Verify: A cube with side 2 cm. SA = 6 × 4 = cm2. Volume = 23 = cm3. Different units, different values.

  2. A cylinder: r = 2.5 m, h = 6 m. Find SA (leave in terms of π, then evaluate).

Section 4 — Surface Area and Volume of Pyramids, Cones, and Spheres

Learning target: I can apply the formulas for pyramids, cones, and spheres, and explain why pyramid/cone volume is one-third of the corresponding prism/cylinder.

Vocabulary

TermDefinition in your own wordsOne example
Pyramid

Cone
Sphere

Guided Notes

Pyramid — Surface Area:

SA = area of + area of triangular faces

For a square-based pyramid (base side s, slant height l):
SA = s2 + 4 × (1/2 × s × l) = s2 + 2sl

Pyramid — Volume:

V = (1/3) × B × h

where B = base area, h = perpendicular height (NOT slant height)

Key insight: Volume of pyramid = ⅓ × volume of matching prism

A square pyramid with the same base and height as a square prism fits exactly times inside the prism. This is not a coincidence — it is a geometric fact.

Similarly, a cone fits times inside a cylinder with the same base and height.

Worked Example 1 — Square-based pyramid:

Base 6 cm × 6 cm, perpendicular height 4 cm, slant height 5 cm.

Base area = 6 × 6 = cm2

SA = + 2(6)(5) = + = cm2

V = (1/3)(36)(4) = cm3

Explain: The slant height (5 cm) is used for surface area but NOT for volume. Why?

Cone — Surface Area and Volume:

SA = πr2 + πrl

  • πr2 = area of the circular
  • πrl = lateral surface area (l = slant height)

V = (1/3)πr2h

  • h = perpendicular height, NOT slant height

Finding slant height from perpendicular height:

l2 = r2 + h2 (Pythagorean theorem — l is the hypotenuse)

r = 6 cm.

Misconception

A cone has half the volume of a cylinder

Wrong idea: "The cone fits inside the cylinder — it must take up half the space."

Why it fails: A cone takes up exactly one-third of the matching cylinder's volume. You can demonstrate this by filling a cone and pouring into the cylinder: you need exactly 3 cone-fulls to fill the cylinder.

The factor (1/3) in the formula is not an estimate — it is precise.

Section 5 — Composite Objects

Learning target: I can find the surface area and volume of composite 3D objects by adding and subtracting the appropriate parts.

Vocabulary

TermDefinition in your own wordsOne example
Composite object
Hidden face
Overlapping surface

Guided Notes

Composite objects are built by combining or removing basic 3D shapes.

The key rule: When two shapes are joined, the faces at the joint are — they are inside the object and not part of the outer surface.

Strategy for surface area:

  1. Identify each component shape
  2. Calculate the full SA of each
  3. Subtract the faces (the joined faces, twice — once from each shape)

Strategy for volume:

  1. Calculate the volume of each component
  2. the volumes (or subtract if one is removed)

Worked Example:

A cylinder (r = 4 cm, h = 10 cm) sits on top of a rectangular prism (20 cm × 20 cm × 5 cm).

Volume:

V = Volume of prism + Volume of cylinder
= (20)(20)(5) + π(4)2(10)
= + π
≈ cm3

Surface area:

The circular base of the cylinder sits on top of the rectangular prism — that circle is hidden on BOTH shapes.

Hidden area = area of one circle = π(4)2 = π cm2

SA = SA of prism − circle on top + SA of cylinder − circle on bottom

SA of prism = 2(20×20) + 4(20×5) = + = cm2
  Subtract top circle: cm2 − π ≈ cm2

SA of cylinder = 2π(4)2 + 2π(4)(10) = π + π = π cm2
  Subtract bottom circle: π − π = π cm2

Total SA ≈ + ≈ cm2

Explain: Why do we subtract the hidden circle from BOTH the prism's surface area and the cylinder's surface area?

Common mistake — forgetting hidden faces:

A student calculates the SA of two boxes glued together by adding both full surface areas. What does this overcount?

How much should be subtracted?
