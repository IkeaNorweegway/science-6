Math 9 — Surface Area and Volume

Teacher Notes Package

Teaching reference — not a script

How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

Label What it contains
SHOW What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAY Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUE The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩︎ Check

Flags appear in callout blocks: > FLAG — Misconception: Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address. > FLAG — Pacing: Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum. > FLAG — Alberta Context: Real-world grounding — worth pausing on briefly to build relevance.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual — formulas, diagrams, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Pre-Knowledge Check

~10 minutes — before any instruction

Beat 1 — Opening SHOW: Board: “What is the difference between surface area and volume? Describe each in your own words.” (Nothing else on the board.) SAY: - “Before we start the unit, I want a snapshot of what’s already in your head.” - “No marks. No correction. No judgment.” - “Write honestly — what you actually think right now. You will come back to this page at the very end of the unit.” - “Don’t change your answers once you start. That’s the whole point.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open to the Pre-Knowledge Check. Answer all three prompts. You have 5 minutes. Go.”

FLAG — PacingDo not give hints and do not comment on what students are writing. Circulate and observe. You are looking for two things: (a) students who already have a clear sense that SA and volume are different — they will move faster through Section 2; (b) students who describe them as measuring “the same thing” — that misconception needs the direct FLAG address in Section 2. Note who falls in each group.

Section 1 — Review: 2D Area Formulas

Learning target: I can recall and apply area formulas for rectangles, triangles, parallelograms, and circles — these are the building blocks of all 3D surface area calculations. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: The four vocabulary terms in a column — Area / Perimeter / Composite shape / π (pi) SAY: - “These four terms are in your vocab table.” - “Don’t fill them in yet — we’re building them as we go.” - “By the end of this section you’ll have your own definition and example for each one.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read the four terms. Which one do you already have a solid definition for? Keep it in your head.”

Beat 2 — Area formula table SHOW: Board: Draw a clean table: Shape | Formula | Key variable. Fill in each row live.

Shape Formula Key variable
Rectangle A = l × w —
Triangle A = (b × h) / 2 h = perpendicular height
Parallelogram A = b × h h = perpendicular height
Circle A = π × r² r = radius
Trapezoid A = (a + b)/2 × h a, b = parallel sides

SAY: - “These formulas are the building blocks for everything in this unit. Know them cold.” - “Rectangle: length times width. No exceptions.” - “Triangle: base times height, divide by two — the divide by two is because a triangle is half a parallelogram.” - “Circle: pi times radius squared.” - “π is approximately [3.14]. Your calculator has a π key — use it for final answers, use 3.14 for estimates.” CUE: ★ Write: “h = [perpendicular] height” and “π = [3.14] (to 2 decimal places)”

Beat 3 — Perpendicular height SHOW: Board: Draw a triangle. Draw two possible heights — one as the slant side, one as the true perpendicular height. Label the perpendicular height “h” with a small right-angle box. Label the slant side with an X through it. SAY: - “The most common error in this unit starts right here — height means perpendicular height.” - “Not the slant side. Not the longest side. The vertical drop from the apex to the base.” - “Your diagram in the notes shows this for a parallelogram and a triangle.” - “If the question gives you the slant height, it does NOT go into the area formula — it only appears in surface area of pyramids and cones, which is Section 4.” CUE: ★ Write: “The height in area formulas is always the [perpendicular] distance from the base to the opposite vertex. It is NOT the slant side.”

FLAG — MisconceptionThe perpendicular vs. slant height confusion costs students marks in every section of this unit. It appears in triangles (Section 1), triangular prisms (Section 2), pyramids and cones (Section 4), and composite objects (Section 5). Address it once here, clearly, and call back to it every time it reappears.

Beat 4 — Worked example: composite 2D shape SHOW: Board: Draw a rectangle (4 cm × 6 cm) with a semicircle sitting on top (diameter 4 cm). Label dimensions. SAY: - “Composite 2D shape — made of a rectangle and a semicircle.” - “Strategy: identify the parts, calculate each area, add them.” - “Rectangle: [4] × [6] = [24] cm².” - “Semicircle: the diameter is 4 cm, so the radius is [2] cm.” - “A semicircle is half a full circle. So the formula is (1/2) × π × r².” - “(1/2) × π × [2]² = (1/2) × π × [4] ≈ [6.28] cm².” - “Total: [24] + [6.28] ≈ [30.28] cm².” CUE: ★ Write: Fill in all blanks in the Worked Example.

Beat 5 — Self-explanation: why (1/2)? SHOW: Board: Full circle formula A = πr². Below it: semicircle = (1/2) × πr². SAY: - “Before we move on — explain this step in your own words.” - “Why do we use (1/2) × πr² for the semicircle rather than the full formula?” - “Don’t copy the question — write what you actually think the reason is.” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the ‘explain’ question in your notes now — one or two sentences, in your own words.”

Allow 60–90 seconds. Expected: a semicircle is half a circle, so we take half the full area. If students write “because it’s a semi” without the reasoning, prompt: “Why is dividing by 2 the right operation — not some other fraction?”

Beat 6 — Practise table SHOW: Board: The four practice shapes listed — Triangle (b = 8, h = 5), Circle (r = 3), Rectangle (l = 12, w = 4.5), Parallelogram (b = 10, h = 7). SAY: - “Work through the four shapes in your practise table.” - “Show the substitution step. Don’t just write the answer.” - Answers: Triangle = [20] cm², Circle = [9π ≈ 28.27] cm², Rectangle = [54] m², Parallelogram = [70] m². CUE: ★ Write: “Complete the practise table — show substitution for each.”

Circulate. Common errors: forgetting to divide by 2 for the triangle, using diameter instead of radius for the circle. Correct individually rather than stopping the class — a brief “which value is r?” is usually enough.

Beat 7 — Vocabulary table fill-in SHOW: Board: The four vocabulary terms still on the board. SAY: - “Now go back to your vocabulary table.” - “Fill in each definition in your own words.” - “Your definition should make sense to someone who missed class.” - “Add one example you came up with yourself.” CUE: ★ Write: “Complete your vocab table now — own words, own examples.”

Allow 3 minutes. Watch for students leaving π blank or writing “3.14” as the definition rather than explaining what pi represents.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed. Answer the five questions from memory — write what you know, leave blank what you don’t.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish (4–5 min): SAY: “Open your notes. Find each answer. Fix anything that was wrong — use a different colour if you have one.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. A = πr² = π(6)² = 36π ≈ 113.10 cm². Both forms required — exact (36π) and evaluated (113.10). 2. A = (14 × 9)/2 = 63 m². 3. The slant height is the side of the triangle, not the vertical drop from apex to base. Area measures the space enclosed, which requires the perpendicular distance between the base and the apex. Using slant height gives a larger (incorrect) value. 4. Diameter = 10 cm, so r = 5 cm. Semicircle = (1/2) × π × 5² = (1/2) × 25π ≈ 39.27 cm². 5. (Stretch) Square area = 8² = 64 cm². Quarter-circle area = (1/4) × π × 8² = 16π ≈ 50.27 cm². Remaining area = 64 − 50.27 ≈ 13.73 cm².

Q3 and Q5 are the stretch items. Students who finish early: prompt them to draw a diagram for Q5 before calculating. Drawing the shape first almost always leads to the correct operation.

Section 2 — Surface Area of Right Prisms and Cylinders

Learning target: I can find the surface area of right prisms and cylinders by identifying and summing all faces. Timing: ~30 minutes direct instruction

Bring to class: unfolded cardboard box (net); cylindrical can.

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Six terms in a column — Surface area / Right prism / Cylinder / Net / Lateral surface / Rectangular prism. SAY: - “Six vocabulary terms. Don’t fill them in yet.” - “Key preview: the word ‘net’ here does not mean a fishing net or a hockey net.” - “In geometry, a net is what you get when you unfold a 3D shape into 2D.” - Hold up the unfolded cardboard box. “This is a net.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Look at the six terms. Which one is new to you? Keep it in your head.”

Beat 2 — What is surface area? SHOW: Board: Draw a cube. Label: Surface area = total area of all outer faces. SAY: - “Surface area is exactly what it sounds like — the total area of the surface.” - “Every face, every face, every face. Add them all.” - “Units are square units — cm², m². Always 2D units, because area is 2D.” - “This is important: surface area is NOT volume. Different measurement. Different units. We’ll come back to that.” CUE: ★ Write: “Surface area = total area of all [outer faces].”

Beat 3 — The net strategy SHOW: Board: Draw a rectangular prism. Beside it, draw its unfolded net — six rectangles in a cross pattern. Label each face. SAY: - “Strategy: if you’re not sure what faces a solid has, unfold it.” - “A net shows every face in 2D. Add up all the face areas — that’s the surface area.” - Hold up the unfolded box again. “This is the strategy in your hand.” - “For the exam, you won’t always have a box. But you can draw the net.” CUE: ✎ Draw: “Sketch the net of a rectangular prism in the margin — three pairs of rectangles.”

Beat 4 — Right prism general formula SHOW: Board: Write: SA = 2 × (area of base) + (perimeter of base) × height of prism. Label each part. SAY: - “The general formula for any right prism has two parts.” - “Two bases — top and bottom. These are always the same shape.” - “The lateral surface — all the rectangular faces around the side. When you unroll them, they form a rectangle.” - “That rectangle has: width = the height of the prism, length = the perimeter of the base.” - “So lateral area = [perimeter of base] × [height of prism].” CUE: ★ Write: “SA = 2 × (area of base) + [perimeter of base] × [height of prism]”

Beat 5 — Worked Example 1: Rectangular prism SHOW: Board: Draw a rectangular prism. Label: l = 5 cm, w = 3 cm, h = 4 cm. Then draw and label the three pairs of faces. SAY: - “Rectangular prism: 5 by 3 by 4.” - “Three pairs of faces — because opposite faces are always equal.” - “Pair 1 (top and bottom): 2 × (5 × 3) = 2 × 15 = [30] cm².” - “Pair 2 (front and back): 2 × (5 × 4) = 2 × 20 = [40] cm².” - “Pair 3 (left and right): 2 × (3 × 4) = 2 × 12 = [24] cm².” - “SA = [30] + [40] + [24] = [94] cm².” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 1.”

Beat 6 — Self-explanation: why three pairs? SHOW: Board: The six-face net, still on the board. Circle the matching pairs. SAY: - “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why does a rectangular prism have exactly three pairs of faces, and why does each pair consist of faces with equal area?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the ‘Explain’ question — why three pairs? — in your own words before moving on.”

Expected: opposite faces are identical rectangles — the top and bottom have the same l × w dimensions, the front and back have the same l × h, and the left and right have the same w × h. Students who write “because there are 6 faces and 6 ÷ 2 = 3” haven’t fully explained it — prompt: “Why are they equal?”

Beat 7 — Worked Example 2: Triangular prism SHOW: Board: Draw a triangular prism. Label: equilateral triangle base, side = 6 cm, triangle height = 5.2 cm, prism height = 10 cm. Draw the net. SAY: - “Triangular prism — same strategy. Two triangular bases plus three rectangular lateral faces.” - “Area of one triangular base: (6 × 5.2)/2 = [15.6] cm².” - “Two bases: 2 × [15.6] = [31.2] cm².” - “Perimeter of base: 6 + 6 + 6 = [18] cm.” - “Lateral area: [18] × 10 = [180] cm².” - “SA = [31.2] + [180] = [211.2] cm².” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 2.”

Beat 8 — Cylinder: unrolling the lateral surface SHOW: Board: Draw a cylinder. Beside it, draw its net — two circles and a rectangle. Label: the rectangle’s width = h, and its length = circumference = 2πr. Show this visually by drawing an arrow unrolling the curved surface. SAY: - “A cylinder is a prism — with a circular base.” - “When you unroll the curved surface, it becomes a rectangle.” - “The width of that rectangle is the height of the cylinder.” - “The length of that rectangle is the circumference of the circle: [2πr].” - “So the lateral area = 2πr × h = 2πrh.” - “Plus the two circular ends: 2 × πr² = 2πr².” - “SA = 2πr² + 2πrh.” CUE: ★ Write: “A cylinder has two [circular] faces and one [rectangular] lateral surface when unrolled.”

Hold up the cylindrical can. Peel the label if possible — point to the label as the lateral surface becoming a rectangle. This is the most effective concrete demonstration for the cylinder formula.

Beat 9 — Worked Example 3: Cylinder SHOW: Board: r = 4 cm, h = 9 cm. Write out the substitution step by step. SAY: - “SA = 2π(4)² + 2π(4)(9).” - “First term: 2π(16) = [32]π.” - “Second term: 2π(36) = [72]π.” - “Total: [32]π + [72]π = [104]π ≈ [326.73] cm².” - “Leave it in exact form (104π) when the question says to. Evaluate when the question asks for a decimal.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 3.”

Beat 10 — Self-explanation: the lateral area step SHOW: Board: Just the formula 2πrh. Nothing else. SAY: - “Before moving on — explain the lateral area step in your own words.” - “Why does 2πrh give the area of the curved surface? What shape does the curved surface become when you unroll it?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the ‘Explain the lateral area step’ question in your notes now.”

Beat 11 — Misconception: Surface area and volume are the same thing SHOW: Board: Draw a flat sheet of metal and a thick cube side by side. Label: Same SA? Different volume. SAY: - “Big misconception alert. Read the misconception box in your notes.” - “Surface area and volume are NOT the same measurement.” - “SA measures the total outer area — in cm², in m². It’s a 2D measurement.” - “Volume measures the space inside — in cm³, in m³. It’s a 3D measurement.” - “A flat sheet of metal can have a huge surface area but almost no volume.” - “A thick cube can have the same surface area as a flat box and much more volume.” - “They measure fundamentally different things. The units alone tell you this.” - “Verify in your notes: cube side 2 cm. SA = 6 × 4 = [24] cm². Volume = 2³ = [8] cm³. Different numbers, different units.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in the verification blanks in the misconception box.”

FLAG — Misconception“Volume and surface area measure the same thing.” This is listed in the course context as a key Shape & Space misconception. Students who hold this belief will conflate formulas and units throughout the entire unit. The verification calculation in the student notes — SA = 24 cm², V = 8 cm³ for a 2-cm cube — is the clearest single counterexample. Walk through it live. Do not skip this beat.

Beat 12 — Connection prompt SHOW: Board: “Surface area = material to build it. Volume = what it can hold. Give one real example of each.” SAY: - “Write your answer before we discuss.” - “Find a situation where you’d care about SA and a different situation where you’d care about volume.” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the connection prompt in your notes — two situations, before reading ahead.”

Allow 60–90 seconds. Good answers: SA = wrapping a gift, painting a room, tinfoil around a baking dish, insulating a pipe; volume = filling a fish tank, how much water a container holds, how much grain a silo stores. Alberta context to offer if students are stuck: “You’re building a cylindrical oil storage tank near Fort McMurray. Would a welder care about surface area or volume?” (SA = how much steel plate needed; volume = how much oil it holds.)

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Surface area formulas — rectangular prism, triangular prism, cylinder. Write them from memory.”

Answer guide: 1. SA = 2πr² + 2πrh 2. Square prism base: SA = 2(5²) + 4(5 × 8) = 50 + 160 = 210 cm². 3. 150π = 2π(5)² + 2π(5)h → 150π = 50π + 10πh → 100π = 10πh → h = 10 cm. 4. A net shows all faces laid flat in 2D, making it easy to identify every face and avoid missing any. It also shows which faces are the base and which are lateral, preventing formula confusion. 5. (Stretch) SA = 2πr² + 2πr(2r) = 2πr² + 4πr² = 6πr².

Section 3 — Volume of Right Prisms and Cylinders

Learning target: I can calculate the volume of right prisms and cylinders using the base area × height formula. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Bring to class: same box and can from Lesson 2; a measuring cup or container of known volume.

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Three terms — Volume / Cubic units / Base area (B). SAY: - “Three vocabulary terms. These are shorter but the concepts are important.” - “Volume — what does it measure? Not area. Not length. What?” - Pause for responses. - “The space inside a 3D object. How much it can hold.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “How would you describe volume to a Grade 5 student? Keep your definition in your head.”

Beat 2 — The single formula for all prisms SHOW: Board: Write large: V = B × h where B = base area, h = height of the solid. SAY: - “One formula. Works for every right prism and every cylinder.” - “V = B × h. B is the area of the base. h is the height of the solid.” - “The shape of B is different for different solids — but the formula is always V = Bh.” - “Rectangular prism: B = l × w, so V = l × w × h.” - “Cylinder: B = πr², so V = πr² × h.” - “Triangular prism: B = (1/2)bh_triangle, so V = (1/2)bh_triangle × h_prism.” - “Learn the framework. The specific formula follows from B.” CUE: ★ Write: “V = B × h, where B = area of the [base] and h = [height of the solid].”

Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: Rectangular prism SHOW: Board: l = 6 m, w = 4 m, h = 3 m. Write the substitution steps. SAY: - “V = B × h. B = l × w for a rectangular prism.” - “B = 6 × 4 = [24] m².” - “V = [24] × 3 = [72] m³.” - “Units: m³. Cubic metres. Always cubic units for volume.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 1.”

Beat 4 — Worked Example 2: Cylinder SHOW: Board: r = 5 cm, h = 12 cm. Write the substitution steps. SAY: - “V = B × h. B = πr² for a cylinder.” - “B = π(5)² = [25]π cm².” - “V = [25]π × 12 = [300]π ≈ [942.48] cm³.” - “Leave the exact answer as 300π for full marks on an exact-form question.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 2.”

Beat 5 — Self-explanation: what B means for each shape SHOW: Board: Two side-by-side boxes: “Rectangular prism: B = ?” and “Cylinder: B = ?” SAY: - “Both shapes use V = Bh. Before moving on — explain what makes them different.” - “Both a cylinder and a rectangular prism use the same formula. What is B for each, and why are they different?” CUE: ★ Write: “Rectangular prism: B = [l × w]. Cylinder: B = [πr²]. Explain in your own words why B is different.”

Beat 6 — Worked Example 3: Triangular prism SHOW: Board: Right triangle base with legs 6 cm and 8 cm. Prism height = 10 cm. SAY: - “Triangular prism — the base is a triangle.” - “B = (6 × 8)/2 = [24] cm².” - “V = [24] × 10 = [240] cm³.” - “Same formula. Different B shape.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in the triangular prism worked example.”

Beat 7 — Units table: SA vs. Volume SHOW: Board: Draw the two-row table. Surface area | cm² | Amount of wrapping paper. Volume | cm³ | Amount of liquid it holds. SAY: - “SA and volume have different units — this is not just a label. It tells you what the number means.” - “cm² = square centimetres = a 2D measurement. You are measuring area of a surface.” - “cm³ = cubic centimetres = a 3D measurement. You are measuring space inside.” - “A number without its units is meaningless here. Always write cm² or cm³.” CUE: ★ Write: “Surface area units: [cm²] (square). Volume units: [cm³] (cubic).”

Beat 8 — Alberta context: Oil tank SHOW: Board: Sketch a cylindrical tank. Label: r = 8 m, h = 15 m. Write the steps. SAY: - “Cylindrical oil storage tank near Fort McMurray. Radius 8 m, height 15 m.” - “How much oil can it hold?” - “V = π × [8]² × [15] = π × [64] × [15] = [960]π ≈ [3015.93] m³.” - “Unit conversion: 1 m³ = 1000 L.” - “So the tank holds approximately [960 000] L. That’s almost a million litres.” - “This is why engineers care about volume — not surface area — when specifying tank capacity.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in the Alberta context oil tank problem.”

FLAG — Alberta ContextThis problem is not cosmetic — remove the context and the math is just numbers. Return to it: “Would the welder who builds this tank care about surface area instead? What would that number mean?” (SA tells them how much steel plate they need.) This is a good moment to reinforce the SA/volume distinction from Section 2.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Write the general volume formula for any prism. Then apply it to two shapes.”

Answer guide: 1. V = B × h, where B is the area of the base. 2. V = πr²h = π(3)²(10) = 90π ≈ 282.74 m³. 3. Trapezoidal base area: B = (6 + 10)/2 × 4 = 32 cm². V = 32 × 8 = 256 cm³. 4. V = B × h → 500 = 25 × h → h = 20 cm. 5. (Stretch) Cylinder A: V = π(4)²(6) = 96π; SA = 2π(16) + 2π(4)(6) = 32π + 48π = 80π. Cylinder B: V = π(6)²(4) = 144π; SA = 2π(36) + 2π(6)(4) = 72π + 48π = 120π. Cylinder B has greater volume AND greater surface area. Key insight: increasing the radius has a squared effect on volume; increasing height has only a linear effect.

Section 4 — Surface Area and Volume of Pyramids, Cones, and Spheres

Learning target: I can apply the formulas for pyramids, cones, and spheres, and explain why pyramid and cone volume is one-third of the corresponding prism and cylinder. Timing: ~35 minutes direct instruction

Bring to class: cone cup and matching cylindrical cup (same base diameter); sand or rice for the fill-three-times demonstration.

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Five terms — Pyramid / Apex / Slant height / Cone / Sphere. SAY: - “Five vocabulary terms. Two are probably new: apex and slant height.” - “Apex: the pointed top of a pyramid or cone.” - “Slant height: the distance along the face from the base edge up to the apex. Not the perpendicular height.” - “This is a critical distinction — slant height appears in surface area formulas. Perpendicular height appears in volume formulas.” - “These two heights are different numbers. Confusing them is the most common error in this section.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Slant height versus perpendicular height. Keep that distinction in your head.”

Beat 2 — Pyramid: surface area SHOW: Board: Draw a square-based pyramid. Label: base side s, slant height l (along a triangular face), perpendicular height h (inside the pyramid, dashed). Draw the net — one square base, four triangles. SAY: - “Surface area of a pyramid = base area + all the triangular faces.” - “For a square-based pyramid: base is a square, four triangular faces.” - “SA = base area + 4 × (area of one triangle).” - “Area of one triangle: (1/2) × base × slant height = (1/2) × s × l.” - “SA = s² + 4 × (1/2)sl = s² + 2sl.” - “Notice: slant height l, not perpendicular height h. The slant height is the height of each triangular face.” CUE: ★ Write: “SA = area of [base] + area of [triangular] faces. For square pyramid: SA = s² + 2sl.”

Beat 3 — Pyramid: volume SHOW: Board: Write: V = (1/3) × B × h. Label: B = base area, h = perpendicular height. SAY: - “Volume of any pyramid: one-third times base area times perpendicular height.” - “The one-third factor is not an estimate — it is exact.” - “Why one-third? Let’s test it.” CUE: ★ Write: “V = (1/3) × B × h, where h = [perpendicular height], NOT slant height.”

Beat 4 — The one-third demonstration SHOW: Board: Draw a prism and a pyramid with the same base and height side by side. SAY: - “Here’s the key insight: a pyramid fills exactly one-third of the matching prism.” - Hold up the cone cup and cylinder cup. - “Same base diameter. Same height. Different shapes.” - Fill the cone with sand. Pour into the cylinder. “One.” - Refill. Pour again. “Two.” - Refill. Pour again. “Three — and the cylinder is full.” - “Three cone-fulls exactly fill the matching cylinder. That’s what (1/3) means.” - “This is a geometric fact. It holds for pyramids and prisms the same way.” - Worked example key insight: a pyramid fits exactly [3] times inside the matching prism. A cone fits [3] times inside a matching cylinder. CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in the Key Insight blanks.”

FLAG — Misconception“A cone has twice the volume of the matching cylinder.” Many students guess half, not one-third, because “the cone fits inside and takes up about half the space.” The physical demonstration with sand or rice is the single most effective fix. Students who see it done will not make this error. If you do not have materials: draw the diagram and ask “How many cones would fill the cylinder?” — students almost always guess 2. The answer of 3 is surprising enough to stick.

Beat 5 — Worked Example 1: Square-based pyramid SHOW: Board: Base 6 cm × 6 cm, perpendicular height 4 cm, slant height 5 cm. Write the substitution steps for both SA and V. SAY: - “Base area = 6 × 6 = [36] cm².” - “SA = [36] + 2(6)(5) = [36] + [60] = [96] cm².” - “V = (1/3)(36)(4) = [48] cm³.” - “SA uses slant height (5 cm). Volume uses perpendicular height (4 cm).” - “Say that back: which height goes where?” - Pause for responses. CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 1.”

Beat 6 — Self-explanation: slant height for SA, perpendicular for V SHOW: Board: Draw a pyramid with both heights labeled — l (slant, along the face) and h (perpendicular, inside). SAY: - “Before moving on — explain this in your own words.” - “The slant height (5 cm) is used for surface area but NOT for volume. Why?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the ‘Explain’ question about slant vs. perpendicular height in your notes now.”

Expected: SA uses the slant height because it is the height of each triangular face — the formula (1/2 × base × height) for each triangle uses the height of that triangle, which runs along the slant. Volume uses the perpendicular height because volume measures the space inside, which is determined by the vertical distance from base to apex — not the slant distance along the outside.

Beat 7 — Cone: formulas SHOW: Board: Draw a cone. Label: r, h (perpendicular), l (slant height, along the surface). Write: SA = πr² + πrl. V = (1/3)πr²h. SAY: - “Same principle as the pyramid, circular base.” - “SA = πr² (circular base) + πrl (lateral surface).” - “Where l is the slant height. Not h.” - “To find slant height from perpendicular height: l² = r² + h². Pythagorean theorem — l is the hypotenuse.” - “V = (1/3)πr²h. Same one-third factor. Perpendicular height only.” CUE: ★ Write: “πr² = area of the circular [base]. πrl = lateral surface area. l² = r² + h² (Pythagorean theorem — l is the hypotenuse).”

Beat 8 — Worked Example 2: Cone SHOW: Board: r = 3 cm, h = 4 cm. Write steps: find l, then SA, then V. SAY: - “Step 1: find slant height.” - “l = √(r² + h²) = √(9 + 16) = √[25] = [5] cm.” - “SA = π(3)² + π(3)([5]) = 9π + [15]π = [24]π ≈ [75.40] cm².” - “V = (1/3)π(3)²(4) = (1/3)π([9])([4]) = [12]π ≈ [37.70] cm³.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 2.”

Beat 9 — Self-explanation: the Pythagorean step SHOW: Board: Right triangle inside the cone — r (horizontal), h (vertical), l (hypotenuse/slant). SAY: - “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why does the Pythagorean theorem give us the slant height? What is the right triangle we’re working with?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the self-explanation question about the Pythagorean step now.”

Expected: there is a right triangle formed inside the cone by the perpendicular height (vertical leg), the radius (horizontal leg), and the slant height (hypotenuse). Because it is a right triangle, Pythagoras applies: l² = r² + h².

Beat 10 — Sphere SHOW: Board: Draw a sphere. Write: SA = 4πr². V = (4/3)πr³. SAY: - “Sphere has no base, no height, no apex. Just a radius.” - “SA = 4πr². Think of it as four full circles’ worth of area wrapped around the surface.” - “V = (4/3)πr³. The (4/3) factor is fixed — do not confuse it with the (1/3) for cones and pyramids.” - “These two formulas are not derived at Grade 9 — you use them, you don’t prove them.” CUE: ★ Write: “SA = 4πr². V = (4/3)πr³.”

Beat 11 — Worked Example 3: Sphere SHOW: Board: r = 6 cm. Write the substitution steps for both SA and V. SAY: - “SA = 4π(6)² = 4π(36) = [144]π ≈ [452.39] cm².” - “V = (4/3)π(6)³ = (4/3)π(216) = [288]π ≈ [904.78] cm³.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all blanks in Worked Example 3.”

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Write the SA and V formulas for: cone, sphere, square pyramid. Then apply them.”

Answer guide: 1. Slant height: l = √(5² + 12²) = √(25 + 144) = √169 = 13 cm. SA = π(5)² + π(5)(13) = 25π + 65π = 90π ≈ 282.74 cm². V = (1/3)π(5)²(12) = (1/3)π(300) = 100π ≈ 314.16 cm³. 2. V = (4/3)π(9)³ = (4/3)π(729) = 972π ≈ 3053.63 m³. SA = 4π(9)² = 324π ≈ 1017.88 m². 3. A pyramid has a pointed apex — as you move up from the base, each horizontal cross-section gets smaller, so the pyramid occupies progressively less space than the matching prism at every height level. Exactly one-third of the prism’s volume is enclosed. 4. SA = (10)² + 4 × (1/2)(10)(13) = 100 + 260 = 360 m². 5. (Stretch) Cylinder: r = r, h = 2r → V_cylinder = πr²(2r) = 2πr³. Sphere r: V_sphere = (4/3)πr³. Ratio: (4/3)πr³ ÷ 2πr³ = (4/3) ÷ 2 = 2/3. The sphere’s volume is (2/3) of the cylinder’s volume.

Section 5 — Composite Objects

Learning target: I can find the surface area and volume of composite 3D objects by adding and subtracting the appropriate parts. Timing: ~35 minutes direct instruction

Bring to class: photo or model of an Alberta grain elevator; composite 3D model if available.

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Three terms — Composite object / Hidden face / Overlapping surface. SAY: - “Three terms. The most important one is hidden face.” - “A hidden face is a face that exists on a shape but is no longer part of the outer surface of the composite object — because another shape is joined to it.” - “It’s hidden from the outside. Not visible. Not on the surface.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “If I glue two cubes face to face, how many faces does the composite shape have? Keep your guess in your head.”

Answer: 12 total faces on the two cubes, minus 2 hidden faces (the joined faces) = 10 visible faces. Students who say 12 are forgetting the hidden faces — this is the core misconception of the section.

Beat 2 — The hidden face rule — dedicated beat SHOW: Board: Draw two shapes being joined. Circle the shared face in red. Write in large letters: HIDDEN FACE = subtract from BOTH shapes. SAY: - “This is the most critical moment in the entire unit. The hidden face rule.” - “When two shapes are joined, the faces at the joint are no longer on the outer surface.” - “They are inside the composite object.” - “That face must be subtracted — and it must be subtracted TWICE: once from each shape.” - “Once from Shape A. Once from Shape B.” - “Because that face was counted in Shape A’s full SA, and it was counted in Shape B’s full SA. Both counts are wrong. Both must be corrected.” - Pause 4–5 seconds. Let this land. - “The most common error in composite shapes: students calculate the full SA of each piece and add them together. That’s wrong. It overcounts the hidden faces.” CUE: ★ Write: “When two shapes are joined, the faces at the joint are [hidden] — subtract [twice]: once from each shape.”

FLAG — MisconceptionHidden faces in composite shapes: students add ALL faces rather than subtracting where shapes join. This is the most common error in Section 5, and it appears on every measurement unit test. The solution is not to repeat the rule — it is to make students name the hidden face explicitly before they calculate anything. Build this as a habit: “Step 0: draw the composite shape and circle the hidden faces before writing any numbers.” Do not let students skip Step 0.

Beat 3 — Strategy: volume SHOW: Board: Write the two-step volume strategy: 1. Calculate the volume of each component. 2. Add the volumes (or subtract if one is removed). SAY: - “Volume of a composite object: add the parts.” - “Simple — there are no hidden volumes. Volume is not affected by how surfaces are joined.” - “Unless one shape is removed from another — like drilling a hole — in which case you subtract.” CUE: ★ Write: “Volume strategy: [add] the volumes of each part. Subtract if a part is removed.”

Beat 4 — Worked Example: cylinder on rectangular prism SHOW: Board: Draw the composite object — cylinder (r = 4 cm, h = 10 cm) sitting on top of a rectangular prism (20 cm × 20 cm × 5 cm). Label all dimensions. Circle the hidden face (the circular base of the cylinder / the circular region on the prism top) in red. SAY: - “Step 0: identify the hidden face.” - “The circular base of the cylinder sits on the prism top. That circle is hidden on both shapes.” - “Hidden area = area of one circle = π(4)² = [16]π cm².” - “Volume first — it’s easier.” - “V = volume of prism + volume of cylinder.” - “V = (20)(20)(5) + π(4)²(10) = [2000] + [160]π ≈ [2502.65] cm³.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in the volume blanks.”

Beat 5 — Worked Example continued: surface area with hidden faces SHOW: Board: Write the SA strategy in steps: 1. SA of prism (full) − one circle 2. SA of cylinder (full) − one circle 3. Total SA = (adjusted prism SA) + (adjusted cylinder SA) SAY: - “Now surface area — this is where students lose marks.” - “SA of full prism = 2(20×20) + 4(20×5) = [800] + [400] = [1200] cm².” - “But the top of the prism has a circular hole where the cylinder sits. That circle is hidden.” - “Subtract: [1200] − [16]π ≈ [1149.73] cm².” - “SA of full cylinder = 2π(4)² + 2π(4)(10) = [32]π + [80]π = [112]π cm².” - “But the bottom circle of the cylinder is hidden — subtract it.” - “[112]π − [16]π = [96]π cm².” - “Total SA ≈ [1149.73] + [96]π ≈ [1149.73] + [301.59] ≈ [1451.32] cm².” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in all SA blanks in the worked example.”

Beat 6 — Self-explanation: why subtract from both? SHOW: Board: The composite diagram with the hidden face circled on both shapes. SAY: - “Before moving on — explain the key step in your own words.” - “Why do we subtract the hidden circle from BOTH the prism’s SA and the cylinder’s SA?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the ‘Explain’ question about subtracting the hidden face from both shapes now.”

Expected: when we calculated the full SA of the prism, we included all 6 faces — including the top, which has a circle on it. That circle is now hidden. When we calculated the full SA of the cylinder, we included both circles — including the bottom, which is now hidden against the prism top. Both full SAs overcounted: one overcounted the prism top, one overcounted the cylinder bottom. Both must be corrected.

Beat 7 — Common mistake reinforcement SHOW: Board: Draw two boxes glued together. Write: “Student calculates full SA of Box A + full SA of Box B. What does this overcount?” SAY: - “A student calculates the SA of two boxes glued together by adding both full surface areas.” - “What does this overcount?” - Pause for responses. - “The two touching faces — one on each box — are hidden. They’ve been counted in both SA calculations.” - “How much should be subtracted? Two times the area of one joining face.” CUE: ★ Write: “How much to subtract: [2 × area of the joining face].”

Beat 8 — Alberta context: Grain elevator SHOW: Board: Draw the grain elevator — rectangular prism (8 m × 8 m × 20 m) with a square pyramid on top (base 8 m × 8 m, slant height 5 m). Label all dimensions. Circle the hidden face (top of prism = base of pyramid) in red. SAY: - “Alberta grain elevator. Classic Prairie landmark.” - “Composite shape: rectangular prism with a square pyramid cap.” - “Step 0: hidden face — the top of the prism and the base of the pyramid. Both are the same 8 × 8 square.” - “Volume of the prism: V = 8 × 8 × 20 = [1280] m³.” - “For the pyramid volume, we need the perpendicular height — not the slant height.” - “h = √(l² − (s/2)²) = √(25 − 16) = √9 = [3] m.” - “Volume of pyramid: V = (1/3)(64)(3) = [64] m³.” - “Total volume: 1280 + 64 = [1344] m³.” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in the volume blanks in the grain elevator context.”

Beat 9 — Grain elevator: surface area SHOW: Board: List the faces to include: 4 rectangular prism sides + prism base (bottom) + 4 pyramid triangular faces. The hidden face (prism top = pyramid base) is circled out. SAY: - “Surface area — identify which faces to include.” - “The prism top is hidden — don’t count it.” - “The pyramid base is hidden — don’t count it.” - “Include: 4 prism sides + 1 prism base + 4 pyramid triangular faces.” - “Prism sides: 4 × (8 × 20) = [640] m².” - “Prism base: 8 × 8 = [64] m².” - “4 pyramid triangular faces: 4 × (1/2 × 8 × 5) = [80] m².” - “Total SA = 640 + 64 + 80 = [784] m².” CUE: ★ Write: “Fill in the SA blank for the grain elevator.”

FLAG — Alberta ContextThe grain elevator problem is not decorative. Ask: “If you were going to paint the outside of this elevator, which calculation would you use — SA or volume? Which faces would you NOT paint?” (The bottom of the prism sits on the ground — it is also hidden from the painter’s perspective, though not from the surface area formula.) This is a good place to reinforce that “hidden” in a real-world problem can mean more than just the joined face.

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Composite shapes — write the hidden face rule, then apply it to the three questions.”

Answer guide: 1. V = V_sphere + V_cylinder = (4/3)π(3)³ + π(3)²(8) = 36π + 72π = 108π ≈ 339.29 cm³. Note: the sphere rests on top — the hemisphere base is not attached/hidden; the problem says “touches the top face,” so the flat circular area does not factor into volume but would factor into SA if asked. 2. (a) Volume decreases: subtract the volume of the removed cylinder = π(2)²(10) = 40π ≈ 125.66 cm³. (b) Surface area: lose the two circular end faces of the hole (top and bottom circles) but gain the cylindrical inner surface area (2πrh = 2π(2)(10) = 40π). Net effect depends on whether the new inner surface area exceeds the lost end circles. 3. When two shapes are joined, the face at the join is no longer on the outer surface (hidden). Subtract it from both shapes’ surface areas — once for each shape. 4. The flat circular face of the hemisphere (bottom) is hidden against the cylinder top. The cylinder’s top circle is also hidden. Both must be subtracted. 5. (Stretch) Volume of cube = 216 cm³. Volume of cone = (1/3)π(3)²(6) = 18π ≈ 56.55 cm³. Remaining volume = 216 − 56.55 ≈ 159.45 cm³. SA: cube SA = 6(36) = 216 cm². The top face of the cube loses the circular base of the cone (π(9) = 9π ≈ 28.27 cm²) but gains the lateral surface of the cone (π(3)(√(9+36)) = 3π√45 ≈ 63.26 cm²).
