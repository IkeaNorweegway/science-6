Slope measures how a line is and in what .

slope = rise/run = change in y / change in x

Using two points (x₁, y₁) and (x₂, y₂):

m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)

Misconception

Wrong idea: "If I use different points on the line, I'll get a different slope."

Why it fails: For a straight line, slope is constant — every pair of points on the same line gives the same rise/run ratio. This is the defining property of a line. If the slope changes depending on which points you pick, the relationship is NOT linear.

Misconception

Wrong idea: "The intercept is where the line hits zero — that's the x-axis."

Why it fails: "Intercept" means "crosses." The y-intercept crosses the y-axis (where x = 0). The x-intercept crosses the x-axis (where y = 0). In y = 3x − 4, the y-intercept is −4 (at the point (0, −4)), and the x-intercept is found by setting y = 0: 0 = 3x − 4, x = 4/3.

Misconception

Wrong idea: "If both x and y increase, the relation is direct variation."

Why it fails: Partial variation also has both variables increasing. Direct variation specifically requires the graph to pass through the origin (0, 0) — meaning when x = 0, y = 0. The phone plan with a monthly fee is NOT direct variation, even though cost rises with usage.
