Section 1 — What Is a Rational Number?

Learning target: I can identify rational numbers, represent them in multiple forms, and compare and order them on a number line.

Guided Notes

A rational number is any number that can be written in the form /, where a and b are integers and b 0.

This includes:

  • All integers: 5 = /1, −3 = /1
  • All fractions: 3/4, −7/8
  • All terminating decimals: 0.75 = /100
  • All repeating decimals: 0.333... = /3

Every rational number has three equivalent sign forms:

−3/4 is the same as: 3/(−4) and −(3/4)

All three represent the same point on the number line.

Comparing rational numbers:

StatementTrue or False?Reason
−1/2 > −3/4
−5/6 < −7/8
2/3 > 3/4

Method — Use a common denominator:

Compare −5/6 and −7/8.

Common denominator:

−5/6 = −/24    −7/8 = −/24

Compare: −/24 −/24, so −5/6 −7/8

Key insight with negatives: For negative fractions, the fraction that is in absolute value is actually in value.
(−1/4 is greater than −3/4 even though 1/4 < 3/4)

Misconception

Wrong idea: "−3/4 is bigger than −1/4 because 3/4 is bigger than 1/4."

Why it fails: On the number line, negative numbers to the right are greater. −1/4 sits to the right of −3/4, so −1/4 > −3/4. Think of it as debt: owing $0.25 is better (closer to 0) than owing $0.75.

Correct: −1/4 > −3/4

Misconception

Wrong idea: 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 (add numerators, add denominators)

Why it fails: Denominators name the kind of pieces (halves, thirds). You cannot add pieces of different sizes by just counting them — just like you cannot add 2 hours + 3 minutes and say "5 hour-minutes." Convert first: 1/2 = 3/6 and 1/3 = 2/6, so 1/2 + 1/3 = 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6.

Check: 2/5 = 0.40. But 1/2 + 1/3 = 0.5 + 0.333 = 0.833. These are very different numbers.

Misconception

Wrong idea: In (3/8) × (4/9), a student cancels the 3 and 9 "because they look diagonal."

Why it fails: Cross-cancelling works between a numerator and a denominator that share a factor — it is the same as dividing both the top and bottom of the eventual answer by the same number. The 3 in the numerator of 3/8 and the 9 in the denominator of 4/9 are on opposite sides of the multiplication sign, so this is valid. But you cannot cancel a numerator with a numerator, or a denominator with a denominator.

Guided Notes

The big idea: Dividing by a number is the same as by its reciprocal.

a ÷ (b/c) = a × /

The reciprocal of a/b is /.

To find a reciprocal: flip the fraction (swap numerator and denominator).

FractionReciprocal
3/4

Misconception

Wrong idea: "If I divide something by a fraction, the answer should be smaller — dividing means making things smaller."

Why it fails: This is only true when dividing by a number greater than 1. When you divide by a fraction less than 1, you are asking "how many of this fraction fit into the whole?" More pieces fit, so the answer is larger.

Example: 6 ÷ (1/2) = 12. There are 12 half-pieces in 6 whole pieces.

Think of it as: 6 ÷ (1/2) = 6 × 2 = 12. Dividing by 1/2 is multiplying by 2.
