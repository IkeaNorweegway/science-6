How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

Label What it contains
SHOW What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAY Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUE The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩︎ Check

Flags appear in callout blocks: > FLAG — Misconception: Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address. > FLAG — Pacing: Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum. > FLAG — Alberta Context: Local anchor — use to make abstract problems concrete and meaningful.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual — number lines, worked examples, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Self-explanation rule: After every worked example in this unit, hold a self-explanation beat before moving on. The prompt is always: “Before we move on — explain Step [X] in your own words: what did we do and why?” Students write the answer (★ Write). Do not skip these. Self-explanation is the highest-yield move in the math notes literature (g ≈ 0.55 for transfer).

Section 1 — What Is a Rational Number?

Learning target: I can identify rational numbers, represent them in multiple forms, and compare and order them on a number line. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Six vocabulary terms in a column — Rational number / Numerator / Denominator / Equivalent fractions / Opposite / Absolute value

SAY

- “Six terms on your vocab table — before we go into the notes.” - “Don’t fill them in yet. We’ll build them as we go through the section.” - “By the end you’ll have your own definition and example for each one.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “Read the six terms. Which one do you think you already know best? Keep it in your head.”

Beat 1.2 — Defining rational numbers

SHOW

Board: RATIONAL NUMBER = any number that can be written as ab, where a and b are integers and b ≠ 0.

SAY

- “A rational number is any number that can be written as a fraction — numerator over denominator — where both are integers and the denominator is not zero.” - “The key word is CAN. It does not have to look like a fraction.” - “The integer 5 qualifies — write it as 51. The decimal 0.75 qualifies — write it as 75100 or 34.” - “What does NOT qualify? A decimal that goes on forever without repeating — like pi. Pi cannot be written as a fraction. It is not rational.” - Pause 3 seconds.

CUE

★ Write: “A rational number can be written as [ab], where a and b are integers and b [≠ 0].”

Beat 1.3 — What counts as rational

SHOW

Board: Four bullet points — All integers: 5 = 51 / All fractions: 34 / All terminating decimals: 0.75 = 75100 / All repeating decimals: 0.333… = 13

SAY

- “Four families of rational numbers. Let’s go through each.” - “Integers first: 5 equals 51. Negative three equals −31. Every integer qualifies.” - “Fractions — that one is obvious.” - “Terminating decimals: 0.75 ends. It equals 75 over 100 — that is a ratio of two integers. Rational.” - “Repeating decimals: 0.333… equals 13. The repeating decimal can always be converted to a fraction.” - “The denominator blank for 0.75: write 100. The blank for 0.333…: write 3.”

CUE

★ Write: “Fill in the four blanks — integers as fractions, terminating decimal as a fraction over [100], repeating decimal as [13].”

Beat 1.4 — Three equivalent sign forms

SHOW

Board: −34 = 3/(−4) = −(34). Draw all three on the number line at the same point.

SAY

- “Every negative rational number has three equivalent forms.” - “Negative 34. Three over negative 4. Negative of the fraction 34.” - “All three land on the same point — 34 of the way between 0 and −1.” - “Why does this matter? Because students often see one form on a test and not recognize it as the same number as another form. They are the same.” - Pause 3 seconds.

CUE

★ Write: “Three equivalent sign forms — all represent the same point on the number line.”

Beat 1.5 — Comparing rational numbers with a number line

SHOW

Board: A number line from −2 to 2, drawn large. Mark and label −34 and −14 with dots.

SAY

- “Comparing negative fractions — this is where the first big error happens.” - “Look at the number line. Is −14 to the left or the right of −34?” - Pause 3 seconds. - “Right. −14 is to the right. And on a number line, right means greater.” - “So −14 is greater than −34. Even though 14 is less than 34 when you ignore the negatives.” - “The negative flips the order.” - “Think: debt. Owing 25 cents is better — closer to zero — than owing 75 cents.”

CUE

 

FLAG — Misconception“−34 is bigger than −14 because 34 is bigger than 14.” This is the most common error in Section 1. Students apply the positive-number comparison rule without adjusting for the negative sign. The number line is your best tool here — show the positions, then ask which is further right. The debt context makes it intuitive. Do not skip the number line SHOW beat. Students who only see the symbolic comparison will revert to the wrong rule on a test.

CUE: ★ Write: “For negative fractions, the fraction [larger / farther from zero] in absolute value is actually [smaller / lesser] in value. −14 [>] −34.”

Beat 1.6 — Comparing method: common denominator

SHOW

Board: Compare −56 and −78. Step 1: LCD. Step 2: Convert. Step 3: Compare.

SAY

- “Worked example — comparing −56 and −78.” - “Step 1: What is the LCD of 6 and 8? Think — 6 times 8 is 48, but can we do better? Multiples of 8: 8, 16, 24. Multiples of 6: 6, 12, 18, 24. LCD is 24.” - “Step 2: Convert. −56 — multiply top and bottom by 4: −2024. −78 — multiply top and bottom by 3: −2124.” - “Step 3: Compare −2024 and −2124. −21 is further from zero, so −2124 is smaller.” - “So −56 is greater than −78.” - Blanks: LCD = [24] / −56 = −[20]/24 / −78 = −[21]/24 / −[20]/24 [>] −[21]/24 / so −56 [>] −78.

CUE

★ Write: “Work through the comparison — fill in LCD [24], converted fractions [−2024] and [−2124], and the result [>].”

Beat 1.7 — Key insight note and number line plotting

SHOW

Board: Number line with −1, −34, −13, 0, 12, 56 plotted and labeled.

SAY

- “Key insight: for negative fractions, larger absolute value means smaller value. Smaller absolute value means larger value. That is the rule — write it.” - “Now: order from least to greatest — 12, −34, −13, 0, 56, −1.” - “Least first. Most negative first. −1, then −34, then −13, then 0, then 12, then 56.” - “Draw them on the number line in your notes.”

CUE

✎ Draw: “Plot all six values on your number line. Label each one.”

Allow 2 minutes. Circulate. Watch for students placing −13 to the left of −34 — that is the target misconception. Also watch for students who correctly order but plot imprecisely: −13 should sit between −12 and 0.

Beat 1.8 — Vocabulary table fill-in

SHOW

Board: Six terms still visible — nothing else.

SAY

- “Go back to your vocab table.” - “Fill in each definition in your own words — not the textbook definition.” - “Your definition should be something a Grade 7 student could understand.” - “Add one example you thought of yourself.”

CUE

★ Write: “Complete your vocab table now — own words, own examples.”

Allow 3–5 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: ‘opposite’ filled in as synonym for ‘negative’ (redirect: the opposite of −34 is +34 — equal distance, other side of zero); ‘absolute value’ left blank (prompt: how far from zero on the number line?).

Answer guide: 1. Yes — −8 = −81, which is a ratio of two integers with denominator not equal to zero. Every integer is rational. 2. Two equivalent sign forms of −53: 5/(−3) and −(53). Any two of the three forms accepted (−53 is the third). 3. −23 vs −34: LCD = 12. −23 = −812. −34 = −912. −812 > −912, so −23 > −34. (On the number line: −23 sits to the right of −34.) 4. Section 2 — Adding and Subtracting Rational Numbers

Learning target: I can add and subtract rational numbers in both fraction and decimal form, including negative values. Timing: ~30 minutes direct instruction

Set up: pre-draw a large number line from −3 to 3 on the board. Keep it up for the whole lesson. Every example should be shown both symbolically and on the number line.

Beat 2.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Four terms — Common denominator / Least common denominator (LCD) / Mixed number / Improper fraction

SAY

- “Four terms — before we get into the procedure.” - “Common denominator — you know this from earlier grades. What does it mean?” - Wait for responses. - “A shared denominator. Both fractions have been rewritten with the same bottom number.” - “LCD — least common denominator — the smallest one available.” - “Mixed number: a whole number plus a fraction. Improper fraction: numerator larger than denominator.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “Which of these four terms feels shakiest? Hold that in mind.”

Beat 2.2 — The core rule: same denominator required

SHOW

Board: To ADD or SUBTRACT fractions: must have the SAME denominator. Step sequence — (1) Find LCD / (2) Rewrite each fraction with LCD / (3) Add or subtract NUMERATORS ONLY / (4) Simplify.

SAY

- “This is the procedure. Four steps. Every time.” - “Why do we need the same denominator? Because denominators name the kind of piece. Thirds and fourths are different-sized pieces — you cannot add them directly.” - “One third plus one fourth. You can’t say two sevenths — that would mean something completely different. You need to convert to a common unit first.” - “Step 3: add numerators ONLY. The denominator stays. You are not adding the types of pieces — you are counting how many you have of the same type.”

CUE

★ Write: “Rule — to add or subtract fractions: need the [same / common] denominator. Step 3: add or subtract the [numerators] only.”

Beat 2.3 — Worked Example 1: positive fractions + number line

SHOW

Board: 34 + 56. Solve symbolically step by step. Then draw on the number line: start at 34, jump forward 56, land at 1912 = 1 712.

SAY

- “Example 1: 34 + 56.” - “Step 1: LCD of 4 and 6. Multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12. Multiples of 6: 6, 12. LCD = 12.” - “Step 2: Rewrite. 34 — multiply top and bottom by 3 — equals 912. 56 — multiply top and bottom by 2 — equals 1012.” - “Step 3: Add numerators: 9 + 10 = 19. Denominator stays 12. Answer: 1912.” - “Step 4: Simplify. 1912 = 1 and 712 as a mixed number.” - “On the number line: start at 34 — that is between 0 and 1, closer to 1. Jump forward 56. Land just past 1 at 1 712.” - Blanks: LCD = [12] / 34 = [9]/12 / 56 = [10]/12 / 912 + 1012 = [19]/12 / simplified: [1 712].

CUE

★ Write: “Work through Example 1 — fill in all blanks including the simplified form [1 712].”

Self-explanation beat: SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: what did we do and why? Specifically — why do we multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the same number when rewriting the fraction?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the Step 2 explanation question now.”

Circulate while students write. Target answer: multiplying top and bottom by the same number is the same as multiplying by 1 — the value of the fraction does not change. We are finding an equivalent fraction, not changing the amount.

Beat 2.4 — Number line demonstration: the additive model

SHOW

Board: Number line from −3 to 3. Mark 0. Mark and label each step in Example 1: start at 34, hop +56, land at 1 712. Use arrows to show the hops.

SAY

- “Every addition problem is a hop on the number line.” - “Positive addend — hop right. Negative addend — hop left.” - “This visual is critical for the mixed-sign examples coming up. Understand the hop model now.” - “34 is between 0 and 1 — right of centre. We hop 56 to the right — about 67 of a unit. We land past 1.”

CUE

✎ Draw: “Mark the hop for 34 + 56 on the number line in your notes.”

Beat 2.5 — Worked Example 2: negative fractions + number line

SHOW

Board: −13 + (−34). Symbolic steps. Then on number line: start at −13, hop left 34, land at −1312.

SAY

- “Example 2: both fractions negative.” - “LCD of 3 and 4 = 12.” - “−13 = −412. −34 = −912.” - “Add: −412 + (−912) = −1312.” - “Simplified: −1 112.” - “On the number line: start at −13 — slightly left of 0. Hop left 34 — a bigger hop. Land at −1312, which is just past −1.” - “Pattern: two negatives added always gives a negative result. You move further left.” - Blanks: LCD = [12] / −13 = −[4]/12 / −34 = −[9]/12 / result = −[13]/12 / simplified: [−1 112] / pattern: always [negative].

CUE

★ Write: “Fill in Example 2 blanks. Pattern: adding two negative rationals always gives a [negative] result.”

Beat 2.6 — Worked Example 3: mixed signs + number line (critical)

SHOW

Board: −56 + 12. Symbolic steps. Then on number line: start at −56, hop right 12, land at −13.

SAY

- “Example 3 — the harder case. One negative, one positive.” - “LCD of 6 and 2 = 6. −56 stays as −56. 12 = 36.” - “−56 + 36 = −26 = −13.” - “On the number line: start at −56 — nearly −1. Hop right 12. We do not make it to 0. We land at −13.” - “Key question: when signs differ, how do you find the sign of the answer? Look at which number is larger in absolute value. −56 has absolute value 56. 12 = 36. 56 is larger. So the answer takes the sign of −56 — it is negative.” - Blanks: LCD = [6] / 12 = [3]/6 / −56 + [3]/6 = −[2]/6 = [−13].

CUE

★ Write: “Fill in Example 3. When signs differ — subtract numerators, take the sign of the [larger absolute value].”

Self-explanation beat: SAY: “Before we move on — explain the mixed-sign case in your own words: when the signs differ, how do you determine the sign of the answer? Write a rule in your own words.” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your own rule for the mixed-sign case — what did we do and why?”

Beat 2.7 — The critical misconception: adding numerators AND denominators

SHOW

Board: 12 + 13. Student error: 25. Correct: 56. Show both on number line — mark 25 and 56 and show they are different points.

SAY

- “This is the most common fraction error — and it is very tempting.” - “A student sees 12 + 13 and thinks: add the tops, add the bottoms. Gets 25.” - “Is that right? Check with decimals. 12 = 0.5. 13 = 0.333. Their sum = 0.833.” - “Is 25 equal to 0.833? No — 25 = 0.4. These are very different numbers.” - “On the number line: 25 is less than half. 56 is close to 1. The error gives an answer that is smaller than either addend — that alone should trigger alarm.” - “The denominator is not a count — it names the type of piece. You cannot add pieces of different sizes by just counting them. Convert first.”

CUE

 

FLAG — Misconception“12 + 13 = 25 — add numerators and add denominators.” This misconception must be directly addressed here. Do not simply state the correct procedure — show why the shortcut is wrong using the decimal check and the number line. Students who have the rule but not the understanding will revert to 25 under time pressure on a test. The decimal check is the fastest correction: if 25 = 0.4 and the sum should be 0.833, something went badly wrong.

CUE: ★ Write: “Misconception — 12 + 13 ≠ 25. Decimal check: 0.5 + 0.333 = 0.833 but 25 = [0.4]. Correct answer: [56].”

Beat 2.8 — Subtracting: adding the opposite

SHOW

Board: 58 − (−38). Rewrite as 58 + 38. Answer: 88 = 1.

SAY

- “Subtraction — the key move is rewriting it as adding the opposite.” - “58 minus negative 38. Subtracting a negative is the same as adding a positive.” - “The two negatives resolve: −(−38) = +38.” - “58 + 38 = 88 = 1.” - “On the number line: start at 58. Subtracting a negative means hopping right — same direction as addition. We hop 38 to the right and land at 1.” - Blanks: = 58 + [38] = [1].

CUE

★ Write: “Subtracting negative: 58 − (−38) = 58 + [38] = [1].”

Beat 2.9 — Alberta context: Fort McMurray temperature

SHOW

Board: Fort McMurray — Monday: −7.5°C. Rise of 11.25°C by Wednesday. What is Wednesday’s temperature?

SAY

- “Real Alberta context. Fort McMurray winters are serious — it gets to −40°C.” - “Monday is −7.5. Temperature rises by 11.25. This is addition: −7.5 + 11.25.” - “Convert to a common form: −7.5 = −7.50. Add: −7.50 + 11.25 = 3.75.” - “Wednesday: 3.75°C.” - “Number line check: start at −7.5, hop right 11.25. We cross 0 and land at +3.75.” - Blanks: −7.5 + [11.25] = [3.75]°C.

CUE

 

FLAG — Alberta ContextTemperature problems are not pseudo-context here — the numbers and direction of change (negative start, positive rise) give this problem meaning. If you remove the context, the operation is ambiguous. Emphasize this: the negative temperature is the starting point; the rise is the addend; the context tells you to add, not subtract.

CUE: ★ Write: “Temperature = −7.5 + [11.25] = [3.75]°C.”

Section 3 — Multiplying Rational Numbers

Learning target: I can multiply rational numbers in fraction form, determine the correct sign, and simplify the result. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 3.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Three terms — Product / Simplify before multiplying (cross-cancel) / Reciprocal

SAY

- “Three terms today. Product and reciprocal are likely familiar.” - “Cross-cancellation is the new technique — and it is worth learning because it keeps numbers small.” - “We will build the reciprocal definition properly here because it is the foundation of Section 4.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “Which of these three is least familiar? Hold that.”

Beat 3.2 — The multiplication rule: no common denominator needed

SHOW

Board: (ab) × (cd) = acbd. Large and clear. Emphasize: multiply numerators, multiply denominators.

SAY

- “This is the good news about multiplication: you do NOT need a common denominator.” - “Multiply the numerators. Multiply the denominators. That is it.” - “Why no common denominator? Because multiplication is fundamentally different from addition. Adding fractions means counting like pieces — you need the pieces to be the same size. Multiplying a fraction by a fraction means taking a fraction of a fraction — the denominators multiply naturally.” - “Example: 12 × 13 means ‘half of one-third.’ That is one-sixth. And 2 × 3 = 6 — it works.”

CUE

★ Write: “(ab) × (cd) = [acbd] — multiply numerators × multiply denominators.”

Beat 3.3 — Sign rules for multiplication

SHOW

Board: Table with four rows — Positive × Positive / Negative × Negative / Positive × Negative / Negative × Positive. Leave the sign-of-product column blank.

SAY

- “Sign rules — these apply to all rational number multiplication.” - “Positive times positive: both factors are positive, product is positive. Easy.” - “Negative times negative: this one surprises students. Two negatives give a positive. We will derive why in a moment.” - “Positive times negative: negative.” - “Negative times positive: negative.” - “Quick rule: count the negative signs. Odd number of negatives — product is negative. Even number — product is positive.”

CUE

★ Write: “Negative × Negative = [positive]. Positive × Negative = [negative]. Quick rule: [odd] number of negatives → negative product.”

Beat 3.6 — Cross-cancellation

SHOW

Board: (815) × (54). Show the cross-cancel before multiplying. Circle 8 and 4 (share factor 4). Circle 5 and 15 (share factor 5). Reduce, then multiply.

SAY

Same answer, much messier.” - Blanks: 8 and 4 share factor [4] / 5 and 15 share factor [5] / 8÷4 = [2] / 5÷15 = [13] / multiply: [(23) × (11) = 23].

CUE

★ Write: “Cross-cancel example — factors [4] and [5]. Result: [23].”

Self-explanation beat: SAY: “Explain why cross-cancelling before multiplying gives the same answer as multiplying then reducing. Which method is easier and why?” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation. Which method do you prefer and why?”

Beat 3.8 — The cross-cancel misconception

SHOW

Board: (38) × (49). Show valid cross-cancel: 3 and 9 (diagonal — valid, one is a numerator, one a denominator from the other fraction). Show invalid: 8 and 4 — are they valid? Yes (8 is denominator, 4 is numerator). Add a fake example: show trying to cancel two numerators — invalid.

SAY

- “Cross-cancellation works between a numerator and a denominator. That is the only rule.” - “38 × 49: can we cancel? 3 (numerator, first fraction) and 9 (denominator, second fraction) share factor 3. Valid — cancel.” - “8 (denominator, first) and 4 (numerator, second) share factor 4. Valid — cancel.” - “What you cannot do: cancel two numerators with each other, or two denominators with each other.” - “In the student notes: can you cancel the 8 and the 4? Yes — 8 is a denominator, 4 is a numerator. Why? They share factor 4, and they are on opposite roles in the multiplication.”

CUE
