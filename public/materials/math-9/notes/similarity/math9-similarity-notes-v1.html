Scale factor:

A scale factor compares the lengths of a scaled version to the original.

Scale factor = length / length

If scale factor > 1: the image is than the original (enlargement).
If scale factor < 1: the image is than the original (reduction).
If scale factor = 1: the image is (congruent).

Guided Notes

Two polygons are similar (~) if:

  1. All corresponding angles are , AND
  2. All corresponding sides are (in proportion)

Both conditions must be met. Angles alone or sides alone are NOT sufficient.

Misconception: Similar means the same (congruent)

Wrong idea: "Similar and congruent both mean the shapes match."

Why it fails: Congruent shapes are identical — same shape AND same size. Similar shapes have the same shape but may be different sizes. All congruent shapes are similar (scale factor = 1), but most similar shapes are NOT congruent.

Two rectangles: one is 2 cm × 4 cm, the other is 4 cm × 8 cm. They are (same proportions). Are they congruent?

The similarity statement:

When polygons ABCD ~ EFGH, the ORDER of the letters shows the correspondence:

  • A corresponds to E → angle A = angle

Read the similarity statement carefully. The first vertex of each polygon corresponds.

Section 3 — Criteria for Similar Triangles

Learning target: I can use AA, SSS, and SAS similarity criteria to prove that two triangles are similar without measuring all six parts.

Guided Notes

For triangles only, you do not need to check all six parts. Three efficient shortcuts exist.

AA — Angle-Angle:

If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar.

Why only two angles? Because the third angle is automatically determined:
Third angle = 180° − −

If angle A = angle D and angle B = angle E, then angle = angle automatically.
Therefore triangle ABC ~ triangle .

SSS — Side-Side-Side:

If all three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional, the triangles are similar.

a/d = b/e = c/f (all equal to the scale factor k)

Worked Example — AA:
Two triangles: Triangle PQR has angle P = 55°, angle Q = 75°.
Triangle XYZ has angle X = 55°, angle Z = 50°.

In PQR: angle R = 180 − 55 − 75 = °
In XYZ: angle Y = 180 − 55 − 50 = °
Do any two angles match between the triangles?
Angle P = Angle X = 55° ✓
Angle R = Angle Z = 50°? (check: PQR has R = °, XYZ has Z = 50°)
Therefore by AA: triangle PQR ~ triangle

SAS — Side-Angle-Side:

If two pairs of sides are proportional AND the included angle (the angle between those sides) is equal, the triangles are similar.

Section 4 — Scale Diagrams and Indirect Measurement

Learning target: I can create and read scale diagrams, and use similar triangles to measure distances that are too large or difficult to measure directly.

Vocabulary

TermDefinition in your own wordsOne example
Scale diagram
Scale
Indirect measurement
Shadow method

Guided Notes

Scale diagrams represent real objects at a reduced (or enlarged) size.

Scale = diagram measurement : real measurement

Written as 1 : n, or as a fraction (1/n).

Reading a scale:

Scale 1 : 5000 means 1 cm on the diagram = cm in real life = m.

Worked Example — reading a map:
A map scale is 1 : 25 000.
A road on the map measures 3.2 cm.
Real length = 3.2 × 25 000 = cm = km
Drawing to scale:
A swimming pool is 50 m long and 25 m wide. Draw to scale 1 : 500.
Diagram length = 50 m / 500 = m = cm
Diagram width = 25 m / 500 = m = cm

Indirect measurement — shadow method:

Similar triangles let you measure tall objects by comparing shadows.

At the same time of day, the sun angle is the same → both triangles are similar (AA).

h/s₁ = 1.5/s₂

Worked Example — Finding tree height:
At 2 PM, a measuring stick 1.5 m tall casts a 0.9 m shadow. A nearby tree casts a 7.2 m shadow. Find the tree's height.
h/7.2 = 1.5/0.9
h = 7.2 × (1.5/0.9)
h = 7.2 × = m

Explain: Why must we measure both shadows at the same time? What happens to the similar triangles if we measure at different times?

Mirror method:

A mirror placed flat on the ground reflects an object's top to an observer's eye. Both triangles (object-to-mirror, observer-to-mirror) are similar because of equal angles of incidence and reflection.

h_object / d₁ = h_observer / d₂

Alberta context — Measuring a grain elevator:
A student stands 60 m from a grain elevator and places a mirror at 10 m from herself. Her eye height is 1.6 m. The mirror reflects the top of the elevator.
h/60 = 1.6/10 → h = m
