Math 9 — Similarity and Scale

Teacher Notes Package

Teaching reference — not a script

How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

Label What it contains
SHOW What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAY Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUE The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩︎ Check

Flags appear in callout blocks: > FLAG — Misconception: Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address. > FLAG — Pacing: Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum. > FLAG — Alberta Context: Locally anchored problem or example worth dwelling on.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual — labels, diagrams, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Pre-Knowledge Check

~10 minutes — before any instruction

Beat 1 — Opening SHOW: Board: “What do you think ‘similar’ means in geometry? Is it the same as ‘congruent’?” (That’s it — no further prompts on board.) SAY: - “Before we start the unit, I want to know what’s already in your head.” - “This is not a test. There are no marks. I’m not going to correct it today.” - “Write honestly. You will come back to this page at the end of the unit.” - “Also: can you use proportions to find a missing side length? Try to write an example.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes to the Pre-Knowledge Check. You have 5 minutes — write exactly what you think right now. Go.”

FLAG — PacingDo not comment on or correct anything students write. The pre-knowledge check has no value if students calibrate their answers to what you reward. Circulate and read — you are looking for: (a) students who already correctly distinguish similar from congruent, (b) students who write “same size and shape” for similar (the most common error), (c) students who can already write a proportion but may not know what a scale factor is. These three categories tell you which misconceptions to hit hardest in Section 2.

Section 1 — Ratio, Rate, and Proportion Review

Learning target: I can write and solve proportions, and distinguish between ratio, rate, and proportion. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: The five vocabulary terms in a column — Ratio / Rate / Proportion / Cross-multiply / Scale factor SAY: - “Five terms on your vocab table. Don’t fill them in yet.” - “We’ll build each one as we go. By the end of the section you’ll have your own definition and example.” - “Which of these five do you think you already know? Keep it in your head.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read the five terms. Think — don’t write yet.”

Beat 2 — Ratio vs. rate SHOW: Board: RATIO — same units | RATE — different units. Examples: 3:5 boys to girls (ratio). 80 km per hour (rate). SAY: - “A ratio compares two quantities with the same units.” - “3 boys to 5 girls — same unit (people). That’s a ratio.” - “80 km per hour — different units: kilometres and hours. That’s a rate.” - “The difference: same units vs. different units. That’s it.” CUE: ★ Write: “Ratio — compares two quantities with the [same] units. Rate — compares two quantities with [different] units.”

Beat 3 — Proportion SHOW: Board: PROPORTION = two equal ratios. Write: a/b = c/d SAY: - “A proportion states that two ratios are equal.” - “a/b equals c/d. That’s a proportion.” - “The key operation: cross-products. a times d equals b times c. Always.” - “Write it: a × d = b × c” CUE: ★ Write: “A proportion states that two [ratios] are equal. Cross-products: a × [d] = b × [c].”

FLAG — PacingStudents will ask “why does cross-multiplication work?” — this is a good question and there’s a self-explanation blank in the notes for it. The answer: we’re multiplying both sides by bd to clear both denominators. Don’t skip this — students who understand why cross-multiplication works set up proportions correctly; students who treat it as a trick make ratio-direction errors on every multi-step problem.

Beat 4 — Worked example: solving a proportion SHOW: Board: Solve: x/6 = 4/9. Work through it step by step — cross-multiply, then divide. SAY: - “Cross-multiply: 9 times x equals 6 times 4.” - “9x = 24.” - “Divide both sides by 9: x = 24 divided by 9.” - “Simplify: 8/3, or 2.67 if the question asks for decimal.” - “Check: does 8/3 divided by 6 equal 4/9? Plug it back in.” CUE: ★ Write: “9x = [24]. x = [24] / 9 = [8/3].”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: what did we do and why did we cross-multiply instead of just dividing?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the Explain prompt under the worked example — what operation are we actually applying to both sides?”

Beat 5 — Scale factor SHOW: Board: SCALE FACTOR = new length ÷ original length. Draw two rectangles: original 4×6, scaled 10×15. SAY: - “Scale factor compares the scaled version to the original.” - “New over original. Always set it up the same way.” - “Scale factor = 10/4 = 2.5. Check: 15/6 = 2.5. Both ratios must match.” - “If scale factor is greater than 1 — what happened to the shape?” - Pause. Wait for response. - “Enlarged. Greater than 1 means enlargement.” - “Less than 1 — reduced. Equal to 1 — same size, congruent.” CUE: ★ Write: “Scale factor = [new] length / [original] length. Greater than 1 = [larger] (enlargement). Less than 1 = [smaller] (reduction). Equal to 1 = [the same size / congruent].”

FLAG — Misconception — Scale factor directionStudents confuse which way to write the scale factor. “New over original” is the convention — but students often write it the other direction when the problem gives the original first. Drill this: “Always ask yourself: what is the image? That goes on top.” Watch also for students who say scale factor > 1 means reduction. Reinforce: enlargement makes things bigger, the number must be bigger than 1. Ask: “If I double every side, what is the scale factor?” If a student says 1/2, this misconception is present.

Beat 6 — Alberta context: map scale SHOW: Board: Map of Banff National Park. Scale 1 : 50 000. Draw: a line segment labeled 6.4 cm. SAY: - “Real map problem. Scale 1 : 50 000 means 1 cm on the map equals 50 000 cm in real life.” - “A trail measures 6.4 cm on the map. How long is the trail?” - “Set up the proportion: 6.4 / 1 = d / 50 000.” - “d = 6.4 × 50 000 = 320 000 cm.” - “Convert: 320 000 cm ÷ 100 ÷ 1000 = 3.2 km.” - “A 3.2 km trail. Does that feel realistic for a park trail?”

FLAG — Alberta ContextIf you have a physical map of Banff or a printed Alberta road map with a scale bar, hand it out here. Have students measure one route and calculate the real distance. The physical act of using a ruler on a real map makes this sticky. Canmore to Banff on a 1 : 500 000 map is about 2.5 cm → 12.5 km in real life.

CUE: ★ Write: “d = [320,000] cm = [3.2] km.”

Beat 7 — Proportional reasoning: recipe context SHOW: Board: 3 cups of flour → 24 cookies. How many cups → 60 cookies? SAY: - “Same skill, different context. Set up the proportion: 3/24 = x/60.” - “Cross-multiply: 24x = 180.” - “x = 7.5 cups.” - “Sanity check: 60 cookies is 2.5 times 24 cookies. 2.5 × 3 = 7.5. ✓” CUE: ★ Write: “x = [7.5] cups.”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain why the sanity check works. What does it tell us about scale factor here?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer: both methods give 7.5. The scale factor for the recipe is 2.5 — same as multiplying by 2.5 directly.”

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed. I’m going to ask you some questions — write the answers from memory.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish (4–5 min): SAY: “Open your notes. Find the answer. Fix anything that was wrong — use a different colour if you have one.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. x/5 = 12/15 → 15x = 60 → x = 4 2. (x + 2)/7 = 6/3 → 3(x + 2) = 42 → 3x + 6 = 42 → 3x = 36 → x = 12 3. Scale factor = 25/10 = 2.5. Missing dimension = 15 × 2.5 = 37.5 cm. 4. Scale factor = 6/18 = 1/3 (reduction; the shape shrank to one-third its original size). 5. Stretch — 3/x = x/12 → x² = 36 → x = 6. This is a quadratic (specifically a proportion where the variable appears in both ratios). It’s not linear. x = 6 makes both ratios equal: 3/6 = 6/12 = 1/2.

Q5 is the stretch — students who arrive at “x² = 36” and recognize that this is not a typical linear proportion are ready for more. Don’t rush to give the answer before the retrieval check ends.

Section 2 — Similar Polygons

Learning target: I can determine if two polygons are similar, find corresponding sides and angles, and use proportions to find missing measurements. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Four terms — Similar polygons / Corresponding sides / Corresponding angles / Congruent SAY: - “Four terms today. Hold off on filling them in — we’ll build them as we go.” - “The most important pair on this list is the last one and the first one.” - “Similar. Congruent. By the end of this section, you’ll be able to say exactly what makes them different.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read the four terms. Think about the difference between similar and congruent. Don’t write yet.”

Beat 2 — The critical distinction: similar vs. congruent

FLAG — This beat is the most important in the unit. Similar vs. congruent is the most commonly confused pair in all of Similarity and Scale. Give it dedicated time before building anything else.

SHOW: Board: Side by side — two rectangles that are similar (2×4 and 4×8). Then two rectangles that are congruent (both 3×5). SAY: - “Look at the left pair. Same shape — both rectangles. Different sizes. These are similar.” - “Look at the right pair. Same shape. Same size. These are congruent.” - “Here’s the rule — and say it with me when I write it:” - Write on board: Similar = same shape, proportional sides, equal angles. Congruent = same shape AND same size. - “All congruent shapes are similar — scale factor 1. Most similar shapes are NOT congruent.” - “When someone says two shapes are ‘the same’ — ask: same shape only, or same shape AND same size?” CUE: ★ Write: “Similar (~) = same [shape], [proportional] sides, [equal] angles. Congruent (≅) = same shape AND same [size].”

FLAG — Misconception — “Similar means identical”Students who write “same” on their pre-knowledge check for similar will revert to this under pressure. The correction is not to repeat the definition — it’s to use a counterexample immediately. Ask: “Are these two rectangles identical?” (Show the 2×4 and 4×8.) “No — one is bigger. Are they similar? Yes. Same shape, different size.” If a student says “they look the same shape but different size, so they’re not really the same,” that’s the misconception. Correct: they ARE similar, because similar doesn’t mean identical.

Beat 3 — Two conditions for similarity SHOW: Board: TWO CONDITIONS: 1. All corresponding angles are equal. 2. All corresponding sides are proportional (same ratio). Both conditions must be met — angles alone or sides alone are NOT sufficient. SAY: - “Two conditions. Both required. Neither one alone is enough.” - “A rectangle and a square both have four 90° angles — all angles equal. Are they similar?” - Pause for response. - “Not necessarily. If the sides aren’t in the same ratio, they’re not similar. You need both.” - “A rhombus and a square — all sides equal. Are they similar? Not if the angles are different.” CUE: ★ Write: “Two polygons are similar if: (1) all corresponding angles are [equal], AND (2) all corresponding sides are [proportional].”

Beat 4 — Reading the similarity statement SHOW: Board: ABCD ~ EFGH. Under it, list the correspondences: A↔︎E, B↔︎F, C↔︎G, D↔︎H. Then: AB/EF = BC/FG = CD/GH = DA/HE. SAY: - “The similarity statement tells you everything. The order of the letters is the correspondence.” - “A is first in ABCD. E is first in EFGH. So A corresponds to E.” - “Angle A equals angle E. Side AB over side EF is the scale factor.” - “If you mix up the order, your proportion is wrong and your answer is wrong.” - “Read the similarity statement carefully every time.” CUE: ★ Write: “A corresponds to E → angle A = angle [E]. AB corresponds to EF → AB/EF = [BC]/[FG].”

Beat 5 — Worked example: finding a missing side SHOW: Board: Triangle ABC ~ Triangle DEF. AB = 6, BC = 9, DE = 4. Find EF. Work through it step by step. SAY: - “Scale factor = DE/AB = 4/6 = 2/3. We use new over original — DEF is the new (image) triangle.” - “EF corresponds to BC.” - “EF/BC = 2/3. So EF/9 = 2/3.” - “EF = 9 × (2/3) = 6.” CUE: ★ Write: “Scale factor = [2/3]. EF/BC = [2/3]. EF = 9 × [2/3] = [6].”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 1: why did we use DE/AB rather than AB/DE to find the scale factor?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the Explain prompt — new triangle over original triangle. DE is in the image (DEF). AB is in the original (ABC).”

FLAG — Misconception — Setting up proportions incorrectlyWatch for students who write AB/DE instead of DE/AB, then apply the scale factor in the wrong direction. The error produces an answer that is larger than the original when the shape has been reduced. Drill the habit: “Which triangle is the image? Write that one on top.” If they’re not told which is the image, they should pick one consistently and stick with it throughout the problem. Mixing directions (DE/AB for one ratio, AB/DE for another) produces incorrect answers and is the most common proportion error in this unit.

Beat 6 — Worked example: checking if two polygons are similar SHOW: Board: Quadrilateral PQRS (angles 70°, 110°, 80°, 100°; sides 4, 6, 5, 3) and WXYZ (angles 70°, 110°, 80°, 100°; sides 8, 12, 10, 6). Work through both checks. SAY: - “Two conditions — check angles first, then sides.” - “Angles: P = W = 70°? Yes. Q = X = 110°? Yes. R = Y = 80°? Yes. S = Z = 100°? Yes.” - “All angles match. Now sides: WX/PQ = 8/4 = 2. XY/QR = 12/6 = 2. YZ/RS = 10/5 = 2. ZW/SP = 6/3 = 2.” - “All ratios equal 2. Scale factor = 2. Both conditions met. Similar.” CUE: ★ Write: “All angle checks: [yes/yes/yes/yes]. All ratio checks: [2/2/2/2]. Similar? [Yes].”

Beat 7 — Perimeter ratio SHOW: Board: PQRS perimeter = 4+6+5+3 = 18. WXYZ perimeter = 8+12+10+6 = 36. Ratio = 36/18 = 2 = scale factor. SAY: - “The ratio of the perimeters equals the scale factor. Every time.” - “Why? The perimeter is the sum of the sides. Each side scaled by 2. So the sum scales by 2.” - “Useful shortcut: if you know the scale factor and one perimeter, you can find the other.” CUE: ★ Write: “PQRS perimeter = [18]. WXYZ perimeter = [36]. Ratio = [36]/[18] = [2] = scale factor ✓.”

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. Two triangles with equal angles are necessarily similar (AA — covered fully in Section 3). They are NOT necessarily congruent — you would also need at least one matching side length. 2. Rectangles A (3 × 8) and B (9 × 24): check ratios — 9/3 = 3, 24/8 = 3. Both ratios equal 3 → similar. Scale factor = 3. 3. Triangle ABC ~ DEF. Scale factor = DE/AB = 10/5 = 2. EF = BC × 2 = 8 × 2 = 16. DF = AC × 2 = 6 × 2 = 12. 4. Two conditions: (1) all corresponding angles equal, AND (2) all corresponding sides proportional. 5. Stretch — scale factor for perimeters is k (demonstrated above). Prediction for areas: k². Test: Rectangle A area = 3 × 8 = 24. Rectangle B area = 9 × 24 = 216. Ratio = 216/24 = 9 = 3² = k². Prediction holds.

Q5 is the stretch — do not give the k² result before students attempt it. Students who arrive at this independently are ready for Grade 10 surface area scaling.

Section 3 — Criteria for Similar Triangles

Learning target: I can use AA, SSS, and SAS similarity criteria to prove that two triangles are similar without measuring all six parts. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Four terms — AA (Angle-Angle) similarity / SSS (Side-Side-Side) similarity / SAS (Side-Angle-Side) similarity / Included angle SAY: - “Four shortcuts for triangles specifically. Triangles are special — you don’t have to check all six parts.” - “Each shortcut has a name. The name tells you what to check.” - “Let’s build each one.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read the four terms. What do you think ‘included angle’ means? Keep it in your head.”

Beat 2 — Why triangles are special SHOW: Board: Triangle with angles A, B, C. Write: A + B + C = 180°. Therefore C = 180° − A − B. SAY: - “Triangles have three angles that must add to 180°. Always.” - “So if you know two angles, you automatically know the third.” - “Third angle = 180° minus the other two. It’s forced.” - “This is why AA works. Two angles tell you the third — so two angles tell you the complete angle picture.” CUE: ★ Write: “Third angle = 180° − [first angle] − [second angle].”

Beat 3 — AA Similarity SHOW: Board: AA — if two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another, the triangles are similar. SAY: - “AA — Angle-Angle. Two matching angles. That’s all.” - “If angle A = angle D and angle B = angle E, then angle C must equal angle F — it’s forced.” - “So all three angles match. Same angle picture = same shape = similar.” - “If angle A = angle D and angle B = angle E, what similarity statement can we write?” - Pause. - “Triangle ABC ~ Triangle DEF.” CUE: ★ Write: “If angle A = angle D and angle B = angle E, then angle [C] = angle [F] automatically. Therefore triangle ABC ~ triangle [DEF].”

Beat 4 — Worked example: AA SHOW: Board: Triangle PQR — angle P = 55°, angle Q = 75°. Triangle XYZ — angle X = 55°, angle Z = 50°. Work through finding the third angle in each and identifying the match. SAY: - “Find angle R in PQR: 180 − 55 − 75 = 50°.” - “Find angle Y in XYZ: 180 − 55 − 50 = 75°.” - “Now match angles: P = X = 55°. Q = Y = 75°. R = Z = 50°.” - “Two angles match? Yes — angle P = angle X, angle R = angle Z.” - “By AA: triangle PQR ~ triangle XZY. Watch the order — Z corresponds to R, Y corresponds to Q.” CUE: ★ Write: “Angle R = [50]°. Angle Y = [75]°. Angle R = Angle Z = 50°? [Yes]. By AA: triangle PQR ~ triangle [XZY].”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain why we had to be careful about the order of letters in the similarity statement.” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer: the letter order shows correspondence. If we write PQR ~ XYZ, we’re saying P↔︎X, Q↔︎Y, R↔︎Z. That’s only correct if those pairs actually have equal angles. Here R = Z, so Z must be listed second, not third.”

Beat 5 — SSS Similarity SHOW: Board: SSS — if all three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional, the triangles are similar. Write: a/d = b/e = c/f = k SAY: - “SSS — Side-Side-Side. Check all three ratios.” - “All three ratios must be equal. If even one is different, it’s not similar by SSS.” - “You’re not checking that the sides are equal — you’re checking that they’re proportional.” CUE: ★ Write under the diagram as you work the example below.*

Beat 6 — Worked example: SSS SHOW: Board: Triangle ABC — a = 6, b = 9, c = 12. Triangle DEF — d = 4, e = 6, f = 8. Work through all three ratios. SAY: - “d/a = 4/6 = 2/3.” - “e/b = 6/9 = 2/3.” - “f/c = 8/12 = 2/3.” - “All three equal? Yes. Scale factor = 2/3. Similar by SSS.” CUE: ★ Write: “d/a = [2/3]. e/b = [2/3]. f/c = [2/3]. All equal? [Yes] → Similar by [SSS].”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain what would happen to the SSS test if one ratio came out differently. What would that tell us?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer: if one ratio is different, the sides are not all proportional. The triangles are not similar by SSS. They might still be similar by another criterion, but SSS alone doesn’t hold.”

Beat 7 — SAS Similarity SHOW: Board: SAS — two pairs of sides proportional AND the included angle (the angle BETWEEN those sides) is equal. SAY: - “SAS — two sides proportional, one angle equal.” - “The angle must be the included angle — the angle sandwiched between the two sides you’re comparing.” - “If the equal angle is not between the proportional sides, SAS does not apply.” - “This is the one students get wrong most often — they check two sides and any angle, not the included angle.” CUE: ★ Write: “SAS — two sides [proportional], included angle [equal]. The angle must be [between] the two sides.”

Beat 8 — Criterion identification table SHOW: Board: Draw the four-row table: (1) Two angles of each equal → AA, (2) All three sides proportional → SSS, (3) Two sides proportional, angle NOT between them equal → Neither (SSA — not a valid criterion), (4) One side proportional, two angles equal → AA (the side is irrelevant once AA is established). SAY: - “Let’s fill in the table. I want you to think before I write.” - Row 1: “Two angles match — which criterion?” AA. - Row 2: “All three sides proportional?” SSS. - Row 3: “Two sides proportional — but the equal angle is NOT between them.” Pause. “This is a trap. SSA does not prove similarity. Neither.” - Row 4: “One side proportional, two angles equal?” AA — the side information doesn’t add to what AA already tells us. CUE: ★ Write: “Complete the criterion table in your notes.”

FLAG — PacingThe “angle NOT between them” case (row 3) trips students up on every test. Emphasize: SAS requires the included angle. If a student can point to the angle physically and show it’s sandwiched between the two sides, SAS applies. If they can’t point to it that way, SAS does not apply.

Beat 9 — Parallel lines and similar triangles SHOW: Board: Draw a large triangle ABC. Draw a line DE parallel to BC, with D on AB and E on AC. Label the inner triangle ADE. SAY: - “When a line is drawn parallel to one side of a triangle, it creates a smaller triangle inside.” - “Are triangle ADE and triangle ABC similar?” - “DE is parallel to BC. What do parallel lines and a transversal create?” - Pause for response: “Corresponding angles — equal.” - “Angle A is shared. Angle ADE = angle ABC (corresponding angles, DE ∥ BC). Two angles equal — AA.” - “Triangle ADE ~ Triangle ABC.” CUE: ★ Write: “Triangle ADE ~ Triangle [ABC] (by AA, since DE ∥ BC creates equal [corresponding] angles).”

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Three criteria. Name each one, state what you check, and give the limitation of SAS.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. AA: If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar. (The third angle is automatically equal because angles sum to 180°.) 2. Triangle ABC with AB = 4, BC = 6, angle B = 50°. Triangle DEF with DE = 8, EF = 12, angle E = 50°. Ratios: DE/AB = 8/4 = 2. EF/BC = 12/6 = 2. The equal angle (B = E = 50°) is between AB and BC (and between DE and EF respectively). Two sides proportional, included angle equal → SAS similarity. Triangle ABC ~ Triangle DEF (scale factor 2). 3. No — one pair of equal angles is not sufficient. You need two pairs (AA), OR all three sides proportional (SSS), OR two sides proportional with the included angle equal (SAS). One angle alone could fit an infinite number of different triangles. 4. Triangle PQR ~ Triangle STU by AA. Angle P = 47°, angle Q = 83°. Angle R = 180 − 47 − 83 = 50°. Since PQR ~ STU with P↔︎S, Q↔︎T, R↔︎U: angle S = 47°, angle T = 83°, angle U = 50°. 5. Stretch — In diagram with DE ∥ BC: triangle ADE ~ triangle ABC by AA. AD/AB = DE/BC. AD = 3, DB = 5 → AB = 8. So 3/8 = 4/BC → BC = 4 × 8/3 = 32/3 ≈ 10.67. Proportion used: AD/AB = DE/BC (ratios of corresponding sides).

Section 4 — Scale Diagrams and Indirect Measurement

Learning target: I can create and read scale diagrams, and use similar triangles to measure distances that are too large or difficult to measure directly. Timing: ~30 minutes direct instruction + optional outdoor shadow activity

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Four terms — Scale diagram / Scale / Indirect measurement / Shadow method SAY: - “Four terms. Hold off filling them in.” - “The big idea in this section: similar triangles are useful outside the classroom. You can measure something without touching it.” - “The shadow method and mirror method are both ways of using similar triangles to measure heights we can’t reach directly.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read ‘indirect measurement.’ What do you think it means to measure something indirectly?”

Beat 2 — Scale diagrams SHOW: Board: Scale diagram = a diagram that represents a real object at a reduced (or enlarged) size, keeping all proportions exact. Write: Scale = 1 : n. SAY: - “A scale diagram represents the real object proportionally — same shape, different size.” - “Scale 1 : n means 1 unit on the diagram equals n units in real life.” - “Scale 1 : 5000 means 1 cm on the diagram equals 5000 cm = 50 m in real life.” - “Always convert units after multiplying — don’t leave the answer in centimetres if metres makes more sense.” CUE: ★ Write: “Scale 1 : 5000 → 1 cm on diagram = [5000] cm in real life = [50] m.”

Beat 3 — Reading a map scale SHOW: Board: Map scale 1 : 25 000. A road measures 3.2 cm on the map. Find the real length. SAY: - “Real length = diagram measurement × scale.” - “3.2 cm × 25 000 = 80 000 cm.” - “Convert: 80 000 ÷ 100 = 800 m. Or 800 ÷ 1000 = 0.8 km.” - “A short road — 800 m. Does that make sense for a map?”

FLAG — Alberta ContextIf you have a printed map of Alberta or a Google Maps screenshot, pause and have students read a scale bar. Ask: “How far is it from your school to the nearest major road in km? Measure on the map and calculate.” This grounds the skill in a real context students have walked or driven.

CUE: ★ Write: “3.2 × 25 000 = [80,000] cm = [0.8] km.”

Beat 4 — Drawing to scale SHOW: Board: A pool is 50 m × 25 m. Draw to scale 1 : 500. Work through both dimensions. SAY: - “To draw to scale, divide the real measurement by the scale factor.” - “Length: 50 m ÷ 500 = 0.1 m = 10 cm on the diagram.” - “Width: 25 m ÷ 500 = 0.05 m = 5 cm on the diagram.” - “Draw the rectangle. Label both dimensions on the diagram.” CUE: ★ Write: “Diagram length = 50 / 500 = [0.1] m = [10] cm. Diagram width = 25 / 500 = [0.05] m = [5] cm.”

Beat 5 — Shadow method: why it works SHOW: Board: Draw the shadow method diagram — tall object (height h) casting shadow s₁; measuring stick (1.5 m) casting shadow s₂. Two right triangles formed. Same sun angle. SAY: - “At the same time of day, the sun is at the same angle for both objects.” - “Each object and its shadow forms a right triangle.” - “Both triangles have a right angle. Both have the same sun angle (AA).” - “Two angles equal → AA → the triangles are similar.” - “Proportion: h / s₁ = 1.5 / s₂.” CUE: ★ Write: “Both shadows measured at the same time → same sun angle → AA → triangles are [similar]. Proportion: h/s₁ = 1.5/s₂.”

FLAG — MisconceptionStudents sometimes set up the proportion as h / 1.5 = s₁ / s₂ — comparing the heights ratio to the shadows ratio instead of writing it as height / shadow = height / shadow. Emphasize the structure: corresponding parts must be in corresponding positions. “Height of tall thing over its shadow equals height of stick over its shadow.” Write it that way.
FLAG — Alberta ContextThis is a natural outdoor activity. Take the class outside with a metre stick (or a student standing upright while measured). Measure the stick’s shadow and one or two tall fixed objects (flagpole, gym wall, basketball standard). Have students set up the proportion and calculate. Banff gondola tower example from the student notes: a tree shadow of 7.2 m with a 1.5 m stick casting a 0.9 m shadow. If you’re at a school near the foothills, a local grain elevator or radio tower photo works as context.

Beat 6 — Worked example: shadow method SHOW: Board: Stick 1.5 m tall, shadow 0.9 m. Tree shadow 7.2 m. Find h. SAY: - “Set up: h / 7.2 = 1.5 / 0.9.” - “Simplify 1.5/0.9 = 5/3.” - “h = 7.2 × (5/3) = 7.2 × 1.667 = 12 m.” - “The tree is 12 m tall. Is that realistic for a large tree? Yes — a mature spruce in Alberta can be 20–30 m.” CUE: ★ Write: “h = 7.2 × [5/3] = [12] m.”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain why we must measure both shadows at the same time. What happens to the similar triangles if we measure at different times of day?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer: the sun angle changes throughout the day. Different times = different angles in the triangles = triangles no longer similar. The proportion breaks down and the answer is wrong.”

Beat 7 — Mirror method SHOW: Board: Draw the mirror method diagram — tall object on the left, mirror on the ground in the middle, observer on the right. Dashed lines from the top of the object to the mirror, and from the mirror to the observer’s eye. Label: h_object, d₁, h_observer, d₂. SAY: - “A mirror placed flat on the ground reflects the top of a tall object to an observer’s eye.” - “The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection — physics law.” - “Both triangles share those equal angles — AA applies.” - “Proportion: h_object / d₁ = h_observer / d₂.” - “You know your own eye height, d₂, and d₁. Solve for h_object.” CUE: ★ Write: “Mirror method proportion: h_object / d₁ = h_observer / d₂.”

Beat 8 — Worked example: mirror method (grain elevator) SHOW: Board: Student stands 60 m from grain elevator. Mirror at 10 m from student. Eye height = 1.6 m. Find elevator height. SAY: - “d₁ = distance from elevator to mirror = 60 − 10 = 50 m.” - “d₂ = distance from student to mirror = 10 m.” - “h_observer = 1.6 m (eye height).” - “h / 50 = 1.6 / 10.” - “h = 50 × (1.6/10) = 50 × 0.16 = 8 m.”

Note: The student notes state d₁ = 60 m based on the student standing 60 m from the elevator and the mirror at 10 m from the student. Re-read: “60 m from a grain elevator and places a mirror at 10 m from herself.” So d₁ = 60 m (elevator to mirror), d₂ = 10 m (mirror to student). h/60 = 1.6/10 → h = 9.6 m.

SAY (corrected): - “d₁ = 60 m (elevator to mirror). d₂ = 10 m (mirror to student). Eye height = 1.6 m.” - “h / 60 = 1.6 / 10.” - “h = 60 × 0.16 = 9.6 m.” - “A grain elevator at 9.6 m — that’s about 3 storeys. Realistic.”

FLAG — Alberta ContextAlberta grain elevators are a genuine context — they’re visible across the prairies and students from rural Alberta will know them. If teaching in an urban school, substitute a flagpole visible from the schoolyard. The mirror method works with any reflective flat surface.

CUE: ★ Write: “h / 60 = 1.6 / 10 → h = [9.6] m.”

After the example: SAY: “Before moving on — explain what role the law of reflection plays in making the triangles similar. Which angles are equal and why?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer: angle of incidence = angle of reflection at the mirror. This means the angle at the mirror is the same in both triangles. Both triangles also have a right angle (the vertical object/observer). AA applies.”

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Scale diagrams and both indirect measurement methods.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. Scale 1 : 200. Wall 40 cm on diagram. Real length = 40 × 200 = 8000 cm = 80 m. 2. Model car at scale 1 : 20. Real car = 4.6 m = 460 cm. Model length = 460 / 20 = 23 cm. 3. Tree shadow 12 m. Pole 2 m, shadow 1.5 m. Proportion: h/12 = 2/1.5. h = 12 × (2/1.5) = 12 × 4/3 = 16 m. The tree is 16 m tall. 4. The triangles in the shadow method are similar because both have a right angle (vertical objects on flat ground) AND the same sun angle (measured at the same time) — two equal angles, so AA applies. 5. Unit Synthesis

~20 minutes — notes closed, then return to Pre-Knowledge Check

Beat 1 — Set up SHOW: Board: The four section headers — blank lines beside each. SAY: - “Notes closed. Everything closed.” - “Four sections. I want one sentence from each — the single most important idea — from memory.” - “This is not a test. This is you finding out what stuck.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Four sections. One sentence each. Go.”

Allow 5–7 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: students who nail Section 1 (proportion mechanics) but can’t produce the two conditions for similarity from Section 2; students who confuse similar and congruent; students who write “AA means angles are equal” without being able to state why two angles are sufficient.

Beat 2 — Unit Synthesis Part A: key ideas SHOW: Board: Part A questions (from synthesis page). SAY: - “Part A — still no notes.” - “Work through the four questions. Show everything — don’t just write an answer.” - “Question 3 asks about perimeter ratio AND area ratio. Think carefully about what happens when you scale a shape.” CUE: ★ Write: “Work through Part A now. Notes closed.”

Expected answers for your reference: - Q1: two conditions — all corresponding angles equal AND all corresponding sides proportional. - Q2: AA, SSS, SAS. AA requires least information — just two angles. - Q3: Perimeter ratio = 3. Area ratio = 9 (= 3²). - Q4: 4 cm × 100,000 = 400,000 cm = 4 km.

Beat 3 — Unit Synthesis Part B: practice from memory SHOW: Board: Triangle ABC ~ Triangle DEF. AB = 10, BC = 15, angle A = 40°, angle C = 65°. DE = 6. SAY: - “Four questions. Work them in order.” - “Use what you know from the similarity statement — the letter order tells you everything.” CUE: ★ Write: “Work through Part B. Notes closed.”

Expected answers: - (a) Scale factor = DE/AB = 6/10 = 3/5. - (b) EF = BC × (3/5) = 15 × 3/5 = 9. - (c) Angle E = angle B = 180 − 40 − 65 = 75°. - (d) Angle F = angle C = 65°.

Beat 4 — Return to Pre-Knowledge Check SHOW: Board: “Turn to page 1. Read what you wrote before the unit started.” SAY: - “Read it. Don’t change it. Don’t erase anything.” - “What did you get right? What were you wrong about? What surprised you?” - “Specifically — look at what you wrote about similar vs. congruent.” - “Write your answers at the bottom of the synthesis page.” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the three end-of-unit questions: right / wrong / surprised.”

Allow 3–5 minutes. This is the most important metacognitive moment in the package. Students seeing their own pre-instruction words in their own handwriting — especially their original definition of ‘similar’ — is more powerful than any correction you can deliver. The most common surprise: students who wrote “similar = same size and shape” now know that was the definition of congruent, not similar. Let it land quietly.

Key Flags Summary

Quick reference for lesson planning — every misconception that needs direct address.

Section Misconception Where it appears
S1 Scale factor > 1 means reduction (direction confusion) Beat 5 — drill “new over original”
S1 Cross-multiplication treated as a trick without understanding why Beat 4 — self-explanation prompt
S2 “Similar means identical” — similar = same shape, different size Beat 2 — dedicated beat before anything else; also Pre-K check
S2 “Congruent and similar are the same thing” — congruent requires equal size AND shape Beat 2 — side-by-side examples
S2 Proportion written in opposite direction (AB/DE instead of DE/AB) inconsistently Beat 5 — “image on top” drill
S2 Setting up ratios inconsistently within the same proportion (small/large for one ratio, large/small for another) Beat 5 — flag and Beat 6 — check all four ratios
S3 SAS applied with a non-included angle (SSA — not a valid criterion) Beat 7 and criterion table
S3 Similarity statement written with letters out of correspondence order Beat 4 — self-explanation prompt
S4 Shadow method proportion set up as height ratio = shadow ratio rather than height/shadow = height/shadow Beat 5 — explicit structure check
S4 Both shadows not measured at the same time (invalidates AA) Beat 6 — self-explanation prompt
S4 Scale factor applied in the wrong direction (multiplying instead of dividing when going from real to diagram) Beat 4 — two-direction worked example
