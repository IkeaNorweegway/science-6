# Math 9 — Powers and Exponents
## Teacher Notes Package
*Teaching reference — not a script*

---

## How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

| Label | What it contains |
|---|---|
| **SHOW** | What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat. |
| **SAY** | Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in **[brackets]**. |
| **CUE** | The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩ Check |

Flags appear in callout blocks:
> **FLAG — Misconception:** Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address.
> **FLAG — Pacing:** Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum.
> **FLAG — Derivation:** A moment where the rule must be derived from first principles — do not skip to the rule.

**The screen and the voice carry different things.** What is SHOW stays visual — diagrams, pattern tables, worked steps. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

**Exponent laws rule:** Every law in this unit is derived by expansion before the rule is stated. Do not state the rule first and show examples second. Show the pattern, let students see it, then formalize the rule. This is not optional — students who receive the rule without the derivation memorize it incorrectly at a rate far higher than students who see where it comes from.

## Section 1 — What Is a Power?
*Learning target: I can write a power, identify its base and exponent, evaluate a power with any integer base, and translate between symbolic, numerical, and verbal representations.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Five vocabulary terms in a column — Power / Base / Exponent / Evaluate / Integral base
**SAY:**
- "Five terms on your vocab table. Don't fill them in yet."
- "We will build each one as we go through the section."
- "By the end of Section 1 you will have your own definition and at least one example for each term."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Look at the five terms. Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head — we'll see if you're right."

---

**Beat 2 — What is a power?**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the power diagram:
```
        exponent
            ↓
          [ 3⁴ ]
            ↑
          base
```
**SAY:**
- "A power is shorthand for repeated multiplication of the same factor."
- "3⁴ means: multiply 3 by itself 4 times. That's it."
- "3⁴ = 3 × 3 × 3 × 3"
- "The base is the number being repeated. The exponent tells you how many times."
- Pause 3 seconds.
**CUE:** ★ Write: "A power is shorthand for **[repeated multiplication]** of the same factor. The **[base]** is the factor being repeated. The **[exponent]** tells you how many times."

---

**Beat 3 — The four representations table**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the four-column table with headers: Verbal / Symbolic / Expanded / Value. Fill in the first row live: "3 multiplied by itself 4 times" / 3⁴ / 3×3×3×3 / [blank].
**SAY:**
- "Four ways to see the same idea. Let's build the table together."
- Point to row 1: "I read it out loud: 3 multiplied by itself 4 times. I write it as 3⁴. I expand it: four 3s multiplied. What's the value?"
- Wait for student responses: **[81]**
- Move to row 2: "5 multiplied by itself 2 times. Say the symbolic form."
- Students: **[5²]**. Write it. "Expand it: 5 × 5. Value?"
- Students: **[25]**. Write it.
- Row 3: "Base is 2, exponent is 6. What is the verbal form?" — **["2 multiplied by itself 6 times"]**. Expanded: **[2×2×2×2×2×2]**. Value: **[64]**.
- Row 4: "Expanded form is 4 × 4 × 4. Everything else?" — Symbolic: **[4³]**. Value: **[64]**. Verbal: **["4 multiplied by itself 3 times"]**.
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete your four-representations table as we build it."

> **FLAG — Misconception: 2³ = 6 (multiply base × exponent)**
> Pre-empt this before students touch their pencils. SAY: "Here is the single most common error in this entire unit — and we're going to kill it right now, before it takes root." Write on the board: 2³ = 6 ✗. Then write: 2³ = 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 ✓. "The exponent tells you how many 2s to multiply together. Three 2s. Not 2 times 3. This mistake costs students marks on every test in every grade from here on out. Expand it every single time until the correct meaning is automatic."

---

**Beat 4 — Identifying base and exponent**
**SHOW:** Board: Table with four powers: 7³ / (−2)⁵ / 10² / (−1)⁸
**SAY:**
- "Identify the base and exponent in each power."
- "Most of these are straightforward. Watch the ones with brackets."
- Point to (−2)⁵: "What is the base here? The whole bracket: negative 2. The exponent is 5."
- "The brackets mean the negative sign is part of the base."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the base/exponent table for all four powers."

*Answers: 7³ → base 7, exp 3. (−2)⁵ → base −2, exp 5. 10² → base 10, exp 2. (−1)⁸ → base −1, exp 8.*

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 1: Positive base**
**SHOW:** Board: Evaluate 2⁵.
```
2⁵ = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = 32
```
**SAY:**
- "Evaluate means calculate the value."
- "2⁵. Five twos multiplied together."
- "Step by step: 2×2 = 4. 4×2 = 8. 8×2 = 16. 16×2 = 32."
- "Answer: 32. Not 10."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Evaluate 2⁵: 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 = **[32]**"

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why do we write five 2s, and not 2 fives?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the Explain Step 1 question in your notes now."

*Allow 90 seconds. Listen for: "because the exponent is 5 and the base is 2, so we use 2 as a factor 5 times." Students who say "because it's repeated multiplication" are correct but vague — push for specificity.*

---

**Beat 6 — Worked Example 2: Negative base**
**SHOW:** Board: Evaluate (−3)⁴. Build the expansion step by step — write each step as you say it.
```
(−3)⁴ = (−3) × (−3) × (−3) × (−3)
Step 1: (−3) × (−3) = +9   [neg × neg = pos]
Step 2: +9 × (−3) = −27    [pos × neg = neg]
Step 3: −27 × (−3) = +81   [neg × neg = pos]
```
**SAY:**
- "Negative base. Same rule — expand it. Four (−3)s multiplied."
- "Work left to right, two factors at a time."
- "Negative times negative is positive. Positive times negative is negative. Keep the sign rules active throughout."
- "Result: +81. Positive. Four factors, even exponent."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in all the blanks in Worked Example 2 as we go through it."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: without calculating anything, predict whether (−3)⁵ will be positive or negative and explain why."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the Explain Step 2 question now."

*Expected answer: negative, because there are five (−3)s and an odd number of negative factors gives a negative product. Students who say "because 5 is odd" need the follow-up: "Explain what being odd has to do with the sign."*

---

**Beat 7 — The brackets misconception: −3⁴ vs (−3)⁴**
**SHOW:** Board: Two expressions side by side:
```
−3⁴ = −(3⁴) = −81
(−3)⁴ = (−3)(−3)(−3)(−3) = +81
```
**SAY:**
- "These look almost identical. They are not the same."
- "In (−3)⁴: the base is −3. The brackets include the negative sign inside the base."
- "In −3⁴: the base is 3 only. Order of operations says the exponent applies first, then the negative sign is applied. So we calculate 3⁴ = 81, then apply the negative: −81."
- "Think of it this way: −3⁴ = −1 × 3⁴."
- Pause 4 seconds.
- "This is a bracket problem, not an exponent problem."

> **FLAG — Misconception: the exponent applies to everything in front**
> This is one of the two places in the unit students are most likely to lose easy marks. Write both expressions on the board and keep them visible for the remainder of Section 1. When students are working on the sign table, point back to them. Verbalize the rule multiple times: "In (−3)⁴, the brackets make the negative part of the base. Without brackets, the negative is OUTSIDE the power."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the misconception box: −2³ = **[−8]** and (−2)³ = **[−8]**" — then SAY: "Same in this case! Why? Because when the base is negative and the exponent is odd, both expressions give a negative. Try −2⁴ vs (−2)⁴ to see them diverge: **[−16]** vs **[+16]**."

---

**Beat 8 — The sign pattern table**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the sign pattern table for (−2)¹ through (−2)⁶. Fill in the given rows, then complete live.
**SAY:**
- "Complete this table. Look for the sign pattern — I want you to see it, not be told it."
- "What determines whether the result is positive or negative?"
- Pause while students work. Then: "The pattern?"
- Expected: positive when exponent is even, negative when exponent is odd.
- "Why? Count the negative signs being multiplied. Even count of negatives: always positive. Odd count: always negative."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the sign pattern table and write the rule in your own words."

*Table answers: (−2)²: 4, positive. (−2)³: −8, negative. (−2)⁴: 16, positive. (−2)⁵: −32, negative. (−2)⁶: 64, positive.*

---

**Beat 9 — Vocabulary table fill-in**
**SHOW:** Board: The five terms still visible.
**SAY:**
- "Now go back to your vocabulary table."
- "Fill in each definition in your own words — not a copied sentence."
- "Your definition should be something you could explain to a Grade 7 student."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete your vocab table — own words, own examples. You have 3 minutes."

*Circulate. Watch for: students leaving "integral base" blank (prompt: what does integral mean — the notes tell you); students defining "power" and "exponent" as the same thing (redirect: one is the whole expression, one is just the raised number).*

---

### Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed. Answer from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish (4–5 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Find each answer. Correct anything wrong or incomplete in a different colour."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. The exponent tells you how many times to use the base as a factor in a repeated multiplication. (Not: "how many times to multiply" vaguely — the answer needs to connect exponent to base and to count-of-factors.)
2. (−4)³ = (−4)(−4)(−4). Step 1: (−4)(−4) = +16. Step 2: +16 × (−4) = −64. Answer: −64.
3. Incorrect. −5² means −(5²) = −(25) = −25. The base is 5, not −5. To get 25, you need (−5)² with brackets. (Students who write "correct because negative times negative is positive" — the error is they've changed the expression; −5² never means (−5)².)
4. (−1)¹⁰⁰ = +1. The exponent 100 is even, so there are an even number of (−1) factors. Even count of negatives multiplied = positive. No calculation needed.
5. Stretch: any power of the form (−n)² where n = 1, 2, or 3 works — e.g., (−1)² = 1, (−2)² = 4, (−3)² = 9. Students should explain: even exponent means positive result; small base means small value. Accept any valid example with correct reasoning.

## Section 2 — The Zero Exponent
*Learning target: I can explain why any nonzero base raised to the power of zero equals 1, using a pattern — not just as a memorized rule.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Setup: discover, don't memorize**
**SHOW:** Board: *"3⁰ = ?"* — just this, nothing more.
**SAY:**
- "What do you think 3⁰ equals? Write your instinct."
- Wait 10–15 seconds.
- "Common answers: zero, one, three. We're going to find out which one — and you're going to see why, not just learn what."
- "We will derive this from a pattern. The pattern is something you already know."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "What is your best guess for 3⁰? Hold it."

> **FLAG — Misconception: 3⁰ = 0**
> This is one of the two highest-risk moments in the unit. The instinct "anything times zero is zero" transfers incorrectly to "anything to the power of zero is zero." Students confuse multiplying BY zero with raising to the power OF zero. Do not state the answer yet. Let the pattern derive it. Students who see the pattern arrive at a⁰ = 1 with understanding rather than a memorized exception.

---

**Beat 2 — The division pattern table: base 3**
**SHOW:** Board: Build this table column by column. Write the "Divide by 3" column first, then fill in the values from the top down. Leave the 3⁰ row blank — derive it live.

```
Power     | Value  | Pattern
3⁵        | 243    | ÷ 3 ↓
3⁴        | 81     | ÷ 3 ↓
3³        | 27     | ÷ 3 ↓
3²        | 9      | ÷ 3 ↓
3¹        | 3      | ÷ 3 ↓
3⁰        | ?      | 
```
**SAY:**
- "Each time the exponent decreases by 1, what happens to the value?"
- Wait: "Divide by 3. Always. That's the pattern."
- "Start at 243. Divide by 3: 81. Divide by 3: 27. Divide by 3: 9. Divide by 3: 3."
- "Now what? 3¹ ÷ 3 = ?"
- Students: 1. Write 1 in the 3⁰ row.
- "3⁰ = 1. Not because I say so — because the pattern demands it."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the base-3 pattern table in your notes."

*Table answers: 3³ = 27, 3² = 9, 3¹ = 3, 3⁰ = 1.*

---

**Beat 3 — Confirm with base 5**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the base-5 table:
```
5³ = 125  ÷ 5 ↓
5² = 25   ÷ 5 ↓
5¹ = 5    ÷ 5 ↓
5⁰ = ?
```
**SAY:**
- "Same pattern, different base. 5³ ÷ 5 = 5². 5² ÷ 5 = 5¹. 5¹ ÷ 5 = ?"
- Wait: "1. 5⁰ = 1."
- "The pattern works for any nonzero base."
- Write: **a⁰ = 1 for any a ≠ 0**
**CUE:** ★ Write: "5⁰ = **[1]**. The rule: a⁰ = **[1]** for any a ≠ 0."

---

**Beat 4 — Explain the logic in own words**
**SHOW:** Board: *"Why is a⁰ = 1? Explain the pattern — not the rule."*
**SAY:**
- "I want the reasoning, not just the answer."
- "Don't write 'because the rule says so.' Write: what happens each time the exponent decreases by one, and what that means when the exponent reaches zero."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the explanation prompt now — 2–3 sentences. Do not copy the notes text."

*Allow 2 minutes. Good answer: "Each time the exponent goes down by one, you divide by the base. Starting from base¹ = base, dividing by the base gives base/base = 1. So base⁰ must equal 1 to keep the pattern consistent." Students who write "because there are no factors" — this is wrong; redirect to the division pattern logic.*

---

**Beat 5 — The exception: 0⁰**
**SHOW:** Board: *0⁰ — undefined*
**SAY:**
- "One exception: 0⁰."
- "If we try to apply the division pattern: 0¹ ÷ 0. What's 0 ÷ 0?"
- Pause. "Division by zero is undefined. The pattern breaks."
- "For this course: 0⁰ is undefined. The rule a⁰ = 1 only applies when a ≠ 0."
- "This is the only case where the rule fails — and it fails for a good reason."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "The rule a⁰ = 1 requires a **[≠ 0]**. When a = 0, we say 0⁰ is **[undefined]**."

---

### Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Three questions — the values, and the explanation."

**Answer guide:**
1. 7⁰ = 1. (If a student writes 0, ask them to rebuild the pattern table for base 7 from scratch.)
2. (−15)⁰ = 1. (The base is −15, which is nonzero, so the rule applies.)
3. Correct answer must reference the pattern: each time the exponent decreases by 1, you divide by the base. Starting from 3¹ = 3 and dividing by 3 gives 1. An exponent of 0 does not mean "multiply by nothing" — it means "one step below the exponent of 1 in the division pattern." The student's error conflates zero as a multiplier with zero as an exponent.
4. Stretch: 3⁰ + 4⁰ = 1 + 1 = 2. Then 2² = 4. So (3⁰ + 4⁰)² = 4 — equal to 4. Students who skip the inner evaluation and write (3 + 4)² = 49 have made the classic order-of-operations error — address this if it appears.

## Section 3 — Product of Powers
*Learning target: I can apply the product of powers rule to simplify expressions, explain why the rule works using expanded form, and recognize when it does and does not apply.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Two terms — Product of powers rule / Like bases
**SAY:**
- "Two terms. Hold off on filling them in."
- "By the end of this section you'll have built the rule yourself."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "What do you think 'like bases' means? Hold it."

---

**Beat 2 — Derivation: expand and count**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the discovery table. Write the first row completely, then build rows 2–4 live.

```
Expression  | Expanded                            | Total factors | Simplified
2³ × 2⁴     | (2×2×2) × (2×2×2×2)               | 7 twos        | 2⁷
3² × 3⁵     | (3×3) × (3×3×3×3×3)               | ? threes      | ?
5⁴ × 5¹     | (5×5×5×5) × (5)                   | ?             | ?
a³ × a⁶     | (a×a×a) × (a×a×a×a×a×a)           | ?             | ?
```
**SAY:**
- "Row 1 is done. What are we doing? Expanding each power, then counting how many total factors of the base we have."
- "2³ gives us three 2s. 2⁴ gives us four 2s. Together: seven 2s. That's 2⁷."
- "Row 2: what's 3² × 3⁵? Expand both. Count total threes." Wait. "Seven threes. 3⁷."
- "Row 3: 5⁴ × 5¹. Five fives total. 5⁵."
- "Row 4: a³ × a⁶. Nine as. a⁹."
- "Look at the exponents. 3 and 4 → 7. 2 and 5 → 7. 4 and 1 → 5. 3 and 6 → 9."
- "What operation connects each pair to the result?"
- Wait: "Adding."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the discovery table as we build it."

> **FLAG — Derivation: do not state the rule before the table is complete**
> Build the entire table before writing the rule. Students who hear "add the exponents" at the beginning will fill in the table mechanically without understanding why. The cognitive payoff is seeing the addition fall out of the expansion — not being told to add.

---

**Beat 3 — Write the rule**
**SHOW:** Board: Write the rule after the table is complete.
```
aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ   (same base only)
```
**SAY:**
- "When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents."
- "Write the rule in words first — your words — then the symbolic form."
- "The reason the rule works: you are counting total factors of the base. Counting means adding."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Write the rule in words: 'When multiplying powers with the same base, **[add the exponents]**.' Rule in symbols: aᵐ × aⁿ = **[aᵐ⁺ⁿ]**."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example: applying the rule**
**SHOW:** Board: Simplify (−4)³ × (−4)⁵. Build the steps.
```
Step 1: Check bases. Both (−4). Same base? Yes.
Step 2: Add exponents: (−4)³ × (−4)⁵ = (−4)^(3+5) = (−4)⁸
Step 3: Sign of (−4)⁸? Positive — exponent 8 is even.
```
**SAY:**
- "Step 1 always: are the bases the same? If not, stop — the rule doesn't apply."
- "Step 2: add the exponents. 3 + 5 = 8."
- "Step 3: if the question asks for a value, evaluate. If not, leave it in power form."
- "For the sign: even exponent, positive result. We know this from Section 1."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in all blanks in the worked example."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: why do we add the exponents instead of multiplying them?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the Explain Step 2 question now."

*Expected answer: because we are counting how many total factors of the base there are — three (−4)s plus five (−4)s equals eight (−4)s. Addition is how you count combined groups. Students who write "because the rule says add" are not explaining — push back: "Why does the rule say add? What does the expansion show?"*

---

**Beat 5 — Multiple terms**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
x⁴ × x² × x³ = x^(4+2+3) = x⁹
```
**SAY:**
- "The rule extends to three or more powers with the same base."
- "Add all the exponents: 4 + 2 + 3 = 9."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the multiple-term example."

---

**Beat 6 — When the rule does NOT apply**
**SHOW:** Board: The five-expression table from the student notes. Work through it live.

```
5⁴ × 5³     → ✓  (same base: 5)
3² × 4²     → ✗  (different bases: 3 and 4)
m⁵ × m      → ✓  (m = m¹ — the exponent 1 is invisible)
2x³ × 3x²  → ✓ for the x terms, multiply coefficients separately: 6x⁵
a⁴b² × a³b → ✓ for each base separately: a⁷b³
```
**SAY:**
- "Two key things here."
- Point to m⁵ × m: "A variable with no visible exponent has an exponent of 1. m = m¹. So m⁵ × m¹ = m⁶."
- Point to 2x³ × 3x²: "Coefficients multiply normally. Exponents on the same variable add. These are separate operations."
- "For a⁴b² × a³b: treat each base separately. a⁴ × a³ = a⁷. b² × b¹ = b³. Result: a⁷b³."

> **FLAG — Misconception: product rule applies to different bases**
> The most common wrong answer: 2³ × 3⁴ = 6⁷. Pre-empt it explicitly. SAY: "Here is the error I see every year. 2³ × 3⁴. Different bases. Can I combine them into 6⁷?" Write 6⁷ and a large ✗. "Expand it: 8 × 81 = 648. Now what's 6⁷? Over 200,000. Not the same number. Not even close." The product rule applies only when the base is identical — not when it looks convenient to combine.

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the apply/don't-apply table."

---

### Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. State the rule, apply it, and spot the limit condition."

**Answer guide:**
1. When multiplying powers with the same base, add the exponents. (Must include: same base is required.)
2. a⁵ × a⁴ = a⁹.
3. (−2)³ × (−2)⁶ = (−2)⁹. Sign: negative (odd exponent).
4. Error: the student multiplied the exponents (3 × 5 = 15) instead of adding them (3 + 5 = 8). Corrected: 4³ × 4⁵ = 4⁸. The product rule applies to multiplying two powers — addition of exponents, not multiplication.
5. Stretch: 3² × 3⁰ × 3³ = 3^(2+0+3) = 3⁵ = 243. The zero exponent term contributes 0 to the sum of exponents — it multiplies by 1, not by 0. The product is unchanged in structure because 3⁰ = 1 and anything times 1 is itself. Students should connect: 3⁰ "adds nothing" to the exponent because it contributes 0 to the sum.

## Section 4 — Quotient of Powers
*Learning target: I can apply the quotient of powers rule to simplify expressions and explain why the rule works using expanded form and cancellation.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Derivation: expand and cancel**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the discovery table. First row done, then build live.

```
Expression      | Expanded                     | After cancelling    | Result
3⁵ ÷ 3²        | (3×3×3×3×3) ÷ (3×3)         | Three 3s remain     | 3³
4⁶ ÷ 4²        | (4×4×4×4×4×4) ÷ (4×4)       | ?                   | ?
a⁷ ÷ a³        | (a×a×a×a×a×a×a) ÷ (a×a×a)   | ?                   | ?
(−2)⁸ ÷ (−2)⁵  | ...                          | ?                   | ?
```
**SAY:**
- "Division means cancellation. Every factor in the denominator cancels one factor in the numerator."
- "Row 1: we start with five 3s on top and two 3s on the bottom. Cancel two: three 3s remain. That's 3³."
- "Row 2: six 4s on top, two 4s below. Cancel two: four 4s remain. 4⁴."
- "Row 3: seven as on top, three as below. Cancel three: four as remain. a⁴."
- "Look at the exponents. 5 and 2 → 3. 6 and 2 → 4. 7 and 3 → 4. 8 and 5 → 3."
- "What operation?"
- Wait: "Subtracting."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the discovery table as we build it."

---

**Beat 2 — Write the rule**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ   (same base, a ≠ 0, m > n for now)
```
**SAY:**
- "When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the exponents."
- "Denominator exponent subtracts from numerator exponent."
- "The condition a ≠ 0: we cannot divide by zero. The condition m > n: if the exponent on top is smaller, we get a negative exponent — that's Section 7."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Rule: aᵐ ÷ aⁿ = **[aᵐ⁻ⁿ]** (a ≠ 0)."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
x⁹ ÷ x⁴ = x^(9−4) = x⁵
```
**SAY:**
- "Same base: x. Subtract: 9 − 4 = 5. Result: x⁵."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the worked example: x⁹ ÷ x⁴ = x^(**[9]** − **[4]**) = x^**[5]**."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain it using the cancellation model: we started with 9 factors of x and cancelled 4 of them. How many are left and why?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the cancellation explanation now."

---

**Beat 4 — Connection to Section 2: equal exponents**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
a⁵ ÷ a⁵ = a^(5−5) = a⁰
Any number ÷ itself = 1
Therefore: a⁰ = 1
```
**SAY:**
- "What if the exponents are equal? Subtract: 5 − 5 = 0. So we get a⁰."
- "But we also know: any nonzero number divided by itself equals 1."
- "These two facts must agree. Therefore a⁰ = 1."
- "The zero exponent is not a separate, arbitrary rule — it falls out of the quotient rule."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "a⁵ ÷ a⁵ = a^(**[5−5]**) = a^**[0]**. Any number ÷ itself = **[1]**. So a⁰ = **[1]**."

---

**Beat 5 — Preview: negative exponents**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
3² ÷ 3⁵ = 3^(2−5) = 3⁻³
Expand and cancel:
(3×3) ÷ (3×3×3×3×3) = 1/(3×3×3) = 1/3³
So: 3⁻³ = 1/3³
```
**SAY:**
- "What if the exponent on top is smaller? The rule still works: 2 − 5 = −3."
- "Expand and cancel: two 3s on top, five on bottom. Cancel two: three 3s remain in the denominator."
- "That gives 1/3³. So a negative exponent means the power goes to the denominator."
- "We'll develop this fully in Section 7. For now: negative exponent = moves to the denominator."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "3⁻³ = **[1/3³]**. A negative exponent means the power moves to the **[denominator]** of a fraction."

### Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. State the rule, apply it, and explain the connection to zero exponents."

**Answer guide:**
1. When dividing powers with the same base, subtract the denominator exponent from the numerator exponent. (Must include: same base is required.)
2. m⁸ ÷ m³ = m⁵.
3. (−5)⁶ ÷ (−5)² = (−5)⁴. Sign: positive (even exponent).
4. First student (a⁴ ÷ a² = a²) is correct — subtract: 4 − 2 = 2. Second student made the product rule error: they added instead of subtracted, or they confused division with multiplication. The error: applying addition instead of subtraction, or confusing ÷ with ×.
5. Stretch: chain of reasoning — aⁿ ÷ aⁿ = a^(n−n) = a⁰ by the quotient rule. But any nonzero quantity divided by itself equals 1. Since both expressions represent the same calculation, a⁰ = 1. The zero exponent is not a memorized exception; it is a necessary consequence of the quotient rule maintaining consistency.

## Section 5 — Power of a Power
*Learning target: I can apply the power of a power rule and explain it using expanded form.*
*Timing: ~15 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Derivation: expand the outer exponent first**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the discovery table.

```
Expression | Expanded (outer exp first)     | Fully expanded         | Simplified
(2³)²      | 2³ × 2³                        | (2×2×2)(2×2×2)         | 2⁶
(3²)⁴      | 3² × 3² × 3² × 3²              | ?                      | ?
(a⁴)³      | a⁴ × a⁴ × a⁴                   | ?                      | ?
(5²)⁵      | 5² × 5² × 5² × 5² × 5²         | ?                      | ?
```
**SAY:**
- "Power of a power: an exponent on the outside of brackets that already contain a power."
- "Expand the outer exponent first: (2³)² means 2³ written twice. That gives 2³ × 2³."
- "Now apply the product rule: 2³ × 2³ = 2^(3+3) = 2⁶."
- "Row 2: (3²)⁴ means 3² written four times. Product rule: 2+2+2+2 = 8. Result: 3⁸."
- "Row 3: (a⁴)³. a⁴ written three times. Product rule: 4+4+4 = 12. Result: a¹²."
- "Row 4: (5²)⁵. 5² written five times. 2+2+2+2+2 = 10. Result: 5¹⁰."
- "Look at the original exponents and the results. 3 and 2 → 6. 2 and 4 → 8. 4 and 3 → 12. 2 and 5 → 10. What operation?"
- Wait: "Multiplying."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the discovery table as we build it."

> **FLAG — Derivation: always go through expanded form**
> The temptation is to just state "multiply the exponents" immediately — resist this. The derivation through expansion connects power of a power back to product of powers. Students who see this connection own both rules more deeply than students who learn them as two separate facts.

---

**Beat 2 — Write the rule**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
(aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ
```
**SAY:**
- "When raising a power to another power, multiply the exponents."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "(aᵐ)ⁿ = **[aᵐⁿ]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
Simplify: ((−2)³)⁴
Step 1: Multiply exponents: (−2)^(3×4) = (−2)¹²
Step 2: Sign? Exponent 12 is even → positive.
```
**SAY:**
- "Apply power of a power: multiply 3 × 4 = 12."
- "Check the sign: even exponent → positive result."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the worked example blanks."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: where does the number 12 come from, and why do we multiply the exponents here but added them in Section 3?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the Explain Step 1 question now."

*This is the most important self-explanation in the unit. Expected answer: "In Section 3 we were combining two separate groups of the same base, so we added. Here we are writing the same power repeatedly — (−2)³ written four times — which creates repeated addition of the same exponent: 3+3+3+3 = 3×4. Repeated addition is multiplication." Students who just write "because the rule says multiply" need the follow-up: "Explain where the multiplication comes from using the expanded form."*

---

**Beat 4 — Product rule vs. Power of a Power: distinguish explicitly**
**SHOW:** Board: Side by side:
```
2³ × 2⁴ = 2⁷    (add — product rule)
(2³)⁴  = 2¹²   (multiply — power of a power)
```

> **FLAG — Misconception: confusing product rule and power of a power rule**
> Students who learn rules without derivation will mix these up under pressure. The distinguishing question is always: "What is connecting the two powers?" If the connection is × (multiplication between two separate powers), it's the product rule — add. If the inner power is inside brackets with an outer exponent, it's power of a power — multiply. Write this on the board as a decision rule and leave it visible for the confusion table.

**SAY:**
- "The operation that connects the two powers tells you what to do with the exponents."
- "Multiplying two separate powers with the same base: add the exponents."
- "Raising a power to a power (outer exponent): multiply the exponents."
- "If you are ever unsure: go back to expansion. Count the factors. You will always get the right answer from expansion."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the confusion table — identify which rule applies to each expression."

*Confusion table answers: (x²)³ → power of a power (multiply) → x⁶. x² × x³ → product rule (add) → x⁵. (y⁵)² → power of a power (multiply) → y¹⁰. y⁵ × y² → product rule (add) → y⁷.*

---

### Section 5 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. State the rule and distinguish it from the product rule."

**Answer guide:**
1. (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ. (Both exponents required in the symbolic statement — not just "multiply.")
2. (a³)⁵ = a¹⁵.
3. ((−3)²)³ = (−3)⁶. Sign: positive (even exponent). Value if asked: 729.
4. (2³)⁴ = 2¹² and (2⁴)³ = 2¹² — they are equal. Both give 2¹² because 3 × 4 = 4 × 3. Multiplication is commutative. Students who guess one is larger without calculating should be asked to check.
5. Stretch: (x³)⁴ × (x²)⁵ = x¹² × x¹⁰ = x²². Step 1: apply power of a power to each. Step 2: apply product rule to combine. Students may attempt to combine in one step — accept if correct, but ask them to show that the two-step process gives the same answer.

## Section 6 — Power of a Product and Power of a Quotient
*Learning target: I can distribute an exponent across a product or quotient and explain why this works, but does not work for sums.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Power of a product: derivation**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the discovery table.

```
Expression  | Expanded              | Regrouped                    | Result
(2 × 3)²   | (2×3)(2×3)            | (2×2)(3×3)                  | 2² × 3² = 36
(ab)³       | (ab)(ab)(ab)          | (a×a×a)(b×b×b)              | a³ × b³
(2x)⁴      | (2x)(2x)(2x)(2x)      | (2×2×2×2)(x×x×x×x)         | 16x⁴
(3mn)²      | (3mn)(3mn)            | (3×3)(m×m)(n×n)             | 9m²n²
```
**SAY:**
- "When a product is raised to a power, the exponent distributes to every factor."
- "Row 1: (2×3)² = (6)² = 36. Check: 2² × 3² = 4 × 9 = 36. Same answer."
- "Why? Because expanding shows us: both 2s multiply together, both 3s multiply together. The exponent applies to each factor."
- "Row 3: (2x)⁴. Critical — the exponent applies to the coefficient 2 as well. 2⁴ = 16. Not just x⁴."
- "The coefficient is a factor. The exponent applies to all factors."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the discovery table. Rule: (ab)ⁿ = **[aⁿbⁿ]**."

---

**Beat 2 — Power of a quotient**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
(a/b)³ = (a/b)(a/b)(a/b) = (a×a×a)/(b×b×b) = a³/b³
```
**SAY:**
- "Same logic for division. The exponent distributes to both numerator and denominator."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "(a/b)ⁿ = **[aⁿ/bⁿ]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example: combining rules**
**SHOW:** Board: Simplify (3x²)⁴. Build each step.
```
Step 1: Distribute the exponent: 3⁴ × (x²)⁴
Step 2: Evaluate 3⁴: 3⁴ = 81
Step 3: Apply power of a power: (x²)⁴ = x^(2×4) = x⁸
Result: 81x⁸
```
**SAY:**
- "Step 1: everything inside the brackets gets the exponent. 3 gets the exponent. x² gets the exponent."
- "Step 2: 3⁴ = 81. Don't skip this — many students forget to raise the coefficient to the power."
- "Step 3: (x²)⁴ = x⁸ — power of a power rule."
- "Answer: 81x⁸."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in all the blanks in the worked example."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why does the exponent apply to the 3 as well as the x²? Many students only apply it to the variable. Explain why the coefficient cannot be left out."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the Explain Step 1 question now."

*Expected answer: because 3 is a factor of the product (3x²) — the product inside the brackets is 3 multiplied by x². The exponent distributes to all factors of a product, not just the variable. The coefficient is a factor just like the variable is. Students who say "because the rule says so" need the expansion: (3x²)⁴ = (3x²)(3x²)(3x²)(3x²) — then regroup to see that four 3s multiply together.*

---

**Beat 4 — The critical misconception: sums**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
(a + b)² ≠ a² + b²
(a + b)² = a² + 2ab + b²   [expand to show]
```
Verify numerically: a = 3, b = 4.
```
(3 + 4)² = 7² = 49
3² + 4²  = 9 + 16 = 25
49 ≠ 25
```

> **FLAG — Misconception: (a + b)ⁿ = aⁿ + bⁿ**
> This is the most persistent algebraic error in all of high school mathematics — it recurs in Grades 10, 11, and 12. The counterexample with numbers is the most efficient fix. Do not just say "it doesn't work for sums" — make students compute both sides with numbers and see the inequality. The algebraic explanation (the missing 2ab middle term from FOIL) is worth stating once even though FOIL is technically Grade 10, because the concept of "a middle term appears from cross-multiplication" is accessible and memorable. Students who understand why there's a middle term remember the rule; students who are only told "it doesn't distribute" forget.

**SAY:**
- "Distributing an exponent over multiplication: valid. Over addition: invalid."
- "Let me show you with numbers. a = 3, b = 4."
- "Left side: (3 + 4)² = 49."
- "Right side: 9 + 16 = 25."
- "49 ≠ 25. The formula fails."
- "Why? When you expand (a + b)², you get cross terms: a² + 2ab + b². The 2ab term disappears if you incorrectly distribute."
- "Multiplication inside brackets: exponent distributes. Addition inside brackets: exponent does NOT distribute."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Verify: (3+4)² = **[49]**. 3² + 4² = **[25]**. Are they equal? **[No]** — this proves distribution over addition is wrong."

### Section 6 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Apply the rule, and identify where it doesn't work."

**Answer guide:**
1. (2a)³ = 8a³. (If student writes 2a³, they failed to apply the exponent to the coefficient — most common error.)
2. (x/3)² = x²/9.
3. (−5m²)³ = −125m⁶. Sign: negative (odd exponent on the base −5). (−5)³ = −125. (m²)³ = m⁶.

5. Stretch: (2x²y³)⁴ = 16x⁸y¹². (4x³y)² = 16x⁶y². Divide: (16x⁸y¹²) ÷ (16x⁶y²) = x²y¹⁰. Students who get a correct intermediate step but fail the final division should receive partial credit and have the quotient rule pointed back to.

## Section 7 — Negative Exponents
*Learning target: I can evaluate and simplify expressions with negative integer exponents and explain what a negative exponent means in terms of reciprocals.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

**Note to teacher: Section 7 is the second of two highest-risk moments in this unit (along with Section 2). The misconception "negative exponent = negative number" is deeply intuitive and will reappear on tests even after correction. Allow extra time. Use the pattern table derivation — do not state the rule first.*

---

**Beat 1 — Connect to Section 4 preview**
**SHOW:** Board: The Section 4 preview result:
```
3² ÷ 3⁵ = 3⁻³ = 1/3³
```
**SAY:**
- "At the end of Section 4 we previewed this. Now we develop it fully."
- "We know from the quotient rule: 3² ÷ 3⁵ = 3^(2−5) = 3⁻³."
- "We know from expansion and cancellation: 3² ÷ 3⁵ = 1/3³."
- "These must be equal: 3⁻³ = 1/3³."
- "That was not a coincidence. Let's see the full pattern."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Before we build the table — predict: what does 2⁻¹ equal?"

---

**Beat 2 — The division pattern table: negative exponents**
**SHOW:** Board: Build the pattern table, starting from 2⁴ and working down. Leave the negative exponent rows blank — complete them live.

```
Power | Value | Pattern
2⁴    | 16    | ÷ 2 ↓
2³    | 8     | ÷ 2 ↓
2²    | ?     | ÷ 2 ↓
2¹    | ?     | ÷ 2 ↓
2⁰    | ?     | ÷ 2 ↓
2⁻¹   | ?     | ÷ 2 ↓
2⁻²   | ?     | ÷ 2 ↓
2⁻³   | ?     | 
```
**SAY:**
- "Same pattern from Section 2. Divide by 2 every time the exponent decreases by 1."
- "Start at 16. ÷ 2 = 8. ÷ 2 = 4. ÷ 2 = 2. ÷ 2 = 1."
- "Now past zero: 1 ÷ 2 = 1/2. 1/2 ÷ 2 = 1/4. 1/4 ÷ 2 = 1/8."
- "2⁻¹ = 1/2. 2⁻² = 1/4. 2⁻³ = 1/8."
- "Write those as fractions with powers in the denominator: 1/2¹, 1/2², 1/2³."
- "Pattern: a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the pattern table as we build it."

*Table answers: 2² = 4. 2¹ = 2. 2⁰ = 1. 2⁻¹ = 1/2. 2⁻² = 1/4. 2⁻³ = 1/8.*

> **FLAG — Misconception: negative exponent means negative number**
> This is the most dangerous misconception in the entire unit. It is intuitive, plausible-sounding, and wrong. Address it explicitly and emphatically before writing the rule. SAY: "Here is the misconception you will be tempted to believe. 2⁻³ = −8." Write it on the board with a large ✗. "Wrong. Completely wrong. Look at the table: 2⁻³ = 1/8. One-eighth. A small positive fraction. The negative exponent is an instruction about WHERE the power lives — it moves to the denominator. It says nothing about the sign of the result."
>
> Clarify the one true exception: "Can a power with a negative exponent ever be negative? Yes — but only if the BASE is negative. (−2)⁻³ = 1/(−2)³ = 1/(−8) = −1/8. The negative comes from the base, not the exponent."

---

**Beat 3 — Write the rule**
**SHOW:** Board:
```
a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ   (a ≠ 0)
```
**SAY:**
- "A negative exponent means: take the reciprocal and make the exponent positive."
- "The negative exponent is a position signal, not a sign signal."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "a⁻ⁿ = **[1/aⁿ]**. In words: a negative exponent means take the **[reciprocal]** and make the exponent **[positive]**."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Examples**
**SHOW:** Board: Two examples — build each one step at a time.
```
Example 1: Evaluate 5⁻²
5⁻² = 1/5² = 1/25

Example 2: Evaluate (1/3)⁻²
(1/3)⁻² = (3/1)² = 9
```
**SAY:**
- "Example 1: move 5² to the denominator, make the exponent positive. 5² = 25. Answer: 1/25."
- "Example 2: interesting case. We have a fraction to a negative exponent. The reciprocal of 1/3 is 3/1 = 3. Squaring: 3² = 9."
- "A negative exponent on a fraction flips the fraction. Another way to remember: negative exponent = flip and make positive."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in both worked examples."

**Self-explanation beat:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain in your own words: why does a negative exponent not make the answer negative?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the explanation question now."

*Expected answer: because a negative exponent is an instruction about position in a fraction (move to the denominator, make exponent positive), not about the sign of the value. The sign of the result depends only on whether the base is positive or negative. 2⁻³ = 1/8, which is positive because the base 2 is positive. Students who write "because of the rule" are not explaining — push: "What does the negative exponent actually tell you to do, and why does that not produce a negative number?"*

**Beat 5 — Alberta context: scientific notation and large/small numbers**

## Key Flags Summary
*Quick reference for lesson planning — every misconception that needs direct address.*

| Section | Misconception | Where it appears |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | "2³ = 6" (multiply base × exponent) | Beat 3 — address before the four-representations table is filled in |
| S1 | The exponent applies to everything in front: −3⁴ = (−3)⁴ | Beat 7 — leave both expressions on the board for the remainder of Section 1 |
| S2 | "3⁰ = 0 because zero means nothing" | Beat 1 — do not state the answer; derive via the division pattern |
| S3 | Product rule applies to different bases: 2³ × 3⁴ = 6⁷ | Beat 6 — numerical verification (evaluate both and compare) |
| S3 | Product rule vs. power of a power: add or multiply? | Previewed in Beat 6; fully addressed in Section 5 Beat 4 |
| S4 | Subtracting vs. adding in the quotient rule | Beat 2 — stay with the cancellation model to make subtraction intuitive |
| S5 | Confusing product rule (add) and power of a power (multiply) | Beat 4 — side-by-side comparison; decision rule written on board |
| S6 | Forgetting to apply the exponent to the coefficient | Beat 3 — explicit in worked example; self-explanation beat addresses it |
| S6 | "(a + b)ⁿ = aⁿ + bⁿ" — distributing over addition | Beat 4 — numerical counterexample is mandatory; do not skip |
| S7 | "Negative exponent = negative number" | Beat 2 — write 2⁻³ = −8 ✗ on the board before writing the correct answer; Alberta context in Beat 5 reinforces |
| S7 | Sign confusion when base is negative: (−2)⁻³ | Beat 2 and Beat 3 — distinguish: the negative comes from the base, not the exponent |
