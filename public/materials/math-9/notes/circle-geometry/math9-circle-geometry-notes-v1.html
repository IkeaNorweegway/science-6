Misconception

A longer chord is farther from the centre

Wrong idea: "The longest chord should be farthest from the centre — bigger chords must extend farther."

Why it fails: It's the opposite. The chord closest to the centre is the longest. The diameter — the longest chord of all — passes through the centre, at distance 0. As a chord moves farther from the centre, it gets shorter.

Think of it: as you move a horizontal chord up toward the top of a circle, it gets shorter and shorter until it disappears at the edge. The closer to the middle, the longer.

The Inscribed Angle Theorem:

The inscribed angle is the central angle that subtends the same arc.

Inscribed angle = (1/2) × Central angle
OR: Central angle = × Inscribed angle

Angle AOB (central) = °
Angle APB (inscribed, same arc AB) = °

Worked Example 1:

A central angle AOB = 80°. Find the inscribed angle APB subtending the same arc.

Inscribed angle = Central angle /

APB = 80° / 2 = °

Worked Example 2:

An inscribed angle APB = 35°. Find the central angle AOB subtending the same arc.

Central angle = 2 × Inscribed angle

AOB = 2 × 35° = °

Explain: An inscribed angle is at the circumference, while a central angle is at the centre. Why does being at the centre make it "see" the arc at twice the angle? (Think about how far each vertex is from the arc.)

Corollary: Inscribed angles subtending the same arc are equal.

If angles APB and AQB are both inscribed in the same circle and subtend the same arc AB, then:

APB = AQB = (1/2) × AOB

Misconception

All inscribed angles in a circle equal 90°

Wrong idea: "Any angle drawn inside a circle is 90°."

Why it fails: Only inscribed angles that subtend a diameter (semicircle) equal 90°. That is a special case. An inscribed angle subtending an arc of 60° (central angle 60°) gives an inscribed angle of 30°. The angle depends on the arc.

The special case: when AB is a diameter, the central angle AOB = 180°. So the inscribed angle = 180°/2 = °. This is why angles in a semicircle are 90°.
