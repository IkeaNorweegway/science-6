## How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

| Label | What it contains |
|---|---|
| **SHOW** | What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat. |
| **SAY** | Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in **[brackets]**. |
| **CUE** | The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩ Check |

Flags appear in callout blocks:
> **FLAG — Misconception:** Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address.
> **FLAG — Pacing:** Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum.
> **FLAG — Alberta Context:** Genuine context problem — the setting matters to the math.

**The screen and the voice carry different things.** What is SHOW stays visual — labels, diagrams, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

You are looking for: (a) students who already know radius, diameter, and chord, (b) students who have no idea what an inscribed angle is (most will not — this is fine), (c) students who write "90°" for the tangent/radius angle question (this is correct — note who wrote it).

## Section 1 — Circle Vocabulary
*Learning target: I can identify and name all major parts of a circle and use precise geometric language when describing circle relationships.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary table setup**
**SHOW:** Board: The ten vocabulary terms in a column — Centre / Radius / Diameter / Chord / Arc / Central angle / Inscribed angle / Tangent / Point of tangency / Semicircle
**SAY:**
- "Ten terms. That's the vocabulary for this entire unit."
- "Don't fill them in yet. We'll build each one as we go."
- "By the end of this section you'll have your own definition and a drawing for each one."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Scan the ten terms. Which ones do you already know something about? Keep it in your head."

---

**Beat 2 — Centre, radius, diameter**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a clean circle. Mark the centre O. Draw radius OA. Draw diameter QR through O. Label all three.
**SAY:**
- "Centre — the point equidistant from all points on the circle. Every point on the circle is the same distance from O."
- "Radius — from the centre to any point on the circle. Label it r."
- "Diameter — the longest chord. Special because it passes through the centre."
- "How does the diameter relate to the radius?" Pause 3 seconds. "d = 2r. Radius = d divided by 2."
- "Every diameter is a chord. Not every chord is a diameter."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Centre is **[equidistant]** from all points on the circle. Radius goes from the **[centre]** to any point. Diameter is the **[longest]** chord — passes through the **[centre]**. d = **[2]** × r and r = d / **[2]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Chord and arc**
**SHOW:** Board: Same circle. Add chord PS (does not pass through O — draw it clearly off-centre). Then draw the minor arc between P and S with a curved accent line.
**SAY:**
- "Chord — a line segment with both endpoints on the circle."
- "PS is a chord. QR is a chord. What makes QR special? It passes through the centre."
- "Arc — the curved part of the circle between two points. Think: a piece of the circle's edge."
- "Minor arc is the shorter piece. Major arc is the longer piece."
- "Semicircle: exactly half the circle — cut by a diameter."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Chord has both endpoints on the **[circle]**. Arc is the **[curved part]** of the circle between two points. Semicircle is exactly **[half]** the circle — cut by a **[diameter]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "A chord is the same as a diameter." Address this directly before moving on. SAY: "Here is a mistake students make: they say any chord that goes through the circle is a diameter. Is that right?" Pause for responses. Then: "No. A diameter is a specific chord — one that passes through the centre. QR passes through centre O, so QR is a **[diameter]**. A chord that does not pass through O — like PS — would be **[shorter than]** the diameter. Every diameter is a chord, but not every chord is a diameter." Point to both QR and PS on the board simultaneously.

---

**Beat 4 — Central angle vs. inscribed angle**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw two circles side by side. In Circle A: mark centre O, draw two radii to points A and B on the circle, label the angle at O. In Circle B: mark a point C on the circle, draw two chords from C to points A and B, label the angle at C.
**SAY:**
- "Central angle — vertex at the centre. Both sides are radii."
- "Inscribed angle — vertex on the circle itself. Both sides are chords."
- "Same arc AB in both diagrams. The difference is where the vertex sits."
- "The arc intercepted by an angle is the arc that lies inside the angle."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Central angle has vertex at the **[centre]**. Inscribed angle has vertex on the **[circle]**, both sides are chords. Arc intercepted is **[inside]** the angle."

---

**Beat 5 — Tangent**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with a horizontal line touching it at exactly one point T. Draw the radius OT. Mark the right angle.
**SAY:**
- "Tangent — a line that touches the circle at exactly **[one]** point. That point is the point of tangency."
- "Is a tangent a chord?" Pause. "No. A chord has both endpoints on the circle. The tangent only touches at one point — it doesn't go inside."
- "The tangent is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency. That right angle marker is always there. We'll prove why in Section 4."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Tangent touches the circle at exactly **[one]** point. At the point of tangency, the tangent is **[perpendicular]** to the radius. Tangent is NOT a chord — **[No]**."

---

**Beat 6 — Minor arc, major arc, semicircle**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with two points marked P and Q. Shade the minor arc (the short way around). Arrow pointing to it: *minor arc*. Arrow pointing to the long way around: *major arc*. Separate diagram: diameter drawn, half the circle shaded: *semicircle*.
**SAY:**
- "Minor arc — the shorter arc between two points. Less than a semicircle."
- "Major arc — the longer arc. More than a semicircle."
- "Semicircle — exactly half. Always formed by a diameter."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Minor arc = **[shorter]** arc. Major arc = **[longer]** arc. Semicircle = **[half]** circle — cut by a **[diameter]**."

---

**Beat 7 — Vocabulary table fill-in**
**SHOW:** Board: The ten terms. Nothing else.
**SAY:**
- "Go back to your vocabulary table at the top of Section 1."
- "Fill in each term in your own words — not copied from your notes."
- "Add a drawing or example you came up with yourself."
- "Your definition should be something you could explain to a Grade 6 student."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete your vocab table now — own words, own drawings."

*Allow 4–5 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: students copying the fill-in notes verbatim (redirect — own words); students leaving 'inscribed angle' blank (prompt: "Where is the vertex? On the circle or at the centre?"); students confusing tangent and chord (redirect to the one-point-versus-two-endpoint distinction).*

---

### Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed. I'm going to ask you some questions — you write the answers from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish (4–5 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Find the answer in your notes. Fix anything that was wrong or incomplete — use a different colour if you have one."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. A chord has both endpoints on the circle. A diameter is a special chord — the only chord that passes through the centre. Every diameter is a chord; not every chord is a diameter.
2. A central angle has its vertex at the centre and its sides are radii. An inscribed angle has its vertex on the circle and its sides are chords.
3. Radius = 14 ÷ 2 = 7 cm.
4. The tangent is perpendicular (90°) to the radius at the point of tangency.
5. *(Stretch)* Yes — two chords can be equal in length. If they are equal in length, they are equidistant from the centre. (This previews Section 2's equal-chords property — note students who get this.)

## Section 2 — Chord Properties
*Learning target: I can apply the two chord properties — the perpendicular bisector property and the equal chords/equal distance property — to solve problems.*
*Timing: ~30 minutes direct instruction (includes two worked examples with Pythagorean theorem)*

*Have calculators available. Students need square root calculations.*

---

**Beat 1 — Property 1 setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with chord AB. From centre O, draw a line segment to the midpoint M of AB. Mark the right angle at M.
**SAY:**
- "Property 1. I'm going to tell you what it says, and then we'll look at why it makes sense."
- "If a line from the centre of a circle is perpendicular to a chord — 90° — then it bisects that chord."
- "Bisects: cuts into two equal halves."
- "AM equals MB. The centre 'sees' both halves equally because the circle is perfectly symmetric."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "If a line from the **[centre]** is perpendicular to a chord, then it **[bisects]** that chord. AM = **[MB]**."

---

**Beat 2 — The converse**
**SHOW:** Board: Same diagram. Add label: *Converse — works both ways.*
**SAY:**
- "The converse is also true. If a line from the centre bisects a chord — cuts it in half — then it is perpendicular to the chord."
- "Both directions. It bisects if and only if it's perpendicular."
- "This is a powerful tool: if you know a line from the centre hits the midpoint of a chord, you know you have a right angle. That means you can use the Pythagorean theorem."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Converse: if a line from the centre bisects a chord, then it is **[perpendicular]** to the chord."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: Bisector + Pythagorean theorem**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the triangle OMA. Label OA = 10 (radius), chord AB = 16. Mark right angle at M. Leave OM blank.
**SAY:**
- "Worked Example 1. Chord AB is 16 cm. Radius is 10 cm. OM perpendicular to AB."
- "Step 1: OM bisects AB. So AM = 16 ÷ 2 = 8 cm."
- "Step 2: Right triangle OMA. Which side is the hypotenuse?" Pause 3 seconds. "OA — the radius. It is the longest side, and it is opposite the right angle at M."
- "Step 3: Pythagorean theorem. OA² = OM² + AM²."
- "10² = OM² + 8². 100 = OM² + 64. OM² = 36. OM = 6 cm."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "AM = **[16]** ÷ 2 = **[8]** cm. OA = **[10]** (radius). AM = **[8]**. OM² = **[100]** − **[64]** = **[36]**. OM = **[6]** cm."

**After students fill in the blanks:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — look at Step 2, where we identified the hypotenuse. In your own words: what did we do there and why?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Explain Step 2 — why is OA the hypotenuse and how do we know?"

*Allow 60–90 seconds. Good answers identify: OA is opposite the right angle at M / OA is the longest side in the triangle / the radius connects O to the circle and M is inside the circle so OA must be longer. Students who write "it's just the longest" need a prompt: "Why is OA longer than OM and AM?"*

---

**Beat 4 — Property 2: Equal chords, equal distances**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with two equal-length chords AB and CD on opposite sides of the centre. From O, draw perpendiculars to each. Label distances d₁ and d₂.
**SAY:**
- "Property 2. Still in the same circle."
- "If two chords are equal in length, they are equidistant from the centre. Same distance."
- "Converse: if two chords are the same distance from the centre, they are equal in length."
- "d₁ equals d₂."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Equal chords in the same circle → **[equidistant]** from centre. d₁ = **[d₂]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "A longer chord is farther from the centre." Students think bigger = farther out. Address this directly. SAY: "Here's an intuition trap. Which chord is farthest from the centre — the longest one or the shortest one?" Pause for responses — most will say the longest. "It's the opposite. The longest chord of all is the diameter, and it passes through the centre: distance zero. As you move a chord farther from the centre, it gets shorter. Think of sliding a horizontal chord up toward the top of the circle — it shrinks and disappears at the edge. Closer to the middle = longer."

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 2: Equal chords**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the triangle OMP for chord PQ. Label OP = 13 (radius), PQ = 24. Mark right angle at M.
**SAY:**
- "Two equal chords PQ and RS, each 24 cm. Radius 13 cm. Find the distance from the centre to each chord."
- "Step 1: Equal chords — equidistant. So we only need to find the distance for one."
- "Step 2: OM bisects PQ. PM = 24 ÷ 2 = 12 cm."
- "Step 3: Right triangle OMP. OP² = OM² + PM². 13² = OM² + 12². 169 = OM² + 144. OM² = 25. OM = 5 cm."
- "Both chords are 5 cm from the centre."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "PM = **[24]** ÷ 2 = **[12]**. OP = 13. OM² = 169 − 144. OM = **[5]** cm. Both chords **[5]** cm from centre."

**After students fill in the blanks:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — look at Step 1 where we used equal chords to simplify the problem. In your own words: what did we do there and why?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Explain Step 1 — why could we just solve for one chord instead of both?"

*Allow 60–90 seconds. Students should identify: the equal-chords property tells us both distances are the same, so one calculation gives both answers.*

---

**Beat 6 — Connection prompt: Finding a centre**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle on the board with no centre marked. No labels.
**SAY:**
- "Here's an application. Imagine you have a circle — maybe a circular lid, a manhole cover, a coin. You can trace it but you don't know where the centre is."
- "You know that the perpendicular bisector of any chord passes through the centre."
- "How would you find the centre using only a ruler and compass?" Pause 4–5 seconds.
- "Write your steps. Don't wait — write while you think."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Describe the steps to find the centre of a circle using two chords and their perpendicular bisectors."

*Allow 90 seconds. Expected answer: draw two chords, construct the perpendicular bisector of each, the intersection is the centre. Students who get this are ready for the retrieval stretch question.*

### Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Two chord properties, and you need to be able to use them with the Pythagorean theorem."

**After students finish (5–6 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Check and correct."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. If a line from the centre is perpendicular to a chord, it bisects the chord (cuts it into two equal halves). Converse: if a line from the centre bisects a chord, it is perpendicular to it.
2. Half-chord = 18 ÷ 2 = 9 cm. Right triangle: r² = 9² + 12². r² = 81 + 144 = 225. r = 15 cm. *(Students frequently subtract instead of adding here — they mix up which side is the hypotenuse. The radius is the hypotenuse because it is opposite the right angle.)*
3. They are equidistant from the centre — the same distance from the centre.
4. Half-chord² = r² − d² = 5² − 3² = 25 − 9 = 16. Half-chord = 4. Full chord = 8 cm.
5. *(Stretch)* Chord 1: half = √(17² − 8²) = √(289 − 64) = √225 = 15 → full = 30 cm. Chord 2: half = √(17² − 15²) = √(289 − 225) = √64 = 8 → full = 16 cm. Chord 1 is longer by 30 − 16 = 14 cm. *(Both calculations require correct hypotenuse identification — award partial credit if one is set up correctly.)*

## Section 3 — Central Angle and Inscribed Angle Theorem
*Learning target: I can apply the inscribed angle theorem to find unknown angles, and explain why inscribed angles subtending the same arc are equal.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

*Have protractors available for Beat 3 if you want students to measure and verify.*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three terms — Inscribed angle theorem / Subtend / Same arc
**SAY:**
- "Three terms for this section. 'Subtend' is the key one."
- "When an angle subtends an arc, it means the angle opens up to face that arc. The arc is what the angle 'sees.'"
- "We'll fill in these definitions as we build the theorem."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Look at the diagram of the inscribed angle and central angle in your notes. Both angles open toward the same arc. That's what subtend means."

---

**Beat 2 — The theorem statement**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the diagram — circle with centre O, points A and B on the circle (at the bottom), point P on the circle (at the top). Draw inscribed angle APB. Draw central angle AOB. Both open toward arc AB at the bottom.
**SAY:**
- "The inscribed angle is half the central angle when both subtend the same arc."
- "Inscribed angle = (1/2) × central angle."
- "Or equivalently: central angle = 2 × inscribed angle."
- "In this diagram: central angle AOB = 2 × inscribed angle APB."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Inscribed angle = **[(1/2)]** × central angle. Central angle = **[2]** × inscribed angle."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: Central → Inscribed**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the scenario — label central angle AOB = 80°. Point P on the circle. Ask: find angle APB.
**SAY:**
- "Worked Example 1. Central angle AOB is 80°. Find inscribed angle APB — same arc AB."
- "Apply the theorem: inscribed = central ÷ 2."
- "APB = 80° ÷ **[2]** = **[40]**°."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "APB = 80° ÷ **[2]** = **[40]**°."

**After students fill in the blank:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — look at the step where we divided by 2. In your own words: what did we do there and why? Don't say 'because the theorem says so' — try to say where the factor of 2 comes from."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Explain the divide-by-2 step — where does the factor of 2 come from?"

*Allow 60–90 seconds. This is a hard self-explanation — students do not need a full proof, but a good answer gestures toward: the inscribed angle vertex is on the circle (farther from the arc), while the central angle vertex is at the centre (closer to the arc), so the central angle "opens wider" to see the same arc. Accept any reasoning that connects vertex position to angle size.*

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example 2: Inscribed → Central**
**SHOW:** Board: New diagram. Label inscribed angle APB = 35°. Ask: find central angle AOB.
**SAY:**
- "Worked Example 2. Inscribed angle APB is 35°. Find central angle AOB."
- "Reverse the theorem: central = 2 × inscribed."
- "AOB = 2 × 35° = **[70]**°."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "AOB = 2 × 35° = **[70]**°."

---

**Beat 5 — Corollary: Same arc, equal inscribed angles**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with arc AB at the bottom. Draw two different inscribed angles from points P and Q on the top arc — both opening toward arc AB. Label angles APB and AQB.
**SAY:**
- "Corollary to the theorem. If two inscribed angles both subtend the same arc, they are equal."
- "Why? Because both equal (1/2) × the same central angle."
- "It doesn't matter where P and Q are on the major arc. The inscribed angle is always the same size."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "APB = AQB = **[(1/2) × central angle AOB]**. No matter where P and Q are on the major arc, the angle stays the **[same]** size."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "All inscribed angles in a circle equal 90°." This is the most common error in this section. Address it directly now, before students practice. SAY: "Here's a mistake I see on tests: students write that any inscribed angle in a circle is 90°. Let's check that. In the diagram on your notes, the central angle AOB = 80°. So the inscribed angle = 40°. Is that 90°?" Pause. "No. 90° is a special case — it only happens when the chord AB is a diameter. When AB is a diameter, the central angle is 180° (a straight line). Inscribed angle = 180° ÷ 2 = **[90]**°. That's why angles in a semicircle are 90°. Not because they're in a circle — because the chord is a diameter." Write on the board: *90° inscribed angle → only when AB is a diameter.*

---

**Beat 6 — Angles in a semicircle**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with diameter AB. Mark point P on the circle. Draw inscribed angle APB. Mark 90°.
**SAY:**
- "Special case. If AB is a diameter, central angle AOB = 180°. Inscribed angle = 180° ÷ 2 = 90°."
- "This is always true. Any point P on the circle will give a 90° angle at P when looking across the diameter."
- "Practical test: if you inscribe an angle and it comes out 90°, the chord subtending it is a diameter."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "If AB is a diameter: central angle = **[180°]**. Inscribed angle APB = **[90°]**."

---

**Beat 7 — Finding unknown angles with major arc**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with central angle AOB = 110° on the minor arc side. Mark inscribed angle APB on the minor arc side. Then separately label: major arc central angle = 360° − 110° = 250°. Mark inscribed angle AQB subtending the major arc.
**SAY:**
- "Central angle AOB = 110°. Two questions."
- "Part a: inscribed angle APB on the same (minor) arc. APB = 110° ÷ 2 = **[55]**°."
- "Part b: inscribed angle AQB subtending the major arc. The major arc has central angle = 360° − 110° = **[250]**°. So AQB = 250° ÷ 2 = **[125]**°."
- "The arc matters. Same circle, different arc, different inscribed angle."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "APB = **[110°]** ÷ 2 = **[55]**°. Major arc central angle = 360° − 110° = **[250]**°. AQB = **[250°]** ÷ 2 = **[125]**°."

---

### Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. State the theorem, apply it, and explain the special case."

**After students finish (4–5 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Check and correct."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. The inscribed angle is half the central angle when both subtend the same arc. (Or equivalently: the central angle is twice the inscribed angle subtending the same arc.)
2. Inscribed angle = 144° ÷ 2 = 72°.
3. Central angle = 2 × 23° = 46°.
4. Because all inscribed angles subtending the same arc equal (1/2) × the same central angle — so they must all be equal to each other.
5. *(Stretch)* Angle APB = 40° (given, consistent with central angle 80°). Angle CPD = 130° ÷ 2 = 65°. *(Students must apply the theorem to the second arc independently. Common error: using 80° again. Redirect: "What is the central angle for arc CD?")*

## Section 4 — Tangent-Radius Property
*Learning target: I can apply the tangent-radius perpendicularity property and use it with the Pythagorean theorem to solve problems.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Four terms — Tangent / Point of tangency / Perpendicular / Tangent from external point
**SAY:**
- "You already know tangent from Section 1 — a line touching the circle at exactly one point."
- "Section 4 is about the angle relationship at that point of tangency, and what it lets us calculate."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Look at the tangent-radius diagram in your notes. Where is the right angle? Why do you think it's there?"

---

**Beat 2 — The property**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with centre O. Draw radius OT to the point of tangency T. Draw the tangent line through T (horizontal). Mark the 90° angle at T.
**SAY:**
- "The tangent-radius property: a tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency."
- "Angle OTP = 90°. Always."
- "This means: every time you see a tangent touching a circle, you automatically get a right angle at the point of tangency."
- "And a right angle means — what tool can we use?" Pause 3 seconds. "The Pythagorean theorem."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Tangent is **[perpendicular]** to the radius at the point of tangency. Angle OTP = **[90]**°."

---

**Beat 3 — The right triangle setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Same diagram. Add an external point P on the tangent line. Draw line segment OP (the hypotenuse). Label: OT = r (radius), PT = tangent length (unknown), OP = distance from P to centre.
**SAY:**
- "Add an external point P. Draw the line from P to the centre O."
- "Now we have a right triangle: legs are OT and PT, hypotenuse is OP."
- "The right angle is at T. So the hypotenuse is OP — the side opposite the right angle."
- "This setup is what allows us to calculate any one of the three lengths if we know the other two."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Radius and tangent form a **[right]** triangle with any line from the external point to the centre."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example: Tangent length**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the triangle OTP. Label OT = 5 cm (radius), OP = 13 cm. Mark right angle at T. Leave PT blank.
**SAY:**
- "Worked Example. Circle radius 5 cm. External point P is 13 cm from the centre. Find tangent length PT."
- "Right angle is at **[T]** — tangent meets radius."
- "Hypotenuse is **[OP]** — the longest side, opposite the right angle."
- "PT² = OP² − OT²."
- "PT² = 13² − 5² = 169 − 25 = 144."
- "PT = 12 cm."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Right angle at **[T]**. Hypotenuse = **[PO = 13]**. PT² = **[13²]** − **[5²]** = **[169]** − **[25]** = **[144]**. PT = **[12]** cm."

**After students fill in the blanks:**
**SAY:** "Before moving on — look at the step where we identified OP as the hypotenuse. In your own words: what did we do there and why?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Explain why PO is the hypotenuse and not PT or OT."

*Allow 60–90 seconds. Good answers: the right angle is at T, so the side opposite T (which is PO) must be the hypotenuse / the hypotenuse is always opposite the right angle / OT and PT are the two legs meeting at the right angle, so PO must be the hypotenuse.*

---

**Beat 5 — Equal tangents from an external point**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a circle with centre O. Mark external point P. Draw two tangent lines from P to the circle — one touching at T₁, one touching at T₂. Mark right angles at T₁ and T₂. Show that PT₁ = PT₂.
**SAY:**
- "From any external point, you can draw two tangents to a circle."
- "The two tangent segments — from the external point to each point of tangency — are equal in length."
- "PT₁ = PT₂."
- "Why? Both form right triangles with the same hypotenuse OP and the same leg OT = r. So the third side must be equal."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "From external point P: PT₁ = **[PT₂]**."

---

**Beat 6 — Alberta context problem**
**SHOW:** Board: Sketch: a circle labelled "circular fountain, r = 3 m." A point labelled "bench, 5 m from centre." Two straight dashed lines from the bench to the circle.
**SAY:**
- "Alberta context. A circular fountain in a Calgary park has radius 3 m. A park bench sits 5 m from the fountain's centre. Two straight paths run from the bench, each touching the fountain at exactly one point."
- "Each path is a tangent. The radius to the point of tangency is 3 m. The distance from bench to centre is 5 m."
- "Sketch the bench as an external point. Draw the radius to the point of tangency. Mark the right angle. Then use the Pythagorean theorem."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Path length² = 5² − 3² = 25 − 9 = 16. Path length = 4 m."

> **FLAG — Alberta Context:** This problem is genuine context — the geometry only makes sense because the paths are tangent to the fountain's edge. Removing "circular fountain" breaks the problem. If students ask why the path doesn't just cross the fountain, use it: "Could the path cross the fountain? No — we said it touches at exactly one point. That's what tangent means. The path runs alongside the fountain edge."

### Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. State the property, use it with the Pythagorean theorem, and explain the equal-tangents result."

**After students finish (4–5 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Check and correct."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. A tangent to a circle is perpendicular to the radius at the point of tangency.
2. PT² = 10² − 6² = 100 − 36 = 64. PT = 8 cm.
3. 8 cm. Equal tangents from an external point are equal in length.
4. The tangent-radius perpendicularity creates a right angle at the point of tangency. With that right angle, the triangle formed by the radius, the tangent, and the line from the external point to the centre is a right triangle — so the Pythagorean theorem applies.
5. *(Stretch)* PT² = 25² − 7² = 625 − 49 = 576. PT = 24 cm. For angle OPT: tan(OPT) = OT/PT = 7/24, so angle OPT ≈ 16°. *(Students who know trigonometry: full marks. Students who describe needing a trig ratio: partial credit. Students who draw the triangle and identify the correct opposite and adjacent sides: partial credit.)*
