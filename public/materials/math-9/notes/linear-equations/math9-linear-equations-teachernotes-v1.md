## How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

| Label | What it contains |
|---|---|
| **SHOW** | What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat. |
| **SAY** | Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in **[brackets]**. |
| **CUE** | The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩ Check |

Flags appear in callout blocks:
> **FLAG — Misconception:** Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address.
> **FLAG — Pacing:** Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum.
> **FLAG — Alberta Context:** Moment to connect the math to a recognizable Alberta setting.

**The screen and the voice carry different things.** What is SHOW stays visual — equations, steps, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

- "You will come back to this page at the end of the unit. Do not erase anything."

> **FLAG — Pacing:** Do not read student responses aloud or comment on them. ## Section 1 — Review: Solving One- and Two-Step Equations
*Learning target: I can solve one- and two-step linear equations by applying inverse operations and verify my solution.*
*Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: The five vocabulary terms in a column — Equation / Solution / Inverse operation / Balance model / Verify (check)
**SAY:**
- "Five terms on your vocab table. Do not fill them in yet."
- "We'll build each definition as we go. By the end of the section you'll have your own definition and example."
- "Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Read the five terms. Decide which one you're most confident about. Hold onto that thought."

---

**Beat 2 — Equation and the balance model**
**SHOW:** Board: *EQUATION = statement that two expressions are EQUAL*. Draw a simple balance scale: left side shows "x + 7", right side shows "−3". Scale is level.
**SAY:**
- "An equation says: these two things are equal."
- "The balance model makes this physical — the scale is level."
- "Whatever you do to one side of a balance, you must do to the other side."
- "Add 5 to the left? Add 5 to the right. Otherwise the scale tips."
- "Solving means finding the value of x that keeps the scale balanced."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "An equation is a mathematical statement that two expressions are **[equal]**. Solving means finding the value of the variable that makes the equation **[true]**. The balance model: whatever you do to one side, you must do to the **[other side]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Inverse operations**
**SHOW:** Board: Two-column table — Operation | Inverse. Leave the Inverse column blank while you talk.
**SAY:**
- "To isolate x, we undo operations using their inverse."
- "Addition and subtraction are inverses."
- "Multiplication and division are inverses."
- "Inverse means: it undoes the operation completely."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete your inverse operations table: Addition → **[subtraction]** / Subtraction → **[addition]** / Multiplication → **[division]** / Division → **[multiplication]**"

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example 1: One-step equation**
**SHOW:** Board: Write out x + 7 = −3. Work through the steps live, one line at a time.

```
x + 7 = −3
x + 7 − 7 = −3 − 7      ← subtract 7 from both sides
x = −10
Verify: (−10) + 7 = −3 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "One-step equation. What operation is being done to x? Adding 7."
- "What is the inverse of addition? Subtraction."
- "Subtract 7 from both sides — the scale stays balanced."
- "x equals negative ten."
- "Now verify. Do not skip this. Substitute negative ten back into the ORIGINAL equation — not the one you derived."
- "Why the original? Because if you made an error, the derived equation carries that error. The original is the ground truth."
- Pause 3 seconds.

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Inverse of + 7 is **[subtract]** 7. x = **[−10]**. Verify: substitute **[−10]** into original: **[−10]** + 7 = **[−3]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain the verify step in your own words: what did we do and why do we substitute into the original?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the verify question in your notes — two lines."

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 2: Two-step equation**
**SHOW:** Board: Write out 2x − 5 = 9. Work through live.

```
2x − 5 = 9
2x − 5 + 5 = 9 + 5      ← undo subtraction first: add 5
2x = 14
2x ÷ 2 = 14 ÷ 2          ← undo multiplication: divide by 2
x = 7
Verify: 2(7) − 5 = 14 − 5 = 9 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Two-step equation — two operations were done to x. We undo them in reverse order."
- "The expression was built as: multiply x by 2, then subtract 5."
- "To undo: reverse the order. Undo the subtraction first, then the multiplication."
- "Undo subtraction — add 5 to both sides."
- "Undo multiplication — divide both sides by 2."
- "Verify: plug 7 into the original."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Step 1: Undo subtraction first — add **[5]** to both sides: 2x = **[14]**. Step 2: Undo multiplication — divide both sides by **[2]**: x = **[7]**. Verify: 2(**[7]**) − 5 = **[9]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why do we undo the subtraction before the multiplication?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Write your explanation — one sentence."

> **FLAG — Pacing:** The "why undo in reverse order?" question consistently produces vague answers ("because you just do"). Push further: "When the expression 2x − 5 was built, which operation came last — the multiply by 2 or the subtract 5?" Students who understand order of operations will see it immediately. Students who don't will need an analogy: "You put on your socks and then your shoes. To undo that, you take off your shoes first, then your socks."

---

**Beat 6 — Worked Example 3: One-step with a fraction**
**SHOW:** Board: x/4 = −3. Solve live.

```
x/4 = −3
x/4 × 4 = −3 × 4         ← multiply both sides by 4
x = −12
Verify: (−12)/4 = −3 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "x divided by 4. The inverse of dividing by 4 is multiplying by 4."
- "Multiply both sides by 4. Fractions clear."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Multiply both sides by **[4]**: x = **[−12]**. Verify: **[−12]** / 4 = **[−3]** ✓"

---

**Beat 7 — Practice table**
**SHOW:** Board: Four equations — x − 9 = −4 / 3x = −18 / x/−2 = 5 / −4 + x = 7
**SAY:**
- "Work through all four in the table — show steps, solution, and a verification."
- "I'm watching for: verify in the original, and clear steps showing what you did to both sides."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the practice table — steps, solution, and verified for each equation."

*Allow 4–5 minutes. Circulate. Common errors: x − 9 = −4 solved as x = −13 (subtracting instead of adding); x/−2 = 5 solved as x = −2.5 (dividing instead of multiplying). Address both if you see them — hold your note for the misconception flag.*

> **FLAG — Misconception — "Dividing by a negative in an equation":** If any student solves x/−2 = 5 and writes x = −10 with a note about "flipping a sign," address it now, before it compounds in Section 4. SAY: "In an equation, you multiply or divide both sides by the same number — including a negative — and equality is preserved. The balance scale stays level no matter what. Flipping the sign is something that happens with inequalities — and only then. We will get to that in Section 4. For now: equations do not flip." This is the single most important pre-emption in this unit.

### Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed. Answer from memory — you're finding out what you retained, not testing yourself."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish (4–5 minutes):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Find each answer. Fix anything incomplete or incorrect — use a different colour if you have one."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. **5x = −35:** Divide both sides by 5. x = −7. Verify: 5(−7) = −35 ✓
2. **x/3 + 4 = 1:** Subtract 4: x/3 = −3. Multiply by 3: x = −9. Verify: (−9)/3 + 4 = −3 + 4 = 1 ✓
3. **Student error on x − 7 = 12:** The student subtracted 7 instead of adding 7. The equation says "x minus 7 equals 12," so to isolate x you undo the subtraction by adding 7. Correct solution: x = 19. Verify: 19 − 7 = 12 ✓
4. **What does "verify" mean?** Substituting the solution back into the original equation to confirm it makes the equation true. Use the original (not a derived step) because the original has not been manipulated — it is the problem as stated.
5. *(Stretch)* **−x = 4:** The inverse of multiplying by −1 is also multiplying by −1. Both sides × −1: x = −4. The solution tells you that x is −4; the negative sign on x means x is the opposite of 4.

*Q3 and Q4 are the diagnostic questions here — if students can articulate the verify reason and catch an error, Section 1 understanding is solid. Move on. If Q3 errors are widespread, take 2 minutes to re-run the balance model before starting Section 2.*

## Section 2 — Multi-Step Equations: Distribution and Like Terms
*Learning target: I can solve multi-step equations that require expanding brackets and collecting like terms before isolating the variable.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocabulary terms — Distribute / Collect like terms / Multi-step equation
**SAY:**
- "Three new terms today. Don't fill them in yet."
- "Two of these you've seen in the polynomials unit — distribution and like terms."
- "A multi-step equation just means we have to do those things before we can isolate x."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "You have 20 seconds — what does 'distribute' mean to you right now? Hold onto that before we define it formally."

---

**Beat 2 — Three situations requiring extra steps**
**SHOW:** Board:
1. Brackets — must be **distributed** first
2. Like terms on the same side — must be **collected** first
3. Variable terms on both sides — must be **moved to one side**

**SAY:**
- "Before Section 1's inverse-operation steps, three situations demand prep work."
- "Situation 1: brackets. You cannot ignore them. The bracket multiplies everything inside."
- "Situation 2: like terms. Two x-terms on the same side must be combined into one before you can isolate."
- "Situation 3: x-terms on both sides. Move them all to one side first."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Two new situations require extra steps before isolating x: (1) Brackets — must be **[distributed / expanded]** first. (2) Like terms on the same side — must be **[collected / combined]** first."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: Distribution**
**SHOW:** Board: 3(x + 4) = 21. Work through live.

```
3(x + 4) = 21
3x + 12 = 21             ← distribute: 3 × x and 3 × 4
3x = 21 − 12
3x = 9
x = 3
Verify: 3(3 + 4) = 3(7) = 21 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Brackets first. Always."
- "3 times the quantity x plus 4. The 3 multiplies every term inside: 3 times x and 3 times 4."
- "3x plus 12 equals 21."
- "Now we're back in Section 1 territory — undo in reverse order."
- "Subtract 12. Then divide by 3. x equals 3."
- "Verify in the original: 3 open bracket 3 plus 4 close bracket — follow order of operations."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Distribute: 3x + **[12]** = 21. Subtract 12: 3x = **[9]**. Divide by 3: x = **[3]**. Verify: 3(**[3]** + 4) = 3(**[7]**) = **[21]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why must we distribute before doing any other steps? What would happen if we divided by 3 first?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Write your explanation — two lines."

> **FLAG — Misconception — Partial distribution:** The most common distribution error is 3(x + 4) = 3x + 4. The student multiplies 3 by x but forgets to multiply 3 by 4. Address directly: SAY "Here's what almost everyone does wrong at least once: they distribute to the first term and forget the second. The bracket means 3 groups of the whole expression — 3 groups of x and 3 groups of 4. If you only multiply the first term, you are leaving the 12 off the answer. The resulting equation is wrong from line 2 and no amount of correct algebra after that can fix it." Write both versions side by side: 3(x+4) = 3x+4 [WRONG] and 3(x+4) = 3x+12 [CORRECT]. Also flag: −2(x − 5). The negative multiplies both terms and both signs: −2x + 10 — not −2x − 10.

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example 2: Variables on both sides**
**SHOW:** Board: 5x − 3 = 2x + 9. Work through live.

```
5x − 3 = 2x + 9
5x − 2x − 3 = 9          ← subtract 2x from both sides (move variable to one side)
3x − 3 = 9
3x = 12
x = 4
Verify: LHS: 5(4) − 3 = 17   RHS: 2(4) + 9 = 17 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "x appears on both sides. We can't isolate something that's in two places."
- "Goal: get all the x-terms onto one side."
- "Subtract 2x from both sides — this removes x from the right side."
- "3x minus 3 equals 9. Now it's a two-step equation."
- "For verify: compute both sides separately. They must match."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Move variable terms to one side (subtract 2x from both): **[3x]** − 3 = 9. Move constant: **[3x]** = 12. Isolate x: x = **[4]**. Verify: 5(**[4]**) − 3 = **[17]** and 2(**[4]**) + 9 = **[17]**. Equal? **[yes]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why does it not matter which side you move the variable term to?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Write your explanation. Then try solving it by moving 5x to the right side instead. Do you get the same answer?"

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 3: All three situations combined**
**SHOW:** Board: 4(x − 2) = 2x + 6. Work through live.

```
4(x − 2) = 2x + 6
4x − 8 = 2x + 6          ← distribute
4x − 2x = 6 + 8          ← move variable terms (subtract 2x); move constant (add 8)
2x = 14
x = 7
Verify: LHS: 4(7 − 2) = 4(5) = 20   RHS: 2(7) + 6 = 14 + 6 = 20 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Three situations at once. Work through them in order."
- "Step 1: distribute — clear the brackets."
- "Step 2: move variable terms to one side."
- "Step 3: isolate. Now solve."
- "Verify: both sides. Must match."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Distribute: **[4]**x − **[8]** = 2x + 6. Move variable terms: **[2]**x = **[14]**. Isolate: x = **[7]**. Verify: LHS: 4(**[7]** − 2) = 4(**[5]**) = **[20]**   RHS: 2(**[7]**) + 6 = **[20]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain what you did and why in the 'move variable terms' step: what were you trying to achieve, and how did you achieve it?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Two-line self-explanation."

---

**Beat 6 — Practice**
**SHOW:** Board: Three equations — 2(3x − 1) = 16 / 5x + 3 = 3x − 7 / −4(x + 2) = 2x − 8
**SAY:**
- "Solve and verify all three. Show every step."
- "For the third one: watch the distribution carefully — negative outside the bracket means both terms inside change sign."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Solve and verify all three. Full working shown."

*Allow 5–6 minutes. Circulate. For −4(x + 2) = 2x − 8: watch for −4x + 2 = 2x − 8 (forgetting the sign on the constant). Answer: −4x − 8 = 2x − 8 → −6x = 0 → x = 0. Many students find x = 0 surprising — pre-empt: "Zero is a perfectly valid solution. Verify it."*

---

### Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Multi-step equations — I want to see all three situation types: distribution, collecting like terms, variables on both sides."

**Answer guide:**
1. **3(2x − 4) = 18:** Distribute: 6x − 12 = 18. Add 12: 6x = 30. Divide: x = 5. Verify: 3(2(5) − 4) = 3(6) = 18 ✓
2. **6x − 5 = 4x + 3:** Move variable: 2x = 8. x = 4. Verify: LHS: 6(4) − 5 = 19. RHS: 4(4) + 3 = 19 ✓
3. **2(x + 3) = −(x − 9):** Distribute both sides: 2x + 6 = −x + 9. Move variable: 3x = 3. x = 1. Verify: LHS: 2(4) = 8. RHS: −(1 − 9) = 8 ✓
4. **Shortcut question — 2(x + 5) = 20:** Dividing both sides by 2 first gives x + 5 = 10, then x = 5. This works because the 2 is a factor of every term. The shortcut fails when the bracket is part of a larger expression — e.g., 3(x + 4) + 7 = 22. You cannot divide by 3 first because the 7 is not inside a "3 × something" structure.
5. *(Stretch)* **3(x + 2) − 2(x − 1) = 5(x − 1) + 3:** Distribute: 3x + 6 − 2x + 2 = 5x − 5 + 3. Collect like terms: x + 8 = 5x − 2. Move variable: −4x = −10. x = 5/2 (or 2.5). Verify: LHS: 3(4.5) − 2(1.5) = 13.5 − 3 = 10.5. RHS: 5(1.5) + 3 = 7.5 + 3 = 10.5 ✓

*Watch for students who correctly distribute in Q3 but forget to distribute the negative sign — −(x − 9) = −x + 9, not −x − 9. If this error appears widely, pause before moving to Section 3 and write both versions on the board.*

## Section 3 — Equations with Rational Coefficients
*Learning target: I can solve linear equations with fractional and decimal coefficients, using multiplication to clear fractions.*
*Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocabulary terms — Rational coefficient / LCD (least common denominator) / Clear the fractions
**SAY:**
- "Rational coefficient means the numbers in front of x are fractions or decimals."
- "This is new notation — same algebra underneath."
- "The big idea: multiply every term by the LCD to eliminate the fractions before solving."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "What's the LCD of 3 and 4? Of 2, 3, and 6? Hold those numbers — you'll need them in a moment."

---

**Beat 2 — The clearing strategy**
**SHOW:** Board: *When an equation has fraction coefficients — multiply every term by the LCD first.*
**SAY:**
- "Why does this work? Multiplying a fraction by its denominator gives a whole number."
- "x/3 times 3 equals x. The fraction disappears."
- "If every term on both sides is multiplied by the same number, the equation stays balanced."
- "We're using the multiplicative property of equality — the same tool as always, just strategically chosen."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "When an equation has fraction coefficients, the most efficient first step is to eliminate all fractions by multiplying every term by the **[LCD]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: Fraction coefficients**
**SHOW:** Board: x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6. Work through live.

```
x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6
LCD of 3, 2, 6 = 6
6(x/3) + 6(1/2) = 6(5/6)    ← multiply every term by 6
2x + 3 = 5                   ← fractions cleared
2x = 2
x = 1
Verify: (1)/3 + 1/2 = 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Find the LCD. Denominators are 3, 2, and 6. LCD is 6."
- "Multiply EVERY term on BOTH sides by 6. Not just the fraction terms — every term."
- "6 times x/3 — what do we get? 6 divided by 3 times x equals 2x. The fraction clears."
- "6 times 1/2 equals 3. 6 times 5/6 equals 5."
- "Now it's a simple two-step equation: 2x + 3 = 5."
- "Verify in the original — substitute, simplify, confirm."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "LCD of 3, 2, 6 = **[6]**. Multiply every term by **[6]**: **[2]**x + **[3]** = **[5]**. Solve: x = **[1]**. Verify: **[1]**/3 + 1/2 = **[2/6]** + **[3/6]** = **[5/6]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain the first step in your own words: why does multiplying every term by the LCD eliminate the fractions? Use the term x/3 as your example."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Two-line self-explanation."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example 2: Mixed fractions and whole numbers**
**SHOW:** Board: (2/3)x − 4 = x/4. Work through live.

```
(2/3)x − 4 = x/4
LCD of 3 and 4 = 12
12(2/3)x − 12(4) = 12(x/4)   ← multiply every term by 12
8x − 48 = 3x                 ← fractions cleared; whole number term × 12 too
5x = 48
x = 48/5
```

**SAY:**
- "LCD of 3 and 4 is 12."
- "Every term — including the whole number 4 — gets multiplied by 12."
- "12 times 4 equals 48. The constant does not get skipped."
- "After clearing: 8x minus 48 equals 3x. Move variable terms, isolate."
- "Answer is a fraction — that's fine. Verify it."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "LCD of 3 and 4 = **[12]**. Multiply all terms by 12: **[8]**x − **[48]** = **[3]**x. Move variable terms: **[5]**x = **[48]**. x = **[48/5]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception — Selective multiplication:** The most common error here is multiplying only the fraction terms and skipping the whole number. Students treat the whole number as "already cleared." SAY: "If I multiply only the fraction terms by 12, I've multiplied the left side by 12 in some places but not others. That changes the equation — the balance tips. The rule is absolute: every term on both sides, no exceptions. Watch your whole number terms." Write the wrong version and the correct version side by side.

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 3: Decimal coefficients**
**SHOW:** Board: 0.3x + 1.2 = 0.9. Show both options.

```
Option 1 — multiply by 10 to clear decimals:
10(0.3x) + 10(1.2) = 10(0.9)
3x + 12 = 9
3x = −3
x = −1

Option 2 — work with decimals directly:
0.3x = 0.9 − 1.2 = −0.3
x = −0.3 / 0.3 = −1

Both give x = −1. Verify: 0.3(−1) + 1.2 = −0.3 + 1.2 = 0.9 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Two options — both valid."
- "Option 1: multiply every term by 10. One decimal place — one factor of 10. Two decimal places — multiply by 100. This converts to integers."
- "Option 2: work with decimals. Algebraically identical — but rounding at any intermediate step can introduce error."
- "Option 1 is recommended: cleaner, less error-prone."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Option 1 — multiply by 10 to clear decimals: 3x + 12 = 9. Solve: x = **[−1]**. Option 2 — work with decimals directly: x = **[−0.3]** / 0.3 = **[−1]**. Both options give the same answer. Option 1 is usually less prone to rounding errors."

---

**Beat 6 — Practice**
**SHOW:** Board: Three equations — x/2 − x/3 = 1/6 / (3/4)x + 2 = (1/2)x + 5 / 0.5x − 3 = 0.2x + 0.6
**SAY:**
- "Solve all three. Show the LCD step explicitly — do not skip it."
- "For the third: what would you multiply by to clear the decimals?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Solve all three. Full LCD or decimal-clearing step shown for each."

*Allow 5–6 minutes. Answers: (1) LCD = 6: 3x − 2x = 1 → x = 1. (2) LCD = 4: 3x + 8 = 2x + 20 → x = 12. (3) Multiply by 10: 5x − 30 = 2x + 6 → 3x = 36 → x = 12.*

### Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Equations with fractions — I want to see the LCD step, the clearing step, and the solve."

**Answer guide:**
1. **x/4 + x/6 = 5/12:** LCD = 12. 3x + 2x = 5. 5x = 5. x = 1. Verify: 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12 ✓
2. **(2/3)x − 1/2 = (5/6)x:** LCD = 6. 4x − 3 = 5x. −3 = x. x = −3. Verify: (2/3)(−3) − 1/2 = −2 − 0.5 = −2.5 and (5/6)(−3) = −2.5 ✓
3. **0.4x + 1.8 = 0.6x − 0.6:** Multiply by 10: 4x + 18 = 6x − 6. 24 = 2x. x = 12. Verify: 0.4(12) + 1.8 = 4.8 + 1.8 = 6.6 and 0.6(12) − 0.6 = 7.2 − 0.6 = 6.6 ✓
4. **Why valid to multiply every term by the same number?** Multiplicative property of equality — multiplying both sides of an equation by the same nonzero number preserves the equality. It is the same operation applied uniformly to both sides; the balance holds.
5. *(Stretch)* **(x + 1)/3 − (x − 2)/4 = 1:** LCD = 12. 4(x + 1) − 3(x − 2) = 12. 4x + 4 − 3x + 6 = 12. x + 10 = 12. x = 2. Verify: (2+1)/3 − (2−2)/4 = 1 − 0 = 1 ✓

*Q5 is the high-value stretch — it combines clearing fractions with distribution. Students who handle it correctly are ready for the complexity of Section 4 worked examples.*

## Section 4 — Solving and Graphing Linear Inequalities
*Learning target: I can solve a one-variable linear inequality, apply the sign-flip rule correctly, and represent the solution on a number line.*
*Timing: ~25–30 minutes direct instruction*

> **Preparation note:** Draw or project a large number line on the board before this lesson. Label it −6 to 6. Leave it visible for the entire lesson — students will reference it repeatedly when graphing solutions.

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Five vocabulary terms — Inequality / Solution set / Open circle / Closed circle / Flip the sign
**SAY:**
- "Five terms. Hold off on definitions — build them as we go."
- "One word — 'solution set.' In equations, the solution was one value. Here it will be a whole set of values. That word choice is deliberate."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "You solved 3x + 5 = 14 on the Pre-Knowledge Check. What do you think will be different about 3x + 5 > 14? One idea — write nothing yet."

---

**Beat 2 — What an inequality is**
**SHOW:** Board: The four inequality symbols table — > less than / < greater than / ≥ greater than or equal to / ≤ less than or equal to — with the number line endpoint (open or closed) beside each.
**SAY:**
- "An inequality states that two expressions are NOT equal."
- "Greater than, less than, greater than or equal to, less than or equal to."
- "The solution is not a single value — it's a range. Every value in the range satisfies the inequality."
- "That range is called the solution set."
- "On a number line: open circle means the endpoint is NOT included — strict inequality, the equals sign is not there. Closed circle means the endpoint IS included."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "An inequality states that two expressions are NOT equal. The solution is a range of values, not a single value."

Then: ★ Write: "Open circle = endpoint **[not included]** (> or <). Closed circle = endpoint **[included]** (≥ or ≤)."

---

**Beat 3 — Worked Example 1: No sign flip**
**SHOW:** Board: 2x + 3 > 7. Work through live. Then draw the number line with open circle at 2, arrow pointing right.

```
2x + 3 > 7
2x > 7 − 3
2x > 4
x > 2          ← divide by positive 2 — NO flip
```

**SAY:**
- "Solve an inequality the same way you solve an equation — with one critical exception we will get to in the next example."
- "Subtract 3 from both sides. 2x greater than 4."
- "Divide both sides by positive 2. Dividing by a positive number: no change to the direction of the inequality."
- "x greater than 2."
- "Now graph it. Open circle at 2 — 2 is NOT in the solution set. Arrow pointing right — all values greater than 2 are in the solution set."
- Indicate the number line on the board.

**CUE:** ★ Write: "2x > **[4]**. x > **[2]** (divide by positive 2 — NO flip). Graph: open circle at **[2]**, arrow pointing **[right]**."

**SAY:** "Verify two points: pick a value IN the solution and a value NOT in the solution."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Verify: Pick x = 3 (in solution): 2(3) + 3 = **[9]** > 7? **[yes]**. Pick x = 1 (not in solution): 2(1) + 3 = **[5]** > 7? **[no]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception — Solving equations vs. solving inequalities:** Some students will write x = 2 as the solution, treating the inequality as an equation. Address before the next example: "In an equation, x = 2 is the answer — a single value. In an inequality, x > 2 is the answer — infinitely many values, all greater than 2. The number line is not decorative. It shows you the full solution set. A single value is not a complete answer to an inequality."

---

**Beat 4 — The sign-flip rule: intuition first**
**SHOW:** Board: *On a number line: 3 > 1. Multiply both sides by −1: −3 ___ −1. Which symbol fills the blank?*
**SAY:**
- "Before the next example — I want you to understand WHY the sign flips. This is the highest-risk moment in this unit."
- "We know 3 is greater than 1. True?"
- "Now multiply both sides by negative 1. Negative 3 versus negative 1."
- Pause 3–4 seconds. Point to the number line.
- "On the number line: negative 3 is to the LEFT of negative 1. It's smaller."
- "So: 3 > 1 became −3 < −1. The direction flipped."
- "Multiplying by a negative reverses the positions of both numbers relative to each other on the number line. The inequality direction must reverse to stay true."
- Pause 4 seconds.
**CUE:** ★ Write: "On a number line: 3 > 1. Multiply both by −1: −3 **[<]** −1. The positions swap sides relative to zero. That is why the symbol flips."

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 2: Sign flip required**
**SHOW:** Board: −3x + 6 ≤ 15. Work through live. Then draw the number line with closed circle at −3, arrow pointing right.

```
−3x + 6 ≤ 15
−3x ≤ 15 − 6
−3x ≤ 9
x ≥ −3          ← divide by −3 — FLIP the inequality sign
```

**SAY:**
- "Subtract 6 from both sides: negative 3x is less than or equal to 9."
- "Now divide both sides by negative 3. This is the moment."
- "Dividing by a negative number — FLIP the inequality sign."
- "Less than or equal to becomes greater than or equal to."
- "x is greater than or equal to −3."
- "Graph: closed circle at −3 — negative 3 IS included because of the 'or equal to.' Arrow pointing right."
- "Verify: pick x = 0 (should be in the solution): −3(0) + 6 = 6 ≤ 15 ✓. Pick x = −5 (should not be): −3(−5) + 6 = 21 ≤ 15 ✗. The solution is confirmed."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "−3x ≤ **[9]**. Divide by −3 — FLIP: x **[≥]** **[−3]**. Graph: **[closed]** circle at **[−3]**, arrow pointing **[right]**."

---

**Beat 6 — FLAG beat: The flip rule in full**
> **FLAG — Misconception — "You always flip the sign in inequalities":** This is the most dangerous misconception in this section — more damaging than forgetting to flip, because it means students flip even when they shouldn't. Address directly.

**SHOW:** Board: Two contrasting examples side by side:
- 2x > 8 → x > 4 (divide by +2, NO flip)
- −2x > 8 → x < −4 (divide by −2, FLIP)

**SAY:**
- "Here is what some students do: they flip the sign on every inequality because they heard 'inequalities flip.'"
- "That is wrong. The sign flips ONLY when you multiply or divide by a NEGATIVE number."
- "Adding or subtracting anything — positive or negative — does NOT flip the sign."
- "Multiplying or dividing by a POSITIVE number — does NOT flip the sign."
- "Only multiplying or dividing by a NEGATIVE number flips the sign."
- "Left example: divide by positive 2. No flip. x > 4."
- "Right example: divide by negative 2. Flip. x < −4."
- "The decision point is not 'am I doing inequalities?' It's 'am I multiplying or dividing by a negative?'"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "The sign flips ONLY when you multiply or divide by a **[negative]** number. Adding/subtracting and dividing by a positive: **[no flip]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception — "When you divide by a negative, flip the sign in equations too":** Some students will transfer the flip rule incorrectly to equations when they are solving equations with negative divisors in later work. Pre-empt: SAY "One more thing — this flip rule is for inequalities only. In equations, dividing both sides by negative 3 gives you the correct result without any sign change. The equality holds regardless. Flipping in an equation would give you the wrong answer." Write: −3x = 9 → x = −3 (no flip, correct). Do not skip this — it is a listed misconception in the course context and it will surface in tests.

---

**Beat 7 — Practice**
**SHOW:** Board: Three inequalities — 4x − 1 < 11 / −2x + 5 ≥ 1 / 3(x − 2) ≤ 2x + 1
**SAY:**
- "Solve and graph all three. Show all steps."
- "For each one: before you divide, ask yourself — positive or negative divisor? Then decide: flip or no flip?"
- "Graph on the number lines in your notes."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Solve and graph all three. Full working shown. Decision point labeled."

*Answers: (1) 4x < 12 → x < 3. Open circle at 3, arrow left. (2) −2x ≥ −4 → x ≤ 2 (flip). Closed circle at 2, arrow left. (3) Distribute: 3x − 6 ≤ 2x + 1 → x ≤ 7. Closed circle at 7, arrow left.*

*Allow 5–6 minutes. Common error on (3): distributing 3(x−2) as 3x − 2. Refer to the Section 2 flag. Also watch for students who graph (2) as an arrow pointing right — the flip changed the direction of the inequality AND the direction of the arrow.*

### Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Solving and graphing inequalities — I want the steps, the flip decision, and a correct graph."

**Answer guide:**
1. **5x + 2 > 17:** 5x > 15. x > 3. Open circle at 3, arrow right.
2. **−4x ≤ 20:** Divide by −4 — flip. x ≥ −5. Closed circle at −5, arrow right.

Dividing by a negative reverses the order of all values on the number line: what was greater becomes lesser. The inequality direction must be reversed to remain true.

## Section 5 — Problem Solving with Equations and Inequalities
*Learning target: I can translate a word problem into a linear equation or inequality, solve it, and interpret the answer in context.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Four vocabulary terms — Define the variable / Translate / Interpret / Reasonable answer
**SAY:**
- "These four terms describe the steps in any word problem."
- "The most-skipped step in Grade 9 math is the interpret step — writing a sentence answering the original question."
- "If you do all the algebra and write 'x = 5' with no sentence, you have not answered the question."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "What information would you need to 'define a variable'? What does that even mean? Hold onto it."

---

**Beat 2 — The four-step sequence**
**SHOW:** Board:
1. **Define:** Let x = ___ (what x represents + units)
2. **Translate:** Write the equation or inequality from the words
3. **Solve:** Apply algebraic steps
4. **Interpret:** Sentence answering the original question, with units

**SAY:**
- "Four steps. Every problem. In order."
- "Step 1 — define. Write 'Let x equal...' and be specific. Not just 'let x equal the number' — let x equal the number of hours, or the number of cups, or the cost in dollars."
- "Units. Always write units."
- "Step 2 — translate. Words become math. We have a translation table in the notes."
- "Step 3 — solve. Everything from Sections 1 through 4."
- "Step 4 — interpret. Write a sentence in English using the context of the problem. Use the variable's definition from Step 1."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "(1) Define: Let x = **[what x represents and its units]**. (2) Translate: Write the equation or inequality from the words. (3) Solve: Apply the algebraic steps. (4) Interpret: Write a sentence answering the original question, using units."

---

**Beat 3 — Translation table**
**SHOW:** Board: Translation table — fill in the Math column while students write.

| Words | Math |
|---|---|
| "is," "equals," "the same as" | = |
| "more than," "increased by" | + |
| "less than," "decreased by" | − |
| "times as much," "of" | × |
| "is at least," "no less than" | ≥ |
| "is at most," "no more than" | ≤ |
| "exceeds," "more than" | > |

**SAY:**
- "Key translation patterns. You'll see these phrases in every problem."
- "Two that trip students up: 'at least' means greater than or equal to — the endpoint is included. 'More than' means strictly greater — no equals sign."
- "Pause on every inequality problem and ask: is the endpoint included or not?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Record the full translation table."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked Example 1: Inequality context**
**SHOW:** Board: Problem: "A plumber charges a $75 call-out fee plus $50 per hour. A homeowner can spend at most $350. How many full hours of work can they have done?" Work through all four steps live.

```
Step 1: Let h = number of hours of work
Step 2: 75 + 50h ≤ 350          ("at most" → ≤)
Step 3: 50h ≤ 275
         h ≤ 5.5
Step 4: The homeowner can have at most 5 full hours of work done.
         (since h must be a whole number of hours, h ≤ 5)
```

**SAY:**
- "Step 1: h equals number of hours. Units: hours."
- "Step 2: total cost. Call-out fee is 75 — flat. Plus 50 for each hour — that's 50h. At most 350 — so less than or equal to."
- "Step 3: subtract 75 from both sides. Divide by 50. h ≤ 5.5."
- "Step 4 — the interpret step. 5.5 hours is not possible for full hours of work. The homeowner gets at most 5 full hours."
- "Notice: the algebra gives 5.5, but the context forces us to round down to 5. You cannot have 0.5 of a work hour in this problem. The context drives the final answer."
- "Also: Why ≤ not <? 'At most' includes the endpoint — the homeowner could spend exactly $350."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Define: h = number of hours of work. Translate: 75 + 50h **[≤]** 350. Solve: 50h ≤ **[275]** → h ≤ **[5.5]**. Interpret: The homeowner can have at most **[5]** full hours of work done. Reasonableness check: 75 + 50(5) = **[325]** ≤ 350 ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain the interpret step: why do we round down to 5 rather than rounding to 6 or leaving the answer as 5.5?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Two-line explanation."

---

**Beat 5 — Worked Example 2: Consecutive integers**
**SHOW:** Board: "Three consecutive even integers sum to 48. Find them." Work through all four steps live.

```
Step 1: Let n = first even integer
        Next two: n + 2 and n + 4  (consecutive even = add 2 each time)
Step 2: n + (n + 2) + (n + 4) = 48
Step 3: 3n + 6 = 48
         3n = 42
         n = 14
Step 4: The three consecutive even integers are 14, 16, and 18.
Verify: 14 + 16 + 18 = 48 ✓
```

**SAY:**
- "Define n as the first even integer. The next even integer is n plus 2 — not n plus 1."
- "Consecutive even integers go up by 2: 4, 6, 8, or 14, 16, 18."
- "Translate: all three sum to 48."
- "Collect like terms: 3n plus 6 equals 48."
- "Solve. n equals 14."
- "Interpret: the three integers are 14, 16, 18. Always write all three — the question asked for them, not just n."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Define: Let n = first even integer. Next two are **[n + 2]** and **[n + 4]**. Translate: n + **[n+2]** + **[n+4]** = 48. Solve: 3n + **[6]** = 48 → 3n = **[42]** → n = **[14]**. Interpret: the three integers are **[14]**, **[16]**, **[18]**. Verify: **[14]** + **[16]** + **[18]** = **[48]** ✓"

**SAY:** "Before moving on — explain Step 1: why is the next consecutive even integer n + 2 rather than n + 1?"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "One-sentence explanation."

**Beat 6 — Alberta context: Break-even**
**SHOW:** Board: "A Lethbridge student council is selling hot chocolate for $2.50 per cup. They spent $75 on supplies. How many cups must they sell to make a profit?"

```
Step 1: Let c = number of cups sold
Step 2: Revenue − Cost > 0
         2.50c − 75 > 0          (profit = revenue − cost, must be > 0)
Step 3: 2.50c > 75
         c > 30
Step 4: They must sell more than 30 cups to make a profit.
```

**SAY:**

- "Solve. c greater than 30. They must sell more than 30 cups."
- "Interpret: note that 30 is not in the solution set — they need the 31st cup to go into profit."

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Define: Let c = number of cups sold. Revenue: **[2.50]**c. Profit = Revenue − Cost > 0 → **[2.50]**c − 75 > 0. Solve: c > **[30]**. Interpret: They must sell more than **[30]** cups to make a profit."

> **FLAG — Alberta Context:** After this example, pause and ask: "Can you think of a similar situation in a school, farm, or community context in Alberta? A canola farmer's break-even on fuel costs per hectare, a hockey team's fundraising target, a pipeline crew's daily labour budget." Allow 30 seconds for one student to offer an example. The goal is to show that the define-translate-solve-interpret structure applies broadly — the math is identical, only the context changes.

### Section 5 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Word problems — four steps every time: define, translate, solve, interpret."

**Answer guide:**
1. **"A number is doubled and then decreased by 9. The result is 15."** Let n = the number. 2n − 9 = 15. 2n = 24. n = 12. The number is 12. Verify: 2(12) − 9 = 15 ✓
2. **"Two friends share $90. One receives $20 more than the other."** Let x = the smaller share. x + (x + 20) = 90. 2x + 20 = 90. 2x = 70. x = 35. The friends receive $35 and $55. Verify: 35 + 55 = 90 ✓ and 55 − 35 = 20 ✓
3. **Minimum mark on last assignment:** Let m = the mark needed. 68 + m ≥ 75. m ≥ 7. She needs at least 7 marks on the final assignment. *(Also check: is 7 marks within the 25-mark range? Yes — 7 ≤ 25. The solution is feasible.)*
4. **Why is the interpret step important?** Without it, the algebraic answer (a number or inequality) has no meaning in the problem's context. "x = 12" does not answer the question "how many full hours?" — "the homeowner can have at most 12 full hours" does. Skipping interpret produces an answer to a math problem but not to the question actually asked.
5. *(Stretch)* **Car catch-up problem from Edmonton:** Let t = hours after the second car leaves. Distance of first car: 80(t + 1.5). Distance of second car: 100t. Set equal when second car catches up: 100t = 80(t + 1.5). 100t = 80t + 120. 20t = 120. t = 6. Interpret: the second car catches up 6 hours after it leaves Edmonton (7.5 hours after the first car). At that point, both cars are 600 km from Edmonton.

*Q5 uses a genuine Alberta context (Edmonton, highway travel). The word "distance" should prompt students to write d = rt — if they don't make that connection, prompt: "What formula connects distance, rate, and time?"*
