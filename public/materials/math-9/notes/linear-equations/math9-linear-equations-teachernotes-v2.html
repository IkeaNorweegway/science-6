How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

Label What it contains
SHOW What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAY Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUE The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩︎ Check

Flags appear in callout blocks: > FLAG — Misconception: Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address. > FLAG — Pacing: Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum. > FLAG — Alberta Context: Moment to connect the math to a recognizable Alberta setting.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual — equations, steps, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Pre-Knowledge Check

~10 minutes — before any instruction

Beat PK.1 — Opening

SHOW

Board: Three prompts only — no additional explanation. 1. Solve: 3x + 5 = 14. Show your steps. 2. What do you think changes when you solve 3x + 5 > 14 instead of an equation? What would be different? 3. Can an equation have no solution? Can it have more than one solution? Write your honest answer.

SAY

 

CUE

 

SAY: - “Before we start this unit, I want to see what’s already in your head.” - “This is not a test. There are no marks. I will not correct it today.” - “Write honestly — especially question 3. Whatever you think, write it.” - “You will come back to this page at the end of the unit. Do not erase anything.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes to the Pre-Knowledge Check. You have 5 minutes — answer all three questions. Go.”

FLAG — PacingDo not read student responses aloud or comment on them. Circulate and look for: (a) students who already solve correctly using inverse operations — they are ready to move quickly through Section 1; (b) students who say “the inequality works the same way” — this is the dominant entry misconception and it tells you Section 4 will require extra time; (c) students who answer “no, an equation always has one solution” to question 3 — this surfaces before the identity/contradiction lesson.

Section 1 — Review: Solving One- and Two-Step Equations

Learning target: I can solve one- and two-step linear equations by applying inverse operations and verify my solution. Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: The five vocabulary terms in a column — Equation / Solution / Inverse operation / Balance model / Verify (check)

SAY

- “Five terms on your vocab table. Do not fill them in yet.” - “We’ll build each definition as we go. By the end of the section you’ll have your own definition and example.” - “Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “Read the five terms. Decide which one you’re most confident about. Hold onto that thought.”

Beat 1.2 — Equation and the balance model

SHOW

Board: EQUATION = statement that two expressions are EQUAL. Draw a simple balance scale: left side shows “x + 7”, right side shows “−3”. Scale is level.

SAY

- “An equation says: these two things are equal.” - “The balance model makes this physical — the scale is level.” - “Whatever you do to one side of a balance, you must do to the other side.” - “Add 5 to the left? Add 5 to the right. Otherwise the scale tips.” - “Solving means finding the value of x that keeps the scale balanced.”

CUE

★ Write: “An equation is a mathematical statement that two expressions are [equal]. Solving means finding the value of the variable that makes the equation [true]. The balance model: whatever you do to one side, you must do to the [other side].”

Beat 1.3 — Inverse operations

SHOW

Board: Two-column table — Operation | Inverse. Leave the Inverse column blank while you talk.

SAY

- “To isolate x, we undo operations using their inverse.” - “Addition and subtraction are inverses.” - “Multiplication and division are inverses.” - “Inverse means: it undoes the operation completely.”

CUE

★ Write: “Complete your inverse operations table: Addition → [subtraction] / Subtraction → [addition] / Multiplication → [division] / Division → [multiplication]”

Beat 1.4 — Worked Example 1: One-step equation

SHOW

Board: Write out x + 7 = −3. Work through the steps live, one line at a time.

SAY

 

CUE

 

x + 7 = −3
x + 7 − 7 = −3 − 7      ← subtract 7 from both sides
x = −10
Verify: (−10) + 7 = −3 ✓

SAY: - “One-step equation. What operation is being done to x? Adding 7.” - “What is the inverse of addition? Subtraction.” - “Subtract 7 from both sides — the scale stays balanced.” - “x equals negative ten.” - “Now verify. Do not skip this. Substitute negative ten back into the ORIGINAL equation — not the one you derived.” - “Why the original? Because if you made an error, the derived equation carries that error. The original is the ground truth.” - Pause 3 seconds.

CUE: ★ Write: “Inverse of + 7 is [subtract] 7. x = [−10]. Verify: substitute [−10] into original: [−10] + 7 = [−3] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain the verify step in your own words: what did we do and why do we substitute into the original?” CUE: ★ Write: “Answer the verify question in your notes — two lines.”

Beat 1.5 — Worked Example 2: Two-step equation

SHOW

Board: Write out 2x − 5 = 9. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

2x − 5 = 9
2x − 5 + 5 = 9 + 5      ← undo subtraction first: add 5
2x = 14
2x ÷ 2 = 14 ÷ 2          ← undo multiplication: divide by 2
x = 7
Verify: 2(7) − 5 = 14 − 5 = 9 ✓

SAY: - “Two-step equation — two operations were done to x. We undo them in reverse order.” - “The expression was built as: multiply x by 2, then subtract 5.” - “To undo: reverse the order. Undo the subtraction first, then the multiplication.” - “Undo subtraction — add 5 to both sides.” - “Undo multiplication — divide both sides by 2.” - “Verify: plug 7 into the original.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Step 1: Undo subtraction first — add [5] to both sides: 2x = [14]. Step 2: Undo multiplication — divide both sides by [2]: x = [7]. Verify: 2([7]) − 5 = [9] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why do we undo the subtraction before the multiplication?” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation — one sentence.”

FLAG — PacingThe “why undo in reverse order?” question consistently produces vague answers (“because you just do”). Push further: “When the expression 2x − 5 was built, which operation came last — the multiply by 2 or the subtract 5?” Students who understand order of operations will see it immediately. Students who don’t will need an analogy: “You put on your socks and then your shoes. To undo that, you take off your shoes first, then your socks.”

Beat 1.6 — Worked Example 3: One-step with a fraction

SHOW

Board: x/4 = −3. Solve live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

x/4 = −3
x/4 × 4 = −3 × 4         ← multiply both sides by 4
x = −12
Verify: (−12)/4 = −3 ✓

SAY: - “x divided by 4. The inverse of dividing by 4 is multiplying by 4.” - “Multiply both sides by 4. Fractions clear.” CUE: ★ Write: “Multiply both sides by [4]: x = [−12]. Verify: [−12] / 4 = [−3] ✓”

Beat 1.7 — Practice table

SHOW

Board: Four equations — x − 9 = −4 / 3x = −18 / x/−2 = 5 / −4 + x = 7

SAY

- “Work through all four in the table — show steps, solution, and a verification.” - “I’m watching for: verify in the original, and clear steps showing what you did to both sides.”

CUE

★ Write: “Complete the practice table — steps, solution, and verified for each equation.”

Allow 4–5 minutes. Circulate. Common errors: x − 9 = −4 solved as x = −13 (subtracting instead of adding); x/−2 = 5 solved as x = −2.5 (dividing instead of multiplying). Address both if you see them — hold your note for the misconception flag.

FLAG — Misconception — “Dividing by a negative in an equation”If any student solves x/−2 = 5 and writes x = −10 with a note about “flipping a sign,” address it now, before it compounds in Section 4. SAY: “In an equation, you multiply or divide both sides by the same number — including a negative — and equality is preserved. The balance scale stays level no matter what. Flipping the sign is something that happens with inequalities — and only then. We will get to that in Section 4. For now: equations do not flip.” This is the single most important pre-emption in this unit.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed. Answer from memory — you’re finding out what you retained, not testing yourself.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish (4–5 minutes): SAY: “Open your notes. Find each answer. Fix anything incomplete or incorrect — use a different colour if you have one.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. 5x = −35: Divide both sides by 5. x = −7. Verify: 5(−7) = −35 ✓ 2. x/3 + 4 = 1: Subtract 4: x/3 = −3. Multiply by 3: x = −9. Verify: (−9)/3 + 4 = −3 + 4 = 1 ✓ 3. Student error on x − 7 = 12: The student subtracted 7 instead of adding 7. The equation says “x minus 7 equals 12,” so to isolate x you undo the subtraction by adding 7. Correct solution: x = 19. Verify: 19 − 7 = 12 ✓ 4. What does “verify” mean? Substituting the solution back into the original equation to confirm it makes the equation true. Use the original (not a derived step) because the original has not been manipulated — it is the problem as stated. 5. (Stretch) −x = 4: The inverse of multiplying by −1 is also multiplying by −1. Both sides × −1: x = −4. The solution tells you that x is −4; the negative sign on x means x is the opposite of 4.

Q3 and Q4 are the diagnostic questions here — if students can articulate the verify reason and catch an error, Section 1 understanding is solid. Move on. If Q3 errors are widespread, take 2 minutes to re-run the balance model before starting Section 2.

Section 2 — Multi-Step Equations: Distribution and Like Terms

Learning target: I can solve multi-step equations that require expanding brackets and collecting like terms before isolating the variable. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 2.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Three vocabulary terms — Distribute / Collect like terms / Multi-step equation

SAY

- “Three new terms today. Don’t fill them in yet.” - “Two of these you’ve seen in the polynomials unit — distribution and like terms.” - “A multi-step equation just means we have to do those things before we can isolate x.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “You have 20 seconds — what does ‘distribute’ mean to you right now? Hold onto that before we define it formally.”

Beat 2.2 — Three situations requiring extra steps

SHOW

Board: 1. Brackets — must be distributed first 2. Like terms on the same side — must be collected first 3. Variable terms on both sides — must be moved to one side

SAY

 

CUE

 

SAY: - “Before Section 1’s inverse-operation steps, three situations demand prep work.” - “Situation 1: brackets. You cannot ignore them. The bracket multiplies everything inside.” - “Situation 2: like terms. Two x-terms on the same side must be combined into one before you can isolate.” - “Situation 3: x-terms on both sides. Move them all to one side first.” CUE: ★ Write: “Two new situations require extra steps before isolating x: (1) Brackets — must be [distributed / expanded] first. (2) Like terms on the same side — must be [collected / combined] first.”

Beat 2.3 — Worked Example 1: Distribution

SHOW

Board: 3(x + 4) = 21. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

3(x + 4) = 21
3x + 12 = 21             ← distribute: 3 × x and 3 × 4
3x = 21 − 12
3x = 9
x = 3
Verify: 3(3 + 4) = 3(7) = 21 ✓

SAY: - “Brackets first. Always.” - “3 times the quantity x plus 4. The 3 multiplies every term inside: 3 times x and 3 times 4.” - “3x plus 12 equals 21.” - “Now we’re back in Section 1 territory — undo in reverse order.” - “Subtract 12. Then divide by 3. x equals 3.” - “Verify in the original: 3 open bracket 3 plus 4 close bracket — follow order of operations.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Distribute: 3x + [12] = 21. Subtract 12: 3x = [9]. Divide by 3: x = [3]. Verify: 3([3] + 4) = 3([7]) = [21] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why must we distribute before doing any other steps? What would happen if we divided by 3 first?” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation — two lines.”

FLAG — Misconception — Partial distributionThe most common distribution error is 3(x + 4) = 3x + 4. The student multiplies 3 by x but forgets to multiply 3 by 4. Address directly: SAY “Here’s what almost everyone does wrong at least once: they distribute to the first term and forget the second. The bracket means 3 groups of the whole expression — 3 groups of x and 3 groups of 4. If you only multiply the first term, you are leaving the 12 off the answer. The resulting equation is wrong from line 2 and no amount of correct algebra after that can fix it.” Write both versions side by side: 3(x+4) = 3x+4 [WRONG] and 3(x+4) = 3x+12 [CORRECT]. Also flag: −2(x − 5). The negative multiplies both terms and both signs: −2x + 10 — not −2x − 10.

Beat 2.4 — Worked Example 2: Variables on both sides

SHOW

Board: 5x − 3 = 2x + 9. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

5x − 3 = 2x + 9
5x − 2x − 3 = 9          ← subtract 2x from both sides (move variable to one side)
3x − 3 = 9
3x = 12
x = 4
Verify: LHS: 5(4) − 3 = 17   RHS: 2(4) + 9 = 17 ✓

SAY: - “x appears on both sides. We can’t isolate something that’s in two places.” - “Goal: get all the x-terms onto one side.” - “Subtract 2x from both sides — this removes x from the right side.” - “3x minus 3 equals 9. Now it’s a two-step equation.” - “For verify: compute both sides separately. They must match.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Move variable terms to one side (subtract 2x from both): [3x] − 3 = 9. Move constant: [3x] = 12. Isolate x: x = [4]. Verify: 5([4]) − 3 = [17] and 2([4]) + 9 = [17]. Equal? [yes] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: why does it not matter which side you move the variable term to?” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation. Then try solving it by moving 5x to the right side instead. Do you get the same answer?”

Beat 2.5 — Worked Example 3: All three situations combined

SHOW

Board: 4(x − 2) = 2x + 6. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

4(x − 2) = 2x + 6
4x − 8 = 2x + 6          ← distribute
4x − 2x = 6 + 8          ← move variable terms (subtract 2x); move constant (add 8)
2x = 14
x = 7
Verify: LHS: 4(7 − 2) = 4(5) = 20   RHS: 2(7) + 6 = 14 + 6 = 20 ✓

SAY: - “Three situations at once. Work through them in order.” - “Step 1: distribute — clear the brackets.” - “Step 2: move variable terms to one side.” - “Step 3: isolate. Now solve.” - “Verify: both sides. Must match.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Distribute: [4]x − [8] = 2x + 6. Move variable terms: [2]x = [14]. Isolate: x = [7]. Verify: LHS: 4([7] − 2) = 4([5]) = [20] RHS: 2([7]) + 6 = [20] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain what you did and why in the ‘move variable terms’ step: what were you trying to achieve, and how did you achieve it?” CUE: ★ Write: “Two-line self-explanation.”

Beat 2.6 — Practice

SHOW

Board: Three equations — 2(3x − 1) = 16 / 5x + 3 = 3x − 7 / −4(x + 2) = 2x − 8

SAY

- “Solve and verify all three. Show every step.” - “For the third one: watch the distribution carefully — negative outside the bracket means both terms inside change sign.”

CUE

★ Write: “Solve and verify all three. Full working shown.”

Allow 5–6 minutes. Circulate. For −4(x + 2) = 2x − 8: watch for −4x + 2 = 2x − 8 (forgetting the sign on the constant). Answer: −4x − 8 = 2x − 8 → −6x = 0 → x = 0. Many students find x = 0 surprising — pre-empt: “Zero is a perfectly valid solution. Verify it.”

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Multi-step equations — I want to see all three situation types: distribution, collecting like terms, variables on both sides.”

Answer guide: 1. 3(2x − 4) = 18: Distribute: 6x − 12 = 18. Add 12: 6x = 30. Divide: x = 5. Verify: 3(2(5) − 4) = 3(6) = 18 ✓ 2. 6x − 5 = 4x + 3: Move variable: 2x = 8. x = 4. Verify: LHS: 6(4) − 5 = 19. RHS: 4(4) + 3 = 19 ✓ 3. 2(x + 3) = −(x − 9): Distribute both sides: 2x + 6 = −x + 9. Move variable: 3x = 3. x = 1. Verify: LHS: 2(4) = 8. RHS: −(1 − 9) = 8 ✓ 4. Shortcut question — 2(x + 5) = 20: Dividing both sides by 2 first gives x + 5 = 10, then x = 5. This works because the 2 is a factor of every term. The shortcut fails when the bracket is part of a larger expression — e.g., 3(x + 4) + 7 = 22. You cannot divide by 3 first because the 7 is not inside a “3 × something” structure. 5. (Stretch) 3(x + 2) − 2(x − 1) = 5(x − 1) + 3: Distribute: 3x + 6 − 2x + 2 = 5x − 5 + 3. Collect like terms: x + 8 = 5x − 2. Move variable: −4x = −10. x = 5/2 (or 2.5). Verify: LHS: 3(4.5) − 2(1.5) = 13.5 − 3 = 10.5. RHS: 5(1.5) + 3 = 7.5 + 3 = 10.5 ✓

Watch for students who correctly distribute in Q3 but forget to distribute the negative sign — −(x − 9) = −x + 9, not −x − 9. If this error appears widely, pause before moving to Section 3 and write both versions on the board.

Section 3 — Equations with Rational Coefficients

Learning target: I can solve linear equations with fractional and decimal coefficients, using multiplication to clear fractions. Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 3.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Three vocabulary terms — Rational coefficient / LCD (least common denominator) / Clear the fractions

SAY

- “Rational coefficient means the numbers in front of x are fractions or decimals.” - “This is new notation — same algebra underneath.” - “The big idea: multiply every term by the LCD to eliminate the fractions before solving.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “What’s the LCD of 3 and 4? Of 2, 3, and 6? Hold those numbers — you’ll need them in a moment.”

Beat 3.2 — The clearing strategy

SHOW

Board: When an equation has fraction coefficients — multiply every term by the LCD first.

SAY

- “Why does this work? Multiplying a fraction by its denominator gives a whole number.” - “x/3 times 3 equals x. The fraction disappears.” - “If every term on both sides is multiplied by the same number, the equation stays balanced.” - “We’re using the multiplicative property of equality — the same tool as always, just strategically chosen.”

CUE

★ Write: “When an equation has fraction coefficients, the most efficient first step is to eliminate all fractions by multiplying every term by the [LCD].”

Beat 3.3 — Worked Example 1: Fraction coefficients

SHOW

Board: x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6
LCD of 3, 2, 6 = 6
6(x/3) + 6(1/2) = 6(5/6)    ← multiply every term by 6
2x + 3 = 5                   ← fractions cleared
2x = 2
x = 1
Verify: (1)/3 + 1/2 = 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6 ✓

SAY: - “Find the LCD. Denominators are 3, 2, and 6. LCD is 6.” - “Multiply EVERY term on BOTH sides by 6. Not just the fraction terms — every term.” - “6 times x/3 — what do we get? 6 divided by 3 times x equals 2x. The fraction clears.” - “6 times 1/2 equals 3. 6 times 5/6 equals 5.” - “Now it’s a simple two-step equation: 2x + 3 = 5.” - “Verify in the original — substitute, simplify, confirm.”

CUE: ★ Write: “LCD of 3, 2, 6 = [6]. Multiply every term by [6]: [2]x + [3] = [5]. Solve: x = [1]. Verify: [1]/3 + 1/2 = [2/6] + [3/6] = [5/6] ✓”

SAY: “Before moving on — explain the first step in your own words: why does multiplying every term by the LCD eliminate the fractions? Use the term x/3 as your example.” CUE: ★ Write: “Two-line self-explanation.”

Beat 3.4 — Worked Example 2: Mixed fractions and whole numbers

SHOW

Board: (2/3)x − 4 = x/4. Work through live.

SAY

 

CUE

 

(2/3)x − 4 = x/4
LCD of 3 and 4 = 12
12(2/3)x − 12(4) = 12(x/4)   ← multiply every term by 12
8x − 48 = 3x                 ← fractions cleared; whole number term × 12 too
5x = 48
x = 48/5

SAY: - “LCD of 3 and 4 is 12.” - “Every term — including the whole number 4 — gets multiplied by 12.” - “12 times 4 equals 48. The constant does not get skipped.” - “After clearing: 8x minus 48 equals 3x. Move variable terms, isolate.” - “Answer is a fraction — that’s fine. Verify it.”

CUE: ★ Write: “LCD of 3 and 4 = [12]. Multiply all terms by 12: [8]x − [48] = [3]x. Move variable terms: [5]x = [48]. x = [48/5].”

FLAG — Misconception — Selective multiplicationThe most common error here is multiplying only the fraction terms and skipping the whole number. Students treat the whole number as “already cleared.” SAY: “If I multiply only the fraction terms by 12, I’ve multiplied the left side by 12 in some places but not others. That changes the equation — the balance tips. The rule is absolute: every term on both sides, no exceptions. Watch your whole number terms.” Write the wrong version and the correct version side by side.

Beat 3.5 — Worked Example 3: Decimal coefficients

SHOW

Board: 0.3x + 1.2 = 0.9. Show both options.

SAY

 

CUE

 

Option 1 — multiply by 10 to clear decimals:
10(0.3x) + 10(1.2) = 10(0.9)
3x + 12 = 9
3x = −3
x = −1

Option 2 — work with decimals directly:
0.3x = 0.9 − 1.2 = −0.3
x = −0.3 / 0.3 = −1

Both give x = −1. Verify: 0.3(−1) + 1.2 = −0.3 + 1.2 = 0.9 ✓

SAY: - “Two options — both valid.” - “Option 1: multiply every term by 10. One decimal place — one factor of 10. Two decimal places — multiply by 100. This converts to integers.” - “Option 2: work with decimals. Algebraically identical — but rounding at any intermediate step can introduce error.” - “Option 1 is recommended: cleaner, less error-prone.” CUE: ★ Write: “Option 1 — multiply by 10 to clear decimals: 3x + 12 = 9. Solve: x = [−1]. Option 2 — work with decimals directly: x = [−0.3] / 0.3 = [−1]. Both options give the same answer. Option 1 is usually less prone to rounding errors.”

Beat 3.6 — Practice

SHOW

Board: Three equations — x/2 − x/3 = 1/6 / (3/4)x + 2 = (1/2)x + 5 / 0.5x − 3 = 0.2x + 0.6

SAY

- “Solve all three. Show the LCD step explicitly — do not skip it.” - “For the third: what would you multiply by to clear the decimals?”

CUE

★ Write: “Solve all three. Full LCD or decimal-clearing step shown for each.”

Allow 5–6 minutes. Answers: (1) LCD = 6: 3x − 2x = 1 → x = 1. (2) LCD = 4: 3x + 8 = 2x + 20 → x = 12. (3) Multiply by 10: 5x − 30 = 2x + 6 → 3x = 36 → x = 12.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Equations with fractions — I want to see the LCD step, the clearing step, and the solve.”

Answer guide: 1. x/4 + x/6 = 5/12: LCD = 12. 3x + 2x = 5. 5x = 5. x = 1. Verify: 1/4 + 1/6 = 3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12 ✓ 2. (2/3)x − 1/2 = (5/6)x: LCD = 6. 4x − 3 = 5x. −3 = x. x = −3. Verify: (2/3)(−3) − 1/2 = −2 − 0.5 = −2.5 and (5/6)(−3) = −2.5 ✓ 3. 0.4x + 1.8 = 0.6x − 0.6: Multiply by 10: 4x + 18 = 6x − 6. 24 = 2x. x = 12. Verify: 0.4(12) + 1.8 = 4.8 + 1.8 = 6.6 and 0.6(12) − 0.6 = 7.2 − 0.6 = 6.6 ✓ 4. Why valid to multiply every term by the same number? Multiplicative property of equality — multiplying both sides of an equation by the same nonzero number preserves the equality. It is the same operation applied uniformly to both sides; the balance holds. 5. (Stretch) (x + 1)/3 − (x − 2)/4 = 1: LCD = 12. 4(x + 1) − 3(x − 2) = 12. 4x + 4 − 3x + 6 = 12. x + 10 = 12. x = 2. Verify: (2+1)/3 − (2−2)/4 = 1 − 0 = 1 ✓

Q5 is the high-value stretch — it combines clearing fractions with distribution. Students who handle it correctly are ready for the complexity of Section 4 worked examples.

Section 4 — Solving and Graphing Linear Inequalities

Learning target: I can solve a one-variable linear inequality, apply the sign-flip rule correctly, and represent the solution on a number line. Timing: ~25–30 minutes direct instruction

Preparation note: Draw or project a large number line on the board before this lesson. Label it −6 to 6. Leave it visible for the entire lesson — students will reference it repeatedly when graphing solutions.

Beat 4.1 — Vocabulary setup

SHOW

Board: Five vocabulary terms — Inequality / Solution set / Open circle / Closed circle / Flip the sign

SAY

- “Five terms. Hold off on definitions — build them as we go.” - “One word — ‘solution set.’ In equations, the solution was one value. Here it will be a whole set of values. That word choice is deliberate.”

CUE

⊡ Think: “You solved 3x + 5 = 14 on the Pre-Knowledge Check. What do you think will be different about 3x + 5 > 14? One idea — write nothing yet.”

Beat 4.2 — What an inequality is

SHOW

Board: The four inequality symbols table — > less than / < greater than / ≥ greater than or equal to / ≤ less than or equal to — with the number line endpoint (open or closed) beside each.

SAY

- “An inequality states that two expressions are NOT equal.” - “Greater than, less than, greater than or equal to, less than or equal to.” - “The solution is not a single value — it’s a range. Beat 4.4 — The sign-flip rule: intuition first

SHOW

Board: On a number line: 3 > 1. Multiply both sides by −1: −3 ___ −1. Which symbol fills the blank?

SAY

True?” - “Now multiply both sides by negative 1. Negative 3 versus negative 1.” - Pause 3–4 seconds. Point to the number line. - “On the number line: negative 3 is to the LEFT of negative 1. It’s smaller.” - “So: 3 > 1 became −3 < −1. The direction flipped.” - “Multiplying by a negative reverses the positions of both numbers relative to each other on the number line. The inequality direction must reverse to stay true.” - Pause 4 seconds.

CUE

★ Write: “On a number line: 3 > 1. Multiply both by −1: −3 [<] −1. The positions swap sides relative to zero. That is why the symbol flips.”

Before releasing: CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Word problems — four steps every time: define, translate, solve, interpret.”

Answer guide: 1. “A number is doubled and then decreased by 9. The result is 15.” Let n = the number. 2n − 9 = 15. 2n = 24. n = 12. The number is 12.
