Misconception

Wrong idea: In x/3 + 1/2 = 5/6, a student multiplies only the fraction terms by 6, missing any whole number terms.

Why it fails: Every term on both sides must be multiplied by the same number. The LCD clears ALL denominators simultaneously. If you miss a term, you have changed the equation's balance.

Rule: If you multiply any term by the LCD, you must multiply EVERY term on BOTH sides.

Misconception

Wrong idea: "Inequalities are different from equations, so the sign always flips somewhere."

Why it fails: You only flip when you multiply or divide by a negative number. Multiplying by a positive number — or adding/subtracting anything — does NOT cause a flip. The sign flips specifically because multiplying by a negative reverses the order of all numbers: if 3 > 1, then −3 < −1.

Test: If 6 > 2 and we divide by −2: −3 −1. Did the direction change?

The sign-flip reason — visualized:

On a number line: 3 > 1

Multiply both by −1: −3 −1 (circle the correct symbol: < >)

The positions swap sides relative to zero. That is why the symbol flips.

Misconception

Wrong idea: "Any time I solve an inequality, the sign flips."

Why it fails: Only negative multipliers/divisors flip the sign. 2x > 8 gives x > 4 (divide by +2, no flip). Test: if 2(5) = 10 > 8, is 5 > 4? Yes. The direction held.

Guided Notes

The four-step problem-solving sequence:

  1. Define: Let x = (specify what x represents and its units)
  2. Translate: Write the equation or inequality from the words
  3. Solve: Apply the algebraic steps
  4. Interpret: Write a sentence answering the original question, using units
