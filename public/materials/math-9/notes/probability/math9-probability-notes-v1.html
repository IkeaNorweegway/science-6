Section 3 — Independent Events

Learning target: I can identify independent events, calculate the probability of both occurring using the multiplication rule, and explain why independence matters.

Vocabulary

TermDefinition in your own wordsOne example
Independent events
Multiplication rule (independent)

Guided Notes

Two events are independent if the outcome of one event does NOT affect the of the other.

Examples of independent events:

  • Flipping a coin twice (first flip does not affect second)
  • Rolling a die and spinning a spinner
  • Drawing a card, replacing it, then drawing again (the replacement is key)

Examples of DEPENDENT events (these come in Section 4):

  • Drawing a card and keeping it, then drawing again
  • Choosing a student from a class and then another student without replacement

The Multiplication Rule for Independent Events:

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

This works because the two probabilities are independent — one does not reduce or alter the other.

Worked Example 1:

Flip a coin and roll a die. Find P(Heads and 3).

P(H) = 1/2    P(3) = 1/6

P(H and 3) = (1/2) × (1/6) = /

Verify using the sample space: The sample space has 12 outcomes. How many show (H, 3)?
P(H and 3) from sample space = /. Same?

Misconception

MISCONCEPTION: Independent events become dependent if one is unlikely

Wrong idea: "If I've gotten heads 4 times in a row, the coin now 'remembers' — it owes me a tails."

This is the Gambler's Fallacy — one of the most persistent errors in probability thinking.

Why it fails: A fair coin has no memory. Each flip is independent. The probability of heads on any flip is always 1/2, regardless of what came before. The coin does not "owe" anything.

Verify: P(heads on flip 5, given heads on flips 1–4) = (same as always)

What IS true: Over very many flips, heads and tails will appear about equally often (Law of Large Numbers). But this happens through future flips balancing out — NOT through the coin compensating for its past.

Identifying independent events — key question:

Ask: "Does knowing the outcome of Event A change what's likely for Event B?"

Vocabulary

Guided Notes

Two events are dependent if the outcome of the first event CHANGES the probability of the second.

This most commonly happens when items are selected without replacement — once chosen, they are not returned to the pool.

The Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events:

P(A then B) = P(A) × P(B | A)

P(B | A) = "the probability of B given that A already happened"

The sample space for the second event is because one item was removed.

Worked Example 1 — Cards:

A standard deck has 52 cards. Draw one card and keep it. Then draw another.

Find P(Ace on draw 1 AND Ace on draw 2).

P(Ace on draw 1) = 4/52 = 1/13

After removing one ace: how many aces remain?    How many cards remain?

P(Ace on draw 2 | Ace on draw 1) = /51

P(both aces) = (4/52) × (/51) = /2652 ≈

Misconception

MISCONCEPTION: Removing one item from a large group doesn't really change the probability

Wrong idea: "If there are 1000 cards and I remove one, the probability barely changes — it's still basically the same."

Why it fails: Technically, removing one item does change the probability. For very large populations, the change is so small it is practically negligible — and statisticians do treat large-population draws as approximately independent. But for small populations (8 marbles, 52 cards, 30 students), the change is significant and must be accounted for.

Verify: 5 red out of 8. After removing one red: P(red next) = 4/7 ≈ 0.571. Before: 5/8 = 0.625. Is the difference meaningful?
