## How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

| Label | What it contains |
|---|---|
| **SHOW** | What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat. |
| **SAY** | Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in **[brackets]**. |
| **CUE** | The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩ Check |

Flags appear in callout blocks:
> **FLAG — Misconception:** Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address.
> **FLAG — Pacing:** Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum.
> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** High-yield metacognitive moment — do not skip.

**The screen and the voice carry different things.** What is SHOW stays visual — labels, diagrams, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

## Section 1: What is a Polynomial?
*Learning target: I can name a polynomial, identify its degree, and identify its coefficients and constant term.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: The ten vocabulary terms in a column — Term / Polynomial / Monomial / Binomial / Trinomial / Coefficient / Constant / Degree of a term / Degree of a polynomial / Leading coefficient
**SAY:**
- "Ten terms today. That's a lot — they're all connected, so they build quickly."
- "Don't fill in the table yet. We'll build each definition together as we go."
- "By the end of this section you'll have your own definition and one example for each."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Read the ten terms. Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head."

---

**Beat 2 — What is a polynomial?**
**SHOW:** Board: Write: *POLYNOMIAL = one or more TERMS joined by + or −*. Draw two examples side by side: *3x + 5* and *x² − 4x + 7*
**SAY:**
- "Poly means many. Nomial comes from the Latin for name or term."
- "A polynomial is just one or more terms joined by addition or subtraction."
- "Each piece separated by a + or − is one term."
- "Three things make up a term: the coefficient, the variable, and the exponent."
- Point to 3x + 5: "Two terms. This is a binomial. We'll name the types in a moment."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "A polynomial is an expression made of one or more **[terms]**, joined by addition or subtraction. Each term has a **[coefficient]** and a variable raised to a whole-number **[exponent]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Naming by number of terms**
**SHOW:** Board: Three-column table — draw it live:

| Number of terms | Name | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | monomial | 5x² |
| 2 | binomial | 3x − 7 |
| 3 | trinomial | x² + 2x − 4 |

**SAY:**
- "Three names. One, two, three terms."
- "Mono = one. Bi = two. Tri = three."
- Point to monomial: "One term — monomial. Just 5x²."
- Point to binomial: "Two terms — binomial. 3x minus 7."
- Point to trinomial: "Three terms — trinomial. Three terms, three different variables or exponents."
- "Four or more terms? We just say 'polynomial.' The three names are for the common cases."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the naming table — three names, three examples."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "A polynomial with 3 terms is always a trinomial." Students may believe this without checking whether like terms have been collected. Before a polynomial is named, it must be in simplified form. A polynomial written as 3x² + x + 2x − 5 has four terms as written — but after simplifying to 3x² + 3x − 5, it is a trinomial. **Address this here, briefly:** "Before you name it, always simplify first. An expression might look like a trinomial but simplify to a binomial." You will revisit this more fully in Section 2.

---

**Beat 4 — Degree of a term**
**SHOW:** Board: *DEGREE OF A TERM = the exponent on the variable.* Write three examples vertically:
- 5x²: degree = 2
- 3x: degree = 1 (x = x¹)
- 7: degree = 0 (constant — no variable, or x⁰)

**SAY:**
- "Degree of a term: look at the exponent."
- "5x² — the exponent is 2. Degree is 2."
- "3x — the exponent is 1, even though we don't write it. Degree is 1."
- "7 — a constant. No variable. Exponent is 0. Degree is 0."
- "The degree tells you the power of the variable — nothing else."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the three degree blanks in your notes: 5x² = degree **[2]**, 3x = degree **[1]**, 7 = degree **[0]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "2x³ has degree 2 because the coefficient is 2." This is the highest-frequency error in Section 1. The coefficient and the degree are two different things. Say directly: "The 2 is the coefficient — it tells you how many. The 3 is the degree — it tells you the power. They live in different places. Coefficient is in front. Degree is the exponent." Write on board: *coefficient: 2 | degree: 3* with arrows pointing to the respective parts of 2x³. Do not move on until students can separate these two quantities.

---

**Beat 5 — Degree of a polynomial**
**SHOW:** Board: *DEGREE OF A POLYNOMIAL = the degree of the highest-degree term.*
Write: 4x³ + 2x² − x + 9. Draw arrows to each term with its degree labeled above: 3, 2, 1, 0.
**SAY:**
- "A polynomial has multiple terms, each with its own degree."
- "The degree of the whole polynomial is the highest degree among all its terms."
- "4x³ + 2x² − x + 9 — terms have degrees 3, 2, 1, 0."
- "Highest is 3. The polynomial has degree 3."
- "Leading coefficient: the coefficient of the highest-degree term. Here, that's 4."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the worked example: degrees are **[3, 2, 1, 0]**. Degree of the polynomial = **[3]**. Leading coefficient = **[4]**."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** After filling in the example, SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: why is the degree of the polynomial 3 and not 9 or 4?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: the degree is 3 because ___."

---

**Beat 6 — Label the parts diagram**
**SHOW:** Board: Write: *5x² − 3x + 2*. Draw labeled arrows pointing to each term:
- 5x²: draw lines to "coefficient: 5" and "degree: 2"
- −3x: draw lines to "coefficient: −3" and "degree: 1"
- 2: draw line to "this is a constant"

**SAY:**
- "Let's label the diagram together."
- "5x²: coefficient is 5, degree is 2."
- "−3x: coefficient is −3 — the negative sign travels with it. Degree is 1."
- "2: no variable. This is the constant term. Degree 0."
- "Leading coefficient: 5. It's the coefficient of the highest-degree term."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the label diagram: coefficient of 5x² = **[5]**, degree = **[2]** / coefficient of −3x = **[−3]**, degree = **[1]** / 2 is a **[constant]**."

---

**Beat 7 — Identify practice table**
**SHOW:** Board: The four-row practice table:
- 6x − 1
- x² + 3x − 5
- −8
- 2x⁴ − x + 7

**SAY:**
- "Four expressions. Work through the table. For each one: type, degree, leading coefficient."
- "Type means monomial, binomial, or trinomial."
- "Work quietly first. Then we'll check together."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Complete the practice table now."

*Allow 3 minutes. Then reveal live on board:*
- 6x − 1: binomial, degree 1, leading coefficient 6
- x² + 3x − 5: trinomial, degree 2, leading coefficient 1
- −8: monomial, degree 0, leading coefficient −8
- 2x⁴ − x + 7: trinomial, degree 4, leading coefficient 2

*Watch for: students calling −8 "degree 8." Redirect immediately. Watch for: students calling the leading coefficient of x² + 3x − 5 "zero." The leading coefficient is 1, not zero — when no coefficient is written, it is 1.*

---

### Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Everything closed. Retrieval check — write the answers from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish (4–5 min):**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Find the answer. Fix anything that was wrong or incomplete — use a different colour if you have one."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open your notes. Check and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. Degree 3 — the highest-degree term is 7x³, which has exponent 3. (Watch for "degree 7" — redirect to the coefficient vs. degree distinction.)
2. Leading coefficient is −3. The highest-degree term is −3x², and its coefficient is −3. (Watch for students writing 3 without the negative.)
3. The error: 5x + 3 has degree 1, not 5. The degree is the exponent on x (which is 1), not the coefficient (which is 5). Correct answer: degree 1, a linear binomial.
4. Any trinomial with degree 2 — for example: x² + 3x − 1, or 2x² − x + 5. Both terms must have unlike variable parts; the highest exponent must be 2.
5. Stretch — 12 is a polynomial (a monomial of degree 0 — a constant). 4/x is NOT a polynomial — the variable is in the denominator, which means the exponent on x is −1 (a negative integer), and polynomials require whole-number exponents only.

*Q5 is the stretch — if students finish early, prompt: "Look at the exponent on the variable in each expression. Does a polynomial allow negative exponents?"*

## Section 2: Like Terms
*Learning target: I can identify like terms in a polynomial and combine them correctly.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

*Algebra tiles recommended: use x²-tiles, x-tiles, and unit tiles to physically group like terms. Tiles are particularly powerful here — students can see that you cannot physically combine an x²-tile and an x-tile because they are different shapes.*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocab terms — Like terms / Unlike terms / Simplify
**SAY:**
- "Three terms today. These carry through every remaining section."
- "Don't fill them in yet — we'll build them as we go."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Read the three terms. Predict a definition for 'like terms' before we discuss."

---

**Beat 2 — What makes terms 'like'?**
**SHOW:** Board: *LIKE TERMS = same variable raised to the same exponent.* Write two columns side by side:

Like:          | Unlike:
3x² and −7x²  | 4x and 4x²
6 and −2      | 5x and 3y

**SAY:**
- "Like terms share the same variable AND the same exponent."
- "3x² and −7x² — both have x². The coefficients are different. That's allowed. They're like terms."
- "4x and 4x² — both have the variable x, but the exponents are different. Not like."
- "5x and 3y — different variables entirely. Not like."
- "6 and −2 — both constants. No variable. They're like terms."
- "The coefficient doesn't determine whether terms are 'like.' Only the variable part does."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Like terms share the same **[variable]** raised to the same **[exponent]**. Coefficients can differ."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "3x + 4x² = 7x² (or 7x³)." Students will attempt to combine unlike terms, either because they lose track of the exponent or because they see two terms and assume they must combine. This error produces either a wrong coefficient or a wrong exponent. **Pre-empt explicitly:** "Here's what I see on every test: a student combines 3x + 4x² and writes 7x². Or 7x³. Both are wrong. You cannot combine them — they represent different quantities, as different as apples and oranges. 3x means 'three x's.' 4x² means 'four x-squares.' They measure different things." If algebra tiles are available, hold up an x-tile and an x²-tile side by side: "These are literally different shapes. You cannot stack them."

---

**Beat 3 — How to combine like terms**
**SHOW:** Board: *Add or subtract the COEFFICIENTS only. The variable part stays unchanged.*
Write: 3x² + 7x² = (3 + 7)x² = 10x²
**SAY:**
- "When you combine like terms, the variable part — the x² — does not change."
- "You only add the coefficients."
- "3x² + 7x² = 10x². Not 10x⁴. Not 10x. The x² stays."
- "Think of it as: 3 groups of x² plus 7 groups of x² equals 10 groups of x²."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "To combine like terms: add or subtract the **[coefficients]** only. The variable part stays the same. 3x² + 7x² = **[10x²]**."

---

**Beat 4 — Like terms comparison table**
**SHOW:** Board: The like-terms table:

| Pair | Like terms? | Why? |
|---|---|---|
| 3x² and −7x² | Yes | Both have x² |
| 4x and 4x² | No | Different exponents |
| 5x and 3y | No | Different variables |
| 6 and −2 | Yes | Both constants |

**SAY:**
- "Let's work through the table together — fill in the 'Why?' as we go."
- "Row 1: both have x²? Same variable, same exponent. Like."
- "Row 2: same variable x, but one has exponent 1 and the other has exponent 2. Different exponents. Not like."
- "Row 3: x and y are different variables. Not like."
- "Row 4: constants — no variable at all, both degree 0. Like."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the Why? column: row 1 — **[both have x²]** / row 2 — **[different exponents]** / row 3 — **[different variables]** / row 4 — **[both constants]**."

---

**Beat 5 — Algebra tile connection**
**SHOW:** Board (or physical tiles if available): Draw or display: x²-tile (large square), x-tile (long rectangle), 1-tile (small square). Label each shape.
**SAY:**
- "This is why algebra tiles work for like terms."
- "Each tile shape represents a different kind of term — x², x, or 1."
- "You can only physically combine tiles that are the same shape."
- "An x²-tile and an x-tile are different shapes. You cannot stack them. They stay separate."
- "This is not a rule someone invented arbitrarily — it follows from what the tiles physically represent."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Only tiles of the same **[shape]** and **[size]** can be combined. An x²-tile and an x-tile are different shapes — they cannot stack."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 3 in your own words: when you simplify 9 − 3x + 2x² − 5 + x, what do you look for first, and why?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: I start by looking for ___ because ___."

---

### Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. I want you to identify like terms and combine them — from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish:**
**SAY:** "Open your notes. Check and correct."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. 4x and −4x: yes, like terms — same variable x, same exponent 1 (coefficients are 4 and −4; doesn't matter). 4x and 4x²: no — same variable but different exponents (1 vs. 2).
2. Simplify: 6x² − 2x + 3x² + x − 4 → combine x²-terms: 6x² + 3x² = 9x². Combine x-terms: −2x + x = −x. Constant: −4. Answer: **9x² − x − 4**.
3. The student combined 5x + 3 by adding the coefficient 5 with the constant 3. These are unlike terms — one has the variable x (degree 1), the other is a constant (degree 0). You cannot add them.
4. Any valid answer — for example: 3x² + x − x + 3. Or: x² + x² + 2x + 3 − 2x. Many valid options; the key is that when collected, four terms reduce to 2x² + 3.
5. Stretch — 3x and 3y are NOT like terms. Like terms require the same variable, not just the same coefficient. The variable in 3x is x; in 3y it is y. They represent different unknown quantities and cannot be combined.

## Section 3: Adding Polynomials
*Learning target: I can add two or more polynomials by combining like terms.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Two vocab terms — Sum / Standard form
**SAY:**
- "Two new terms today. Standard form comes up in every section from here on — learn it now."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Predict: what do you think 'standard form' means for a polynomial?"

---

**Beat 2 — Standard form**
**SHOW:** Board: *STANDARD FORM = terms written from highest degree to lowest, left to right.*
Write: −x + 4 + 3x² → standard form: 3x² − x + 4
**SAY:**
- "Standard form is the agreed-upon way to write a polynomial: highest degree first, descending."
- "3x² − x + 4 is in standard form. The degrees go: 2, 1, 0."
- "If you write it as −x + 4 + 3x², that's the same polynomial, just not in standard form."
- "Always re-write in standard form as your final step."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Standard form = terms written from **[highest]** degree to lowest, left to right."

---

**Beat 3 — Steps to add polynomials**
**SHOW:** Board: Three numbered steps:
1. Remove the brackets (addition does not change any signs)
2. Identify like terms
3. Combine like terms, write in standard form

**SAY:**
- "Adding polynomials: three steps."
- "Step 1 — remove brackets. When you add, the bracket comes off cleanly. Nothing changes."
- "Step 2 — find your like terms. Colour-code them if it helps: circle all x²-terms in one colour, x-terms in another."
- "Step 3 — combine and write in standard form. Highest degree first."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Three steps: remove **[brackets]** / identify **[like terms]** / combine and write in **[standard]** form."

---

**Beat 4 — Horizontal method worked example**
**SHOW:** Board: Work through this live, step by step:

(3x² + 2x − 1) + (x² − 5x + 4)
= 3x² + 2x − 1 + x² − 5x + 4    ← remove brackets
= 3x² + x² + 2x − 5x − 1 + 4    ← group like terms
= 4x² − 3x + 3                   ← simplified

**SAY:**
- "Horizontal method — everything stays on one line."
- "Step 1: the brackets come off. No sign changes — this is addition."
- "Step 2: group like terms. I'll circle x²-terms together, x-terms together, constants together."
- "Step 3: 3x² + x² = 4x². 2x − 5x = −3x. −1 + 4 = 3."
- "Final answer: 4x² − 3x + 3. That's in standard form — degree 2, then 1, then 0."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Work through the horizontal example: remove brackets = **[3x² + 2x − 1 + x² − 5x + 4]** / group like terms = **[3x² + x² + 2x − 5x − 1 + 4]** / simplified = **[4x² − 3x + 3]**."

---

**Beat 5 — Vertical method**
**SHOW:** Board: The same example, vertical format:

  3x² + 2x − 1
+ x² − 5x + 4
─────────────
  4x² − 3x + 3

**SAY:**
- "Vertical method: line up like terms in columns. One column per variable power."
- "x² column: 3x² + x² = 4x²."
- "x column: 2x + (−5x) = −3x."
- "Constant column: −1 + 4 = 3."
- "Vertical method is useful when you have three or more polynomials to add."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the vertical example answer: **[4x²]**, **[−3x]**, **[3]**."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: when you removed the brackets and then grouped, what exactly were you looking for, and how did you know which terms to group together?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: I grouped the terms by ___."

---

**Beat 6 — Alberta context**
**SHOW:** Board: "Two field sections in the Lethbridge area have widths (2x + 3) m and (x − 1) m. What is the total width?"
**SAY:**
- "This is a genuine context — the total width is the sum of both widths."
- "Total width = (2x + 3) + (x − 1)"
- "Remove brackets: 2x + 3 + x − 1"
- "Combine like terms: 3x + 2"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Total width = **[3x + 2]** m."

---

### Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Add the polynomials — all steps, from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish:**
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. (4x² − x + 2) + (−x² + 3x − 5): remove brackets → 4x² − x + 2 − x² + 3x − 5. Combine: **3x² + 2x − 3**.
2. (x² + 2x) + (3x − 4) + (−x² + 1): remove brackets → x² + 2x + 3x − 4 − x² + 1. Combine x²: x² − x² = 0. Combine x: 2x + 3x = 5x. Constants: −4 + 1 = −3. Answer: **5x − 3**.
3. Two field sections with lengths (5x + 2) m and (2x − 3) m. Total: (5x + 2) + (2x − 3) = 5x + 2 + 2x − 3 = **7x − 1** m.
4. Adding polynomials never requires sign changes because the bracket only separates terms — it has no multiplying effect on addition. (Subtraction is different; this sets up Section 4.)
5. Stretch — (ax² + 3x − 1) + (2x² − bx + 4) = 5x² + x + 3. Matching x²-terms: a + 2 = 5, so a = 3. Matching x-terms: 3 − b = 1, so b = 2. Answer: **a = 3, b = 2**.

## Section 4: Subtracting Polynomials
*Learning target: I can subtract polynomials by distributing the negative sign and combining like terms.*
*Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocab terms — Difference / Opposite (of a polynomial) / Distribute
**SAY:**
- "Three terms. 'Distribute' is the most important one in this unit — it will come up again in Sections 5 and 6."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Where have you seen the word 'distribute' in math before? Keep it in your head."

---

**Beat 2 — Opposite of a polynomial**
**SHOW:** Board: *OPPOSITE of a polynomial = change the sign of EVERY term.*
Write: Opposite of (2x² − 3x + 1) = −2x² + 3x − 1
**SAY:**
- "The opposite of a polynomial means flipping every sign."
- "Every single term. Every one."
- "Opposite of (2x² − 3x + 1): flip 2x² to −2x², flip −3x to +3x, flip +1 to −1."
- "Result: −2x² + 3x − 1."
- "This is the same as multiplying the entire polynomial by −1."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Subtracting a polynomial means adding its **[opposite]**. To find the opposite, change the sign of **[every term]**. Opposite of (2x² − 3x + 1) = **[−2x² + 3x − 1]**."

---

**Beat 3 — The sign-distribution error**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw a large box labeled "DANGER ZONE." Inside: −(2x² − x + 6). Below: Wrong: −2x² − x + 6. Below that: Correct: −2x² + x − 6.
**SAY:**
- "Here is the most common error in this section — and it appears on tests every year."
- "When you distribute the negative, it multiplies EVERY term."
- "−(2x² − x + 6): distribute to 2x² → −2x². Distribute to −x → +x. Distribute to +6 → −6."
- "Wrong version: students flip the first term but forget the others. Every sign. Every time."
- "Write the correct version in the misconception box in your notes."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Correct: −(2x² − x + 6) = **[−2x² + x − 6]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** Students write −(2x² − x + 6) = −2x² − x + 6 by distributing the negative to the first term only. The middle term's sign does not change, but it should. This error is nearly universal and survives through to Grade 10 if not addressed explicitly. Use the language "every term, no exceptions" and have students physically circle each term as they flip it.

---

**Beat 4 — Worked example: distribute the negative sign**
**SHOW:** Board: Work through this live:

(5x² + 3x − 4) − (2x² − x + 6)
= 5x² + 3x − 4 − 2x² + x − 6      ← distribute: every sign in (2x² − x + 6) flips
= 5x² − 2x² + 3x + x − 4 − 6      ← group like terms
= 3x² + 4x − 10                     ← simplified

**SAY:**
- "Step 1: distribute the negative to every term inside the bracket."
- "2x² becomes −2x². −x becomes +x. +6 becomes −6."
- "Now it's an addition problem. The bracket is gone."
- "Step 2: group like terms. Combine. Write in standard form."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the distribute line: **[−]** 2x² **[+]** x **[−]** 6 / simplified: **[3x² + 4x − 10]**."

---

**Beat 5 — Vertical method**
**SHOW:** Board: Vertical layout — flip signs first, then add:

  5x² + 3x − 4
− 2x² −  x + 6     ← flip all signs here first → −2x² + x − 6
─────────────────
  3x² + 4x − 10

**SAY:**
- "Vertical method for subtraction: flip every sign in the bottom row first. Then add."
- "Rewrite the bottom row with flipped signs. Then it's just column addition."
- "Same answer: 3x² + 4x − 10."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the vertical method answer: **[3x²]**, **[4x]**, **[−10]**."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 1 in your own words: what did we do with the negative sign, and why did every term's sign change?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: we distributed the negative because ___."

---

**Beat 6 — Alberta context**
**SHOW:** Board: "Total floor area of a Fort McMurray building: (6x² + 4x − 2) m². A section of (2x² − x + 3) m² is removed. What is the remaining area?"
**SAY:**
- "Subtract the removed section from the total."
- "(6x² + 4x − 2) − (2x² − x + 3)"
- "Distribute: 6x² + 4x − 2 − 2x² + x − 3"
- "Combine: 4x² + 5x − 5"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Remaining area = **[4x² + 5x − 5]** m²."

---

### Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Subtract the polynomials — all steps. Pay special attention to distributing the negative."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish:**
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. Opposite of (−3x² + 2x − 5): flip every sign → **3x² − 2x + 5**.
2. (4x² + x − 3) − (x² − 2x + 1): distribute → 4x² + x − 3 − x² + 2x − 1. Combine: **3x² + 3x − 4**.
3. (2x + 7) − (5x − 3): distribute → 2x + 7 − 5x + 3. Combine: **−3x + 10**.
4. If only the first term's sign is flipped: the result will be wrong for every remaining term. For example, −(x² − 2x + 1) mistakenly becomes −x² − 2x + 1 instead of −x² + 2x − 1. Every subsequent step carries the error.
5. Stretch — What must be subtracted from (5x² + 3x − 1) to get (2x² + x + 2)? Set up: (5x² + 3x − 1) − P = 2x² + x + 2. Solving: P = (5x² + 3x − 1) − (2x² + x + 2) = **3x² + 2x − 3**.

## Section 5: Multiplying a Monomial by a Polynomial
*Learning target: I can multiply a monomial by a polynomial by distributing and applying exponent rules.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

*Algebra tiles useful here: show 3x(2x + 1) as three rows of (2x + 1) — students can see the physical area being distributed.*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocab terms — Distribute / Expand / Product
**SAY:**
- "Three terms. 'Distribute' you know from Section 4. Same idea — broader application."
- "Expand means to apply the distributive property and remove the brackets."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "You used the distributive property in Section 4. How was it similar to what we're about to do?"

---

**Beat 2 — Exponent rule: multiplying powers with the same base**
**SHOW:** Board: Rule box:
- x^m × x^n = x^(m+n)   ← add the exponents
- x × x = x²            ← not 2x
- x² × x³ = x⁵

**SAY:**
- "Before we can multiply a monomial by a polynomial, we need the exponent rule."
- "When multiplying two powers with the same base, you ADD the exponents."
- "x times x: x¹ × x¹ = x². Not 2x."
- "This is the most important distinction: x + x = 2x, but x × x = x²."
- "Addition combines coefficients. Multiplication combines exponents."
- "x² × x³: add the exponents. 2 + 3 = 5. Answer: x⁵."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Exponent rule: x^m × x^n = x**[m+n]** / x × x = x**[²]** / x² × x³ = x**[⁵]**."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "x × x = 2x." Students confuse addition and multiplication of the same variable. This error makes every monomial-by-polynomial problem wrong. Draw side by side on the board: *x + x = 2x* (adding tiles — two x-tiles, two of them) versus *x × x = x²* (x rows of x-tiles, an area). The area model makes the distinction visual. If algebra tiles are available, physically show: laying two x-tiles end to end (addition = 2x) versus arranging them as rows and columns (multiplication = x²).

---

**Beat 3 — The distributive property**
**SHOW:** Board: *a(b + c) = ab + ac* — the monomial multiplies EVERY term inside.
Then write: 3x(2x² − 4x + 1) — with arrows showing 3x distributing to each of the three terms.
**SAY:**
- "Distributive property: a times (b + c) = a times b, plus a times c."
- "The monomial outside must multiply every term inside the brackets."
- "Three terms inside: 3x multiplies the first, the second, and the third. Every one."
- "Missing even one term is the same error as missing a sign in subtraction — every subsequent step is wrong."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Distributive property: a(b + c) = **[ab + ac]**. To multiply a monomial by a polynomial: multiply the monomial by **[every term]** in the brackets."

---

**Beat 4 — Worked example**
**SHOW:** Board: Work through step by step:

3x(2x² − 4x + 1)
= 3x × 2x²  +  3x × (−4x)  +  3x × 1
= 6x³        −  12x²         +  3x

**SAY:**
- "Three terms in the bracket — three products."
- "First term: 3x × 2x². Coefficients: 3 × 2 = 6. Variables: x × x² = x³. Answer: 6x³."
- "Second term: 3x × (−4x). Coefficients: 3 × −4 = −12. Variables: x × x = x². Answer: −12x²."
- "Third term: 3x × 1. Coefficients: 3 × 1 = 3. Variable stays: x. Answer: 3x."
- "Final: 6x³ − 12x² + 3x. Already in standard form."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the example: **[6x³]** + **[−12x²]** + **[3x]**."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: when you multiplied 3x × 2x², why is the answer 6x³ and not 6x²?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: the exponent becomes 3 because ___."

---

**Beat 5 — Practice: expand step by step**
**SHOW:** Board: 4x(3x² − 2x + 5)
**SAY:**
- "Your turn. Fill in the expanded form step by step."
- "First: write out the three multiplications."
- "Then: calculate each product."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "4x × **[3x²]** + 4x × **[−2x]** + 4x × **[5]** = **[12x³]** + **[−8x²]** + **[20x]**."

*Walk the room. Common errors: 4x × 3x² = 12x² (forgetting x × x² = x³) and 4x × 5 = 20 (dropping the x). Address both when reviewing.*

---

**Beat 6 — Alberta context**
**SHOW:** Board: "Width: 2x m. Length: (x² + 3x − 1) m. Write a simplified expression for the area."
**SAY:**
- "Area = length × width."
- "2x(x² + 3x − 1)"
- "Distribute: 2x × x² = 2x³. 2x × 3x = 6x². 2x × (−1) = −2x."
- "Area = 2x³ + 6x² − 2x"
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Area = 2x(**[x² + 3x − 1]**) = **[2x³ + 6x² − 2x]** m²."

---

### Section 5 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Exponent rule, distributive property, expand — from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish:**
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. x² × x³ = x⁵ (add the exponents: 2 + 3 = 5).
2. 2x(x + 5) = 2x × x + 2x × 5 = **2x² + 10x**.
3. −3x(2x² − x + 4): distribute → −3x × 2x² = −6x³. −3x × (−x) = 3x². −3x × 4 = −12x. Answer: **−6x³ + 3x² − 12x**.
4. Width x m, length (4x − 3) m. Area = x(4x − 3) = **4x² − 3x** m².
5. Stretch — 2x(x + 3) + x(x − 1): expand each bracket → 2x² + 6x + x² − x. Combine like terms: **3x² + 5x**.

## Section 6: Multiplying Two Binomials
*Learning target: I can multiply two binomials using an area model and write the simplified product.*
*Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction*

*Algebra tiles are powerful here: use large x²-tiles, x-tiles, and unit tiles to build the area model physically. Students arranging (x + 3)(x + 2) as a rectangle with tiles will see all four partial products before computing a single number.*

---

**Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup**
**SHOW:** Board: Three vocab terms — Binomial product / Area model / Partial product
**SAY:**
- "Three terms. 'Area model' is the scaffold — once you understand why it works, you won't need the grid every time."
**CUE:** ⊡ Think: "Where have you seen an area model before — in any grade?"

---

**Beat 2 — Four partial products**
**SHOW:** Board: *Every term in the first bracket multiplies every term in the second bracket. Two binomials always produce FOUR partial products.*
Write: (x + 3)(x + 4) → four arrows: x→x, x→4, 3→x, 3→4

**SAY:**
- "When you multiply two binomials, every term in the first bracket must reach every term in the second bracket."
- "Two terms times two terms. How many multiplications? Four."
- "We call each of those four a partial product."
- "The most common error is to multiply only the 'matching' terms — x × x and 3 × 4 — and miss the middle two."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Every term in the first bracket must multiply every term in the **[second]** bracket. Two binomials always produce **[4]** partial products."

> **FLAG — Misconception:** "(x + 2)(x + 3) = x² + 6 — just multiply the numbers." Students multiply the first terms and the last terms, ignoring the cross terms. The area model is specifically designed to prevent this. Every cell in the grid is a partial product. **Before the area model beat, say directly:** "Some students write (x + 2)(x + 3) = x² + 6. They got x by multiplying x × x, and 6 by multiplying 2 × 3. They missed two entire products. The middle terms — 3x and 2x — disappear entirely. The area model won't let you do this because you can see every cell."

---

**Beat 3 — Area model: (x + 3)(x + 4)**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the grid live — label the top row: ×, x, +4. Left column: ×, x, +3. Fill in the four cells:

|   ×   |   x   |  +4   |
|-------|-------|-------|
|   x   |  x²   |  4x   |
|  +3   |  3x   |  12   |

**SAY:**
- "Area model: put the first binomial on the top, the second on the left."
- "Each cell is a product: row header times column header."
- "Top-left: x × x = x²."
- "Top-right: x × 4 = 4x."
- "Bottom-left: 3 × x = 3x."
- "Bottom-right: 3 × 4 = 12."
- "Four partial products: x², 4x, 3x, 12."
- "Now collect like terms: 4x + 3x = 7x."
- "Final answer: x² + 7x + 12."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the four cells: **[x²]**, **[4x]**, **[3x]**, **[12]**. Sum: **[x²]** + **[4x]** + **[3x]** + **[12]**. Simplified: **[x² + 7x + 12]**."

---

**Beat 4 — Second example: (2x + 1)(x + 5)**
**SHOW:** Board: Draw the grid:

|   ×   |   x   |  +5   |
|-------|-------|-------|
|  2x   |  2x²  |  10x  |
|  +1   |   x   |   5   |

**SAY:**
- "Same process. Top: x and +5. Left: 2x and +1."
- "Top-left: 2x × x = 2x²."
- "Top-right: 2x × 5 = 10x."
- "Bottom-left: 1 × x = x."
- "Bottom-right: 1 × 5 = 5."
- "Like terms: 10x + x = 11x."
- "Simplified: 2x² + 11x + 5."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Fill in the four cells: **[2x²]**, **[10x]**, **[x]**, **[5]**. Simplified: **[2x² + 11x + 5]**."

> **FLAG — Self-Explanation:** SAY: "Before moving on — explain Step 3 in your own words: after you found all four partial products, which ones did you combine and why could you combine them?"
> **CUE:** ★ Write: "Write one sentence: I combined 10x and x because ___."

---

**Beat 5 — Alberta context**
**SHOW:** Board: "An Edmonton hockey rink expansion adds a section with length (x + 8) m and width (x + 5) m. What is the area?"
**SAY:**
- "Area = length × width. Expand (x + 8)(x + 5)."
- "Four partial products: x², 5x, 8x, 40."
- "Combine like terms: 5x + 8x = 13x."
- "Area = x² + 13x + 40 m²."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Area = **[x² + 13x + 40]** m²."

---

**Beat 6 — Thread-back: the distributive property in three situations**
**SHOW:** Board: Three bullet points:
- Subtracting polynomials: distributing −1 to every term
- Monomial × polynomial: distributing the monomial to every term
- Binomial × binomial: distributing both terms of the first bracket to both terms of the second

**SAY:**
- "You've now used the distributive property three different ways."
- "Every single time, the rule is the same: every term outside must reach every term inside."
- "In subtraction, the 'outside' is −1. In monomial multiplication, it's a variable term. In binomial multiplication, it's two terms."
- "One rule. Three applications."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Write your answer to the connection prompt: the one rule that applies in all three cases is **[the distributive property — every term outside multiplies every term inside]**."

---

### Section 6 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

**Before releasing:**
**SAY:** "Close your notes. Four partial products. Area model. Expand and simplify — from memory."
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now."

**After students finish:**
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Open and correct now."

**Answer guide:**
1. Two binomials always produce **4** partial products.
2. (x + 2)(x + 7): partial products → x², 7x, 2x, 14. Like terms: 7x + 2x = 9x. Answer: **x² + 9x + 14**.
3. (2x − 1)(x + 3): partial products → 2x², 6x, −x, −3. Like terms: 6x − x = 5x. Answer: **2x² + 5x − 3**.
4. Student got x² + 8 from (x + 4)(x + 2) — they multiplied x × x = x² and 4 × 2 = 8, skipping the two middle products (4x and 2x). Correct: partial products are x², 2x, 4x, 8. Like terms: 2x + 4x = 6x. Correct answer: **x² + 6x + 8**.
5. Stretch — (x + 3)² = (x + 3)(x + 3): partial products → x², 3x, 3x, 9. Like terms: 3x + 3x = 6x. Answer: **x² + 6x + 9**.

## Unit Synthesis
*~20 minutes — notes closed, then compare to pre-knowledge check*

---

**Beat 1 — Set up**
**SHOW:** Board: The six section headers listed — blank lines beside each.
**SAY:**
- "Notes closed. Everything closed."
- "Six sections. I want the single most important idea from each — from memory."
- "This is not a test. This is you finding out what stuck and what didn't."
- "Write content sentences — not 'polynomials are important' but 'a polynomial is... because...'"
**CUE:** ↩ Check: "Notes closed. Six sections. One sentence each. Go."

*Allow 5–7 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: students who can write clearly about Sections 1 and 2 but struggle with Sections 5 and 6 — distribution fluency is the most commonly unresolved item. Students who write vague sentences about "combining terms" without specifying "like terms" — prompt: "Can you be more specific?"*

---

**Beat 2 — Unit Synthesis Part B — combined problem**
**SHOW:** Board: (3x² + x − 2) − (x² − 4x + 1) + 2x(x + 3)
**SAY:**
- "This problem requires three sections in one."
- "Subtraction: distribute the negative. Then monomial multiplication. Then combine like terms."
- "Work in three columns if it helps — one operation at a time."
- Pause. Let students work silently 4–5 minutes.
- "Let's go through it step by step on the board."

*Board work:*
Step 1 — Subtraction: (3x² + x − 2) − (x² − 4x + 1) = 3x² + x − 2 − x² + 4x − 1 = 2x² + 5x − 3
Step 2 — Expand: 2x(x + 3) = 2x² + 6x
Step 3 — Combine all: (2x² + 5x − 3) + (2x² + 6x) = 4x² + 11x − 3

**CUE:** ★ Write: "Final answer: **[4x² + 11x − 3]**."

---

**Beat 3 — Return to Pre-Knowledge Check**
**SHOW:** Board: *"Turn to page 1. Read what you wrote before the unit started."*
**SAY:**
- "Read it. Don't change it. Don't erase anything."
- "What did you get right? What were you wrong about? What surprised you?"
- "Write your answers at the bottom of the synthesis page."
**CUE:** ★ Write: "Answer the three end-of-unit synthesis questions: right / surprised / biggest misconception — in your own words."

*Allow 3–5 minutes. This is the highest-value metacognitive moment in the package. Students reading their pre-instruction words in their own handwriting is more powerful than any correction you can deliver. Let it land quietly. Many students will have written "3x + 5" in their pre-knowledge check — they knew something but couldn't name it. That recognition is meaningful.*
