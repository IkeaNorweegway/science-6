Retrieval Check — Section 1

QuestionAnswer
1. Univariate vs. bivariateUnivariate: one trait per subject (e.g., test scores). Bivariate: two traits on same subject (e.g., study time AND test score).
2. When to use scatter plotWhen you want to see if/how two variables are related — whether knowing one helps predict the other.
3. Each dot representsOne subject's simultaneous measurements on both variables.
4. Why independent on x-axisThe independent variable is the input — it "drives" the dependent. Convention places the input horizontally.
5. (Stretch) Absences vs. marksAbsences on x-axis (independent — controlled by student). Final mark on y-axis (depends on absences).

BlankAnswer
Correlation: whether two variables change ___together
Outlier: falls far away from the general pattern. An outlier can pull the trend line toward it, making the correlation appear weaker or change its direction slightly. Always note outliers — do not automatically remove them.
Misconception: Correlation ≠ causation. A third variable (confounder) can drive both observed variables. Strong correlation is necessary but NOT sufficient for causation.
Splash pad: Strong positive correlation between temperature and visitors.

Retrieval Check — Section 2

QuestionAnswer
1. Positive vs. negative correlationPositive: both variables increase together. Negative: one increases as the other decreases — inverse relationship.
2. More sleep → less reaction timeNegative correlation
3. Strong vs. weak appearanceStrong: points cluster tightly along a clear line. Weak: points spread widely, trend is hard to see.
4. Correlation ≠ causationA third variable (confounder) may drive both. E.g., hot weather → both ice cream sales AND outdoor activity/drowning — not ice cream causing drowning.
5. (Stretch) Fire stations and firesBoth driven by city size: larger cities have both more fire stations AND more fires. Stations don't cause fires.

Why choose points ON the line (not data points): Data points have measurement error. The line is a smoothed model of the trend — points on the line give the "theoretical" relationship, not noise-affected observations.
Verbal description: "For each 1°C increase in temperature, approximately 10 more visitors are expected."

Retrieval Check — Section 3

QuestionAnswer
1. Four rules for line of best fit(1) Through the middle. (2) Equal points above and below. (3) Follows general direction. (4) No requirement to pass through origin or any data point.
2. Student A vs. B (origin vs. balanced)Student B is more likely correct. Requiring the line to pass through the origin is an unwarranted constraint not justified by the data.
3. Predict at 29°CUsing v≈10t−115: v=10(29)−115=175 visitors (varies by student's line)
4. Slope meaningFor each 1°C increase in temperature, approximately 10 more visitors are expected — it is the rate of change.
5. (Stretch) y=−3x+95Negative correlation (slope is negative). Slope means: for each unit increase in x, y decreases by 3.

ExtrapolationPredicting y for an x-value OUTSIDE the observed data rangePredicting at 40°C when data only goes to 34°C

Unit Synthesis — Answer Key

Part A

QuestionAnswer
1. Univariate vs. bivariateUnivariate: one variable per subject. Bivariate: two variables per subject, analyzed for relationship.
2. Three types, sketchesPositive (rise L→R) / Negative (fall L→R) / None (random scatter)
3. Most important ruleEqual numbers of points above and below, following the general direction.
4. Interpolation vs. extrapolationInterpolation: within range, more reliable. Extrapolation: outside range, less reliable (trend may not continue).
5. Correlation ≠ causationNo. Counter-example: ice cream sales and crime rates both correlate — both driven by summer heat, not each other.

Part B — Study time vs. score

(a) Strong positive correlation expected.

(b) Graph: points rise clearly from (1,52) to (6,90).

(c) Approximate: m≈(90−52)/(6−1)=7.6. b≈44. Equation: y ≈ 7.6x + 44

(d) x=4.5: y=7.6(4.5)+44≈78% — interpolation (inside 1–6 range).

(e) x=10: y=7.6(10)+44=120% — extrapolation, not reliable. Score cannot exceed 100%; the linear model breaks down beyond 100%.
