Math 9 — Statistics and Scatter Plots

Teacher Notes Package

Teaching reference — not a script

How to use this document

Each teaching beat has three parts:

Label What it contains
SHOW What is visible to students — draw on the board or project. One thing per beat.
SAY Key phrases and questions. Not sentences to read — prompts for fluent delivery. Answers to blanks shown in [brackets].
CUE The exact signal for students to write. Four types: ★ Write / ✎ Draw / ⊡ Think / ↩︎ Check

Flags appear in callout blocks: > FLAG — Misconception: Red alert. Students will get this wrong without a direct address. > FLAG — Pacing: Warning for where lessons tend to lose momentum. > FLAG — Connection: Embedded cross-curricular moment — worth lingering on.

The screen and the voice carry different things. What is SHOW stays visual — labels, diagrams, key terms. What is SAY carries the reasoning. Never put your SAY on the screen.

Pre-Knowledge Check

~10 minutes — before any instruction

Beat 1 — Opening SHOW: Board: “What is the difference between a bar graph and a scatter plot?” (That’s it — nothing else on the board.) SAY: - “Before we start the unit, I want to know what’s already in your head.” - “This is not a test. There are no marks. I’m not going to correct it today.” - “Write honestly. You will come back to this page at the end of the unit — do not erase your answers.” - “Three questions — just write what you think right now, even if it is a guess.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes to page 1. You have 5 minutes — answer all three questions. Go.”

FLAG — PacingDo not read student responses aloud or comment on correctness. Circulate and watch for: (a) students who already understand what a scatter plot shows (bivariate data, looking for relationships), (b) students who say correlation means causation (very common — note who says this, because it’s your highest-priority misconception to unpack in Section 2). The pre-knowledge check loses its value if students hear you reward certain answers and calibrate to that.

Section 1: Types of Data and Graphs

Learning target: I can identify when to use a scatter plot versus other graph types, and explain what types of questions scatter plots can answer that other graphs cannot. Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: The five vocabulary terms in a column — Univariate data / Bivariate data / Scatter plot / Variable / Data point SAY: - “Before we go into the notes — these five terms are on your vocab table.” - “Don’t fill them in yet. We’ll build them as we go.” - “By the end of this section you will have your own definition and one example for each.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Read the five terms. Which one do you think you already know? Keep it in your head.”

Beat 2 — Univariate data SHOW: Board: UNIVARIATE = ONE variable. Write examples: heights of students, test scores, monthly temperatures. SAY: - “Univariate — prefix uni, meaning one.” - “Univariate data is about one variable. You’re measuring one thing about each subject.” - “Heights of all students in this class — that’s one variable.” - “What are the best graph types for univariate data? Bar graph for categories. Line graph for change over time. Histogram for numerical data grouped into intervals.” - Pause 3 seconds. CUE: ★ Write: “Univariate data: data about [one] variable. Line graph is used for [change over time].”

Beat 3 — Bivariate data SHOW: Board: BIVARIATE = TWO variables on the same subject. Write examples: height AND shoe size, study time AND test score, temperature AND ice cream sales. SAY: - “Bivariate — bi, meaning two.” - “Two variables. But not just any two — both measured on the same subject.” - “Height AND shoe size. Same person. That’s bivariate.” - “Temperature AND ice cream sales. Same day. That’s bivariate.” - “The key question bivariate data answers: does knowing one variable help you predict the other?” CUE: ★ Write: “Bivariate data: data about [two] variables measured on the same subject. Best graph: [scatter plot].”

Beat 4 — What is a scatter plot? SHOW: Board: Draw the basic scatter plot axes diagram — x-axis labeled “independent variable,” y-axis labeled “dependent variable,” six scattered dots. SAY: - “A scatter plot lets you see if there is a relationship between two variables.” - “Each dot is one subject — one person, one day, one city.” - “That dot captures both measurements at once.” - “Convention: independent variable on the x-axis. Dependent variable on the y-axis.” - “Independent — the one you think might be driving the relationship. Dependent — the one that might be responding.” CUE: ★ Write: “Each dot = [one] data point. Independent variable: [x]-axis. Dependent variable: [y]-axis.”

FLAG — PacingStudents sometimes confuse independent and dependent variables with the slope/intercept usage from linear relations. A quick bridge: “Same convention — the x-axis variable is the input, y is the output. You’ve seen this all year.”

Beat 5 — Plotting from a table of values SHOW: Board: Draw the Calgary splash pad table (Temperature °C vs. Visitors). Leave it up while students plot. SAY: - “Here is real data — eight temperature/visitor pairs from a Calgary splash pad.” - “Your job: plot each pair as a point on the grid in your notes.” - “Temperature goes on the x-axis. Visitors goes on the y-axis.” - “Before you start — check the scale. What does each grid line represent on each axis?” - Pause for students to examine their grid. - “Plot all eight points. Then describe in one sentence what you notice.” CUE: ✎ Draw: “Plot all eight data points on your grid now.”

Circulate while students plot. Watch for: reversed axes (temperature on y); points plotted at the wrong scale (common if students do not check the axis intervals first); missing points. A quick board check-plot of the first point (20, 85) helps orient everyone before they continue.

FLAG — PacingSome students will attempt to connect the dots with a line segment rather than leaving them as individual points. Address immediately: “These are scatter plots — not line graphs. Each dot stands alone. Do not connect them.”

Beat 6 — Describe the pattern SHOW: Board: Completed scatter plot with all eight points (draw it quickly alongside students). Do not draw the line of best fit — that’s Section 3. SAY: - “What do you notice? What is the pattern?” - “Don’t use the word ‘correlation’ yet — just describe what you see.” - “As temperature increases, what happens to the number of visitors?” - Pause for student responses. - “Right — they increase together. The points rise as you move left to right.” CUE: ★ Write: “Describe the pattern you see: as temperature increases, visitors [increase / also increase].”

Beat 7 — Vocabulary table fill-in SHOW: Board: The five vocabulary terms still in the column. SAY: - “Now go back to your vocabulary table.” - “Fill in definitions in your own words — not the textbook definition. Something you could explain to a Grade 7 student.” - “Add one example you thought of yourself — not one from the notes.” CUE: ★ Write: “Complete your vocab table now — own words, own examples.”

Allow 3–4 minutes. Circulate. Watch for: students writing ‘data point = a point on the graph’ (too thin — redirect to ‘one subject’s measurements on both variables’); students leaving ‘variable’ blank.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed. I’m going to ask you some questions — write the answers from memory.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish (4–5 min): SAY: “Open your notes. Find the answer. Fix anything that was wrong or incomplete — use a different colour if you have one.” CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct now.”

Answer guide: 1. Univariate = one variable per subject (e.g., test scores). Bivariate = two variables per subject measured together (e.g., study time AND test score). 2. When you have two variables measured on the same subjects and want to see if they are related — if knowing one might help predict the other. 3. Each point represents one subject’s measurements on both variables — one person, one day, etc. 4. Convention: the independent variable (the “input” or driver) goes on the x-axis; this is standard across all bivariate graphs and matches the linear relations convention students already know. 5. (Stretch) Absences on the x-axis (independent — the variable you think might be influencing the outcome); final mark on the y-axis (dependent — what you are trying to predict). Rationale: you’re asking whether absences predict marks, not the other way around.

Section 2: Correlation and Scatter Plots

Learning target: I can identify and describe the type and strength of correlation in a scatter plot. Timing: ~20–25 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Six vocabulary terms — Correlation / Positive correlation / Negative correlation / No correlation / Strong correlation / Weak correlation SAY: - “Six terms. Same approach — don’t fill them in yet.” - “Think about the word ‘correlation.’ It comes from the Latin for ‘together’ and ‘relation.’” - “What do you think it means, just from the word?” CUE: ⊡ Think: “What does correlation mean? Hold your answer in your head for the next two minutes.”

Beat 2 — Correlation defined SHOW: Board: CORRELATION = whether two variables tend to change together SAY: - “Correlation describes whether two variables tend to change together.” - “Tend to — not always, not perfectly. Tend to.” - Pause 3 seconds. CUE: ★ Write: “Correlation describes whether two variables tend to change [together].”

Beat 3 — Types of correlation with diagrams SHOW: Board: Draw all four scatter patterns side by side, roughly: strong positive (dots rising), strong negative (dots falling), no correlation (dots scattered), weak positive (loose upward trend). Label each. SAY: - “Four patterns — learn to recognize them visually.” - “Positive correlation: both variables increase together. Points rise from left to right. Height and weight — both go up together.” - “Negative correlation: one increases as the other decreases. Points fall from left to right. Hours of TV and test scores — as TV time goes up, scores tend to go down.” - “No correlation: no pattern at all. Shoe size and test scores — knowing one tells you nothing about the other.” - “Weak positive: a general upward trend but with lots of scatter. You can see the direction but the points are spread widely.” CUE: ★ Write: “Sketch all four patterns in your notes (strong positive, strong negative, none, weak positive).”

Allow 2–3 minutes for students to sketch. Confirm by circulating — positive goes up left to right, negative goes down left to right.

Beat 4 — Strength of correlation SHOW: Board: Draw two scatter plots side by side — strong positive (dots close to diagonal line), moderate positive (more spread). Label: Strong = points close to a straight line. Moderate = general pattern with scatter. Weak = barely a direction. SAY: - “Strength tells you how tightly the points cluster around the trend.” - “Strong: you could nearly draw the line of best fit by connecting the dots. Clear, consistent relationship.” - “Moderate: you can see the direction, but more scatter.” - “Weak: you can just barely see a direction. Hard to predict from one variable.” CUE: ★ Write: “Strong correlation: points [close to a straight line]. Weak correlation: points [loosely suggest a direction].”

Beat 5 — Precise language for describing correlation SHOW: Board: Description template: [strength] + [direction] + correlation. Example: “strong positive correlation.” SAY: - “When you describe correlation on a test or in written work, you always give both — direction AND strength.” - “Not just ‘positive.’ Not just ‘strong.’ Both.” - “Look back at the scatter plot you made in Section 1 — temperature vs. visitors.” - “What direction? What strength?” - Wait for student responses. - “Correct — strong positive. Now write the full description.” CUE: ★ Write: “Temperature vs. visitors: [strong] positive correlation.”

Beat 6 — Outliers SHOW: Board: Draw a scatter plot with a clear positive trend and one dot far off the pattern. Label it: outlier. SAY: - “An outlier is a point that falls far away from the general pattern.” - “Outliers can happen for two reasons: measurement error, or a genuinely unusual case.” - “Unusual case example: in a dataset of Edmonton daily temperatures vs. heating costs, one day has very low heating cost despite cold temperature — maybe the furnace was off because of maintenance.” - “Do NOT automatically remove outliers. Investigate first.” - “How does an outlier affect your correlation description? It can make a strong correlation look weaker.” CUE: ★ Write: “An outlier is a point that falls [far away] from the general pattern. Should be noted but not [automatically removed].”

Beat 7 — The most important FLAG in this unit: Correlation is not causation

FLAG — Misconception (HIGHEST PRIORITY)This is the most durable and dangerous misconception in the unit. Spend a full 3–4 minutes here. Do not compress this beat.

SHOW: Board: Draw two graphs side by side — both with strong positive correlation. Label one: Ice cream sales vs. month. Label the other: Drowning rates vs. month. Then write below both: Does ice cream cause drowning? SAY: - “Here is the single most important idea in this entire unit.” - “Both of these scatter plots show strong positive correlation. Ice cream sales go up in summer. Drowning rates go up in summer.” - “Does ice cream cause drowning?” - Pause 4–5 seconds. Let students react. - “Obviously not. So what’s going on?” - Pause. Wait for a student to say ‘summer’ or ‘weather.’ - “Exactly. Hot weather is the third variable. Hot weather drives both. When it’s hot, more people buy ice cream. When it’s hot, more people swim, and some drown.” - “Neither variable causes the other. A third variable — a confounder — is driving both.” - “Correlation tells you: these two things move together. It does NOT tell you why.” - “To claim causation, you need a mechanism — a direct explanation of how one causes the other — and you need to rule out confounders. That usually requires a controlled experiment.” - “Correlation is necessary but NOT sufficient for causation.” CUE: ★ Write: “Correlation means two variables move together. It does NOT mean one [causes] the other. A third variable may be driving both.”

After writing: “Can anyone give me another example? Something two things that go up together but neither causes the other?” Some good answers students give: hospitals and doctors (larger cities have more of both — driven by population); shoe size and reading ability in children (both increase with age); Nicolas Cage films per year and pool drownings (a famous absurd correlation dataset). Credit any reasonable example.

Beat 8 — Apply to the Section 1 data SHOW: Board: The temperature vs. visitors scatter plot. SAY: - “One more practice — describe the full correlation for our splash pad data.” - “Direction. Strength. And: does temperature cause more visitors, or is this correlation only?” - “Think about it — what would you need to know to claim causation?” CUE: ★ Write: “Full description: [strong positive correlation]. This is correlation — temperature may influence visitors but other factors (school schedule, holidays, marketing) also contribute.”

Beat 9 — Vocabulary table fill-in SHOW: Board: The six vocabulary terms. SAY: - “Now fill in your vocabulary table — own definitions, own examples.” - “For ‘correlation’: give a definition and an example pair of variables.” - “For positive and negative: write what the scatter plot looks like, not just the direction.” CUE: ★ Write: “Complete your vocab table — all six terms.”

Allow 3–4 minutes. Watch for: students defining strong/weak by the word alone without referencing the visual appearance of the scatter plot.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Everything closed.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Correlation types, strength, and the big misconception. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct.”

Answer guide: 1. Positive correlation: both variables increase together; points rise left to right. Negative correlation: one increases as the other decreases; points fall left to right. 2. Negative correlation — as sleep increases, reaction time decreases; points fall from left to right. 3. Strong = points cluster closely around a straight-line trend; you could almost connect them. Weak = points only vaguely suggest a direction; lots of scatter, hard to predict from one variable. 4. Correlation does not prove causation because a third variable may be driving both, or the relationship may be coincidental. Example: ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer — neither causes the other; hot weather drives both. (Accept any reasonable example with a plausible third variable.) 5. (Stretch) No — larger cities have more fires because they have more buildings and people. More fires require more fire stations. Population is the third variable driving both. Fire stations do not cause fires; they respond to them.

Section 3: Line of Best Fit

Learning target: I can draw a line of best fit by inspection, write its equation, and use it to make predictions. Timing: ~25 minutes direct instruction

Bring to class: ruler for each student (or shared). Two-colour markers on the board — one for data points, one for the line of best fit.

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Four terms — Line of best fit / By inspection / Trend line / Equation of the line of best fit SAY: - “Four terms. Hold off on filling them in — we’ll build them through the example.” - “‘By inspection’ — what does inspection mean? Looking carefully. We’re drawing the line by looking carefully at the data, not by computing it.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “What do you think ‘by inspection’ means for drawing a line? Hold it.”

Beat 2 — What is the line of best fit? SHOW: Board: Draw a scatter plot with a clear positive trend. Then draw the line of best fit through the middle. Label it: Line of best fit (trend line). SAY: - “The line of best fit is a straight line drawn to best represent the overall trend of the data.” - “It is NOT a connect-the-dots. It is NOT drawn through the most points.” - “It is a model — a summary of the trend — that lets you make predictions.” - “We only draw it when there IS a correlation — positive or negative. If there is no correlation, there is no trend to represent.” CUE: ★ Write: “Line of best fit: a straight line drawn to represent the [overall trend] of points. Used when there is a [positive] or [negative] correlation.”

Beat 3 — Four rules for drawing by inspection SHOW: Board: Four rules listed (build them one at a time as you SAY each): 1. The line passes through the middle of the data 2. Approximately equal numbers of points above and below the line 3. The line follows the general direction (slope) of the data 4. The line does NOT need to pass through the origin or any particular point SAY: - “Rule 1: pass through the middle — not the top, not the bottom. The middle of the whole cluster.” - “Rule 2: balance. Count the points above. Count the points below. They should be roughly equal. If you have 6 above and 1 below, your line is too low.” - “Rule 3: the line’s slope should match the slope of the data. Rising data → rising line. Falling data → falling line.” - “Rule 4 — this one is new for a lot of students.” - Pause. - “The line does NOT need to pass through the origin. Unless your data strongly suggests it does, your line will usually not start at (0, 0). It will cross the y-axis wherever the trend actually leads.” CUE: ★ Write: “Rules: (1) passes [through the middle] / (2) [equal] points above and below / (3) follows the general [direction / slope] / (4) does NOT need to pass through the [origin].”

FLAG — MisconceptionMany students believe the line of best fit must pass through the origin (0, 0). This is a deeply held intuition — likely because the y = mx equations from linear relations often do pass through the origin. Address it directly after rule 4. SAY: “Who was thinking the line has to start at zero? That’s a really common assumption. Ask yourself: does our splash pad data make sense at 0°C? Would anyone go to a splash pad at 0°C? No — the linear trend from our data range doesn’t extend to the origin. The line starts where the data tells it to.”

Beat 4 — Worked example: drawing the line and finding the equation SHOW: Board: Draw the temperature vs. visitors scatter plot with all eight points. Use one colour for the dots. SAY: - “Back to our Calgary splash pad data. Let’s draw the line of best fit and find its equation.” - “Step 1: draw the line. Get your ruler.” - Demonstrate on the board — place the ruler roughly through the middle, adjust until roughly equal dots on each side, draw the line in a second colour. CUE: ✎ Draw: “Draw your line of best fit on the scatter plot in your notes. Use a ruler. Check: equal points above and below.”

Allow 2 minutes. Circulate and check: (a) is the line straight? (b) is it balanced above and below? (c) does it follow the rising trend? Common error: students draw the line starting from the lowest point and ending at the highest — this often gives the right slope but passes through actual data points rather than the middle. Redirect.

SHOW: Board: Step 2 — pick two points ON the line (not data points). Mark them with an X. Example: (20, 90) and (34, 230). These are approximations from the line, not data. SAY: - “Step 2: choose two points on the LINE — not data points.” - “Why? Because we want the equation of the line, not an equation that connects two actual measurements. The line of best fit doesn’t pass through most data points exactly.” - “I chose (20, 90) and (34, 230) from my line. Yours may be slightly different — that’s fine. The line is drawn by inspection, so reasonable variations are expected.” CUE: ★ Write: “Point 1: (20, [~90])    Point 2: (34, [~230]). These are points ON the line, not data points.”

Note: the exact values here will depend on where each student drew their line. The key is that students pick points that lie on their own line. Accept any reasonable pair that lies on the trend.

SHOW: Board: - Step 3: slope calculation - m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁) = (230 − 90) / (34 − 20) = 140 / 14 = 10 - Step 4: y-intercept - 90 = 10(20) + b → 90 = 200 + b → b = −110 - Step 5: equation - v = 10t − 110

SAY: - “Step 3: calculate the slope. Rise over run. Same formula as linear relations — nothing new.” - “Change in y over change in x. 230 minus 90 is 140. 34 minus 20 is 14. Slope is 10.” - “What does slope 10 mean in this context? For each 1-degree increase in temperature, we predict about 10 more visitors.” - Pause 3 seconds. - “Step 4: y-intercept. Substitute one of your points into y = mx + b. Solve for b.” - “90 = 10 times 20 plus b. 90 = 200 plus b. b = negative 110.” - “Step 5: write the equation. v equals 10t minus 110.” CUE: ★ Write: “m = [10]. b = [−110]. Equation: v = [10]t + [−110].”

Note to teacher: student answers will vary slightly depending on which points they chose. Accept any equation where m is in the range 8–12 and b is in the range −150 to −70. What matters is the method.

Beat 5 — Self-explanation beat SHOW: Board: “Before moving on — explain Step 2 in your own words: what did we do and why?” SAY: - “Before we move on — explain Step 2 in your own words.” - “Not ‘we picked two points.’ Why do we pick points ON the line rather than actual data points?” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation of Step 2 in the space in your notes. What did we do and why?”

Allow 2 minutes. Look for: ‘the line of best fit doesn’t pass through actual data points — we need points that are ON the line to get the equation of the line.’ Students who write ‘we picked two points and calculated slope’ are describing procedure without explaining reasoning. Prompt: “Why not use the actual data points?”

Beat 6 — The misconception about passing through the most points

FLAG — Misconception“A line of best fit must connect as many dots as possible.”

SHOW: Board: Draw two lines on the same scatter plot — one passing through several actual data points (but tilted off the trend), and one following the true trend but missing most points. SAY: - “Which of these two lines is the better line of best fit?” - Pause for student responses. Most will initially say the one through more points. - “Think about rule 2: equal points above and below. Which line is balanced?” - Point to the one following the trend. “This one. Even though it passes through fewer actual points.” - “The line of best fit is a model of the trend. Its job is to represent the direction and center of the data — not to touch as many dots as possible.” - “A line that passes through many points but angles away from the trend is a worse model.” CUE: ★ Write: “Line of best fit goal: model the [overall trend], not connect the most points. Most points will NOT be exactly on the line.”

Beat 7 — Rule of Four: the line in all four representations SHOW: Board: Draw the four-form table — Graph / Equation / Table / Verbal — and fill in each while talking. SAY: - “We’ve seen this all year in linear relations: a relationship can appear in four forms.” - “Graph: the trend line through the scatter plot.” - “Equation: v = 10t − 110.” - “Table: you can generate predicted values by substituting into the equation. These are NOT data points — they are what the line predicts.” - “Verbal: for each 1°C increase in temperature, the line predicts approximately 10 more visitors.” CUE: ★ Write: “Verbal description: for each [1°C] increase in temperature, approximately [10] more visitors are expected.”

Note: students fill in their own m-value in the verbal description based on their own line.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Drawing a line of best fit, finding its equation, and interpreting the slope.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct.”

Answer guide: 1. (1) Through the middle of the data; (2) approximately equal points above and below; (3) follows the general direction/slope of the data; (4) does not need to pass through the origin or any particular data point. 2. Student B is more likely correct. A line through the origin may not represent the data’s trend; the balance criterion (equal points above and below) is the operative rule. The origin is irrelevant unless the data specifically supports it. 3. Using v = 10t − 110: v = 10(29) − 110 = 290 − 110 = 180 visitors. (Accept answers within a reasonable range if the student’s own line of best fit has a slightly different equation.) 4. The slope represents the rate of change: for each additional degree of temperature, approximately 10 more visitors are expected at the splash pad. 5. (Stretch) The scatter plot likely shows negative correlation (points falling from left to right) because the slope is negative (−3). The slope means: for each one-unit increase in x, y decreases by 3 units. The exact context interpretation depends on what x and y represent.

Section 4: Interpolation and Extrapolation

Learning target: I can use a line of best fit to interpolate and extrapolate values, and explain why extrapolation is less reliable. Timing: ~20 minutes direct instruction

Beat 1 — Vocabulary setup SHOW: Board: Four terms — Interpolation / Extrapolation / Reliability / Domain of the data SAY: - “Four terms. The two hardest words in this section are interpolation and extrapolation.” - “Interpolation: inter — between. Inside the data range.” - “Extrapolation: extra — beyond. Outside the data range.” CUE: ⊡ Think: “Which one sounds more reliable to you just from the meaning of the prefixes?”

Beat 2 — Interpolation defined SHOW: Board: Draw a simple number line showing the data range 20°C to 34°C. Mark a point at 26°C inside the range. Label it: INTERPOLATION — within the range. SAY: - “Interpolation means predicting a y-value for an x-value inside the range of your data.” - “Our data goes from 20°C to 34°C. Predicting visitors at 26°C — that’s inside the range. That’s interpolation.” - “You’re filling in a gap within what you observed.” CUE: ★ Write: “Interpolation: predicting a y-value for an x-value [inside / within] the range of the data.”

Beat 3 — Extrapolation defined SHOW: Board: Same number line. Now mark a point at 40°C outside the range. Label it: EXTRAPOLATION — beyond the range. Draw a question mark after the line extends past 34°C. SAY: - “Extrapolation means predicting a y-value for an x-value outside the range of your data.” - “Predicting visitors at 40°C — that’s beyond our data. That’s extrapolation.” - “You’re extending the pattern beyond what you observed.” - “The question is: how much should you trust that extension?” CUE: ★ Write: “Extrapolation: predicting a y-value for an x-value [outside / beyond] the range of the data.”

Beat 4 — Classification table: fill in together SHOW: Board: Draw the four-row prediction table. Fill it in with students: - 26°C: inside range → interpolation - 22°C: inside range → interpolation - 40°C: outside range → extrapolation - 18°C: outside range → extrapolation (below the range) SAY: - “Let’s classify each prediction. 26°C — inside or outside our data range of 20 to 34?” - “Inside. Interpolation.” - “22°C — inside. Also interpolation.” - “40°C — outside. Extrapolation.” - “18°C — also outside. Below the range. Still extrapolation.” - “Extrapolation can go in either direction — above OR below the data range.” CUE: ★ Write: “Complete the table in your notes — all four rows.”

Beat 5 — Worked example: Interpolation SHOW: Board: - x = 26°C, inside range - v = 10(26) − 110 = 260 − 110 = 150 visitors - “This is interpolation because 26 is inside the data range (20 to 34).” SAY: - “Using our equation v = 10t − 110, predict visitors at 26°C.” - “Substitute 26 for t. 10 times 26 is 260. 260 minus 110 is 150.” - “150 visitors. And this is interpolation — we’re predicting inside the range of the data we collected.” CUE: ★ Write: “v = 10(26) + (your b) = [~150] visitors. This is interpolation because 26 is [inside / within] the data range.”

Beat 6 — Worked example: Extrapolation SHOW: Board: - x = 40°C, outside range - v = 10(40) − 110 = 400 − 110 = 290 visitors - “This is extrapolation because 40 is outside the data range.” SAY: - “Predict visitors at 40°C. Same calculation — substitute 40.” - “10 times 40 is 400. 400 minus 110 is 290.” - “290 visitors. But — and this is critical — this is extrapolation.” - “Ask yourself: is it reasonable that the linear trend continues all the way to 40°C? What else might change at extreme temperatures that we didn’t measure in our data?” - Pause for student responses. - “Exactly. At extremely hot temperatures, attendance might actually drop — heatstroke risk, park closure rules. Our data doesn’t show us that. The model doesn’t know.” CUE: ★ Write: “v = 10(40) + (your b) = [~290] visitors. This is extrapolation because 40 is [outside / beyond] the data range.”

Beat 7 — Why extrapolation is less reliable SHOW: Board: Draw a trend line with dashed extensions going left and right beyond the data. Label the solid section: Data range — interpolation — reliable. Label the dashed sections: Beyond data range — extrapolation — increasingly unreliable. SAY: - “The trend seen in the data might not continue outside the data range.” - “Our linear model is built from what we observed. It is a model of that range. Beyond that range, other factors may change the relationship — and we have no data to tell us.” - “Think about predicting splash pad visitors at −10°C. Our equation gives: 10(−10) − 110 = −210 visitors. Negative visitors. Clearly the model breaks down.” - “That’s the problem with extrapolation: the further from the data range, the more the model is guessing.”

FLAG — Misconception“Extrapolation is as reliable as interpolation.” SAY: “Here’s the exact wrong idea: ‘the equation works for data I measured, so it works just as well beyond those boundaries.’ Why is that wrong?” Pause for responses. Then: “The relationship may only hold within the observed range. Beyond it, additional factors come into play that you did not observe. The linear model is a model of what you measured — not a law of nature.”

CUE: ★ Write: “Extrapolation is less reliable because the trend may not [continue] outside the data range. The further from the data range, the [less reliable / more uncertain] the prediction.”

Beat 8 — Alberta context: Canola yield SHOW: Board: Write the canola yield equation: yield = 0.015 × fertilizer + 1.2. Write: data range = 0–200 kg/hectare. SAY: - “Here’s a context from southern Alberta: a farmer measures canola yield at different fertilizer application rates.” - “The data covers 0 to 200 kg per hectare.” - “Two predictions. You classify each.” - “Part (a): fertilizer = 150 kg/hectare. What is the yield prediction? Is it interpolation or extrapolation?” - Pause for student work. - “150 is inside the range. Interpolation. Yield = 0.015 times 150 plus 1.2 = 2.25 plus 1.2 = 3.45 tonnes per hectare.” - “Part (b): fertilizer = 400 kg/hectare. Classify first.” - “Outside the range. Extrapolation. Less reliable.” - “And why might this extrapolation be particularly unreliable in this context?” - Pause for responses. - “Exactly — too much fertilizer damages crops. The relationship that holds from 0–200 does NOT hold at 400. The yield would probably drop, not keep rising.” CUE: ★ Write: “(a) 150 kg/ha: yield = [3.45] t/ha, [interpolation]. (b) 400 kg/ha: [extrapolation] — unreliable because too much fertilizer [damages / decreases yield].”

Beat 9 — Self-explanation beat SHOW: Board: “Before moving on — explain why extrapolation is less reliable than interpolation in your own words. Use the canola example.” SAY: - “Before we move on — explain why extrapolation is less reliable. Use the canola example, not the splash pad.” - “New context, same concept. If you can apply it to a new situation, you understand it.” CUE: ★ Write: “Write your explanation in the space in your notes. Why is extrapolation less reliable? Use the canola context.”

Allow 2 minutes. Good answers reference: the trend holding within observed range; additional factors outside the range; the model not knowing what happens beyond the data. Thin answers that only state ‘it’s outside the range’ need a follow-up question: ‘And why does outside the range matter?’

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Facilitation

Before releasing: SAY: “Close your notes. Interpolation versus extrapolation — definitions, examples, and why reliability differs.” CUE: ↩︎ Check: “Notes closed. Retrieval check begins now.”

After students finish: CUE: ★ Write: “Open your notes. Check and correct.”

Answer guide: 1. Interpolation: predicting a y-value for an x-value inside the observed data range. Extrapolation: predicting for an x-value outside the observed data range. 2. Interpolation: values of x from 5 to 30 (inside the range). Extrapolation: any value of x below 5 or above 30. 3. y = 4(20) − 10 = 70. This is interpolation — 20 is inside the data range of 5 to 30. 4. Any of: the relationship may not continue the same way outside the data range; factors not observed in the data may change the relationship; the linear model is valid only where it was built. 5. (Stretch) Predicting into 2100 is extreme extrapolation — 95 years beyond the end of the data. The linear trend from 1990–2005 cannot reasonably be expected to continue for nearly a century. The species count would eventually reach zero on the line, but real populations don’t follow linear trends indefinitely — other factors (environmental changes, conservation efforts, stochastic events) intervene. The prediction is mathematically possible but ecologically meaningless.

Comment on reliability.” - “Work through this in order. Do not skip ahead.” CUE: ★ Write: “Complete all five parts of Part B in your notes.”

Allow 8–10 minutes. Expected: (a) strong positive correlation; (b) points rising from lower left to upper right; (c) slope approximately 8–9, y-intercept approximately 40–48, so something like score ≈ 8h + 44; (d) about 80% (within reasonable range); (e) score = 8(10) + 44 = 124% — impossible, clearly a problem, extrapolation is unreliable here because no one can score above 100% and the linear model breaks down. Students who get (e) and recognize the 100% ceiling issue are demonstrating strong contextual reasoning — affirm it.

The correlation/causation question is the one to watch. Students who wrote “yes, more hospitals means hospitals cause more doctors” at the start should see clearly how much their thinking has developed. That recognition is the point.
