Univariate data: data about variable. Describes one trait of each subject.

Examples: heights of students, scores on a test, monthly temperatures.

Best graph types for univariate data:

  • Bar graph: categories or groups
  • Line graph: over time
  • Histogram: continuous numerical data, grouped into intervals

Bivariate data: data about variables measured on the same subject.

Examples: height AND shoe size, study time AND test score, temperature AND ice cream sales.

Describing correlation — use precise language:

A good description includes: direction (positive/negative/none) + strength (strong/moderate/weak/none).

"The scatter plot shows a positive correlation between temperature and splash pad visitors."

Outlier on a scatter plot:

An outlier is a point that falls away from the general pattern.

Outliers:

  • May indicate a measurement error
  • May indicate a genuinely unusual case
  • Should be noted but NOT automatically removed

How does an outlier affect the correlation description?

Misconception

MISCONCEPTION: Correlation means causation

Wrong idea: "If two variables have a strong positive correlation, one must be causing the other."

Why it fails: Two things can change together because a third factor drives both, or because the relationship is coincidental. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both increase in summer — but ice cream does not cause drowning. Both are caused by the third variable: hot weather.

Before claiming cause, you must be able to explain a direct mechanism, rule out third variables (confounders), and ideally run a controlled experiment.

Strong correlation is necessary but not sufficient for causation.

Section 3 — Line of Best Fit

Learning target: I can draw a line of best fit by inspection, write its equation, and use it to make predictions.

Vocabulary

Guided Notes

The line of best fit (trend line) is a straight line drawn to best represent the of points in a scatter plot.

It is used when there is a or correlation (not for no correlation).

Rules for drawing a line of best fit by inspection:

  1. The line passes the middle of the data (not necessarily through any specific point)
  2. Approximately equal numbers of points above and the line
  3. The line should follow the general of the data
  4. The line does NOT need to pass through the or any particular point

Misconception

MISCONCEPTION: The line of best fit must pass through the most points

Wrong idea: "A good line of best fit connects as many dots as possible."

Why it fails: The line of best fit is a model of the trend, not a connect-the-dots. It minimizes the total distance from all points to the line — this means most points will NOT be exactly on it. A line that passes through many points but ignores the overall direction is a worse fit than a line that misses all points but captures the trend.

The goal: equal numbers of points above and below, following the general direction.

Section 4 — Interpolation and Extrapolation

Learning target: I can use a line of best fit to interpolate and extrapolate values, and explain why extrapolation is less reliable.

Vocabulary

TermDefinition in your own wordsOne example
Interpolation
Extrapolation
Reliability
Domain of the data

Guided Notes

Interpolation means predicting a y-value for an x-value the range of the data.

Extrapolation means predicting a y-value for an x-value the range of the data.

Example — using the temperature/visitors line:

Data range: temperatures 20°C to 34°C.

Predictionx-valueInside or outside data range?Interpolation or Extrapolation?Reliability
Visitors at 26°C26
Visitors at 22°C22
Visitors at 40°C40
Visitors at 18°C18

Worked Example — Interpolation:

Using the equation v = 10t + (your b-value), predict visitors at 26°C.

v = 10(26) + = visitors

This is interpolation because 26 is the data range (20 to 34).

Worked Example — Extrapolation:

Predict visitors at 40°C.

v = 10(40) + = visitors

This is extrapolation because 40 is the data range.

Why extrapolation is less reliable:

The trend seen within the data might not continue outside it. At very high temperatures (45°C), the real relationship might change — perhaps fewer people go out because it's dangerously hot, or the park closes. The line of best fit is a model of what was observed, not a law of nature.

Misconception

MISCONCEPTION: Extrapolation is as reliable as interpolation

Wrong idea: "The equation works for data we measured, so it works just as well beyond those boundaries."

Why it fails: The relationship may only hold within the observed range. Beyond the range, additional factors come into play that we did not observe and cannot model from our data. A linear model predicting visitors at −10°C (using our splash pad equation) would give a nonsensical negative number — the linear trend clearly does not extend to freezing temperatures.

Rule of thumb: interpolation is valid as long as the line of best fit fits the data well. Extrapolation should always be noted as "less certain" and the further from the data range, the less reliable.

Evaluating a prediction:

When you make a prediction, always state:

  1. Whether it is interpolation or extrapolation
  2. How confident you are and why
  3. Any conditions that might make the prediction unreliable
