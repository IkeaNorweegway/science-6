5 ★★ Extended Rock Cycle · LO 7-5-02 · Evaluate
Calgary sits on thick layers of sedimentary rock — primarily limestone and sandstone. Below those sedimentary layers, at depths of kilometres, lies the Canadian Shield: ancient Precambrian igneous and metamorphic rock that forms the stable core of North America. Calgary is far from any active volcanoes or plate boundaries.

(a) Limestone is a sedimentary rock composed mainly of calcium carbonate from marine shells and skeletons. What does the presence of limestone beneath Calgary tell you about this region's geological past? Be specific about the environment that must have existed here and approximately when.

(b) The rock layers beneath Calgary run, from top to bottom: sedimentary layers, then Precambrian igneous/metamorphic basement. Explain how igneous and metamorphic rock ended up below sedimentary rock here. What does this sequence tell you about the history of this region?

(c) Rainwater becomes slightly acidic as it absorbs CO2 from the air. Predict what happens when this mildly acidic water passes through the limestone layers underground near Banff. What type of weathering is this, and what landforms could this process create over thousands of years?

Student Claim
"Earthquakes and volcanoes can happen anywhere on Earth — I've seen news stories about eruptions in different places all over the world, so they must be spread out randomly."

(a) Is this claim accurate, partially accurate, or inaccurate? Use specific evidence from plate tectonic theory to support your evaluation. Your answer must reference at least two specific locations and explain why those locations have the geological activity they do.

(b) The claim is based on news media coverage. Identify one reason why news coverage of geological events might give a misleading picture of where earthquakes and volcanoes actually occur most frequently.

8 ★ Core Fossils & Geological Time · LO 7-5-06 · Analyze
A geologist finds a fossil ammonite — a spiral-shelled marine invertebrate — embedded in limestone on a mountainside in Banff National Park, approximately 2,000 metres above sea level. Ammonites were ocean-dwelling creatures that went extinct approximately 66 million years ago.

(a) Explain, using geological processes, how a marine fossil ends up 2,000 metres above sea level in a mountain range. Your explanation must include at least three processes in the correct sequence.

(b) The fossil is found in limestone — a sedimentary rock. Why is this significant? Could you find the same ammonite fossil in a granite layer or a marble layer? Explain your reasoning for each rock type.

(c) A paleontologist says this layer can be used as an index fossil to help date other rock layers worldwide. What properties must a fossil have to be useful as an index fossil? Does the ammonite qualify? Justify your answer with at least two specific properties.

9 ★★ Extended Weathering, Erosion & Deposition · LO 7-5-05 · Evaluate
The Rocky Mountains formed approximately 80–50 million years ago through convergent plate tectonics. At their peak, they may have been as high or higher than the modern Himalayas, which are still rising today. Alberta's Rocky Mountain foothills are now significantly lower in elevation than the main peaks, and the peaks themselves have been eroded substantially from their maximum height.

(a) What geological process or processes best explain why the foothills and peaks are now much lower than they once were? Distinguish between at least two different processes that have both contributed to this reduction in height.

(b) If you were a geologist trying to find evidence in the rock record that the Rockies were once much taller, what specific feature or deposit would you look for, and where would you look? Justify why this evidence would support your hypothesis.

(c) The Himalayas are still rising — the Indian Plate is still colliding with the Eurasian Plate. The Rockies stopped rising ~50 Ma ago when the driving tectonic forces ended. What does this comparison tell you about the relationship between tectonic activity and mountain height over geological time?

10 ★★ Extended Plate Tectonics · LO 7-5-03 · Evaluate
Iceland sits directly on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — a divergent plate boundary where the Eurasian and North American plates are pulling apart. Iceland has frequent, relatively gentle volcanic eruptions with fluid basaltic lava flows. Japan sits on a convergent boundary where the Pacific Plate subducts beneath the Eurasian Plate. Japan has large, explosive volcanic eruptions (Mt. Fuji is a composite/stratovolcano).

(a) Using the diagram from Question 4 and your knowledge of plate tectonic theory, explain why the type of lava and eruption style differs between Iceland and Japan. Your answer must refer to where the magma comes from in each case and why the composition differs.

(b) Both Iceland and Japan have frequent seismic activity (earthquakes). Explain why — are the causes of these earthquakes the same at both locations or different? Be specific about what is happening to the rock at each location.

(c) Evaluate: "All volcanoes are equally dangerous." Using the Iceland-Japan comparison, assess whether this claim is accurate. What factors would a scientist consider when evaluating the hazard level of a specific volcano?

11 ★ Core Earthquakes & Volcanoes · LO 7-5-04 · Analyze
A magnitude 6.0 earthquake strikes with a focus 15 km deep, directly beneath a city of 500,000 people. A second earthquake registering magnitude 4.0 occurs 1,000 km away, with a focus 200 km deep, beneath a small town of 2,000 people. Emergency managers must decide which event requires more resources.

(a) The Richter scale is logarithmic — each whole-number step represents approximately 10 times more ground shaking (and roughly 32 times more energy released). How much more energy was released by the magnitude 6.0 compared to the magnitude 4.0 earthquake? Show your reasoning.

(b) Distinguish between the Richter scale and the Modified Mercalli Intensity scale. Which scale would be more useful for deciding which earthquake caused more damage, and why?

(c) The magnitude 4.0 earthquake is farther away, much weaker, and deeper — yet the community it affects may still require significant support. What factors other than magnitude and depth determine how damaging an earthquake is to a community? Name at least three factors and explain how each one increases or decreases actual damage.

Stretch Problems

Three challenging questions. No hints. Draw on everything you know — including connections across clusters. These go beyond standard grade-level expectations.

S1 Cross-cluster · Plate Tectonics + Rock Cycle + Alberta · Evaluate

Alfred Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912 but his colleagues rejected the hypothesis — not because the evidence was weak, but because he could not explain a mechanism. He could not say what force was moving the continents.

Identify and explain the mechanism we now use to explain plate movement. Then explain why the rock cycle and plate tectonics are fundamentally connected — why you cannot fully explain the rock cycle without plate tectonics, and vice versa. Use at least two specific examples from Alberta's geology to support your argument.

S2 Cross-cluster · Earth's Interior + Plate Tectonics + Fossils · Analyze / Evaluate

Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection currents in the liquid outer core. This field has reversed (flipped north and south poles) hundreds of times in geological history. Evidence of these reversals is "frozen" into the ocean floor as alternating stripes of magnetized rock on either side of mid-ocean ridges — a phenomenon called paleomagnetic striping.

Explain how paleomagnetic striping provides evidence for both sea-floor spreading and magnetic field reversals simultaneously. Why is the ocean floor — rather than continental rock — a better record of these reversals? What does this tell you about the relative age of oceanic crust compared to continental crust, and why?

S3 Cross-cluster · Full Unit · Create / Evaluate

Write a "geological biography" of Alberta — a summary of what this land has been through from the Precambrian to the present. Your biography must include: at least four geological eras or time periods, at least three geological processes from different clusters in this unit, and at least two pieces of physical evidence that a person could visit in Alberta today.

Write in continuous prose — not point form — as if you are explaining to an intelligent adult who has not studied geology. Use accurate geological vocabulary throughout.
