Part B: Short Answer

Q11 (4 marks):

100°C = 373 K | −40°C = 233 K | 310 K = 37°C | 0 K = −273°C

Q12 (4 marks):

Temperature = average kinetic energy of particles; same temperature means particles in both move at the same average speed; heat = total kinetic energy transferred; the lake has trillions more particles at the same speed → vastly more total heat energy; cup at 90°C and lake at 90°C have same temperature but radically different heat content

Q13 (4 marks):

Cold solid: particles close together, small or no movement arrows; Warm gas: particles far apart, fast-moving arrows in random directions; explanation: heating adds kinetic energy → particles move faster and spread apart; in gas, particles have completely escaped the surface

Q14 (4 marks):

Power lines expand in summer heat (particles spread apart → material longer); contract in winter cold; if installed tightly in summer, winter contraction would put them under extreme tension and they could snap; slack allows for winter contraction

Q15 (4 marks):

During melting: heat energy is used to break the bonds (attractive forces) between water molecules in ice; this is structural work, not kinetic speedup; particles don't move faster during the transition → temperature stays at 0°C; once all ice has melted → no more bonds to break → heat goes back into speeding particles → temperature rises again

Q17 (4 marks): Cup at 80°C and lake at 80°C have the same temperature (particles moving at same average speed). However, the lake has an enormously greater number of particles. Heat energy = total kinetic energy = number of particles × their speed. Same temperature, far more particles = far more heat energy. If you spilled the cup it's a burn; falling into a lake at the same temperature would take days to cool down because of the vast heat stored.

Part C: Application (15 marks)

Q20 — Insulation design (8 marks):

Problem Mode Improvement
Single-pane windows Conduction + radiation Double-pane (glass sandwich with air layer)
Uninsulated walls Conduction Fibreglass batt or spray foam insulation
Door gaps Convection Weatherstripping seals air movement
Dark roof Radiation Light-coloured/reflective roofing material

(a) Insulation in walls — walls have largest surface area; greatest potential heat loss; highest impact per dollar

(b) Dark roof absorbs radiation in summer → building hotter; in winter, absorbs solar radiation → slight heating benefit; trade-off; most Alberta houses prioritize winter performance

Q21 — Specific heat (7 marks):

a) Pacific Ocean stores summer heat and releases slowly in winter (high specific heat capacity); Vancouver's ocean moderates temperatures; Calgary has no large water body → air and soil heat/cool rapidly → extreme swings

b) Reservoir water likely warmer — smaller water body, surrounded by land that radiates heat at night; ocean retains cold from deeper water mixing

c) Cold snowmelt water enters rivers in spring (low specific heat of ice/snow → melts quickly when temps rise, but water remains cold); summer: long sunny days gradually warm water; late summer peak is the lagged response of high-specific-heat water slowly accumulating heat energy over months

Part D: Extended Response (10 marks)

Rubric: 3 concepts correctly identified + applied (1 each) = 3 marks; connects to specific Alberta scenario = 2 marks; uses unit vocabulary correctly (≥3 terms) = 2 marks; clear logical organization = 1 mark; addresses question completely = 2 marks

Option A — Vaccine cold chain: Outer insulation layer prevents conduction through walls (thick foam or fibreglass); vacuum seal if available blocks all conduction and convection; inner shiny liner reflects radiation back; thermal mass (cool packs with high specific heat) buffers against heat entering; seal prevents convection currents of warm air entering when opened; double walls with air gap block conduction; in −35°C Alberta winter, container prevents heat from leaving cold packs while also insulating against very cold outside temps entering and affecting the above-zero target range

Option B — Thermal pollution: Industrial process transfers heat (thermal energy) to cooling water via conduction; hot water returned to river = heat transfer to river water via convection and mixing; river temperature increases; warm water holds less dissolved oxygen (specific heat/solubility relationship); fish and invertebrates require minimum dissolved O₂; below threshold, organisms die or flee; solution: cooling towers allow water to cool before discharge (radiation/convection to air); or pass water through cooling ponds first

Option C — Alberta vs. Vancouver: Pacific Ocean has extremely high specific heat capacity; stores summer heat → releases slowly in winter → moderates Vancouver temperatures; Ocean is a thermal reservoir; Alberta far from any large water body → no thermal buffer; air over prairies has low specific heat → heats rapidly in summer, cools rapidly in winter; Rocky Mountains block Pacific air masses from moderating Alberta's climate; result: 70°C annual temperature range in Alberta vs. 30°C in Vancouver
