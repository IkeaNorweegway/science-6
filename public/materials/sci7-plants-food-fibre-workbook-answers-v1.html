Part A: Cell Theory and Plant Cells (20 marks)

Q1 — Cell theory tenets: (3 marks)

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function in all living things.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Q2 — Diagram labels: (5 marks)

  • a = cell wall (thick outer boundary)
  • b = cell membrane (thin inner boundary)
  • c = nucleus (dark oval in centre)
  • d = chloroplasts (green ovals)
  • e = central vacuole (large clear area)

Q3 — Two differences: (4 marks — 2 per difference)

Any two of:

  1. Plant cell has a cell wall; animal cell does not.
  2. Plant cell has chloroplasts; animal cell does not.
  3. Plant cell has a large central vacuole; animal cell has small or no vacuole.
  4. Plant cell is rectangular/regular shaped; animal cell is irregular.

Q4 — Cell identification: (3 marks)

Animal cell. Reason: no visible cell wall → not a plant cell (plant cells always have rigid cell walls); no chloroplasts → consistent with animal cell; small vacuole → animals have small, irregular vacuoles, not the large central vacuole of plant cells. All three observations together point to animal cell.

Q5 — Importance of cell theory: (5 marks)

Cell theory matters because: it provides the unified framework for all biology (everything living = cells); guides medicine (disease = cell malfunction), genetics (DNA in cells), ecology (organisms → cells). If cells could arise from non-cellular matter (violating tenet 3), biology would need to explain spontaneous generation; it would be impossible to understand reproduction, inheritance, or infection; medicine couldn't track how cells become cancerous or how bacterial infections spread — because pathogens could arise anywhere, not just from existing cells.

Part B: Plant Systems and Vascular Tissue (20 marks)

Q6 — Vascular tissue table: (6 marks)

Tissue What it carries Direction of flow System
Xylem Water and dissolved minerals Upward (roots → leaves) Root, stem, leaf
Phloem Sugars (glucose) Downward (leaves → rest of plant) Root, stem, leaf

Q7 — Dye experiment: (3 marks)

Xylem. Xylem transports water (and anything dissolved in it, including the dye) from roots up to leaves. The rings visible in the stem cross-section are xylem bundles; they stained because the dye traveled with the water through the xylem tissue.

Q8 — Root functions: (3 marks)

Any three: (1) absorb water and dissolved minerals from soil; (2) anchor plant in ground; (3) store food/energy (e.g., carrot, potato); (4) provide structural stability against wind/gravity

Q9 — Vascular tissue and height: (4 marks)

Non-vascular plants must absorb water directly through their surfaces — this limits them to small sizes close to water. Vascular tissue (xylem) acts as a plumbing system, moving water under negative pressure from roots to the canopy; the continuous water column in xylem can reach 100+ metres. Without this active transport system, the distance between soil water and leaf cells is simply too great for diffusion alone to supply water at the rate photosynthesis requires.

Q10 — Phloem disease: (4 marks)

The roots will eventually starve and die, then the whole tree will follow. Leaves produce glucose via photosynthesis but require phloem to transport that glucose to roots (which have no chloroplasts and cannot photosynthesize). With phloem destroyed, leaves continue making sugar but it cannot reach roots. Roots have no energy supply → stop growing → eventually die. Without functional roots, water and mineral absorption fail → leaves also die. The tree dies from the bottom up, even though leaves appear healthy initially.

Part C: Photosynthesis (20 marks)

Q11 — Photosynthesis equation: (6 marks)

Carbon dioxide + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen

  • (a) CO₂ from: air, entering through stomata (pores on leaf surface)
  • (b) Water from: soil, absorbed by roots, transported up via xylem
  • (c) Glucose goes to: plant cells for energy/growth; excess stored as starch; some transported to other plant parts via phloem
  • (d) Oxygen released: through stomata into the atmosphere

Q12 — Why leaves are green: (2 marks)

Chlorophyll (the pigment in chloroplasts) absorbs red and blue light for photosynthesis but reflects green light → reflected green light reaches our eyes → we perceive leaves as green.

Q13 — Photosynthesis and ecosystems: (4 marks)

Photosynthesis converts solar energy into chemical energy (glucose) that producers store. Primary consumers (herbivores — deer, caterpillars) eat producers and access this stored energy; secondary consumers (wolves, birds of prey) eat herbivores. Without photosynthesis, there would be no producers → no energy input to food webs → all consumers at every trophic level would be without food → ecosystem collapse. Photosynthesis also produces the atmospheric oxygen all aerobic organisms (including humans) need for cellular respiration.

Q14 — Refuting "plants eat soil": (4 marks)

Plants do not eat soil — they make their own food through photosynthesis using CO₂ from air and water absorbed through roots. Jan Baptista van Helmont grew a willow tree for 5 years: the soil lost only 57 grams while the tree gained 74 kg — that mass could not have come from soil. The mass came from CO₂ in the air (carbon forms the backbone of all glucose molecules) and hydrogen from water; soil contributes only dissolved minerals in trace amounts.

Q15 — Sealed container O₂ prediction: (4 marks)

O₂ level rises. The plant will photosynthesize (using light from outside the clear container + CO₂ from air inside + water from soil) and release O₂ as a by-product. The plant also does cellular respiration (consuming O₂, releasing CO₂), but during daylight hours, photosynthesis typically exceeds respiration in rate → net O₂ production. Over two weeks with adequate light and water, O₂ accumulates inside the sealed container. (Note: CO₂ will correspondingly decrease as the plant uses it; eventually CO₂ could become a limiting factor and photosynthesis would slow.)

Q17 — Flower parts:

  • Petal: attracts pollinators (insects, birds) with colour and/or scent
  • a = stamen (anther + filament): produces pollen (male reproductive cells)
  • b = pistil/carpel: receives pollen; contains ovule; female reproductive structure
  • c = stem/pedicel: supports and elevates the flower for pollinator access
  • d = receptacle/ovary base: base of pistil; contains developing ovules that become seeds

Accept reasonable variants; check that function is correct for each part named.

Q18 — Strawberry runner: Asexual reproduction. The new plant is a clone — genetically identical to the parent plant. No genetic recombination occurred; the runner simply extended and the new root system established vegetatively.

Q19 — Seed dispersal: Spreading seeds prevents competition between parent and offspring for light, water, and nutrients (same location = same resource pool); allows colonization of new habitats.

Two strategies:

  1. Wind dispersal (dandelion, maple keys): seeds have wings or parachutes that catch air currents → disperse long distances with no energy cost to plant
  2. Animal dispersal (berries, hooks like burdock): animals eat fruits → seeds pass through digestive system and deposited elsewhere; or seeds hook onto fur/clothing → carried away; reaches areas wind cannot

Q21 — Selective breeding vs. GMO:

Selective breeding: farmers/breeders identify individuals with a desired trait and allow only those to reproduce; offspring with best trait selected again; repeated over many generations → trait becomes more common. Slower but no lab work required. GMO (genetic modification): specific gene identified, isolated from any organism, and inserted directly into plant DNA in a lab; change is precise and fast (years vs. decades). Key differences: GMO can insert genes from completely different species; selective breeding works only within a species or close relatives; selective breeding is imprecise (whole genomes mixed); GMO changes specific genes.

Q22 — Herbicide resistance canola:

  • Benefit: farmers can spray a broad-spectrum herbicide that kills all weeds in the field but leaves canola unharmed → reduced competition, higher yield, potentially fewer herbicide applications overall
  • Concern: herbicide-resistant genes could cross-pollinate into wild mustard relatives → create herbicide-resistant "superweeds"; loss of genetic diversity if all canola is one herbicide-resistant variety; resistance in weeds may develop over time

Q23 — Drought-tolerant wheat comparison:

Selective breeding: take naturally drought-tolerant wheat plants (they exist), breed only those for many generations; may take 10–20 years of field seasons; imprecise — introduces many genetic changes along with drought tolerance; risk: reduces genetic diversity; low ecological risk (no foreign genes). GMO: identify the specific gene(s) controlling drought tolerance (e.g., from drought-adapted plants), insert into wheat genome; can produce results in 3–5 years; highly precise — only target gene changed; risk: gene flow to wild relatives possible; regulatory approval required; public acceptance challenging in Canadian market. Faster: GMO; More precise: GMO; Risks: GMO has ecological (gene flow) and regulatory risks; selective breeding has time and precision risks.

Part F: Synthesis (★) — answer-only

Q24:

a. Light (full spectrum, long photoperiod), CO₂ supply (supplementation possible), water availability, temperature (optimal ~20–25°C for most food crops), nutrients in growth medium

b. Vascular system (phloem + xylem) must be intact; monitor leaf colour (yellowing = nutrient/chlorophyll issue), stem firmness (wilting = water deficit), root health

c. Asexual (vegetative propagation — cuttings, tissue culture): uniform crop, faster production cycle, no need to wait for seed formation, all plants carry desired traits; sexual reproduction introduces variation (desirable in breeding programs, not in production)

d. Accept any justified answer: GMO for targeted trait (e.g., disease resistance, extended shelf life); selective breeding for gradual improvement without regulatory issues; organic methods for pesticide-free operation; key = justification tied to greenhouse context
