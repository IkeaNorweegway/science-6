Students arrive thinking ecology is about naming animals. It's actually about relationships — energy moving through a system, species depending on each other in non-obvious ways, and humans embedded in (not above) the system. The biggest conceptual move is from "list of species" thinking to "system" thinking. The energy pyramid and the wolf reintroduction scenario are the two highest-leverage moments in the unit.

Section 1 — Ecosystems and Components

Lessons 1–2 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — What Is an Ecosystem?

Show

Board: Play a 3-minute clip of an Alberta ecosystem (boreal forest or grassland). Ask: "What did you see?" List all answers on board. Then draw a line — one side "Living (biotic)," one side "Non-living (abiotic)." Let students do the sorting.

Say

"An ecosystem isn't just the animals — it's everything, including the water, the soil, the temperature, the light, and all the invisible organisms. A list of species is not an ecosystem."

Cue — ★ Write

Pre-knowledge check (page 1 of notes) — do NOT correct. Collect observations of what students believe before instruction. These are your teaching targets. Then Section 1 vocabulary table and ecosystems definition.

Misconception Flag — Humans Are External to Ecosystems

Frame from Day 1: humans are not observers of ecosystems — we are embedded in them. Reinforce this in Lesson 13 (ecosystem services). If students describe ecosystems as things "out in nature" separate from human communities, address it here. "What ecosystem are you in right now?" is a useful opening move.

Beat 1.2 — Alberta Ecosystems

Show

Board: Map of Alberta's five ecosystem types. Images of each. Ask students to predict: "Which abiotic factors differ between grassland and boreal forest?"

Say

"The abiotic factors set the rules. Everything living in an ecosystem has adapted to those specific conditions. Change the abiotic factors and the biotic community changes."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 1 notes: biotic vs. abiotic factors, Alberta ecosystem types. Exit Ticket 1.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Biotic vs. abiotic: Biotic = living (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, decomposers); Abiotic = non-living (water, temperature, sunlight, soil, wind).
  2. Levels of organization: Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem.
  3. Alberta ecosystem comparison: Any two valid differences (e.g., more moisture in boreal; higher winter temperatures in boreal vs. grassland).
  4. Why "ecosystem" not just "community": Ecosystems include both biotic AND abiotic factors, plus their interactions.
  5. Stretch: Biotic effects could include reduced plant diversity from drought stress; shift in species composition; possible loss of species that cannot adapt; new species invasions; abiotic effects cascade into biotic changes.

Section 2 — Energy Flow

Lessons 3–5 3 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 3–5 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — Producers, Consumers, and Decomposers

Show

Board: Dead mouse in the forest thought experiment. "What happens to this mouse over the next year? Name every organism involved." Draw a web of organisms that respond to this mouse's death.

Say

"Decomposers are not cleanup crew. They are the nutrient recyclers — without them, nutrients lock up permanently in dead matter and producers starve."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 2 vocabulary table: producer, consumer, decomposer, trophic level. Guided notes on trophic levels. Address decomposer misconception before moving on.

Misconception Flag — Decomposers Are Not Important

State the wrong idea explicitly; use the dead mouse scenario to show what happens without decomposers (nutrients locked in dead matter, producers starve, system collapses). This is the opening beat for Lesson 3 — address before notes. If more than 25% of students miss the decomposer question on Exit Ticket 2, reteach at the start of Lesson 5 before moving on.

Beat 2.2 — Building Food Webs

Show

Board: Give students organism cards (boreal forest species). Have them arrange and connect with arrows. Compare webs across groups.
Key card set needed: aspen, willow, grass, snowshoe hare, moose, beaver, lynx, wolf, great horned owl, raven, decomposer bacteria, mushrooms.

Say

"The arrow means energy — it flows FROM what is eaten TO what eats it. The arrow is not 'who chases whom.'"

"Why is the food web harder to draw than a food chain?" — discussion, not a written question. Let groups compare webs and explain differences.

Cue — ⊡ Think

"Why is the food web harder to draw than a food chain?" Discussion question. Students note one observation about raven's position in their web (raven is an omnivore and scavenger that links many chains).

Beat 2.3 — Food Webs in Alberta

Show

Board: A real Alberta food web diagram (boreal forest or grassland, 8–10 species). Ask: "What happens if we remove beavers? Trace every chain that includes them."

Say

"Removing one species from a food web rarely affects only its immediate predator or prey. The effects cascade."

Cue — ★ Write

Students complete Section 2 continued notes. Exit Ticket 2.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Producer role: Makes own food via photosynthesis; essential because producers are the energy source for all consumers.
  2. Wolf trophic level: Tertiary consumer (3rd trophic level above producers).
  3. Decomposers as consumers: True — decomposers consume dead organisms (a form of consuming).
  4. Arrow direction: Arrows represent energy flow — from food source to the organism that eats it.
  5. Stretch: Beaver removal → dams collapse → wetlands drain → loss of willow/aspen → fewer snowshoe hares → fewer lynx; loss of open water affects waterfowl, muskrat, fish populations.

Section 3 — Energy Pyramids

Lessons 6–7 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 6–7 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — The 10% Rule

Show

Board: 1000 kg of grass → how much deer? → how much wolf? Walk through visually, not mathematically.
Use a stack of 1000 paperclips at the base → 100 → 10 → 1. Or draw stacked bars shrinking by 90% each level.

Say

"Most of the energy at each level is lost as heat — used for movement, body maintenance, digestion. Only ~10% passes on. This is not inefficiency — it's physics."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students draw and label the energy pyramid in Section 3 notes with the 10% rule at each level. Address misconception before the pyramid notes.

Misconception Flag — Energy Increases Up the Food Chain

Address in Lesson 6 before pyramid notes. Use the paperclip visual to invert student expectation before explaining. Students intuitively think "more powerful = more energy" — the wolf at the top of the pyramid has the least energy available to it. The pyramid inverts what they expect.

Beat 3.2 — Energy Pyramids and Ecosystem Implications

Show

Board: Why are there so few wolves compared to moose? Map the population sizes onto the pyramid. Then: "What happens to the wolf population if the moose population drops by 50%?"

Say

"Large territories exist because there isn't much energy at the top. A wolf doesn't have a large territory because it enjoys hiking — it needs that much area to find enough food."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 3 notes: pyramid implications, territory size explanation. Exit Ticket 3.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. The 10% rule: Only about 10% of energy at one trophic level is available to the level above; the rest is lost as heat.
  2. Energy calculation: Primary consumers: 5,000 units; Secondary consumers: 500 units.
  3. Alberta pyramid: Base (grass) 10,000 → rabbits/ground squirrels 1,000 → hawks/foxes 100 → coyotes/eagles 10.
  4. Large territories: So little energy reaches top predator level that they must range widely to find enough prey.
  5. Stretch: Meadowlarks decline (less food); coyotes that eat only meadowlarks decline most; generalist coyotes that eat voles/rabbits less affected; indirect effect on grass (less grazing by grasshoppers = more grass temporarily).

Section 4 — Biodiversity

Lessons 8–9 2 beats 0 flags

Lessons 8–9 · Student notes: Section 4

Beat 4.1 — What Is Biodiversity?

Show

Board: Monoculture field vs. wild meadow images side by side. Ask: "Which is more resilient to a pest? Why?"

Say

"Biodiversity is insurance. If 30 species eat grasshoppers, a disease that kills grasshoppers doesn't collapse the ecosystem. If only one species does, the collapse cascades."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 vocabulary: biodiversity, species richness, resilience.

Beat 4.2 — Keystone Species

Show

Board: Wolf reintroduction at Yellowstone (Alberta analog works too). Tell the story: wolf removal → ecosystem cascade → wolf reintroduction → recovery. Have students map each cause onto a food web.

Say

"A keystone species is like a load-bearing wall. Remove it and everything else shifts. The wolf doesn't just eat elk — it changes elk behaviour, which changes vegetation, which changes beavers, which changes rivers."

Cue — ⊡ Think

"What keystone species might be most important in a boreal forest? Why?" — pair-share, not collected. Section 4 continued notes. Exit Ticket 4.

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Biodiversity definition: Variety of living species and ecosystems in an area; measured by number of species and their abundance.
  2. Why biodiversity matters: More species = more redundancy; if one species declines, others fill its role; ecosystem resists collapse.
  3. Keystone species: Has disproportionately large effect on ecosystem; examples: grizzly bear (Rockies), beaver (wetlands), wolf (boreal/mountain).
  4. Habitat vs. niche: Habitat = where an organism lives (location); niche = everything about how it lives (food, timing, behaviour, reproduction).
  5. Stretch: Wolf removal → more elk → more grazing of riverbank plants → fewer beavers (less willow/aspen) → eroding riverbanks → less songbird habitat. Wolf = keystone; its behavioural effects (elk avoiding riverbanks) ripple through entire ecosystem ("trophic cascade").

Section 5 — Environmental Change

Lessons 10–12 3 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 10–12 · Student notes: Section 5

Beat 5.1 — Natural Disturbances and Succession

Show

Board: Boreal forest fire — before and after photos (5 years post-fire). Ask: "Is this ecosystem destroyed?" Let students predict recovery timeline before explaining.

Say

"Natural disturbances are part of the system. Boreal forest fires are actually necessary — jack pine cones only open and release seeds after exposure to fire. The forest evolved with fire."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5 first half: natural disturbances, succession, resilience examples.

Beat 5.2 — Human-Caused Change and Invasive Species

Show

Board: Zebra mussel prevention video (Alberta government). Show purple loosestrife choking a wetland vs. a healthy native wetland side by side.

Say

"Invasive species are not just aggressive competitors. They succeed because they escaped the predators and parasites that controlled them in their home ecosystem. We import only the competitor, not the controls."

Tipping point concept — briefly: "Some changes can't be undone. Name one example you already know about."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5 second half: invasive species definition, tipping point concept. Exit Ticket 5.

Misconception Flag — Nature Always Recovers

Address in Lesson 11 before environmental change notes. Show tipping point examples and contrast: fire in boreal forest (recovers because system evolved with fire) vs. permafrost loss or coral bleaching (may not recover — tipping point crossed). The key distinction: resilience has limits, and some human-caused changes push past those limits.

Beat 5.3 — Alberta Species at Risk

Show

Board: Table of Alberta species at risk. Students choose one, do a 10-minute research sprint, then share one fact with the class.

Say

"Being 'at risk' doesn't mean endangered everywhere — it means the specific population or subspecies in Canada/Alberta is threatened. Context matters."

Cue — ★ Write

Students record one species, the threat, and one fact. Brief whole-class share.

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Natural disturbances: Fire (boreal forest regenerates in 5-10 years; fire-adapted species return); drought (most grassland species adapted to periodic drought; recover when rain returns).
  2. Invasive species mechanism: Lack natural predators/parasites in new ecosystem; can grow unchecked; outcompete native species.
  3. Habitat fragmentation: Breaks up migration corridors; isolates populations; reduces gene flow; increases edge habitat (often lower quality).
  4. Alberta species at risk: Any at-risk species with specific threats and conservation needs.
  5. Stretch: Boreal forest is large but not infinite; habitat loss has cumulative effects; individual clearcuts create fragmentation that affects caribou even if total forest area unchanged; some species require continuous old-growth, not fragments; tipping point = "minimum viable habitat" below which species cannot persist.

Section 6 — Sustainability and Stewardship

Lessons 13–14 2 beats 1 pacing flag

Lessons 13–14 · Student notes: Section 6

Beat 6.1 — Ecosystem Services

Show

Board: "If the boreal forest disappeared tomorrow, what would it cost?" Walk through: water filtration, carbon storage, biodiversity maintenance, climate regulation.
Reference: municipal water treatment plant cost ($50–200M CAD). A wetland does the same job for free.

Say

"Ecosystem services are invisible until they're gone. We don't pay for the air we breathe or the water filtering that wetlands do. That doesn't mean they have no value — it means we're not charging ourselves the true cost of destroying them."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 6 ecosystem services list and the water treatment plant cost comparison. "What is the wetland 'worth'?" — written response.

Beat 6.2 — TEK and Indigenous Stewardship

Show

Board: Invite a Knowledge Keeper if possible — or use video of Blackfoot burning practices, Cree land relationships. Frame TEK as a different kind of knowledge production, not a lesser kind.

Say

"TEK is built from thousands of years of observation in one specific place. Western ecology has been practising for about 200 years globally. These are complementary — not competing — ways of knowing."

Cue — ★ Write

"What does it mean to be a steward of an ecosystem rather than just a user of it?" — written reflection, not collected. Section 6 continued. Exit Ticket 6.

Pacing Flag — TEK Lesson Needs Full Class Time

TEK is not a cultural add-on — it is core content about what counts as evidence and how different knowledge systems contribute. If inviting a Knowledge Keeper, arrange well in advance. If using video, preview it for classroom suitability. The goal: students understand TEK as a legitimate, long-term, empirically grounded knowledge system — not as folklore or separate from science but as part of it.

Section 6 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Ecosystem services: Clean water (replacement: water treatment plant, $50-200M); carbon sequestration (replacement: industrial carbon capture, enormously expensive); pollination (replacement: hand pollination, economically impossible at scale).
  2. TEK: Knowledge built from generations of careful observation in specific ecosystems; valuable because it captures long-term patterns, species relationships, and changes over time that Western science has not studied yet in those specific places.
  3. Stewardship: Responsibility to maintain the health and function of the land/ecosystem for present and future generations; not just using resources, but actively protecting them.
  4. Stretch: Policy should reference: ecosystem services at risk; biodiversity principle (maintaining species diversity for resilience); Indigenous stewardship practices (e.g., prescribed burning, traditional harvesting practices that maintain balance).
