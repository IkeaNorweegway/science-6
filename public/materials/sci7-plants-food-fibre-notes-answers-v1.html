Section 1: Cell Theory and Plant Cells

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Three tenets of cell theory 1. All living things made of cells 2. Cells are basic unit of life 3. All cells from pre-existing cells
Cell wall — function structural support and shape; made of cellulose
Cell membrane — controls what enters and exits the cell
Central vacuole — stores water; waste products; maintains turgor pressure
Chloroplasts — site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll
Nucleus — contains DNA; controls cell activities
Plant vs. animal — 3 differences cell wall, large central vacuole, chloroplasts — all absent in animal cells

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. (1) All living things made of cells (2) Cells are basic unit of life (3) All cells from pre-existing cells
  2. (a) cell membrane (b) nucleus (c) chloroplast
  3. Stores water/maintains cell pressure; stores waste products
  4. Plant cell — has rigid outer wall (cell wall), large central space (vacuole), and green bodies (chloroplasts)
  5. Stretch: Animal cells need to move, change shape, migrate, engulf things — a rigid cell wall would prevent this; plant cells are stationary and need structural rigidity

Section 2: Plant Systems and Vascular Tissue

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Root system functions absorption, anchoring, storage
Shoot system functions support, photosynthesis, reproduction
Root hairs increase surface area
Taproot examples carrot, beet, dandelion
Xylem carries water and dissolved minerals; upward from roots to leaves
Phloem carries sugars (glucose); downward from leaves to rest of plant
Vascular plants grow taller because they can transport water up from roots regardless of height

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Root: absorption, anchoring, storage; Shoot: photosynthesis, support, reproduction
  2. Xylem: water and minerals, roots to leaves; Phloem: sugars, leaves downward
  3. Xylem — it carries water (dye dissolved in water) from roots upward
  4. Xylem allows water to travel up from roots to any height; non-vascular plants must stay near water
  5. Stretch: Leaves (cannot receive sugars from leaves) accumulate excess glucose and die; roots (cannot receive sugar from leaves) starve and die — roots have no chloroplasts so cannot photosynthesize

Section 3: Photosynthesis

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Van Helmont thought experiment: mass came from air (CO₂) and water
CO₂ from air via leaf pores (stomata)
Water from soil/roots via xylem
Light captured by chlorophyll, the green pigment in chloroplasts
Glucose = food/energy; stored as starch
Oxygen = by-product released into atmosphere
Photosynthesis site chloroplasts in green parts of plant

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. CO₂ + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen
  2. Chloroplasts (in leaf cells)
  3. Chlorophyll absorbs most colours of light but reflects green — leaves appear green
  4. Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose); it does not cause "breathing" — that is respiration (a separate process)
  5. Stretch: In darkness: photosynthesis stops; CO₂ rises (plant still does respiration); O₂ falls; plant consumes its own stored glucose

Section 4: Plant Reproduction

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Sexual — offspring are genetically different from parents
Sexual advantage genetic variation; adaptation to changing conditions
Asexual — offspring are genetically identical to parent (clone)
Asexual advantage fast colonization; no need for pollinator
Stamen produces pollen
Pistil contains egg/ovary
Petals attract pollinators
Pollination = transfer of pollen from anther to stigma
Runners example strawberry
Tubers example potato

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Stamen: produces pollen; Pistil: receives pollen, contains eggs, develops into fruit after fertilization
  2. Runners (strawberry, grass); bulbs (tulip, garlic); tubers (potato); cuttings (coleus, geranium)
  3. Animal hitchhike (hooks/burrs); advantage: seeds carried far from parent; reduces competition for resources
  4. Genetic variation creates individuals with different traits; some may be better adapted to new conditions (disease, drought, climate change)
  5. Stretch: One organism biologically, but many stems visually; advantage: rapid colonization, shared root resources; risk: one disease could kill the entire connected system simultaneously (no genetic diversity)

Section 5: Plants for Food and Fibre

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Wheat before processing grain seed — eaten directly or ground to flour
Canola before processing seed; used as animal feed; oil extracted after
Barley after processing malted for beer; sometimes rolled for food
Flax before processing seeds eaten (flaxseed); after: linseed oil, linen
Monoculture advantages easier mechanized harvest; economies of scale
Monoculture disadvantages vulnerable to single pest/disease; lower biodiversity

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Food crops: wheat, canola, barley, peas, lentils; Fibre crops: flax, hemp
  2. Food crop: grown for eating (or oil for eating); fibre crop: grown for material (fabric, rope, paper)
  3. Advantage: efficient mechanized production; Disadvantage: single pest or disease can destroy entire crop
  4. Bees pollinate canola and other flowering crops; without pollinators, these crops cannot set seed and produce fruit/grain — reduced yields or crop failure

Section 6: Biotechnology

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Selective breeding — choose plants with desired traits
Selective breeding result dramatic changes over many generations
Genetic modification — directly changes specific genes in DNA
Selective breeding timeline thousands of years
GMO timeline since ~1990
~95% of Alberta canola genetically modified for herbicide resistance

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Selective breeding: choosing organisms with desired traits and breeding only those, over many generations; used for ~10,000 years
  2. Herbicide resistance (crop survives when field is sprayed); pest resistance (built-in insect repellent)
  3. Selective breeding: natural processes over generations, less precise, takes a long time; GMO: direct gene insertion/modification in lab, much faster, very precise
  4. Stretch: Both claims oversimplify; "dangerous" and "safe" are properties of specific applications, not the technique itself; questions to evaluate: What gene was inserted? What is the protein it produces? Has it been tested for allergenicity, toxicity, or environmental spread? Different GMOs warrant individual evaluation, not blanket judgements
