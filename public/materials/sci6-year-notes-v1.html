Unit 1: Matter

Guiding question: How do particles change when matter is heated or cooled?

Big Ideas

  • All matter is made of tiny particles. Particles always move.
  • Heating makes particles move faster and spread apart.
  • Matter can change state. The particles stay the same — only their arrangement changes.
  • Water is a special case. Ice is less dense than liquid water.

The Particle Model

Matter is anything with mass and volume.

📌matter — has mass, takes up space

The particle model says four things. All matter is made of tiny particles. There is space between particles. Particles always move. Temperature changes how fast they move.

📌particle model — explains how matter behaves

When matter heats up, particles move faster. They push apart. The material takes up more space. When matter cools, particles slow down. They pull closer. The material shrinks.

COLD particles              HOT particles

  o  o  o                   o       o
   o  o  o                      o
  o  o  o                   o       o

Slow, close together        Fast, further apart

Alberta example: The steel railway tracks near Lethbridge get longer in summer heat. They get shorter in January cold. Engineers leave gaps between rail sections — called expansion joints — so the steel can expand without buckling.

📌expansion joint — gap that lets material expand
Misconception

"A particle is just a tiny piece of steel."

A particle is not a mini version of the material. Particles have empty space between them. That space is what allows matter to expand and contract.

When matter changes state, mass is conserved. The number of particles does not change. Only their arrangement changes.

100 g of ice melts → still 100 g of liquid water.

Expansion, Contraction, and the Water Anomaly

The general rule: heating causes expansion. Cooling causes contraction.

This is why Lethbridge rail sections have small gaps. In summer, steel expands and the gaps close. In winter, steel contracts and the gaps open. Without gaps, expanding steel would buckle or crack the track.

📌expansion — material takes up more space
📌contraction — material takes up less space

Water breaks the general rule. This is the water anomaly.

Most solids are denser than their liquid form. Ice is less dense than liquid water. This is why ice floats. When water freezes, particles lock into a hexagonal crystal structure. That structure pushes particles slightly further apart — so ice takes up more space, not less.

LIQUID WATER                ICE
  o  o  o                o       o
o    o  o             o     o     o
  o  o                   o       o
                      (hexagonal — MORE space)

Density: HIGHER           Density: LOWER → ice floats

Because ice floats, it forms a layer on top of lakes in winter. Liquid water stays below. Fish survive in the liquid layer underneath.

📌water anomaly — ice is less dense than liquid water
Misconception

"Solids are always denser than liquids."

For most materials this is true. Water is the major exception. Ice is less dense than liquid water. Ice floats. This matters for every lake and river in Alberta every winter.

Key Ideas — Matter

  • All matter is made of particles. Particles always move. Heat makes them move faster.
  • Solids have fixed shape. Liquids flow. Gases fill their container.
  • Phase changes are changes in particle arrangement. Mass is always conserved.
  • Most materials expand when heated and contract when cooled.
  • Water is an exception. Ice is less dense than liquid water, so ice floats.

Unit 2: Living Systems

Guiding question: How do living and non-living parts of an ecosystem depend on each other?

Big Ideas

  • Every ecosystem has biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) parts. Both matter.
  • Plants make their own food through photosynthesis. They do not get food from soil.
  • Energy flows from producers to consumers through food webs.
  • Every organism in an ecosystem depends on others. Remove one part and the whole system shifts.

Biotic and Abiotic — What Makes Up an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem includes all the living things and all the non-living things in an area — and all the ways they interact.

Biotic means living. Abiotic means non-living.

Biotic (living)Abiotic (non-living)
Black spruce treesSunlight
Moose, wolves, beaversTemperature
Insects, birdsWater and precipitation
Fungi, bacteriaSoil type and nutrients

In the boreal forest near Fort McMurray, sunlight drives photosynthesis in the spruce trees. Cold winters limit which animals can survive year-round. The soil holds nutrients returned by decomposers. Every abiotic factor shapes which organisms can live there.

📌ecosystem — living + non-living things, interacting
📌biotic — anything alive or recently alive
📌abiotic — non-living physical conditions

Decomposers break down dead matter. They return nutrients to the soil.

Without decomposers, dead organisms would pile up. Nutrients would stay locked in dead tissue. Producers would have nothing to grow from.

📌decomposer — breaks dead matter into nutrients

Photosynthesis — Where Does a Plant's Food Come From?

Plants do not get their food from soil. Soil provides minerals — not food. Plants make their own food. This process is called photosynthesis.

The photosynthesis equation:

         Light energy
              ↓
  CO₂    +   H₂O  ────────────→  Glucose  +  Oxygen
(from air)  (from roots)          (food)    (released)

Chlorophyll in the leaves captures light.

Glucose is the food the plant makes.

Glucose provides energy for growth. Extra glucose is stored as starch in roots, seeds, and fruit. The black spruce trees near Fort McMurray make their food from CO₂ in the air — not from the soil beneath them.

📌photosynthesis — plants make food using light
📌chlorophyll — green pigment that captures light
📌glucose — sugar made by photosynthesis
Misconception

"Plants get their food from soil through their roots."

Soil provides minerals. Minerals are not food. Plants make glucose using light, water, and carbon dioxide. Almost all the mass of a tree comes from CO₂ in the air — not from the ground.

Food Webs and Interdependence

A food chain shows one path of energy flow. A food web shows the full network. Each arrow means "is eaten by" — energy flows in the direction of the arrow.

      [Sun]
        ↓
  [Black Spruce / Shrubs]
       ↙        ↘
  [Moose]      [Beetles / Insects]
     ↓               ↓
  [Wolf]          [Woodpecker]
                      ↓
                  [Hawk]
                      ↓
              [Decomposers (fungi)]

Ecosystems are systems. Every part connects to every other part. In the boreal forest, if moose populations dropped sharply, wolf populations would follow. Vegetation would change without moose grazing. Insects, birds, and soil organisms would all shift in response.

Animals depend on plants for food, oxygen, and shelter. Plants depend on animals for pollination, seed dispersal, CO₂, and decomposition. Interdependence means neither can survive without the other.

📌food web — full network of who eats whom
📌interdependence — organisms need each other to survive

Key Ideas — Living Systems

  • Ecosystems have biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) parts. Both are essential.
  • Plants make food through photosynthesis. CO₂ + water + light → glucose + oxygen.
  • Food webs show how energy flows from producers to consumers to decomposers.
  • Removing one part of an ecosystem causes changes throughout the whole system.
  • Decomposers are essential — they return nutrients to the soil for producers to use.

Unit 3: Forces

Guiding question: How do forces act on structures, and what happens to materials when forces are applied?

Big Ideas

  • A force is a push or a pull. It has size (magnitude) and direction.
  • Forces can be external (from outside) or internal (built up inside the material).
  • Materials respond to forces with elastic deformation or plastic deformation.
  • Newton's Third Law: every action force has an equal and opposite reaction force on a different object.

What Is a Force?

A force is a push or a pull. Every force has two properties:

  1. Magnitude — how big the force is, measured in Newtons (N)
  2. Direction — which way the force acts

We draw forces as arrows. Longer arrows mean larger forces.

  20 N →    [BRIDGE DECK]    ← 8 N

  Net force = 12 N to the right (forces in opposite directions subtract)

Alberta example: The timber pedestrian bridge at Riverside School carries many forces at once. Students walking across it apply a downward applied force. Friction between boots and planks acts horizontally. The structure must handle all of these forces at the same time.

📌force — push or pull on an object

📌internal force — force acting inside the material
Misconception

"Friction is not a real force — it just slows things down."

Friction is a real force. It has direction (opposite to motion) and size (measured in Newtons). On the Riverside bridge, friction keeps boots from sliding on wet planks.

Elasticity, Plasticity, and Newton's Third Law

When a force acts on a material, the material changes shape — this is deformation.

Elastic deformation: the material returns to its original shape when the force is removed. This is a temporary change. The bridge cables at Riverside flex when students walk across — but return to their original shape when the students leave.

Plastic deformation: the material keeps its new shape. This is a permanent change. If a rail on the bridge deck bends sharply beyond its elastic limit, it stays bent.

ELASTIC:  force applied → material stretches → force removed → returns to original shape
PLASTIC:  force applied → material stretches → force removed → stays changed
📌deformation — change in shape due to force
📌elastic limit — force above which deformation is permanent

Newton's Third Law: When one object pushes on another, the second object pushes back with an equal force in the opposite direction.

SituationAction forceReaction force
Student steps on bridge deckStudent pushes DOWN on deckDeck pushes UP on student
Student pushes on bridge railingStudent pushes railing OUTWARDRailing pushes student INWARD
Boots push backward on planksBoots push planks backwardPlanks push boots forward

Action and reaction forces do not cancel. They act on different objects. Each object's movement is controlled by forces acting on that object only.

Misconception

"Action and reaction forces cancel out, so nothing moves."

They cannot cancel. They act on different objects. The bridge deck pushes up on the student — that acts on the student only. Two separate objects, two separate effects.

Key Ideas — Forces

  • Every force has magnitude (Newtons) and direction.
  • External forces come from outside. Internal forces build up inside the material.
  • The four internal forces are tension, compression, shear, and torsion.
  • Elastic deformation is temporary. Plastic deformation is permanent.
  • Newton's Third Law: action and reaction forces are equal, opposite, and act on different objects.

Unit 4: Climate

Guiding question: What causes climate to change, and how does it affect Alberta?

Big Ideas

  • Weather is short-term. Climate is a long-term pattern measured over decades.
  • The greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm. Extra greenhouse gases trap extra heat.
  • Climate change is happening now in Alberta — it is not only a future issue.
  • Scientists use many tools to measure climate. Traditional ecological knowledge is a valid long-term data source.

Weather vs. Climate

Weather is the state of the atmosphere at one place and one time.

Climate is the average weather pattern over at least 30 years.

One cold week in High River tells you about weather. Flood data from the Bow River over 50 years tells you about climate.

TIMESCALE COMPARISON

Weather:  |──day──|──day──|──day──|
          useful for: "Should I bring an umbrella?"

Climate:  |──10 years──|──10 years──|──10 years──|
          useful for: "Is the Bow River flooding more often?"

Climate claims need long-term data. A single cold January in High River does not tell us anything about a 30-year climate trend.

📌weather — atmosphere right now, one place
📌climate — average pattern over decades
Misconception

"A cold week proves climate change isn't real."

Weather and climate operate on different timescales. A cold week is one data point. Climate is the average of thousands of data points over decades. One cold week inside a long-term warming trend is still a warming trend.

Earth's Climate Systems

The Sun drives Earth's climate. Sunlight reaches Earth. Some is reflected back to space. Some is absorbed by land, ocean, and atmosphere. Absorbed energy warms the surface. Warmth rises back toward space as heat.

The greenhouse effect slows that heat from leaving.

THE GREENHOUSE EFFECT

     ☀ SUNLIGHT ☀
         ↓↓↓↓
  ┌─────────────────────────┐
  │  ATMOSPHERE             │ ← Greenhouse gases absorb
  │  CO₂, CH₄, H₂O vapour  │   outgoing heat and re-emit
  └─────────────────────────┘   it back toward Earth
       ↓↓↓↓         ↑↑↑↑
    Sunlight in   Heat rises
       ↓↓↓↓         ↑↑↑↑
  ═══════════════════════════
       EARTH'S SURFACE
  ═══════════════════════════

Without the natural greenhouse effect, Earth's average temperature would be about −18°C — far too cold for liquid water and most life.

The problem: human activities add extra greenhouse gases. Extra gases trap extra heat. Average temperatures rise.

📌greenhouse effect — atmosphere slows heat escaping Earth

Albedo is how much sunlight a surface reflects.

Fresh snow reflects about 80% of sunlight. Dark ocean water reflects about 6%. When ice melts, darker surface is exposed. That darker surface absorbs more heat. More heat melts more ice. This self-reinforcing cycle is the ice-albedo feedback loop.

📌albedo — fraction of sunlight reflected by a surface

Causes and Effects of Climate Change

Natural causes of climate change include volcanic eruptions, changes in Earth's orbit, and changes in solar output. These operate over thousands to millions of years.

Human causes are now the dominant driver of current climate change. Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ stored underground for millions of years — very quickly.

Deforestation releases stored CO₂ and removes trees that would absorb future CO₂. CO₂ levels today (~420 ppm) are higher than at any point in the past 800,000 years.

Effects already measured in Alberta:

ChangeWhat we observe
Average temperatureUp ~1.5°C since 1950
SpringArrives earlier — rivers ice-free sooner
Wildfire seasonStarts earlier, ends later
Mountain glaciersShrinking — less summer meltwater for rivers
FloodingHigh River 2013 — extreme rainfall events increasing
FNMI ice roadsFewer weeks of safe travel each year

Traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) from Indigenous Elders often covers longer time periods than instrument records. Elders along the Bow River have documented spring ice break-up timing across generations — break-up now happens about two weeks earlier than 40 years ago. TEK is systematic long-term observation. It is a valid scientific data source.

📌fossil fuel — ancient carbon stored underground

Key Ideas — Climate

  • Weather is short-term. Climate is a decades-long average. They need different kinds of evidence.
  • The greenhouse effect is natural. Extra greenhouse gases from human activity trap extra heat.
  • Human causes (fossil fuels, deforestation) are now the main driver of current climate change.
  • Alberta shows measurable effects now: warmer temperatures, longer wildfire seasons, shrinking glaciers, more extreme flooding.
  • Scientists and Indigenous Knowledge Keepers both gather long-term climate evidence.

Guiding question: How do we choose energy resources wisely?

Big Ideas

  • Energy resources are renewable or non-renewable depending on how fast they are replenished.
  • Some resources are used directly (before processing). Others must be converted first (after processing).
  • Choosing an energy resource involves five factors. No single resource is perfect.
  • Renewable does not mean clean. Every resource has trade-offs.

Renewable and Non-Renewable Resources

An energy resource is anything we use to do work, produce heat, or generate electricity.

Renewable resources are replenished naturally. We do not run out.

Non-renewable resources took millions of years to form. Once used, they are gone.

RenewableNon-Renewable

📌energy resource — source of usable energy
📌renewable — nature replaces it fast enough
📌non-renewable — cannot be replaced in a human lifetime
Misconception

"Renewable = clean."

Renewable means it will not run out. It does not mean harmless. Large hydro dams flood ecosystems. Wind turbines require land and affect bird migration. Solar panel manufacturing uses toxic materials. Renewable and clean are separate ideas.

Before and After Processing

Some resources are used before processing — directly in their natural form. Some resources are used after processing — converted into another form first. This has nothing to do with renewable vs. non-renewable.

Selecting Energy Resources — Five Factors

Choosing an energy resource requires weighing five factors:

  1. Availability — Is the resource present in usable amounts, where and when needed?
  2. Societal impact — How does use affect people, jobs, and communities?
  3. Economic impact — What does it cost to build, maintain, and run?
  4. Environmental impact — How does it affect air, land, water, and wildlife?
  5. Responsible management — Will it still be available and affordable in 50 years?

Key Ideas — Energy Resources

  • Renewable resources are replenished naturally. Non-renewable resources cannot be replaced in a human lifetime.
  • Resources can be used before processing (directly) or after processing (converted first).
  • Five factors guide energy decisions: availability, societal impact, economic impact, environmental impact, and responsible management.
  • No resource is perfect. Every choice involves trade-offs.

Solar System Objects and Scale

The solar system is the Sun and everything that orbits it.

The eight planets (in order from the Sun):

Mercury → Venus → Earth → Mars → Jupiter → Saturn → Uranus → Neptune

Mnemonic: My Very Excellent Mother Just Served Us Nachos

Inner planets (Mercury–Mars) are small and rocky. Outer planets (Jupiter–Neptune) are large and made mostly of gas.

ObjectEmit or reflect light?Key feature
Star (Sun)EMITSProduces light through nuclear fusion

The Sun emits light. Everything else reflects it. When you see Venus in the night sky, you are seeing reflected sunlight — not light Venus generates itself.

Scale: If the Sun were a basketball (24 cm across), Earth would be a pinhead about 30 metres away. Neptune would be about 1 kilometre away. The solar system is almost entirely empty space.

Misconception

"Planets produce their own light, like stars."

Planets do not produce light. They reflect sunlight. A star is massive enough for nuclear fusion — that is what generates light. Planets are not. This is a fundamental difference between stars and planets.

Natural vs. Artificial Satellites, and Living in Space

A natural satellite orbits another body — formed through natural processes.

An artificial satellite is human-built and placed into orbit.

Artificial satellite typeWhat it does

Satellites stay in orbit because they move sideways so fast that as they fall toward Earth, Earth's surface curves away beneath them at the same rate. They are always falling — always missing the ground.

Pluto was called a planet from 1930 until 2006. When astronomers discovered many similar-sized icy objects in the Kuiper Belt, scientists updated the definition of planet: orbits the Sun, is round, and has cleared its orbital neighbourhood. Pluto meets the first two but not the third. New evidence → better classification → more accurate understanding.

📌natural satellite — formed naturally, orbits another body
📌artificial satellite — designed and launched by humans
Misconception

"Scientists changed the rules to be unfair to Pluto."

The reclassification was based on new evidence — dozens of similar-sized icy objects found beyond Neptune. The new definition is more precise and applies consistently. Updating a classification when evidence demands it is exactly what science is supposed to do.

Key Ideas — Space

  • Stars emit light. Planets, moons, and asteroids reflect light from the Sun.
  • The solar system is almost entirely empty space. Textbook diagrams are not to scale.
  • Different exploration tools (telescopes, probes, rovers, models) answer different questions.
  • Ex-Alta 1 is a student-built CubeSat that gathers data on Earth's upper atmosphere.
  • Science updates its classifications when evidence changes. Pluto's reclassification is a healthy example.
