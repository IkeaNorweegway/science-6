Part A: Vocabulary (20 marks)

Q1 Matching: a→5 (biotic = living); b→4 (decomposer = breaks down dead); c→6 (keystone = disproportionate effect); d→2 (abiotic = non-living); e→1 (biodiversity = variety); f→3 (producer = makes own food)

Q2 Definitions: Accept student wording if conceptually accurate.

  • Trophic level: position in food chain based on energy source
  • Ecological niche: the complete role of a species in its ecosystem (what it eats, when active, how it reproduces)
  • Invasive species: organism introduced outside its native ecosystem; typically lacks natural predators
  • Ecosystem services: benefits ecosystems provide to humans for free (water filtration, carbon storage, pollination, etc.)

Part B: Food Webs and Energy Flow (24 marks)

Q4 — Food web questions:

a) Grass, willow, or aspen (any producer from the web)

b) Secondary or tertiary (lynx eats snowshoe hare which eats willow/grass; 3rd trophic level = tertiary consumer)

c) Snowshoe hare or moose

d) Lynx (eats hare), wolf (eats moose), great horned owl (eats hare)

e) Snowshoe hare: declines (loses food source); Lynx: declines (less prey); Wolf: may partly compensate by eating more moose if moose population unaffected, but overall predator population under pressure

f) Producers (willow) = 10,000 → hare (primary) = 1,000 → wolf (secondary/tertiary): wolf eating hare = 100 units; wolf eating moose (which ate willow) = 1,000 → wolf = 100

Q5 (4 marks): 10% rule means only 10% of energy passes to next level; top predators (3–4 levels up) receive ~0.01–0.1% of original producer energy; must range over enormous area to find sufficient prey energy

Q6 (4 marks): Any two: (1) most organisms eat multiple food sources, not just one; (2) most organisms are eaten by multiple predators; (3) omnivores eat at multiple trophic levels; (4) decomposers connect all trophic levels

Q7 (4 marks): Decomposers break down dead material → return nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, carbon) to soil → producers need these minerals to grow → without decomposers, dead material piles up and nutrients become unavailable → producers decline → all trophic levels decline

Part C: Ecosystems and Biodiversity (20 marks)

Q8: Biotic: living (wolves, trees, fungi); Abiotic: non-living (temperature, soil moisture, sunlight); Interaction: abiotic factors set limits; biotic community adapts to abiotic conditions; biotic organisms change abiotic environment (beavers change water flow)

Q9: Any biotic/abiotic differences:

  • Grassland biotic: grasses, pronghorn, burrowing owls; Wetland biotic: cattails, great blue herons, beavers
  • Grassland abiotic: lower annual precipitation, higher summer temperatures; Wetland abiotic: standing water present, higher humidity

Q10: Biodiversity = variety of species in an ecosystem; matters because more species = more resilience; example: monoculture crop destroyed by one disease vs. polyculture or wild ecosystem where only some species affected

Q11 (6 marks — full solution): Bears = keystone species (regulate ground squirrel populations); removal → squirrels unchecked → overgraze native prairie grass → grass declines → shifts food web (fewer other grass-dependent species); food web connections are non-linear — removing one species affects unexpected organisms several steps away; reintroduction restored balance at multiple trophic levels

Q13 (4 marks): Tipping point: threshold beyond which ecosystem does not return to prior state; example: repeated coral bleaching (each bleaching event weakens coral; after enough stress, structural community collapses permanently); "recovered" ecosystem may have different species composition, lost keystone species, or reduced biodiversity — surface appearance ≠ functional recovery

Q14 (4 marks): Zebra mussels filter feed continuously → outcompete native bivalves and zooplankton; disrupt base of food chain; no natural predators in Alberta → rapid population growth; clog water intake pipes for municipalities → significant economic infrastructure costs; spread via boat hulls and live bait → difficult to prevent

Q15 (4 marks): Any 3 effects with food web connections:

  • Habitat fragmentation: woodland caribou lose continuous old-growth → population isolation, wolf access via roads
  • Water disruption: road runoff changes stream chemistry → affects aquatic invertebrates → affects fish → affects bears/osprey
  • Loss of old-growth trees: cavity-nesting birds lose nesting sites
  • Light/temperature change at clear-cut edges: different plant species establish → different herbivore community

Part E: Sustainability and Stewardship (16 marks)

Q16 (4 marks): TEK = knowledge built over generations through careful observation of specific ecosystems; example: Blackfoot prescribed burning (deliberately burned grassland for bison habitat) — modern fire management now incorporates this practice; or: Cree knowledge of boreal mushroom and berry cycles informing habitat monitoring

Q18 (6 marks): Any of the three options with specific evidence:

  • Farmers: pollinators (bees) service canola and pulse crops; soil organisms (decomposers) maintain soil fertility; both are ecosystem services
  • Indigenous communities: traditional food systems (moose, fish, berries) directly depend on healthy boreal/wetland ecosystems; land-based healing practices rely on ecosystem health
  • Urban development: impervious surfaces increase runoff → floods downstream; clearing riparian vegetation → stream bank erosion → sedimentation → fish habitat loss far beyond city limits

Part F: Challenge (★)

Q19 (8 marks) — Conservation plan rubric:

  • Current threat identified with evidence: 2 marks
  • Trophic level effects of decline: 2 marks
  • Conservation action tied to ecosystem concept: 2 marks
  • Measurable indicator proposed: 2 marks

Specific examples for woodland caribou:

  • Threat: habitat fragmentation from oil and gas roads; wolves use roads to access previously remote areas
  • Effects: decline of caribou (3rd/4th trophic level) → less prey for wolves in some areas; changes in lichen distribution (caribou food)
  • Action: road reclamation in critical habitat zones + "road-free" zones in designated caribou ranges → reconnects habitat, reduces wolf access
  • Measurable indicator: annual caribou calf survival rate (% calves surviving first winter); GPS-tracked population movements; wolf encounter rates in protected zones
