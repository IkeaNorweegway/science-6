Structures and Forces — Teacher Guide

Grade 7 Science — Teacher Guide

Read alongside: `_courses/grade-7-science/structures-forces/_context.md` and the student notes package.

Unit Overview

Suggested length: 5–6 weeks (15–18 classes) Tier: 2 — full depth on structures and simple machines; engineering design opportunities throughout Unit colour: Violet

What this unit is really about: Students think strength = material. The central conceptual move is: form matters as much as material. A cardboard tube beats a flat cardboard sheet. A triangulated frame beats a square frame. Shape is not decoration — it is structural engineering. The other major move is: simple machines don't give you free energy. They trade force for distance. This is conservation of energy made tangible.

Lesson Sequence

Week 1 — Types of Structures
Lesson 1 — Structure types: solid, frame, shell
Show Hold a flat sheet of paper horizontally — it collapses under its own weight. Roll it into a tube — now it holds a textbook. Same material, different form.
Say "The form of a structure changes what it can do. Shells are incredibly strong in compression along their curved surface — that's why a raw egg can hold a person standing on it if applied straight down. The shape matters more than the material."
Notes Section 1, structure types table.
Lesson 2 — Combination structures and Alberta examples
Show Images of the Lethbridge Viaduct (frame), Peace Bridge Calgary (arch/shell), an oil storage tank (shell cylinder), a house (frame + shell + solid foundation).
Say "Almost every real structure combines types because different parts have different needs. The frame carries lateral loads. The shell sheds wind and snow. The solid foundation resists compression from above."
Notes Section 1 continued. Exit Ticket 1.
Week 2 — Loads
Lesson 3 — Types of loads
Show A model bridge (or table). Ask: "What forces are acting on this right now?" Guide students toward: weight of the structure (dead), weight of what it carries (live), movement and lateral forces (dynamic).
Say "Dead load is always there — you can calculate and account for it. Live load varies — you design for the maximum. Dynamic load is the most dangerous because it can come from any direction and at any magnitude."
Notes Section 2, loads table.
Lesson 4 — Dynamic loads and resonance
Show Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse video (1940) — freely available, 3 minutes. Ask: "What was the wind speed? Was it a hurricane?" (No — it was moderate wind.)
Say "The bridge failed not because of an extreme wind, but because the wind matched the bridge's natural vibration frequency. Resonance — like pushing a swing at just the right moment — caused the oscillations to grow until the bridge tore itself apart. Modern bridges have stiffening trusses and open-mesh decks specifically to prevent this."
Notes Section 2 continued. Exit Ticket 2.
Week 3 — Internal Forces
Lesson 5 — Compression and tension
Show Use a foam block or sponge. Squeeze it from both ends: compression. Pull it from both ends: tension. A simple beam on two supports with a weight in the middle: push down on the top surface (compression) while the bottom surface is being stretched (tension).
Say "The same beam experiences compression on top and tension on the bottom simultaneously. This is why an I-beam has flanges — material where the compression and tension are greatest, removed from the neutral axis where neither force is large."
Notes Section 3, compression and tension.
Lesson 6 — Shear, torsion, and material selection
Show Shear: hold two sheets of paper together and try to slide them in opposite directions. Torsion: twist a foam noodle or pencil.
Say "Concrete is excellent in compression — stone arch bridges have lasted 2000 years. But tension? Plain concrete cracks and fails. That's why rebar is added. The steel handles what the concrete can't."
Notes Section 3 continued. Exit Ticket 3.
Week 4 — Stability and Design
Lesson 7 — Centre of gravity and stability
Show Balance point demonstration — find the centre of gravity of irregular shapes by balancing them on a finger or pencil. Racing car vs. bus stability demo (toy cars if available, or sketches).
Say "Stability is about the relationship between where the centre of gravity is and where the base of support is. Wide base and low centre of gravity = stable. Narrow base and high centre of gravity = unstable. This is why racing cars are low and wide."
Cue Address misconception: "Heavy bottom = always stable" — show a tall box with most weight at the top sitting on a narrow base; it topples despite being heavy at the bottom.
Notes Section 4, stability.
Lesson 8 — Triangles, arches, and failure modes
Show Build two frames from popsicle sticks and tape: one square, one triangular. Apply force to each. The square deforms into a rhombus; the triangle doesn't move.
Say "A triangle cannot deform without changing its side lengths. A square can become a rhombus without any side changing — which is why every bridge truss, every crane, every tower is built from triangles."
Notes Section 4 continued. Exit Ticket 4.
Week 5 — Simple Machines
Lesson 9 — Levers and mechanical advantage
Show Lever demonstration: 10 kg mass on one side of a metre stick balanced on a pencil (fulcrum). Move the fulcrum closer to the mass — how hard do you have to push on the other side?
Say "Mechanical advantage means you apply less force — but you apply it over more distance. The total work (force × distance) is constant. A machine doesn't create energy. It trades one kind of inconvenience (too much force) for another (more distance to travel)."
Cue Address the "machines create energy" misconception directly and immediately. Show the calculation: less force × more distance = same work.
Notes Section 5, levers and MA.
Lesson 10 — Other simple machines
Show Demonstrate: inclined plane (ramp vs. vertical lift with a luggage bag), pulley (single fixed vs. compound), wheel and axle (doorknob).
Say "Every simple machine is doing the same thing: trading force for distance. The inclined plane — a ramp — makes lifting a box easier by spreading the required force over a longer path."
Notes Section 5 continued. Exit Ticket 5.
Week 6 — Materials and Synthesis
Lesson 11 — Material properties and structural choices
Show Material sample collection if available: steel rod (bend test), wood (grain direction strength), concrete chunk, rubber band. What breaks? What deforms?
Say "Every material has a range of properties. Choosing the right material for the right job requires matching the property to the demand. Steel is flexible under seismic loads. Concrete is strong in compression and cheap. Wood is renewable and locally available in Alberta. These trade-offs matter."
Notes Section 6. Exit Ticket 6.
Lesson 12 — Bridge design challenge (1–2 classes)
• Format: small groups, limited materials (popsicle sticks, tape, index cards, or paper only)
• Challenge: span a 30 cm gap and support the maximum load
• Requirement: before building, sketch the structural type and explain which type(s) you chose and why
• After testing: identify the failure mode and explain it using internal force language
Lesson 13 — Retrieval practice
• Workbook parts independently, pair-check.
Lesson 14 — Synthesis and test

Misconception Management

Misconception When to address Strategy
Stronger material = stronger structure Lesson 1 opener Flat cardboard vs. tube demo; form matters as much as material
Heavy bottom = always stable Lesson 7 Show a top-heavy object on a narrow base toppling; use "centre of gravity" not weight
Machines create energy Lesson 9, before notes Calculate: MA = 3 means 3× force but must travel 3× the distance; total work is the same
Compression and tension are the same force Lesson 5 Sponge demos for both; beam diagram showing both occurring simultaneously

Lab and Activity Notes

Square vs. triangle frame (Lesson 8):

  • Popsicle sticks and tape or paper clips and drinking straws both work
  • Key: the square must be able to deform (don't reinforce corners); the triangle should be rigid
  • Have students measure the force needed to deform each — even rough estimates are convincing

Lever demonstration (Lesson 9):

  • A metre stick and pencil fulcrum is the simplest setup
  • Load: a full 1L water bottle or stack of textbooks
  • Move the fulcrum to 10 cm from the load — very little force needed on the other end but pushed 9× farther
  • Have students calculate MA each time you move the fulcrum

Bridge challenge materials per group:

  • 20 popsicle sticks, 50 cm of masking tape, 3 index cards
  • Or: 10 sheets of paper and 1 m of tape (harder constraint, more creative results)
  • Judges: load with pennies or weights until failure; record max load

Assessment Notes

Quiz (after Section 3): Structure types, loads, internal forces.

Test: All LOs. Must include: structure classification question, load/internal force identification, one MA calculation, one stability question.

Look for in extended response (Q21/Test): Students who conflate "stronger material" with "stronger structure" are holding the main unit misconception. Push them to describe the role of structural form explicitly.
