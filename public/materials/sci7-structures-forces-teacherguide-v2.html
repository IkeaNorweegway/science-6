Students think strength = material. The central conceptual move is: form matters as much as material. A cardboard tube beats a flat cardboard sheet. A triangulated frame beats a square frame. Shape is not decoration — it is structural engineering. The other major move is: simple machines don't give you free energy. They trade force for distance. This is conservation of energy made tangible.

Section 1 — Types of Structures

Lessons 1–2 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — Structure Types: Solid, Frame, Shell

Show

Board: Hold a flat sheet of paper horizontally — it collapses under its own weight. Roll it into a tube — now it holds a textbook. Same material, different form.
Ask: "What changed?" Let students articulate that nothing about the paper changed — only its shape.

Say

"The form of a structure changes what it can do. Shells are incredibly strong in compression along their curved surface — that's why a raw egg can hold a person standing on it if applied straight down. The shape matters more than the material."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 1: structure types table (solid, frame, shell) with examples and characteristics.

Misconception Flag — Stronger Material = Stronger Structure

Address in Lesson 1 opener before any notes. The flat-paper-vs-tube demonstration is the core evidence: same material, dramatically different structural performance. Structural form matters as much as material choice. Students who conflate material strength with structural strength are holding the main unit misconception — push them to describe the role of structural form explicitly in any extended response.

Beat 1.2 — Combination Structures and Alberta Examples

Show

Board: Images of Alberta structures:
Lethbridge Viaduct (frame) · Peace Bridge Calgary (arch/shell) · oil storage tank (shell cylinder) · a house (frame + shell + solid foundation).

Say

"Almost every real structure combines types because different parts have different needs. The frame carries lateral loads. The shell sheds wind and snow. The solid foundation resists compression from above."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 1 continued: combination structure examples. Exit Ticket 1.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Frame vs. shell: Frame: rigid skeleton with open spaces; Shell: thin curved outer surface that carries load through its shape.
  2. Classification: (a) egg = shell (b) bicycle = frame (c) concrete dam = solid.
  3. Combination structure: Example: a house (frame skeleton + shell exterior + solid foundation); reasons match the function of each part.
  4. Tube vs. flat sheet: The curved tube shape distributes force around the circumference; the flat sheet buckles under direct compression.

Section 2 — Loads

Lessons 3–4 2 beats 0 flags

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — Types of Loads

Show

Board: A model bridge (or table). Ask: "What forces are acting on this right now?" Guide students toward: weight of the structure itself (dead), weight of what it carries (live), movement and lateral forces (dynamic).

Say

"Dead load is always there — you can calculate and account for it. Live load varies — you design for the maximum. Dynamic load is the most dangerous because it can come from any direction and at any magnitude."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 2 loads table: dead, live, and dynamic loads with definitions and examples.

Beat 2.2 — Dynamic Loads and Resonance

Show

Board: Play Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse video (1940 — freely available, 3 minutes). Ask before starting: "What was the wind speed? Was it a hurricane?" (No — it was moderate wind.)

Say

"The bridge failed not because of an extreme wind, but because the wind matched the bridge's natural vibration frequency. Resonance — like pushing a swing at just the right moment — caused the oscillations to grow until the bridge tore itself apart. Modern bridges have stiffening trusses and open-mesh decks specifically to prevent this."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 2 continued: resonance definition, Tacoma Narrows as case study. Exit Ticket 2.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Dead vs. live load: Dead = structure's own weight (steel of a bridge); Live = loads the structure is designed to carry (cars, people).
  2. Why dynamic loads are dangerous: Can come from any direction, be unpredictable in magnitude, and cause resonance oscillations that grow over time.
  3. Roof collapse scenario: Snow accumulation = live load; no wind or seismic = dynamic loads ruled out; accumulated snow = excessive live load.
  4. Load classification: (a) dead (b) dynamic (c) dynamic (d) live.

Section 3 — Internal Forces

Lessons 5–6 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 5–6 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — Compression and Tension

Show

Board: Use a foam block or sponge. Squeeze it from both ends: compression (particles pushed together). Pull it from both ends: tension (particles pulled apart).
Then: a simple beam on two supports with a weight in the middle — show compression on top surface, tension on bottom surface simultaneously.

Say

"The same beam experiences compression on top and tension on the bottom simultaneously. This is why an I-beam has flanges — material where the compression and tension are greatest, removed from the neutral axis where neither force is large."

Cue — ✎ Draw + ★ Write

Students draw the beam diagram with compression/tension labels and fill Section 3 notes.

Misconception Flag — Compression and Tension Are the Same Force

Sponge demos for both — they are physically opposite. Then show the beam diagram with both occurring simultaneously in different parts of the same object. The I-beam shape is a direct engineering response to this understanding: more material where forces are greatest, less where they aren't.

Beat 3.2 — Shear, Torsion, and Material Selection

Show

Board: Shear: hold two sheets of paper together and try to slide them in opposite directions. Torsion: twist a foam noodle or pencil.
Then: material comparison — concrete (strong in compression, weak in tension) + rebar (steel handles tension).

Say

"Concrete is excellent in compression — stone arch bridges have lasted 2000 years. But tension? Plain concrete cracks and fails. That's why rebar is added. The steel handles what the concrete can't."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 3 continued: shear and torsion definitions, reinforced concrete as material selection example. Exit Ticket 3.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Compression vs. tension: Compression pushes material together (inward); tension pulls apart (outward); key difference: direction of force.
  2. Reinforced concrete: Plain concrete resists compression well but cracks under tension; steel rebar handles the tension that concrete cannot.
  3. Force identification: (a) tension (b) compression (c) shear.
  4. Beam diagram: Beam supported at both ends, load in middle; C marked on top surface; T marked on bottom surface.
  5. Stretch: I-beam under torsion: would twist and deform at the flanges, possible failure at welds; concrete under tension: brittle fracture — cracks and fails suddenly with little deformation warning.

Section 4 — Stability and Design

Lessons 7–8 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 7–8 · Student notes: Section 4

Beat 4.1 — Centre of Gravity and Stability

Show

Board: Balance point demonstration — find the centre of gravity of irregular shapes by balancing them on a finger or pencil.
Racing car vs. bus stability demo (toy cars or sketches): racing car (low, wide) vs. bus (high, narrow).

Say

"Stability is about the relationship between where the centre of gravity is and where the base of support is. Wide base and low centre of gravity = stable. Narrow base and high centre of gravity = unstable. This is why racing cars are low and wide."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 stability notes. Address misconception here.

Misconception Flag — Heavy Bottom = Always Stable

Show a tall box with most weight at the top sitting on a narrow base — it topples despite being heavy at the bottom. Stability requires low centre of gravity relative to the base of support. Weight distribution matters, but so does base width and centre of gravity height. Use "centre of gravity" language, not just "weight."

Beat 4.2 — Triangles, Arches, and Failure Modes

Show

Board: Build two frames from popsicle sticks and tape: one square, one triangular. Apply force to each.
The square deforms into a rhombus; the triangle doesn't move.

Say

"A triangle cannot deform without changing its side lengths. A square can become a rhombus without any side changing — which is why every bridge truss, every crane, every tower is built from triangles."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 continued: triangulation principle, arch mechanics. Exit Ticket 4. Prep: Popsicle sticks and tape or paper clips and drinking straws both work. Key: the square must be able to deform (don't reinforce corners).

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Toppling condition: Structure topples when its centre of gravity moves outside its base of support.
  2. Triangles vs. squares: Triangle cannot change shape (deform) without changing its side lengths; a square can become a rhombus without any length change — triangles resist lateral deformation that squares cannot.
  3. Racing car stability: Low body = low centre of gravity; same wheelbase (base of support); lower CoG relative to base = more stable.
  4. How to increase stability: Widen the base; lower the centre of gravity (place heavy components lower in the structure).
  5. Stretch: Small load: CoG within base, crane stable; large load at far end: CoG shifts toward load end; if CoG passes beyond edge of base → toppling. Base design: wide outriggers/counterweights on opposite side.

Section 5 — Simple Machines

Lessons 9–10 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 9–10 · Student notes: Section 5

Beat 5.1 — Levers and Mechanical Advantage

Show

Board: Lever demonstration — 10 kg mass on one side of a metre stick balanced on a pencil (fulcrum). Move the fulcrum closer to the mass — ask: "How hard do you have to push on the other side now?"
Calculate: MA = load arm / effort arm. Show that less force × more distance = same work.

Say

"Mechanical advantage means you apply less force — but you apply it over more distance. The total work (force × distance) is constant. A machine doesn't create energy. It trades one kind of inconvenience (too much force) for another (more distance to travel)."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5 levers and MA. Address the machines-create-energy misconception directly and immediately here — show the calculation: MA = 3 means 3× less force but 3× more distance; total work is the same.

Misconception Flag — Machines Create Energy

Address in Lesson 9 before notes. Calculate explicitly: MA = 3 means you apply 1/3 the force but must push 3× the distance. Total work (force × distance) is constant — the machine redistributes it but does not create it. This is conservation of energy made tangible. Students who think machines "help" by adding energy have not understood the unit's physics.

Beat 5.2 — Other Simple Machines

Show

Board: Demonstrate three machines:
1. Inclined plane — ramp vs. vertical lift with a luggage bag
2. Pulley — single fixed vs. compound pulley comparison
3. Wheel and axle — doorknob demonstration

Say

"Every simple machine is doing the same thing: trading force for distance. The inclined plane — a ramp — makes lifting a box easier by spreading the required force over a longer path."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5 continued: inclined plane, pulley, wheel and axle with MA explanations. Exit Ticket 5.

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Mechanical advantage: MA = output force / input force; MA of 3 means output force is 3x input force (applied over 3x the distance).
  2. Machine identification: (a) inclined plane (b) 3rd class lever (c) single fixed pulley (d) wheel and axle (jar lid = screw).
  3. MA calculation: MA = 200 / 50 = 4.
  4. Do machines create energy? No — work is conserved; MA of 4 means 1/4 the force but 4x the distance; total work (force x distance) is the same; machines do not create energy.
  5. Stretch: MA = 600 / 150 = 4 rope segments; diagram shows 4 ropes supporting the lower movable pulley.

Section 6 — Materials and Design

Lessons 11–13 2 beats 1 pacing flag

Lessons 11–13 · Student notes: Section 6

Beat 6.1 — Material Properties and Structural Choices

Show

Board: Material sample collection if available: steel rod (bend test), wood (grain direction strength), concrete chunk, rubber band. What breaks? What deforms?
Show trade-offs: steel (flexible under seismic loads) · concrete (strong in compression, cheap) · wood (renewable, locally available in Alberta).

Say

"Every material has a range of properties. Choosing the right material for the right job requires matching the property to the demand. These trade-offs matter."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 6: material properties table and trade-off analysis. Exit Ticket 6.

Beat 6.2 — Bridge Design Challenge

Show

Board: Challenge parameters:
• Small groups, limited materials (popsicle sticks, tape, index cards, or paper only)
• Challenge: span a 30 cm gap and support the maximum load
• Before building: sketch the structural type and explain which type(s) you chose and why
• After testing: identify the failure mode and explain it using internal force language

Say

"Before you build anything, sketch it and explain your structural reasoning. After it fails — and it will — explain the failure using the vocabulary of internal forces."

Cue — ★ Write + ⊡ Think

Students complete pre-build sketch and justification. Post-test: written failure analysis using compression, tension, shear, or torsion language.

Pacing Flag — Bridge Challenge Needs 1–2 Full Classes

Plan the pre-build sketch and justification as homework or first 15 minutes of class 1. Building and testing takes a full period. Failure analysis can be done while other groups are still testing. Judges: load with pennies or weights until failure; record max load per group. Materials per group: 20 popsicle sticks, 50 cm masking tape, 3 index cards — or 10 sheets of paper and 1 m of tape (harder constraint, more creative results).

Section 6 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Strength vs. hardness: Strength: resists breaking under force; Hardness: resists surface damage (scratching/denting) — a material can be hard but brittle.
  2. Earthquake building material: Steel is flexible; deforms rather than shattering under sudden lateral seismic forces; concrete is brittle — cracks catastrophically.
  3. Property matching: (a) tensile strength (b) compressive strength (c) flexibility/impact resistance.
  4. Stretch: Wood advantages: locally available (Alberta forests), renewable, good strength-to-weight ratio, workable; limitations: susceptible to fire, rot, insects; requires treatment for moisture; modern mass timber (CLT) viable for up to ~18 storeys.
