MISCONCEPTION

"Top predators like wolves and eagles have the most energy because they eat everything below them." Why this is wrong: At each level, about 90% of the energy is lost. By the time you reach the top predator, only a tiny fraction of the original energy is available. Top predators are energy-poor compared to producers. This is why top predators need large territories — there simply isn't much energy available at the top.

MISCONCEPTION

"Nature always recovers — it's resilient." Why this is wrong: Ecosystems have tipping points — thresholds beyond which recovery does not happen. Examples: once a coral reef bleaches repeatedly, the structural community collapses and does not rebuild on human timescales; once grassland is broken by cultivation, returning it to native prairie takes decades of active restoration; once a species goes extinct, it is gone permanently. Resilience is real but limited.

Invasive species are organisms introduced to an ecosystem where they did not evolve. Because they often lack   predators, their populations can grow unchecked and outcompete or prey on native species.

Alberta at-risk invasive species:

  • Zebra mussels — not yet established; prevention program at borders; would devastate native bivalves and clog water infrastructure
  • Purple loosestrife — wetland plant; creates dense monocultures that eliminate native vegetation and waterfowl nesting habitat
