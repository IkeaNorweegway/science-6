Heat and Temperature — Teacher Guide

Grade 7 Science — Teacher Guide

Read alongside: `_courses/grade-7-science/heat-temperature/_context.md` and the student notes package.

Unit Overview

Suggested length: 5–6 weeks (15–18 classes) Tier: 1 — full depth; three major misconceptions to address explicitly Unit colour: Orange

What this unit is really about: This unit has the densest misconception load in Grade 7 Science. Students bring strong intuitive (but wrong) beliefs about heat: that cold is a substance, that metal is colder than wood, that heat and temperature are the same. The first three weeks are largely misconception correction before building the correct conceptual framework. Don't rush past this — a student who "knows" all three modes of heat transfer but still believes cold travels has not learned the unit.

Lesson Sequence

Week 1 — Temperature and Measurement
Lesson 1 — What is temperature?
Show Two beakers of water — one at 20°C, one at 80°C. Ask: "What is different about these two samples at the particle level?" Hear answers. Guide toward particle speed.
Say "Temperature is a number that tells you how fast the particles in a substance are moving on average. That's all it is. Faster particles = higher temperature."
Notes Section 1, temperature and Celsius scale.
Lesson 2 — The Kelvin scale and absolute zero
Show Ask: "What happens if we keep cooling something? Can we cool it forever?" Build toward absolute zero as a concept: the coldest possible temperature, where particles theoretically stop.
Say "The Kelvin scale starts at the actual bottom. There is no negative Kelvin because there is no 'below stopped.' Celsius is convenient for daily use; Kelvin is what physics needs because it starts at zero, not at an arbitrary point."
Notes Section 1 continued, Kelvin scale, conversions. Exit Ticket 1.
Week 2 — Heat vs. Temperature
Lesson 3 — The misconception (CRITICAL)
Show Two containers: a cup of water at 90°C and a bathtub full of water at 90°C (describe this, don't actually have both). Ask: "Which has more heat energy?"
• Let students respond — most will say "same temperature = same heat." Then: "If you spilled the cup on your hand versus fell into the bathtub — which causes more harm? Why, if they're both 90°C?"
Say "Heat is not the same as temperature. Temperature tells you how fast particles are moving. Heat is the total energy transferred — which depends on how many particles there are AND how fast they're moving. The bathtub has trillions more particles. Same temperature, radically more heat."
Lesson 4 — "Cold comes in through the window"
Show Ask students: "On a cold winter day, do you feel cold air come in through window cracks?" All will say yes. Then: "Is cold a substance that travels?"
Say "Cold is not a substance. Nothing called 'cold' moves from outside to inside. What actually happens is that warm air from inside your house flows out through the crack — you lose heat, not gain cold. The door is bleeding heat, not inhaling cold."
Cue Have students rephrase "the cold is coming in" in the correct direction: "heat is going out."
Notes Section 2. Exit Ticket 2.
Week 3 — Particle Model and Heat
Lesson 5 — Expansion and contraction
Show Railroad track photo showing buckled track in summer heat. Ball-and-ring demonstration (if available): metal ball passes through ring at room temperature, doesn't fit after being heated.
Say "When you add heat to a solid, particles move faster and push farther apart. The material takes up more space. This is why every bridge, road, and railway has expansion gaps — not as an accident, but as a required design feature."
Notes Section 3, thermal expansion.
Lesson 6 — Changes of state
Show Set up a heating curve demonstration: heat ice (crushed, in a beaker), record temperature every minute. Plot on board as students call out readings. Observe the plateau at 0°C and again at 100°C.
Say "The temperature isn't rising during the plateau because the heat is being used to break the bonds between particles, not to speed them up. All the energy goes into the structural change, not the speed change."
Notes Section 3 continued. Exit Ticket 3.
Week 4 — Heat Transfer Modes
Lesson 7 — Conduction (and the metal misconception)
Show Set up metal desk and wooden desk at the same temperature (measure with thermometer to confirm). Have students touch both. Record: "which felt colder?"
Say "They're the same temperature. This is the most testable misconception in the unit. Touch both. Read both with a thermometer. They're the same. The metal felt colder because it conducted heat AWAY from your hand faster. Your hand is the warm object. You measured your own heat loss."
Notes Section 4, conduction. This is the misconception callout box — read it aloud.
Lesson 8 — Convection
Show Food colouring demonstration — drop red food colouring into very hot water in a clear container; drop blue into cold water. Watch the movement patterns. Alternatively: paper spiral held above a heat source rotates from convection.
Say "Convection only works in fluids — liquids and gases. The fluid circulates because hot fluid is less dense and rises while cool fluid sinks. This is how your house heats — baseboard heaters warm air near the floor, which rises, which pushes cool air down."
Notes Section 4, convection.
Lesson 9 — Radiation
Show Hold your hand near (but not touching) a hot lamp or heat lamp. Feel the warmth from a distance. No contact, no air movement.
Say "Radiation requires no medium at all. It's electromagnetic energy — the same family as light. This is how the Sun heats Earth across 150 million km of vacuum. No air, no contact, no fluid. Just electromagnetic waves."
Notes Section 4, radiation. Exit Ticket 4.
Week 5 — Specific Heat and Applications
Lesson 10 — Specific heat capacity
Show Two identical containers, one with water, one with sand. Put both in sunlight (or under a heat lamp) for 10 minutes. Measure temperature of both before and after. Sand heats up dramatically more.
Say "Water requires about four times more energy to raise its temperature by 1 degree compared to most metals. This has enormous consequences for climate, engineering, and daily life. The ocean is a giant heat battery."
Notes Section 5. Exit Ticket 5.
Lesson 11 — Insulation and applications
Show Insulation challenge setup (if doing the inquiry): which material keeps ice frozen longest?
Say "Insulation doesn't make things warm. It slows heat transfer — in either direction. A cooler bag and a thermos both work on the same principle. The insulation doesn't generate cold for one and warmth for the other — it slows the flow in both cases."
Cue Alberta building context — show R-value requirements for Alberta vs. BC. Ask: "Why are they different?"
Notes Section 6. Exit Ticket 6.
Lesson 12 — Thermal pollution
Show Map of thermal plume from a power plant near a river. Temperature data upstream vs. downstream. Dissolved oxygen vs. temperature graph.
Say "Warm water holds less oxygen than cold water. Adding heat to a river is not a neutral act — it lowers the oxygen level that fish and invertebrates need. This is a form of pollution even though no chemicals are added."

Misconception Management

Misconception When to address Strategy
Cold is a substance that travels Lesson 4, before notes Ask students to describe the experience; then reframe: heat leaves, cold doesn't arrive
Metal is colder than wood Lesson 7, conduction opener Touch and measure demonstration; make the misconception fail in real time
Heat and temperature are the same Lesson 3, before notes Cup-and-bathtub thought experiment; get the wrong answer on the board first, then correct
Radiation needs a medium Lesson 9, radiation section Sun-to-Earth example; ask "then how does the sun warm us?" before explaining
Insulation keeps things warm Lesson 11, insulation section Cooler bag example; insulation slows heat flow in both directions

Lab and Activity Notes

Heating curve lab (Lesson 6):

  • Use crushed ice for a faster phase change
  • Record temperature every 60 seconds
  • Students graph as they go (time on x-axis, temperature on y-axis)
  • The 0°C plateau typically lasts 5–8 minutes; the 100°C plateau is harder to sustain in an open beaker but should be visible
  • If graphing in real time is too slow, assign the graphing as post-lab work

Metal-wood conduction demo (Lesson 7):

  • Objects must have been in the room long enough to equilibrate to room temperature (ideally left out overnight)
  • Use the same thermometer to measure both before the touch test
  • Student who is most convinced metal "must be colder" is the best candidate to do the touch-then-measure sequence

Sand-water specific heat demo (Lesson 10):

  • Use identical volume of sand and water in identical clear containers
  • Consistent distance from heat source
  • Measure initial temperature, then after 5 and 10 minutes
  • Expected: sand increases ~4–6°C; water increases ~1–2°C

Assessment Notes

Quiz (after Section 3): Heat vs. temperature, particle model, expansion/contraction.

Test: All LOs. Pay special attention to the heat-vs-temperature question (LO 7-3-01) — if a student still conflates them after instruction, the unit's central concept has not landed.

Pre-Knowledge Check diagnostic: Questions 1 and 2 are your best indicators. If Q2 ("metal vs. wood") remains "metal is colder" on the synthesis page, the conduction lesson did not correct the misconception.
