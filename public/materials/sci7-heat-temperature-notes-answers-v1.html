Section 1: Temperature and Measurement

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Temperature =   kinetic energy average
Kinetic energy means energy of movement
When particles move faster, temperature   increases
Water freezes at 0°C
Water boils at 100°C
Normal body temperature 37°C
Room temperature ~20–22°C
Alberta January ~−15 to −25°C (range acceptable)
Kelvin used in scientific contexts
0 K = absolute zero = −273°C
Conversion formula K = °C + 273

Conversion table answers:

0°C = 273 K | 100°C = 373 K | −40°C = 233 K | 20°C = 293 K | 310 K = 37°C

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Temperature measures the average kinetic energy (speed) of particles in a substance
  2. 25°C = 298 K; −10°C = 263 K
  3. Absolute zero (0 K / −273°C) is the temperature where particles theoretically stop moving; can't go lower because there's nothing left to remove
  4. Liquid expands (particles move farther apart) when heated → rises in tube; calibrated scale converts height to temperature

Section 2: Heat vs. Temperature

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Bathtub at 80°C vs. cup at 80°C: bathtub has more heat energy (more particles, same speed)
Heat flows from hotter to cooler
Heat stops when thermal equilibrium is reached (same temperature)
"Cold comes in" correction heat leaves; cold is not a substance
When cold wind hits you your body loses heat to the cold moving air faster

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Heat: energy transferred between objects at different temperatures; Temperature: average kinetic energy of particles
  2. Heat flows from warm to cool; when both reach same temperature, heat transfer stops (thermal equilibrium)
  3. Swimming pool — has far more particles at same temperature; total kinetic energy is much larger
  4. Correct: "You're letting the heat out" — heat energy is leaving through the open door; nothing called "cold" is entering
  5. Stretch: Spoon gets warm from hot soup: conduction — hot soup particles transfer energy to spoon particles; spoon cools on plate: heat flows from warm spoon to cooler ceramic plate; stops when both reach same temperature

Section 3: Particle Model and Heat

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Particles move   when heated faster; spread farther apart
Thermal expansion definition material expands as particles spread apart when heated
Railroad tracks — gaps because tracks expand in summer heat; without gaps, they buckle
Bridges — expansion joints allow expansion/contraction without cracking
Power lines — hung slack because contract in cold and could snap if too tight
Melting: solid → liquid heat absorbed; particles overcome attractive forces
Freezing: liquid → solid heat released
Boiling/evaporation heat absorbed; particles escape liquid entirely
Condensation heat released; particles slow enough to attract and form liquid
Temperature plateau during phase change heat goes into breaking bonds, not speeding particles

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. When heated, particles absorb energy, vibrate/move faster, push farther apart from each other → material occupies more space
  2. In summer heat, steel expands; without gap, track has nowhere to go and buckles sideways (compression failure)
  3. All heat energy is going into breaking the bonds between ice molecules (change of state); none is available to increase particle speed; temperature stays at 0°C until all ice has melted
  4. Cold solid: particles close together, small vibration arrows; warm solid: particles slightly farther apart, larger vibration arrows
  5. Stretch: ΔT = 35 − (−40) = 75°C; expansion = 2000 m × 0.0000012 × 75 = 0.18 m = 18 cm (approximately)

Section 4: Modes of Heat Transfer

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Conduction — particle-to-particle in solids (direct contact)
Good conductors metals (copper, aluminum, steel)
Poor conductors (insulators) wood, rubber, foam, air
Metal feels colder because conducts heat away from your hand faster
Both metal and wood at same temperature (room temperature)
Convection — heat transfer through movement of fluids (liquids and gases)
Hot fluid  , cool fluid   rises; sinks
Convection only works in liquids and gases (not solids — solids can't circulate)
Radiation — electromagnetic   waves; no medium required
Radiation travels through vacuum
Dark/matte surfaces absorb radiation better
Light/shiny surfaces reflect radiation

Comparison table: Conduction: medium yes, solids yes, fluids yes, vacuum no; Convection: medium yes, solids no, fluids yes, vacuum no; Radiation: medium no, solids yes, fluids yes, vacuum yes

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Campfire warmth from 2m: radiation; spoon in hot soup: conduction; hot air rising from radiator: convection
  2. Radiation is electromagnetic energy — it doesn't need matter to travel; it IS energy moving through space as waves
  3. Both at 22°C; metal conducts heat away from your hand ~20× faster than wood; your hand loses heat quickly → feels cold; wood conducts so slowly your hand barely loses heat → feels warm
  4. Convection requires fluid to circulate; solid particles are fixed in position and can't move past each other; only conduction works in solids
  5. Stretch: Vacuum layer blocks conduction and convection (no medium); shiny inner walls reflect radiation back in (reduces radiation loss); plastic cap blocks conduction at the top opening

Section 5: Specific Heat Capacity

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Specific heat capacity — amount of heat to raise   by   1 gram by 1°C
Water heats up   than metals slower (requires more energy per gram per degree)
Water relative heat very high
Metals relative heat low
Water as coolant absorbs large amounts of heat without large temperature change
Oceans act as   thermal reservoir / heat battery
Inland Alberta vs. coastal BC larger temperature swings (no ocean to moderate)

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Specific heat: amount of energy needed to raise 1g of substance by 1°C; water has higher specific heat than iron
  2. Water can absorb huge amounts of heat with little temperature change → effectively removes heat from engine
  3. Sand has very low specific heat; heats rapidly in sunlight; water has very high specific heat; heats slowly despite same solar input
  4. Stretch: Pacific Ocean acts as thermal reservoir; stores summer heat, releases it slowly in winter → Vancouver stays mild; Calgary has no large water body; air over prairies heats and cools rapidly → extreme swings

Section 6: Insulation and Applications

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Insulation works by slowing heat transfer in both directions
Trapped air slows conduction (air is poor conductor) and convection (bubbles too small to circulate)
Vacuum blocks conduction and convection (no medium)
Shiny surfaces block radiation (reflect instead of absorb)
R-value = thermal resistance; higher = better insulator
Alberta building code higher R-value because colder winters; greater temperature differential
Thermal pollution: warm water → lower dissolved oxygen → harms fish and aquatic invertebrates
Insulation doesn't create heat/cold it only slows the rate of heat transfer in both directions

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Insulation slows heat transfer; it does NOT generate warmth; a thermos with hot coffee and a cooler with ice both work by slowing heat flow — just in opposite situations
  2. Alberta: −40°C winters; BC: −5 to −10°C; greater temperature difference in Alberta = faster heat loss = higher insulation required
  3. Thermal pollution: industrial/power plant warm water discharged into rivers; warm water holds less dissolved oxygen; reduced O₂ kills fish, invertebrates, disrupts aquatic food chains
  4. Stretch: Outer layer: shiny (reflect radiation); middle layer: vacuum or foam (block conduction and convection); cold packs inside; insulated lid (block conduction through top)
