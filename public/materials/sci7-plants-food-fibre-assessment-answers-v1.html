Q11 (6 marks — 2 per tenet):

  1. All living things are made of cells — important: explains unity of all life; basis of medicine
  2. Cells are the basic unit of life — important: all vital processes occur in cells
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells — important: explains reproduction, refutes spontaneous generation

Q12 (4 marks — 1 per correctly labelled structure with function):

  • Thick rigid outer layer: cell wall — structural support and shape
  • Thin inner layer: cell membrane — controls what enters/exits
  • Dark round body: nucleus — contains DNA; controls cell
  • Green ovals: chloroplasts — site of photosynthesis
  • Large central area: vacuole — stores water, maintains pressure

Q13 (3 marks):

Xylem — it transports water (and anything dissolved in water, including dye) from roots upward to leaves

Q14 (4 marks):

CO₂ + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen

CO₂ from: air through stomata; Water from: soil via roots; Light from: sun

Glucose goes to: plant cells for energy/growth; stored as starch; Oxygen goes to: atmosphere

Q15 (3 marks):

Plants make their own food via photosynthesis using CO₂ from air and water from soil; soil provides only minerals (water and dissolved minerals); van Helmont's experiment showed the plant gained 74 kg while soil lost only 57 grams — the mass came from air and water, not soil

Q16 (8 marks): Plant cell with 5 structures: cell wall (support), cell membrane (selective permeability), central vacuole (water storage/turgor), chloroplast (photosynthesis), nucleus (DNA/control) — 2 marks per structure: 1 label + 1 function

Q17 (4 marks):

CO₂ + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen

Importance: photosynthesis converts solar energy into chemical energy (glucose) that ALL consumers depend on; it is the energy input for virtually every food chain on Earth; without it, no producers, no consumers

Q18 (4 marks):

a) Sugars (glucose) made by photosynthesis

b) Roots harmed first — roots have no chloroplasts; cannot photosynthesize; rely entirely on sugars delivered by phloem; without sugar delivery, roots starve

Q19 (4 marks):

  • Sexual advantage: genetic variation; offspring can adapt to changing conditions; disease resistant individuals survive
  • Asexual advantage: rapid colonization; no pollinator needed; all offspring vigorous (clone of successful parent)
  • Asexual reproduction would be better for rapidly colonizing bare soil after fire: fast establishment, no need to wait for pollinator visits, rapid spread via runners/rhizomes (e.g., fireweed)

Part C: Application (15 marks)

Q20 — van Helmont (7 marks):

a) Plants do not get their mass from soil; most mass comes from CO₂ in air and water

b) Soil provided dissolved minerals (tiny amounts) and water

c) Carbon from CO₂ in air; hydrogen and oxygen from water (H₂O)

d) The belief that plants eat soil is disproven; plants build their mass from light, air, and water; soil is the anchor and mineral source, not the food source

Q21 — Biotechnology comparison (8 marks):

a) Option A advantages: faster (traits achieved in years, not decades); more precise (specific gene changed, not whole genome)

b) Option B advantages: proven safe over long history; no regulatory hurdles; public acceptance; maintains genetic diversity within species

c) Option A concern: gene may spread to related wild plants (gene flow); unintended effects on non-pest insects; Option B concern: very slow — 10–15 years while pest destroys crops; may reduce genetic diversity if only drought-tolerant lines kept

d) Option A recommended for 3-year timeline; selective breeding cannot deliver in 3 years; GMO precision allows targeted solution; regulatory approval may be possible if fast-tracked; justify by urgency of crop loss

Part D: Extended Response (10 marks)

Rubric — Pollinator decline essay:

  • States how declining bees affect food crops (with Alberta crop examples): 3 marks
  • Connects pollinator decline to monoculture: 2 marks
  • Proposes one solution from unit knowledge: 2 marks
  • Uses correct scientific vocabulary: 1 mark
  • Clear, logically organized response: 2 marks

Sample answer: Alberta's canola (pollinated by bees) and pulse crops (peas, lentils — partially bee-dependent) would experience significant yield loss without bee pollinators; bees transfer pollen from stamen to pistil, enabling fertilization and seed/fruit production; without this, canola cannot produce oil-bearing seeds. Monoculture farming contributes to pollinator decline: large fields of one crop bloom for a short window then offer nothing; bees need diverse flowering plants throughout the season; monocultures eliminate the wildflower diversity bees need between crop blooms. One solution: using selective breeding to develop crops that attract a wider range of wild pollinators (not just honeybees), combined with leaving wildflower strips along field edges to maintain pollinator populations between crop flowering periods.
