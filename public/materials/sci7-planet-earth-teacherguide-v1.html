Planet Earth — Teacher Guide

Grade 7 Science — Teacher Guide

Read alongside: `_courses/grade-7-science/planet-earth/_context.md` and the student notes package.

Unit Overview

Suggested length: 4–5 weeks (12–15 classes) Tier: 2 — full depth; strong Alberta context required throughout Unit colour: Amber

What this unit is really about: Two conceptual moves. First: Earth is dynamic — everything we see is a result of slow, ongoing processes, not a fixed backdrop. Second: we can read the past from the present — rock layers, fossils, and seismic data are all recordings of geological history. Alberta is one of the best places on Earth to teach this unit because the evidence is local: Rockies, Badlands, oil sands, glacial erratics. This unit should feel grounded in place, not abstract.

Lesson Sequence

Week 1 — Earth's Interior
Lesson 1 — What's inside Earth?
Show Pre-knowledge check question: "Draw what you think Earth's interior looks like." Have students sketch this quickly. You'll see everything from solid all the way through, to a hollow Earth, to a fiery core with no structure.
Say "We have never drilled to the mantle. The deepest hole ever drilled (Kola Superdeep, Russia) reached 12 km after 24 years. The mantle begins at 30–70 km down. Everything we know about Earth's interior comes from seismic waves — the 'echoes' of earthquakes."
Notes Section 1, Earth's layers.
Lesson 2 — Layers and their properties
Show Cross-section diagram of Earth. Ask students to predict: "Why is the inner core solid but the outer core is liquid?" Let them reason from what they know about temperature and pressure.
Say "The inner core is hotter than the outer core, but it's under so much pressure that the atoms can't escape into a liquid state. Pressure keeps it solid even at extreme temperatures. This is also why diamonds form deep underground."
Cue "How does the liquid outer core produce the magnetic field?" — brief, no equations: moving liquid metal conducts electricity; moving electricity generates magnetism.
• Exit Ticket 1.
Week 2 — The Rock Cycle
Lesson 3 — Three families of rock
Show Rock samples — granite, basalt, sandstone, shale, marble, quartzite. Pass around. Ask students to classify by texture and appearance before they know the names.
Say "Every rock on Earth is one of three types. And every rock can become any other type given the right conditions. There is no permanent rock — just rock in different stages of a continuous cycle."
Notes Section 2, rock families.
Lesson 4 — The rock cycle and Alberta's rocks
Show Photos of Alberta's rock types: oil sands (sedimentary), Rockies limestone and quartzite, Badlands shale. Ask: "What does each tell us about what Alberta looked like when it formed?"
Say "Alberta's Badlands are Cretaceous sedimentary rock — formed at the bottom of an inland sea 75 million years ago. The sediments that make up the Rockies were once seafloor. Alberta's oil sands are the compressed remains of marine organisms. Alberta's rocks are a deep-time record of this place."
Notes Section 2 continued. Exit Ticket 2.
Week 3 — Plate Tectonics
Lesson 5 — The evidence for moving plates
Show Outline map of the continents. Have students cut them out (or trace and rearrange) to see how South America and Africa fit together. Show the fossil distribution map: same species on continents now separated by thousands of km of ocean.
Say "Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912 and was ridiculed. Scientists asked: 'What force could move continents?' The answer came 50 years later: mantle convection. The evidence was right. The mechanism was missing."
Notes Section 3, plate tectonic evidence.
Lesson 6 — Three boundary types
Show Animated plate boundary diagram (or draw on board step by step). At each boundary, ask: "What happens when plates move this way?"
Say "The Rockies formed when an oceanic plate pushed under North America. The plates are moving today — at about the same rate your fingernails grow. In 50 million years, Alberta will look very different."
Cue "Alberta is far from active plate boundaries today. But its rocks record a convergent collision. When?" — build toward Cretaceous context.
Notes Section 3 continued. Exit Ticket 3.
Week 4 — Earthquakes, Volcanoes, and Surface Processes
Lesson 7 — Earthquakes
Show Richter scale — logarithmic. Ask: "How many times more energy does a magnitude 7 release than a magnitude 6?" (10×) "Than a 5?" (100×). The largest earthquakes release more energy than all the world's nuclear weapons combined.
Say "Earthquakes happen when rock that has been locked — held in place by friction while plates try to move — suddenly slips. The energy stored over decades or centuries releases in seconds. This is why the ground can be perfectly still for 50 years and then shake violently."
Notes Section 4, earthquakes.
Lesson 8 — Volcanoes and surface processes
Show Shield vs. composite volcano comparison. Show lava viscosity demonstration: thick honey vs. thin water in a tilted container.
Say "Lava viscosity determines whether a volcano is gentle or explosive. Fluid lava releases gas as it goes — gradual flow. Thick lava traps gas until the pressure overcomes the resistance — explosion."
Cue Alberta weathering: "What has been happening to Alberta's surface since the glaciers left?"
Notes Sections 4 and 5. Exit Ticket 4 and 5.
Week 5 — Fossils, Geological Time, and Review
Lesson 9 — Fossils and geological time
Show Images of Alberta Badlands, Royal Tyrrell Museum, hoodoos. Ask: "What does this landscape tell us about Alberta's past?"
Say "The Badlands expose 75-million-year-old Cretaceous sedimentary rock. Alberta was covered by a shallow inland sea. The marine and dinosaur fossils found there are the remains of organisms that lived in and around that sea. When you walk through the Badlands, you're walking through 75 million years of geological time, exposed by 10,000 years of river erosion."
Notes Section 6, fossils and geological time.
Lesson 10 — Reading rock layers
Show Rock column diagram with multiple layers. Have students use law of superposition to interpret the history. Add an angular unconformity (tilted layer) for extension.
Say "Rock layers are like pages in a history book — the oldest pages are at the bottom. Geologists can read Alberta's entire geological history from the layers exposed in the Badlands, the Rocky Mountains, and in well cores from oil and gas exploration."
• Exit Ticket 6.

Lessons 11–12 — Retrieval practice and synthesis

• Workbook geological time questions and Alberta applications
• Unit synthesis from memory; return to Pre-Knowledge Check
Lesson 13 — Test

Misconception Management

Misconception When to address Strategy
Plates are the same as continents Lesson 6 Draw a world map showing plate boundaries — most are underwater; Pacific Plate is almost entirely ocean
Geological change is always fast/catastrophic Lesson 5 + Lesson 8 Fingernail rate of plate movement; calculate: 3 cm/year × 100 million years = 3,000 km
Alberta has no geological history Lessons 4 and 9 Use Alberta rocks and landforms throughout; oil sands, Badlands, Rockies are all in this unit
Fossils are mostly dinosaur bones Lesson 9 Show variety: marine shells, fish scales, plant imprints, insect in amber, microfossils; Alberta Cambrian/Cretaceous connection

Lab and Activity Notes

Rock identification activity (Lesson 3):

  • Rock kits available from science supply companies; or collect your own
  • Must-haves: granite, basalt, sandstone, shale, limestone, marble, quartzite
  • Have students develop their own classification before you introduce the three families
  • Connect each sample to an Alberta location if possible

Continental fit activity (Lesson 5):

  • Print a map with only coastlines — no country names, no political borders
  • Students cut and reassemble; show them the mid-Atlantic ridge location after
  • Extension: show paleomagnetic striping image alongside the puzzle

Rock column interpretation (Lesson 10):

  • Draw a 6-layer column with: one unconformity, one igneous intrusion cutting through layers, one fold
  • Each element has a different "rule" for interpretation (intrusion is younger than what it cuts; folds happen after deposition)
  • This is extension level for most Grade 7 students — use as challenge only

Alberta Context — Must Appear in This Unit

At minimum, use these Alberta-specific examples in class:

  1. Rocky Mountains — Lesson 6 (plate tectonics) and Lesson 7 (earthquakes: Alberta is quiet, but Rockies show tectonic past)
  2. Badlands (Drumheller) — Lessons 4, 9, 10 (sedimentary rock, Cretaceous fossils, river erosion)
  3. Oil sands — Lesson 4 (sedimentary origin) and Lesson 9 (Cretaceous inland sea)
  4. Glacial erratics — Lesson 8 (glaciation, deposition)
  5. Athabasca Sand Dunes — Lesson 8 (wind erosion, aeolian deposition)

Assessment Notes

Quiz (after Section 3): Earth's interior, rock cycle, plate tectonics.

Test: All six LOs. Must include: rock cycle diagram question, plate boundary identification, Alberta geological context question.

Extended response watch: Students who cannot connect Alberta's landscape to geological processes (Rockies = plate tectonics, Badlands = sedimentary erosion, prairies = glaciation) have not achieved LO 7-5-05 or 7-5-06 in the Alberta context. The Alberta frame is not a nice-to-have — it's an outcome.
