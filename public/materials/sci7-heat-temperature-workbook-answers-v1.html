Q2 — What temperature measures: (2 marks)

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles (atoms or molecules) in a substance. Higher temperature = particles moving faster on average.

Q3 — No negative Kelvin: (2 marks)

Kelvin starts at absolute zero — the point where particles have minimum kinetic energy (essentially no movement). You cannot have less energy than zero kinetic energy; therefore you cannot go below 0 K. The scale measures energy, not just a relative comparison.

Q4 — Who is right: (4 marks)

Both are right — they are using different temperature scales. 100°C = 373 K (K = °C + 273). Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius or 373 Kelvin — these are the same physical temperature expressed in two different units. Neither student is wrong; they should specify their scale to avoid confusion.

Part B: Heat vs. Temperature (20 marks)

Q5 — Cup and bathtub analogy: (4 marks)

Temperature: the cup of coffee and the bathtub might be at the same temperature (both at 40°C) — average particle speed is equal in both. Heat: the bathtub has enormously more particles at that same speed; total kinetic energy (= heat) is vastly greater. Temperature is "how fast"; heat is "how much total energy." Same average speed, very different total amounts.

Q6 — Cup vs. swimming pool: (3 marks)

The swimming pool has more heat energy. Both are at 40°C, so particles are moving at the same average speed. But the pool contains trillions more water molecules than the cup. Total heat energy = number of particles × average kinetic energy per particle. With the same "per particle" value but far more particles, the pool stores an incomparably larger total amount of heat energy.

Q7 — Direction of heat flow: (3 marks)

Heat always flows from a region of higher temperature to a region of lower temperature (hot → cold). When two objects reach the same temperature, heat flow stops — they are in thermal equilibrium. Net energy transfer = zero at equilibrium.

Q8 — Scientific rewrite: (4 marks)

Wrong: "The cold is coming in through the window."

Correct: "Heat is escaping through the window" (or: "Heat is transferring from inside the warm room to the cold outside air through the window glass.")

Explanation: Cold is not a substance — it cannot "come in." Cold is simply the absence of heat. What actually happens is that heat (thermal energy) moves from the warm interior to the cold exterior because heat always flows from high to low temperature; this makes the room feel colder because energy is leaving.

Q9 — Metal vs. plastic chair: (6 marks)

Both chairs are at 22°C — the thermometer confirms this. Your hand is warmer (~37°C). When you touch metal, heat flows from your hand (warm) to the metal (cooler). Metal is an excellent thermal conductor — its particles are closely packed and transmit kinetic energy rapidly through the material; heat moves away from your skin very quickly. When you touch plastic/wood (poor conductors — insulators), heat still flows from your hand, but so slowly that very little leaves per second; your skin doesn't notice the loss. Your brain interprets "heat leaving my hand quickly" as "cold surface." Neither chair is actually colder — the metal just conducts heat away from you much faster.

Part C: The Particle Model and Heat (20 marks)

Q10 — Railroad track expansion: (4 marks)

The track will expand — it will become slightly longer and the gap between sections will close (or the track will buckle if no gap is present). Reason: when temperature increases, particles in the steel absorb kinetic energy and vibrate more vigorously, pushing against each other; average distance between particles increases slightly; this increase across 25 metres (trillions of particles) becomes a measurable length increase. Temperature change = 35 − (−30) = 65°C; steel expands ~0.000012 m/m/°C; 25 m × 65°C × 0.000012 ≈ 0.02 m (2 cm) of expansion. If no expansion gap is left, the track buckles under compressive force.

Q11 — Particle diagrams: (6 marks)

Mark for:

  • Cold solid: particles close together, regular arrangement, small or no movement arrows; minimal spacing
  • Warm liquid: particles slightly farther apart, no fixed arrangement, moderate movement arrows in various directions
  • Hot gas: particles far apart, completely random, fast/large movement arrows; particles occupy entire box space

Q12 — Temperature plateau at 0°C: (4 marks)

At 0°C, the stove is still adding energy, but that energy is being used entirely to break the intermolecular bonds holding ice crystals together (the bonds between water molecules in the solid lattice). Breaking bonds requires energy; it does not speed up particle movement. Since temperature reflects average kinetic energy (speed of movement), and the energy input is going into bond-breaking rather than speeding particles up, temperature remains flat. Once all bonds are broken and all ice has melted → no more bonds to break → added energy increases particle speed → temperature begins to rise again.

Q13 — Power lines in Alberta: (3 marks)

Alberta's winter temperatures can reach −40°C or colder. When metal cools, particles slow down and pull closer together — the wire contracts and becomes shorter. If power lines were strung tightly in summer, this winter contraction would place them under extreme tension stress → they could snap. The extra slack installed in warmer weather accommodates winter contraction without the wire going taut.

Part D: Modes of Heat Transfer (24 marks)

Q15 — Identify mode: (6 marks)

a. Conduction (spoon in soup — direct particle-to-particle contact)

b. Radiation (campfire at distance — infrared electromagnetic waves through air)

c. Convection (hot air rises and circulates — fluid movement carrying heat)

d. Radiation (Sun to Earth through vacuum — electromagnetic waves only)

e. Conduction blocked (oven mitt insulates — reduces conduction to hand)

f. Convection (lava lamp — density-driven fluid circulation)

Q16 — Convection cannot occur in solids:

In a solid, particles are locked in a fixed lattice — they cannot flow or change position (they can only vibrate in place). Convection requires that the warmer, less-dense portion of a fluid physically moves upward (carrying its kinetic energy with it) while cooler fluid sinks to replace it. In a solid, no such bulk movement is possible. For convection: the medium must be a fluid (liquid or gas) that can flow freely.

Q17 — Conductors vs. insulators:

  • Conductors: metals (copper, aluminum); particles closely packed with freely mobile electrons; energy transferred rapidly between adjacent particles. Examples: copper pipe, aluminum frying pan.
  • Insulators: wood, foam, air pockets in wool/down; particles loosely arranged with few mobile charge carriers; very slow energy transfer. Examples: fibreglass batt insulation, wooden spoon handle.

Particle structure: in metals, free electrons carry energy rapidly from hot end to cool end; in insulators, electrons are bound to atoms and no efficient energy carrier exists.

Q18 — Thermos design:

Feature Mode blocked How
Vacuum between walls Conduction and convection No particles = no medium for particle-to-particle transfer or fluid circulation
Silvered inner walls Radiation Shiny surfaces reflect infrared radiation back rather than absorbing and re-emitting it
Tight-fitting cap Convection Seals off air movement; prevents warm air from rising out of container

Q19 — Sun warms Earth:

Radiation. The Sun emits electromagnetic energy (infrared, visible light, ultraviolet) that travels through the vacuum of space without any medium. When this electromagnetic radiation reaches Earth's surface, it is absorbed and converted into thermal energy (heat). No particles are needed — the energy propagates as waves.

Part E: Specific Heat and Insulation (20 marks)

Q20 — Specific heat capacity:

Specific heat capacity = the amount of energy (joules) needed to raise the temperature of 1 gram of a substance by 1°C. Water has a much higher specific heat capacity than iron (water ≈ 4.18 J/g°C vs. iron ≈ 0.45 J/g°C). Practically: water heats up and cools down slowly; iron heats quickly. This is why a small iron frying pan gets dangerously hot quickly while a full pot of water takes a long time to boil.

Q21 — Alberta vs. Vancouver temperature swings:

The Pacific Ocean has an extremely high specific heat capacity — it stores enormous amounts of solar energy in summer and releases it slowly in fall and winter, moderating Vancouver's air temperatures. Vancouver rarely gets very cold in winter (ocean releasing stored heat) or very hot in summer (ocean absorbing excess heat). Alberta is far from any large body of water; air and soil have low specific heat capacities → heat up quickly in summer (→ +35°C) and cool rapidly in winter (→ −40°C); no thermal reservoir exists to moderate these swings. The Rocky Mountains also block the moderating Pacific air masses from reaching Alberta.

Q22 — Sand vs. water in same sunlight:

Sand has a much lower specific heat capacity than water. For the same amount of solar energy absorbed over the day, sand requires far less energy to raise each gram's temperature → sand heats to 50°C easily. Water requires ~4× more energy per gram per °C → absorbs the same solar energy but only reaches 20°C. Additionally, water circulates (mixing cooler deep water into surface layer) and some evaporates (cooling the surface), both of which reduce surface temperature. Sand has no such cooling mechanisms.

Q23 — How insulation works:

Insulation slows the rate of heat transfer — it does not generate heat. A good insulator creates barriers (trapped air, low-conductivity material) that reduce conduction through walls and convection through air movement. Trapped air pockets in wool, fibreglass batts, or foam are very poor conductors; heat moves through them slowly. In winter, insulation slows heat loss from inside to outside; in summer, it slows heat gain from outside to inside. The warmth you feel under a blanket comes from your own body heat being retained — the blanket generates nothing.

Q24 — Two insulation improvements:

Accept any two reasonable improvements — full solutions:

  1. Upgrade attic insulation (conduction): heat rises; inadequate attic insulation allows conducted heat to move through ceiling material into cold attic; thick batts of fibreglass or blown cellulose reduce conduction rate drastically; greatest single improvement per dollar for Alberta homes
  2. Install double/triple-pane windows (conduction + radiation): single-pane glass conducts heat out rapidly; double-pane adds an air (or argon) gap — poor conductor — between glass layers; low-emissivity coating on inner pane reduces radiation heat loss; windows can account for 25–30% of home heat loss in Alberta winter

Part F: Synthesis (★) — answer-only

Q25 — Eco-home design brief:

Award 1 mark per scientific principle correctly named and applied; mark for at least 3 from the unit.

Examples: thick wall insulation (reduce conduction); triple-pane windows (reduce conduction + radiation); south-facing windows with roof overhang (solar radiation gains in winter when sun is low; overhang blocks summer sun when high); thermal mass (concrete/stone floor stores daytime solar radiation heat; releases slowly at night — specific heat capacity); airtight construction with HRV ventilation (prevent convection air leakage); light-coloured exterior or green roof (reduce radiation absorption in summer). Each design choice must be linked to the specific heat transfer principle it addresses.
