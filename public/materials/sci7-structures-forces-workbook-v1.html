Q3 ★★ Extended Evaluate — LO 7-4-02
West Edmonton Mall has a multi-level parking structure. On a busy Saturday, a heavy delivery truck (mass approximately 8,000 kg) parks on the top level, directly above a ground-floor support column. Later that night, a severe windstorm moves through Edmonton.

(a) Identify the type of load the delivery truck represents. Explain why it is classified that way rather than as a dead load.

(b) The windstorm applies forces to the parking structure as well. Identify the load type wind represents, and explain what makes it more destructive than a live load of the same magnitude. Give one other example of this same load type that a parking structure in Edmonton might experience.

(c) In the concrete support column beneath the truck: where is compression highest? Would tension appear anywhere in the column under this purely vertical load? Name one design feature engineers add to concrete columns to address concrete's well-known weakness in tension, and explain how it works.

Q4 ★ Core Apply / Analyze — LO 7-4-03
A cylindrical grain silo on a Peace River farm stores 50,000 kg of grain. The walls of the silo are noticeably thicker at the base than near the top.

(a) A full silo is more stable against toppling than an empty one, even though it is much heavier overall. Use the concept of centre of gravity to explain this apparent paradox.

(b) The stored grain pushes outward on the cylindrical walls (called lateral pressure — it acts similarly to a liquid). Identify which internal force this creates in the silo walls. Explain why this force is greatest at the base, and why the base walls must therefore be thickest.

(c) A strong wind applies a lateral (sideways) force to the silo. Would a full silo or an empty silo be more likely to topple? Justify your prediction using both centre of gravity and base of support.

Q7 ★★ Extended Evaluate — LO 7-4-01
The TELUS World of Science Edmonton features a geodesic dome — a near-sphere made entirely of triangulated frame panels. A nearby exhibition hall of the same floor area is a conventional rectangular box building.

(a) A geodesic dome is classified as a combination structure (frame + shell). Identify the frame element and the shell element in its design. Explain how they work together to distribute load to the foundation.

(b) The geodesic dome encloses the same volume as the box building but uses significantly less material. Evaluate this trade-off: name one structural advantage the dome has over the box, one practical disadvantage, and make a reasoned judgment about which design is better suited for a science centre — and why.

(c) Why is the triangle the strongest 2D shape for frame structures? Use the concept of geometric rigidity — compare what happens when a lateral force is applied to a triangular panel vs. a square panel of the same material.

Q8 ★★ Extended Create / Evaluate — LO 7-4-03, 7-4-05
Parks Canada wants to build a pedestrian footbridge over a fast-moving creek in Banff National Park. The bridge must span 8 m, support up to 20 hikers at once, withstand heavy snow loads in winter, handle spring flooding, and blend with the natural environment. All construction materials must be transported in by pack horse — nothing heavier than 80 kg per piece.

(a) Identify all the loads this bridge must be designed for. Classify each as dead, live, or dynamic. Explain what makes the dynamic loads especially challenging in a mountain environment.

(b) Which structure type (solid, frame, shell, or combination) would you choose for this bridge? Justify your choice using structural reasoning, material reasoning, and logistical reasoning. A single-factor answer is not sufficient.

(c) Identify the failure mode you are most concerned about for your chosen design. Name one specific design feature that would reduce that risk, and explain how it works mechanically.

Stretch — Three Challenges

No scaffolding. No hints. These go beyond the unit — use everything you know and reason carefully. Only attempt these after finishing Q1–Q12.

S1 Synthesis — Structures + Forces + Materials

The Lethbridge Viaduct (CPR High Level Bridge) is a steel trestle bridge — one of the longest railway bridges in Canada. It spans a wide valley 96 m above the Oldman River and carries fully loaded freight trains.

Analyze the Lethbridge Viaduct across three dimensions: (a) classify its structure type and explain why a frame trestle was chosen over a solid or shell structure for this span and height; (b) identify and explain at least four distinct loads it experiences, including at least one dynamic load specific to its location and use; (c) identify two material properties critical for the steel members and explain why failure in tension would have different consequences than failure in compression for a railway bridge.

S2 Systems Thinking — Compound Machine + Energy

A compound machine combines a wheel and axle with a movable pulley system. The wheel has a radius of 0.4 m; the axle has a radius of 0.05 m. The axle is connected to a movable pulley system with 3 rope segments supporting the load.

(a) Calculate the mechanical advantage of the wheel and axle alone. (b) Calculate the mechanical advantage of the pulley system alone. (c) Calculate the overall mechanical advantage of the combined system. (d) If an effort force of 20 N is applied at the wheel rim, what is the maximum load the system can theoretically lift (ignore friction)? (e) In practice the system lifts only 85% of the theoretical load. Where did the "missing" energy go? Confirm that the law of conservation of energy is still satisfied.
