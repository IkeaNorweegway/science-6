Why this difference matters for making claims

When scientists make claims about climate change, they are using   data — often from thousands of weather stations, ocean sensors, and satellites, measured over decades.

The timescale of your evidence must match the   of your claim.

Misconception

"Weather and climate are the same thing — one cold year proves climate change is wrong."

Weather and climate operate on entirely different timescales. A cold week or even a cold year tells you nothing about a 30-year trend, just as one bad hair day tells you nothing about whether your hair is growing longer over the year. Climate scientists look at decades of data, not individual events. A single cold year inside a long-term warming trend is still a warming trend — the trend is defined by the full data set, not the exceptions inside it. Claims about climate require climate-scale evidence: decades of data, not days or months.

Notes

Earth's climate has always changed. It has been much warmer and much colder in its past — long before humans existed. Understanding what caused those changes helps us understand what is causing the changes happening now.

Natural causes of climate change

1. Volcanic eruptions
Large volcanic eruptions release huge amounts of   and tiny particles (aerosols) into the atmosphere. The particles can temporarily   sunlight from reaching Earth's surface, causing short-term  .

2. Changes in Earth's orbit (Milankovitch cycles)
Over tens of thousands of years, the shape of Earth's   around the Sun changes slightly — it goes from more circular to more elliptical. This changes how much solar energy Earth receives at different times of year. These cycles explain the timing of past   ages.

3. Variation in solar output
The Sun does not put out perfectly constant energy. Its output varies by about   over an approximately 11-year cycle. Slightly more energy from the Sun warms Earth slightly; slightly less cools it.

4. Large meteor impacts
A very large impact can throw   dust and debris into the atmosphere, blocking sunlight for months or years. Scientists believe an impact 66 million years ago contributed to mass cooling and extinction.

Since the Industrial Revolution (~1850), human activities have added large amounts of greenhouse gases to the atmosphere — far   than natural processes alone.

1. Burning fossil fuels
Coal, oil, and natural gas are made of carbon that was stored underground for millions of years. When we burn them, we release that stored   as CO₂ into the atmosphere very rapidly. This is the   human contributor to current climate change.

Misconception

"Climate has always changed naturally, so current climate change is just natural variation."

Natural climate changes are real — Earth has had ice ages and warm periods. But the rate and pattern of current change is outside natural variation. Ice core data shows that CO₂ levels today (~420 ppm) are higher than at any point in the past 800,000 years of natural cycles. Natural variations happen over thousands to tens of thousands of years; current warming has happened in roughly 150 years. The pattern — and the speed — match the rise in human fossil fuel use, not any natural cycle scientists can identify.

Natural climate drivers have not changed significantly in the last 150 years:

  • Volcanic activity:   — would cause temporary cooling, not sustained warming
  • Orbital cycles:   — these operate over tens of thousands of years

The only major change that matches the timing and rate of observed warming is the rapid increase in atmospheric CO₂ and other greenhouse gases from   activity.

Connection to Computer Science

Climate scientists build computer climate models — programs that simulate how Earth's climate system works. These models are computational artifacts: tools created through programming and design to solve a specific problem. A climate model is an abstraction — it represents the key variables (CO₂ levels, ocean temperature, ice cover, solar output, atmospheric circulation) and removes everything else. This is the same principle as a force diagram: keep the essential relationships, remove the unnecessary detail.

Scientists write code that represents physical laws, run the model forward in time, and compare the output to observed data. They then use these models to project what happens under different scenarios — what if emissions stay high? What if they drop sharply?

Greenhouse Effect vs. Ozone Hole — Important Distinction

Misconception

"The greenhouse effect and the ozone hole are the same thing — both are caused by pollution and both make the planet hotter."

These are two completely separate phenomena. They involve different gases, different layers of the atmosphere, and different effects:

Greenhouse EffectOzone Hole
Gases involvedCO₂, methane, water vapourCFCs (chlorofluorocarbons)
Layer of atmosphereTroposphere (lower atmosphere)Stratosphere (~15–35 km up)
What it doesTraps heat — warms the planetThins UV protection — allows more UV radiation to reach Earth's surface
Main effectGlobal temperature increaseIncreased UV exposure (skin cancer, ecosystem damage)
Main human causeBurning fossil fuels, deforestationAerosol sprays, old refrigerants (now banned under Montreal Protocol)

Confusing these two produces wrong conclusions about causes and solutions. Fixing ozone depletion (banning CFCs) does not fix climate change (still need to reduce CO₂). They are separate problems requiring separate responses.
