Sample document. This is a demonstration of the workbook format applied to Grade 9 Polynomials. A full unit workbook would include ~30 questions, additional visual tasks, and a teacher copy with lesson-release markers and misconception target labels.

About this workbook: Questions are mixed up on purpose. You will not know which polynomial skill each question is testing until you read it. Figuring that out is part of the work.

★ Core  Core questions — attempt all of these. Some include a hint or sentence starter.

★★ Extended  Extended questions — same ideas, higher demand. Explain, find errors, or apply to a new context. Attempt after finishing Core.

Show all working. A correct answer with no working earns half marks. A clear incorrect answer with good working earns most marks.

Stretch Zone

Attempt these only after completing all earlier questions. Each one connects two or more ideas.

S1 ★★
A hockey rink in Edmonton has a rectangular playing surface with length (3x + 8) m and width (2x + 5) m. Around it is a uniform concrete border of width 2 m.
(a) Write a simplified polynomial for the area of the playing surface.
(b) Write a simplified polynomial for the total area (playing surface plus border).
(c) Write a simplified expression for the area of the border alone. What degree is it, and what does that tell you?
S2 ★★

Two polynomials add to give (5x² − 2x + 3). One of the polynomials is (2x² + x − 1).

(a) Find the other polynomial. Show your method.
(b) Check your answer by adding the two polynomials together.
(c) What operation did you use to find the missing polynomial? Explain in one sentence why that operation works here.
S3 ★★
A student claims that (x + 2)(x + 3) and x² + 6 are the same expression, because "you just multiply the numbers."
(a) Expand (x + 2)(x + 3) correctly using the area model or distribution.
(b) Evaluate both (x + 2)(x + 3) and x² + 6 when x = 1. Are they equal?
(c) Explain in two or three sentences what error the student made, and why the area model shows their reasoning is wrong.
