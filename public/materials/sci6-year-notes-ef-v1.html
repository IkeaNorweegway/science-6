Unit 1: Matter

Big question: What happens to matter when it gets hot or cold?

What Is Matter?

Everything around you is matter.

Matter — anything that takes up space and has weight. Examples: water, air, rocks, your pencil.

Matter is made of particles.

Particles are too tiny to see.

Particle — a tiny piece that matter is made of. You cannot see one particle. A grain of sand has millions.

Particles always move.

Hot and Cold

Temperature tells us how fast particles move.

Temperature — a measure of how fast particles move. Cold = slow. Hot = fast.

When matter gets hot:

  • Particles move faster.
  • Particles spread apart.
  • Matter takes up more space.

When matter gets cold:

  • Particles slow down.
  • Particles move closer together.
  • Matter takes up less space.
COLD                          HOT

  o o o                    o       o
  o o o                        o
  o o o                    o       o

Slow. Close together.    Fast. Far apart.

Growing bigger from heat is called expansion.

Expansion — matter takes up more space when heated.

Shrinking from cold is called contraction.

Contraction — matter takes up less space when cooled.
Alberta example: Steel railway tracks get longer in summer heat. They get shorter in winter cold. Engineers leave small gaps in the track. The gaps let the steel expand. Without gaps, the track would buckle.
Expansion joint — a gap left so material can expand safely.

Solid, Liquid, Gas

Matter has three states.

State — the form matter is in. (Solid, liquid, or gas.)
StateWhat it looks likeExample
SolidFixed shape. Particles packed tight.Ice
LiquidNo fixed shape. Flows and pours.Water
GasSpreads to fill any space.Steam
SOLID        LIQUID       GAS

 o o o       o  o  o     o         o
 o o o      o    o  o        o
 o o o       o  o  o     o         o

Packed        Loose        Very spread out

Matter can change from one state to another.

This is called a phase change.

Phase change — matter moves from one state to another. Example: ice melting into water.

When matter changes state, the amount stays the same.

100 g of ice melts into 100 g of water.

The Water Exception

Most materials get denser when they freeze.

Water is different.

Ice is less dense than liquid water.

This means ice floats.

Density — how much matter is packed into a space. More packed = more dense. Less packed = less dense.

When a lake freezes in winter, ice forms on top.

Liquid water stays below.

Fish survive in the water under the ice.

Key Ideas — Matter

  • Matter is made of tiny particles that always move.
  • Heat makes particles move faster and spread apart.
  • Cold makes particles slow down and move closer.
  • Matter can be solid, liquid, or gas.
  • When matter changes state, the amount stays the same.
  • Ice floats because it is less dense than liquid water.

Unit 2: Living Systems

Big question: How do living things and non-living things depend on each other?

What Is an Ecosystem?

An ecosystem is an area where living things and non-living things interact.

Ecosystem — all the living and non-living things in one area, working together. Example: a boreal forest near Fort McMurray.

Living things in an ecosystem are called biotic.

Biotic — anything that is alive or was recently alive. Examples: trees, moose, insects, fungi.

Non-living things are called abiotic.

Abiotic — non-living parts of an ecosystem. Examples: sunlight, water, temperature, soil.

Both biotic and abiotic parts matter.

If the sunlight changes, the plants change.

If the plants change, the animals change.

Everything is connected.

How Do Plants Get Food?

Plants do not get food from soil.

Soil gives plants minerals. Minerals are not food.

Plants make their own food. This is called photosynthesis.

Photosynthesis — the way plants make their own food using sunlight.

Here is how it works:

  Sunlight
     ↓
Air (CO₂)  +  Water  →  Food (glucose)  +  Oxygen
Glucose — the sugar a plant makes. It is the plant's food.

Plants have a green substance called chlorophyll.

Chlorophyll captures light from the sun.

Chlorophyll — the green substance in leaves that captures sunlight.
Remember: Almost all of a tree's mass comes from air — not from soil.

Who Eats Whom?

A producer makes its own food.

Producer — a living thing that makes its own food. Plants are producers.

A consumer eats other living things.

Consumer — a living thing that eats producers or other consumers. Examples: moose, wolf, hawk.

A decomposer breaks down dead things.

Decomposers return nutrients to the soil.

Producers use those nutrients to grow.

Decomposer — breaks down dead matter and returns nutrients to the soil. Examples: fungi, bacteria.

A food web shows who eats whom in an ecosystem.

Food web — a diagram showing all the feeding connections in an ecosystem. 
       [Sun]
          ↓
  [Spruce Trees / Plants]
       ↙         ↘
  [Moose]     [Insects]
     ↓              ↓
  [Wolf]       [Woodpecker]
                    ↓
                 [Hawk]
                    ↓
          [Decomposers — fungi]

Every living thing in the ecosystem depends on others.

This is called interdependence.

If one part of the ecosystem changes, everything else changes too.

Interdependence — living things need each other to survive.

Key Ideas — Living Systems

  • An ecosystem has biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) parts.
  • Plants make their own food using sunlight. This is photosynthesis.
  • Producers make food. Consumers eat. Decomposers break things down.
  • A food web shows all the feeding connections in an ecosystem.
  • All living things in an ecosystem depend on each other.

Unit 3: Forces

Big question: How do forces affect materials and structures?

What Is a Force?

A force is a push or a pull.

Force — a push or a pull on an object.

Every force has two things:

  1. A size — how strong is the push or pull?
  2. A direction — which way does it push or pull?

We measure force in Newtons (N).

Newton (N) — the unit we use to measure force. A textbook weighs about 10 N.

We draw forces as arrows. A longer arrow means a bigger force.

   20 N →   [BRIDGE DECK]   ← 8 N

   Net force = 12 N to the right

Forces From Outside and Inside

Forces that act on an object from outside are called external forces.

External force — a force that comes from outside the object. Examples: a person pushing, gravity pulling down.

When an external force acts on a material, forces build up inside the material too.

These are called internal forces.

Internal force — a force that builds up inside a material.

There are four internal forces:

ForceWhat it doesExample
TensionPulls material apartA bridge cable being stretched
CompressionSqueezes material togetherA post holding up a bridge deck
ShearSlides layers past each otherA bolt connecting two planks
TorsionTwists materialA handrail being twisted
Tension — pulling apart from two ends.

Compression — squeezing together from two ends.

Shear — sliding layers past each other.

Torsion — twisting.
Alberta example: The pedestrian bridge at Riverside School carries all four internal forces at once when students walk across it.

What Happens to Materials Under Force?

When a force acts on a material, it changes shape. This is called deformation.

Deformation — a change in shape caused by a force.

Sometimes the shape goes back to normal when the force stops.

This is elastic deformation.

Elastic deformation — the material returns to its original shape after the force is removed. Example: a spring stretches and then bounces back.

Sometimes the shape stays changed.

This is plastic deformation.

Plastic deformation — the material stays changed after the force is removed. Example: a bent railing stays bent.

Every material has an elastic limit.

Past the elastic limit, the change is permanent.

Elastic limit — the point where deformation becomes permanent.

Newton's Third Law

When you push on something, it pushes back.

The pushback is the same size. It goes the opposite direction.

This is Newton's Third Law.

You push…It pushes back…
Down on the bridgeBridge pushes up on you
On the railingRailing pushes back on you
Your boots backGround pushes your boots forward

The two forces act on different objects. They do not cancel each other out.

Key Ideas — Forces

  • A force is a push or a pull. It has size (Newtons) and direction.
  • External forces come from outside. Internal forces build up inside.
  • The four internal forces are tension, compression, shear, and torsion.
  • Elastic deformation is temporary. Plastic deformation is permanent.
  • Newton's Third Law: every push gets an equal push back — on a different object.

Unit 4: Climate

Big question: What causes climate change, and how does it affect Alberta?

Weather vs. Climate

Weather is what the sky is doing right now.

Weather — the conditions outside right now, in one place. Examples: sunny, rainy, windy, cold.

Climate is the pattern over a very long time.

Scientists look at 30 years or more of data.

Climate — the long-term pattern of weather in a place, measured over decades.

One cold week does not tell you about climate. You need many years of data.

WEATHER:  one day → one week → one month
CLIMATE:  10 years → 20 years → 30 years+

The Greenhouse Effect

The Sun sends energy to Earth.

Some energy bounces back into space.

Some energy warms the ground.

The ground sends heat back up toward space.

Greenhouse gases slow that heat from escaping.

Greenhouse gas — a gas that slows heat from leaving Earth. Examples: carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane, water vapour.

Greenhouse effect — the way greenhouse gases trap heat near Earth's surface. 
  ☀ Sunlight comes in
       ↓
  Earth gets warm
       ↓
  Heat tries to escape
       ↑
  Greenhouse gases slow it down
  Heat stays near Earth → warmer temperatures

The greenhouse effect is natural. Without it, Earth would be frozen.

The problem is too many greenhouse gases.

More gases = more heat trapped = temperatures rise.

Albedo is how much sunlight a surface reflects.

When ice melts, dark water is uncovered. Dark water absorbs more heat. More heat melts more ice. This cycle keeps going and speeds up warming.

Albedo — how much sunlight bounces off a surface. Snow reflects a lot. Dark water reflects very little.

What Causes Climate Change?

Burning fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide (CO₂) into the air.

Fossil fuel — fuel made from ancient living things buried underground. Examples: coal, oil, natural gas.

Cutting down forests also releases CO₂. Forests store carbon. When trees are cut and burned, that carbon goes into the air.

Deforestation — cutting down forests.

Changes already happening in Alberta:

What is changingWhat we see
TemperatureUp about 1.5°C since 1950
SpringArrives earlier each year
WildfiresSeason starts earlier and lasts longer
Mountain glaciersShrinking — less water in rivers in summer
FloodingBigger floods, like High River in 2013
Ice roads in the northFewer safe weeks each winter

Indigenous Elders have watched the land for many generations. Their knowledge shows how the land has changed over long periods of time. It is a valid and important source of climate information.

Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) — long-term Indigenous observations about how ecosystems change over time.

Key Ideas — Climate

  • Weather is what is happening now. Climate is the pattern over decades.
  • The greenhouse effect keeps Earth warm. Too many greenhouse gases trap extra heat.
  • Burning fossil fuels and cutting forests adds more CO₂ to the air.
  • Alberta is already seeing changes: warmer temperatures, longer wildfires, shrinking glaciers.
  • Scientists and Indigenous Knowledge Keepers both gather long-term climate evidence.

Unit 5: Energy Resources

Big question: How do we choose energy resources wisely?

Renewable and Non-Renewable

An energy resource is something we use to make power or heat.

Energy resource — something we use to produce power or heat. Examples: wind, natural gas, sunlight.

Some resources are renewable. Nature replaces them quickly. We will not run out.

Renewable — a resource that nature replaces fast enough that we will not run out. Examples: wind, sunlight, moving water.

Some resources are non-renewable. They took millions of years to form. Once used, they are gone.

Non-renewable — a resource that cannot be replaced in a human lifetime. Examples: coal, oil, natural gas.
RenewableNon-Renewable
WindCoal
Sunlight (solar)Oil
Moving water (hydro)Natural gas
Plants and animals (biomass)Uranium

Before and After Processing

Some resources are used directly — just as they are in nature.

Some resources must be changed first before we can use them.

Before processing — using a resource directly in its natural form. Example: a windmill using wind to grind grain.

After processing — changing a resource into another form before using it. Example: a wind turbine turning wind into electricity.
ResourceBefore processingAfter processing
WindWindmill grinds grainTurbine makes electricity
SunlightWindow heats a roomSolar panel makes electricity
WoodLogs burn to heat a roomWood made into charcoal

How Do We Choose?

Choosing an energy resource is not simple. We look at five things:

  1. Availability — Is it actually where and when we need it?
  2. Societal impact — How does it affect people and communities?
  3. Economic impact — How much does it cost to build and run?
  4. Environmental impact — How does it affect air, land, water, and animals?
  5. Responsible management — Will it still work well in 50 years?

No single resource is perfect. Every resource has trade-offs.

Important: Renewable does not always mean clean. Wind turbines take up land. Solar panels use chemicals to build. Hydro dams can flood ecosystems. Renewable means it will not run out — not that it has no impact.

Key Ideas — Energy Resources

  • Renewable resources are replaced by nature. Non-renewable resources cannot be replaced.
  • Some resources are used directly (before processing). Some are changed first (after processing).
  • We compare resources using five factors: availability, societal impact, economic impact, environmental impact, and responsible management.
  • No resource is perfect. Every choice involves trade-offs.
  • Renewable does not mean clean — it just means it will not run out.

Unit 6: Space

Big question: In what ways can we explore the solar system?

Objects in the Solar System

The solar system is the Sun and everything that orbits it.

Solar system — the Sun and all the objects that travel around it.

The eight planets in order from the Sun:

Mercury → Venus → Earth → Mars
→ Jupiter → Saturn → Uranus → Neptune

Memory helper: My Very Excellent Mother Just Served Us Nachos

Stars produce their own light. Everything else reflects light from the Sun.

Star — a massive body that produces its own light and heat. The Sun is our star.

Planet — a large body that orbits the Sun. It does not produce its own light.

The solar system is mostly empty space. Diagrams in textbooks make it look crowded. They are not to scale. If the Sun were the size of a basketball, Earth would be a tiny dot 30 metres away.

How Do We Explore Space?

Scientists use different tools to explore space. Each tool answers a different question.

ToolWhat it does
TelescopeCollects light to see distant objects

Telescope — a tool that collects light to let us see far-away objects.

Space probe — an uncrewed spacecraft sent to explore other planets.

Rover — a vehicle that travels on the surface of another world.
Alberta example: Students at the University of Alberta built a tiny satellite called Ex-Alta 1. It orbits Earth. It collects data on the upper atmosphere. This is a real scientific mission built by students.

Natural and Artificial Satellites

A natural satellite orbits a planet and was made by nature. Earth's Moon is a natural satellite.

Natural satellite — a body that orbits a planet; formed naturally.

An artificial satellite is built by people and launched into orbit.

Artificial satellite — a human-built object placed into orbit. Examples: GPS satellites, weather satellites, Ex-Alta 1.

Satellites stay in orbit because they move sideways very fast. As they fall toward Earth, the ground curves away beneath them. They always miss the ground. They keep circling.

Microgravity — the near-weightlessness astronauts feel when in orbit.

Why Pluto Is No Longer a Planet

Pluto was called a planet from 1930 to 2006.

Scientists discovered many other icy objects near Pluto.

Pluto is just one of many objects in that area.

Scientists updated the definition of "planet."

A planet must: orbit the Sun, be round, and clear its path of other objects.

Pluto does not clear its path.

So Pluto became a dwarf planet.

Dwarf planet — orbits the Sun, is round, but has not cleared its orbital path. Example: Pluto.

This is not a mistake. This is how science works.

New evidence → updated definition → better understanding.

Key Ideas — Space

  • Stars produce their own light. Planets, moons, and asteroids reflect sunlight.
  • The solar system is mostly empty space. Textbook diagrams are not to scale.
  • Different exploration tools answer different kinds of questions.
  • Ex-Alta 1 is a student-built satellite from the University of Alberta.
  • Science updates its ideas when new evidence arrives. Pluto's reclassification is an example.
