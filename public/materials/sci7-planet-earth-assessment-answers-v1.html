Part B: Short Answer

Q11 (8 marks — 2 per layer):

  • Outermost = Crust: solid rock; oceanic (thin, dense) and continental (thick, less dense)
  • 2nd = Mantle: solid but flows over geological time; contains convection currents
  • 3rd = Outer core: liquid iron-nickel; generates Earth's magnetic field
  • Innermost = Inner core: solid iron-nickel; extremely hot but pressure keeps it solid

Q12 (6 marks — 2 per rock):

a) Basalt = igneous (extrusive); cooled quickly from lava at surface → tiny crystals

b) Limestone = sedimentary (biogenic); compressed shells and marine organisms with calcite replacement over time

c) Schist = metamorphic; existing rock transformed by high heat and pressure (without melting); foliated (layered) appearance

Q13 (6 marks — 2 per boundary):

  1. Divergent: plates separate; new seafloor created; mid-ocean ridges; rift valleys
  2. Convergent: plates collide; subduction (ocean-continent) → volcanoes + trenches; continent-continent → mountain chains
  3. Transform: plates slide sideways; earthquakes; no new crust; example: San Andreas Fault

Part B: Short Answer (20 marks)

Q16 (8 marks):

  • Crust: solid; oceanic = thin/dense basalt; continental = thick/less dense granite; broken into tectonic plates
  • Mantle: solid but flows; convection currents drive plate motion; largest layer
  • Outer core: liquid; iron and nickel; generates Earth's magnetic field through convection
  • Inner core: solid; iron and nickel; extreme pressure keeps it solid; hottest layer

Q17 (5 marks): Rock cycle triangle diagram: Igneous ↔ Sedimentary ↔ Metamorphic, all connecting arrows labelled with: melting/cooling, weathering/erosion/deposition/compaction, heat+pressure (no melting), and melting paths from all types

Q18 (6 marks):

  1. Divergent: plates move apart; mid-ocean ridges form; new oceanic crust created; example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge
  2. Convergent: plates collide; ocean-continent = subduction, trench, volcanoes; continent-continent = mountain building; example: Rockies (N American plate + oceanic plate), Himalayas (India + Eurasia)
  3. Transform: plates slide sideways; earthquakes; no new crust; example: San Andreas Fault

Q19 (3 marks):

Shield: low-viscosity lava (thin, fluid); gas escapes gradually as lava flows → gentle eruption; lava spreads widely → broad, low dome shape. Composite: high-viscosity lava (thick); traps gas → pressure builds → explosive eruption; steep-sided cone

Part C: Application (15 marks)

Q20 — Badlands rock column (8 marks):

a) Oldest: Layer 1; Most recent: Layer 5 (law of superposition)

b) Alberta was covered by a shallow marine sea (Western Interior Seaway); limestone forms from marine organisms; suggests warm, shallow ocean environment

c) Alberta had a Cretaceous coastal/inland sea environment ~75 million years ago; dinosaurs lived along its shores; marine and terrestrial organisms both preserved in the sediments

d) Iridium layer = likely asteroid/meteorite impact; Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) boundary event; organisms in Layer 3 (including dinosaurs) went extinct shortly after this event

e) Alberta had a consistent low-energy depositional environment (marine or fluvial) for an extended period; no volcanism, erosion events, or tectonic disruption that would destroy layers; gradual sediment accumulation in stable basin

Q21 — Alberta landforms (7 marks):

a) Mechanical: water enters rock cracks, freezes, expands, widens crack; repeated freeze-thaw breaks rock (common in Rocky Mountain trails, spring melt). Chemical: acid rain (CO₂ + water = carbonic acid) dissolves limestone; oxidation turns iron-bearing rocks orange/red (Alberta Badlands red layers). Difference: mechanical = only size/shape changes; chemical = mineral composition changes

b) Fluvial (river) erosion by the Red Deer River; softer shale erodes faster; harder caprock (ironstone, sandstone) protects the column below; differential erosion creates the characteristic pillar-with-cap shape

c) Glaciation — boulders frozen into glacial ice sheets were transported hundreds of km; when glaciers melted (~10,000 years ago), boulders were deposited wherever the ice stopped

Part D: Extended Response (10 marks)

Rubric: 4+ geological events/features referenced and described (4 marks); approximately chronological order (2 marks); specific Alberta landmarks named (2 marks); correct use of geological vocabulary (2 marks)

Sample answer (chronological Alberta geological history):

Alberta's geological story begins over 600 million years ago, when Precambrian basement rock formed the stable base of the continent — these ancient metamorphic and igneous rocks lie buried beneath Alberta today. During the Paleozoic era (540–252 Ma), Alberta was covered by warm shallow seas; marine organisms deposited limestone and other sedimentary layers that eventually became parts of the Rocky Mountains. By the Cretaceous period (~75 Ma), the Western Interior Seaway covered Alberta — a shallow marine environment in which marine invertebrates, fish, and nearby dinosaurs were preserved in the sedimentary layers now exposed in the Badlands. The organic remains of marine organisms in this Cretaceous sediment were compressed over millions of years under heat and pressure into the bitumen-rich oil sands of northeastern Alberta. Beginning ~100 Ma and continuing through the Paleogene, oceanic plates pushing under North America caused the Rocky Mountains to fold and thrust upward along convergent plate boundaries. Finally, during the last ice age (~20,000 years ago), glaciers covered most of Alberta, grinding and sculpting the Rockies, depositing till across the prairies (creating today's agricultural soils), transporting glacial erratics hundreds of kilometres, and carving the river valleys that rivers continue to erode today. Alberta's landscape is a layer cake of these events, readable in its rocks, fossils, and landforms.
