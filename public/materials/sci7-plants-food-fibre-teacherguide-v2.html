The most powerful misconception in all of K–12 biology is the soil-food misconception — the belief that plants get their food from the ground. This unit's primary job is to upend that belief cleanly and permanently. Everything else (cell theory, vascular tissue, reproduction) supports a coherent picture of how plants work. The van Helmont thought experiment is the single most effective teaching moment in this unit.

Materials per Lesson

  • Every lesson: Student notes packages, whiteboard / projector
  • Lesson 2: Compound microscopes, prepared slides (onion skin with iodine, Elodea leaf). Safety: iodine is a mild irritant — avoid eye contact, have paper towels available.
  • Lesson 4: Celery stalks in food colouring set up 24–48 hours before class (blue or red); white carnations optional
  • Lesson 5: No materials needed — van Helmont is a thought experiment presented verbally
  • Lesson 8: Real flowers for dissection (lilies, tulips, or alstroemerias); gloves optional (some students allergic to pollen)
  • Lesson 9: Variety of seeds — dandelion, burdock, maple key, acorn, berry
  • Lesson 10: Map of Alberta agricultural regions; crop images at different stages
  • Lesson 11: Wild mustard vs. broccoli vs. cabbage vs. kale vs. Brussels sprouts images

Section 1 — Cell Theory and Plant Cells

Lessons 1–2 2 beats 1 pacing flag

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — Cell Theory

Show

Board: Ask students: "What do a mushroom, a whale, and a blade of grass have in common?" Take answers. Then reveal: all three are made of cells, and every one of their cells came from a pre-existing cell.

Say

"Cell theory is one of the most powerful unifying ideas in all of science. Before 1839, biology was a collection of separate observations. Cell theory made it a single science."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 1: the three tenets of cell theory.

Beat 1.2 — Plant Cell Under the Microscope

Show

Board: Set up student microscopes before class. Students find and sketch a cell at 100× and 400×.
Safety: Iodine is a mild irritant — avoid eye contact, have paper towels available for spills.

Say

"You're looking at the basic unit of life. Every structure you see has a job. Your job today is to find three structures and hypothesize their function before I tell you."

Cue — ✎ Draw + ★ Write

Label diagram in Section 1 notes. Compare plant (Elodea, green chloroplasts) vs. animal cells (cheek cells — optional if time allows). Exit Ticket 1. Prep: Elodea shows chloroplasts beautifully — put in bright light for 30 minutes before class. Red onion skin shows cell wall and vacuole more clearly than white onion.

Pacing Flag — Microscopy Lab Setup Required Before Class

Set up slides before students arrive. Have a pre-drawn blank cell diagram for students to fill in during observation. If cheek cells are added, plan extra time. Elodea preparation: place in bright light 30 minutes before class for best chloroplast visibility.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Cell theory: (1) All living things made of cells (2) Cells are basic unit of life (3) All cells from pre-existing cells.
  2. Organelle identification: (a) cell membrane (b) nucleus (c) chloroplast.
  3. Vacuole function: Stores water/maintains cell pressure (turgor); stores waste products.
  4. Plant vs. animal cell: Plant cell has rigid outer wall (cell wall), large central space (vacuole), and green bodies (chloroplasts) — all absent in animal cells.
  5. Stretch: Animal cells need to move, change shape, migrate, engulf things — a rigid cell wall would prevent this; plant cells are stationary and need structural rigidity.

Section 2 — Plant Systems and Vascular Tissue

Lessons 3–4 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — Root and Shoot Systems

Show

Board: Bring in a whole plant with roots intact (dandelion or grass works). Trace the complete structure: root hairs → roots → stem → leaves → flowers. Ask: "What job does each part do?"

Say

"Every plant is two systems working together. The root system gathers water and minerals from below. The shoot system captures light and turns it into food above. They cannot function without each other."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students fill Section 2 plant systems table and label the diagram.

Misconception Flag — Roots Absorb Food

Address at root function (Lesson 3) and again at van Helmont (Lesson 5). Roots absorb water and dissolved minerals — not food. Food is made in leaves through photosynthesis. Connect directly to van Helmont in Lesson 5: "If roots absorbed food from soil, where did van Helmont's 74 kg of willow come from?"

Beat 2.2 — Vascular Tissue: Xylem and Phloem

Show

Board: Celery-in-food-colouring demonstration (set up the day before) — cut cross-sections, show blue dye concentrated in xylem bundles.
Label: "Xylem = up (water + minerals). Phloem = down (sugar from leaves)."

Say

"Xylem is like the supply pipeline going up — water and dissolved minerals from soil to leaves. Phloem is the distribution network going down — sugar made in leaves to the rest of the plant. No vascular tissue = no tall plants. Mosses stay low because they have no pipes."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 2 vascular tissue comparison: xylem vs. phloem. Exit Ticket 2.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Two systems: Root: absorption, anchoring, storage; Shoot: photosynthesis, support, reproduction.
  2. Vascular tissue: Xylem: water and minerals, roots to leaves; Phloem: sugars, leaves downward.
  3. Celery dye experiment: Xylem — it carries water (dye dissolved in water) from roots upward.
  4. Vascular advantage: Xylem allows water to travel up from roots to any height; non-vascular plants must stay near water.
  5. Stretch: Leaves cannot receive sugars from leaves and accumulate excess glucose and die; roots cannot receive sugar from leaves and starve — roots have no chloroplasts so cannot photosynthesize.

Section 3 — Photosynthesis

Lessons 5–7 3 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 5–7 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — The Soil Misconception (CRITICAL)

Show

Board: Tell van Helmont's experiment before any notes.
"A willow seedling weighs 2 kg. He plants it in 90 kg of dry soil and waters it only. Five years later: the willow weighs 76 kg. The soil weighs 57 grams less. Where did the 74 kg of willow come from?"
Let students work in pairs for 3 minutes. Hear all answers.

Say

"It came from air and water — almost entirely. Plants are made mostly of CO₂ from the air and water."

"Most people believe plants eat soil for their entire lives and never think to question it. Van Helmont's experiment — from 1648 — was one of the first controlled biological experiments in history, and it showed that belief was wrong. Today you are correcting a 400-year-old misconception."

Important: Do not start the photosynthesis notes until after this misconception is confronted. The correction must precede the explanation.

Cue — ⊡ Think

Pair discussion: "Where did the 74 kg come from? What does that tell us about what plants are made of?" Record initial prediction before hearing the answer.

Misconception Flag — Plants Get Food from Soil (CRITICAL — Highest-Stakes Error in Unit)

This is the central misconception of all of K–12 biology. Do not start photosynthesis notes until this is confronted and resolved. The van Helmont experiment must come first — let students struggle with the puzzle before you explain. If a student's Pre-Knowledge Check Q1 still says "soil/fertilizer" on the synthesis page, the unit's primary job was not completed. Track this explicitly.

Beat 3.2 — Photosynthesis

Show

Board: Walk through the equation step by step.
CO₂ + H₂O + light energy → glucose + O₂
For each input: "Where does this come from?" For each output: "Where does this go?"

Say

"The equation isn't chemistry to memorize — it's a description of a real process happening right now in every green leaf in the room. This plant on my desk is splitting water and fixing carbon dioxide with light."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 3 guided notes: photosynthesis equation, inputs and outputs with sources/destinations. Exit Ticket 3.

Beat 3.3 — Photosynthesis and Ecosystems

Show

Board: Go back to the food web from the Ecosystems unit (if taught first) or briefly establish one. Ask: "Where does the energy in this food web come from?"
Draw the chain back: wolf ← moose ← grass ← photosynthesis ← sun.

Say

"All the energy in almost every food chain traces back to one source: photosynthesis. The wolf, the moose, the beaver — ultimately powered by a plant fixing CO₂."

Cue — ★ Write

Students complete the connection: photosynthesis → ecosystem energy. Section 3 continued.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Photosynthesis equation: CO₂ + water + light energy → glucose + oxygen.
  2. Site of photosynthesis: Chloroplasts (in leaf cells).
  3. Why leaves are green: Chlorophyll absorbs most colours of light but reflects green — leaves appear green.
  4. Photosynthesis vs. respiration: Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy (glucose); it does not cause "breathing" — that is respiration (a separate process).
  5. Stretch: In darkness: photosynthesis stops; CO₂ rises (plant still does respiration); O₂ falls; plant consumes its own stored glucose.

Section 4 — Plant Reproduction

Lessons 8–9 2 beats 2 misconception flags

Lessons 8–9 · Student notes: Section 4

Beat 4.1 — Flower Structure and Sexual Reproduction

Show

Board: Bring in real flowers. Dissect one per pair of students. Identify stamen, pistil, petals, sepals.
Any large flower with visible stamen and pistil: lilies, tulips, alstroemerias from grocery stores. Gloves optional; some students are allergic to pollen. Have magnifying glasses available.

Say

"Flowers didn't evolve to be beautiful for humans. They evolved to attract pollinators. The colours and shapes are advertisements targeted at specific insects and birds. What we call 'beautiful' is actually a very effective commercial."

Cue — ✎ Draw + ★ Write

Students label the flower dissection diagram in Section 4 notes: stamen, pistil, petals, sepals, anther, ovary.

Misconception Flag — Flowers Are for Aesthetics

Dissect flowers; frame everything in terms of pollinator attraction and reproduction function, not beauty. Every structural feature has an evolutionary purpose. "Beautiful" is a human interpretation of signals evolved for entirely different purposes.

Beat 4.2 — Pollination, Seed Dispersal, and Asexual Reproduction

Show

Board: Variety of seeds — dandelion, burdock, maple key, acorn, berry. Sort by dispersal method: wind, water, animal (eaten), animal (attached), gravity.

Say

"Every seed has a travel strategy. Plants can't move, so their seeds have to. The strategy evolved to match the available dispersal agents in that ecosystem."

Address asexual reproduction: "Cloning isn't unnatural — strawberry runners are cloning. An aspen grove is often one organism. This is millions of years older than any lab."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 continued: seed dispersal methods table, asexual reproduction examples. Exit Ticket 4.

Misconception Flag — Cloning Is Unnatural

Address in Lesson 9. Runners, bulbs, tubers — all natural asexual reproduction. An aspen grove is often a single organism connected at the roots. Cloning as a laboratory technique is new; the strategy is ancient. Connect to evolutionary advantage: same successful genotype, rapid expansion of territory.

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Flower parts: Stamen: produces pollen; Pistil: receives pollen, contains eggs, develops into fruit after fertilization.
  2. Asexual reproduction: Runners (strawberry, grass); bulbs (tulip, garlic); tubers (potato); cuttings (coleus, geranium).
  3. Seed dispersal — animal hitchhike: Hooks/burrs; advantage: seeds carried far from parent; reduces competition for resources.
  4. Genetic variation advantage: Creates individuals with different traits; some may be better adapted to new conditions (disease, drought, climate change).
  5. Stretch: One organism biologically, but many stems visually; advantage: rapid colonization, shared root resources; risk: one disease could kill the entire connected system simultaneously (no genetic diversity).

Section 5 — Alberta Crops: Food and Fibre

Lesson 10 1 beat 0 flags

Lesson 10 · Student notes: Section 5

Beat 5.1 — Alberta Crops

Show

Board: Map of Alberta agricultural regions. Show where canola, wheat, barley, flax, hemp are grown. Show what these crops look like at different stages of growth.

Say

"Alberta is one of the world's most productive agricultural regions. This is a direct result of the same photosynthesis we've been studying — scaled up to millions of hectares."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5: Alberta crop map notes, food vs. fibre distinction.

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Alberta crops: Food crops: wheat, canola, barley, peas, lentils; Fibre crops: flax, hemp.
  2. Food vs. fibre: Food crop: grown for eating (or oil for eating); fibre crop: grown for material (fabric, rope, paper).
  3. Monoculture: Advantage: efficient mechanized production; Disadvantage: single pest or disease can destroy entire crop.
  4. Pollinator importance: Bees pollinate canola and other flowering crops; without pollinators, these crops cannot set seed and produce fruit/grain — reduced yields or crop failure.

Section 6 — Agriculture and Biotechnology

Lessons 11–12 2 beats 0 flags

Lessons 11–12 · Student notes: Section 6

Beat 6.1 — Biotechnology: Selective Breeding vs. GMOs

Show

Board: Wild mustard vs. broccoli vs. cabbage vs. kale vs. Brussels sprouts — all the same species, different selective breeding for different traits. Show this is an ancient practice (10,000+ years).

Say

"Selective breeding and genetic modification are both biotechnology. The difference is precision and speed, not nature vs. unnatural. Wild mustard would not be recognized as 'natural' by anyone today — it has been transformed by humans for 10,000 years."

Cue — ★ Write

Balanced discussion prompt: "What are the specific concerns about GMOs? What are the specific benefits? Distinguish the technique from any specific application." Students fill Section 6 notes. Exit Ticket 6.

Beat 6.2 — Biotechnology Debate / Discussion

Show

Board: Three positions assigned to students:
(1) Pro-GMO conventional farmer
(2) Organic consumer
(3) Environmental scientist
Format: Socratic seminar or structured discussion.

Say

"This is a genuine scientific and social debate. There is no simple right answer. Your job is to understand all three positions, not just confirm the one you started with."

Cue — ⊡ Think

Each student must argue their position using facts. After debate: "Did any argument change your thinking? Write one sentence explaining why or why not."

Section 6 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Selective breeding: Choosing organisms with desired traits and breeding only those, over many generations; used for ~10,000 years.
  2. GMO advantages: Herbicide resistance (crop survives when field is sprayed); pest resistance (built-in insect repellent).
  3. Selective breeding vs. GMO: Selective breeding: natural processes over generations, less precise, takes a long time; GMO: direct gene insertion/modification in lab, much faster, very precise.
  4. Stretch: Both claims oversimplify; "dangerous" and "safe" are properties of specific applications, not the technique itself; different GMOs warrant individual evaluation, not blanket judgements.

Assessment Notes

Watch for in Pre-Knowledge Check Q1: If a student's answer to "where do plants get their food" changes from "soil/fertilizer" to anything involving light/photosynthesis/air, that's the core learning outcome. Track this student-by-student.
