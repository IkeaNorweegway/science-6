Exit Ticket Answer Keys

ET1 — Ecosystems and Components

  1. Biotic: any 2 of: trees, bears, wolves, birds, fungi, bacteria, insects. Abiotic: any 2 of: water, temperature, sunlight, soil, wind, nutrients
  2. An ecosystem includes both living (biotic) AND non-living (abiotic) factors, plus their interactions; it's not just animals
  3. Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem

ET2 — Energy Flow

  1. Lynx = tertiary consumer (3rd trophic level). Arrows mean energy flows from eaten to eater (food source to consumer)
  2. Decomposers break down dead material and return nutrients to soil; without them, nutrients lock permanently in dead matter and producers cannot grow — the whole food web collapses
  3. True

ET3 — Energy Pyramid

  1. 40,000 → 4,000 → 400; 2. Each level has fewer organisms because less energy is available (~10% passes up); more wolves needed = bigger range needed
  2. Pyramid: grassland producers (10,000) → grasshoppers / mice (1,000) → meadowlarks / hawks (100)

ET4 — Biodiversity

  1. Biodiversity = variety of species and ecosystems in an area
  2. Keystone species: disproportionately large effect on ecosystem; Alberta example: grizzly bear (regulates prey, disperses seeds, creates nutrient patches)
  3. More species → more redundancy → if one species declines, others fill its role → ecosystem resists collapse

ET5 — Environmental Change

  1. Natural: forest fire (opens canopy, releases nutrients, fire-adapted species return); Drought (grassland species adapted, recover when rain returns). Human: habitat loss (destroys living space for many species)
  2. Invasive species: introduced organism with no natural predators in new ecosystem; outcompetes native species
  3. Tipping point: threshold beyond which ecosystem does not recover; example: coral reef repeatedly bleached, or forest fully converted to agriculture

ET6 — Sustainability and Stewardship

  1. Water filtration; carbon sequestration (any 2 appropriate wetland services)
  2. TEK: knowledge built from generations of careful observation of specific ecosystems; valuable because captures long-term ecological knowledge and specific local patterns
  3. Any specific example connecting human activity to ecosystem health in Alberta (e.g., farmers depending on pollinators, Indigenous communities and boreal health, urban development changing hydrology)

Q13 (4 marks):

  • 10% rule: ~10% of energy at one level transfers to the next; ~90% lost as heat
  • Top predators need large territories because so little energy reaches their trophic level; must range widely to find sufficient food; a wolf territory must be large enough to contain enough prey with enough energy

Q14 (4 marks):

  • Decomposers break down dead matter; return nutrients to soil
  • Without them: nutrients lock permanently in dead material; producers cannot access minerals; all trophic levels eventually collapse
  • Example: without fungi/bacteria in boreal forest, fallen trees would pile up; soil would have no nitrogen/phosphorus; trees would stop growing

Q18 (5 marks):

  • Biodiversity: variety of species, habitats, and genetic diversity in an area
  • Low biodiversity = less resilience: if one species is removed, nothing fills its role → cascade failure
  • Example: monoculture farm — single crop disease can destroy entire harvest; vs. diverse meadow where pest affects only some species

Q19 (5 marks):

Any three from: habitat loss (destroys territory and food for higher trophic levels); pollution (accumulates up food chain; affects water quality); invasive species (outcompetes native species at multiple trophic levels); climate change (shifts species ranges, disrupts timing of food chain); overharvesting (removes species from one trophic level, cascades up/down)

Part C: Application (15 marks)

Q20 — Wolf reintroduction (8 marks):

a) Keystone species: disproportionate effect on ecosystem relative to population size; wolf qualifies: removal/return had cascading effects on 4+ other species and physical landscape

b) Elk (overgrazing without wolf pressure → riverbank degradation); Willow/aspen (overgrazed → declined); Beavers (less food → declined → less wetland habitat); Songbirds (fewer trees → declined) — any 4 species with logical connection to wolf

c) Removing one species can destabilize an entire ecosystem; food web connections are non-linear and unexpected

Q21 — Woodland caribou (7 marks):

a) Fragmentation creates "islands" of habitat separated by roads/pipelines; caribou cannot cross industrial corridors safely; predators (wolves) use industrial roads to access previously remote caribou territory; population isolation reduces genetic diversity

b) Wolves benefit (roads give access to new territory); moose/deer in boreal may increase if caribou decline reduces competition; lichens (caribou food) may increase; any logical cascade

c) Any specific action with ecosystem rationale: e.g., "Reclaim industrial roads in mature caribou habitat to restore travel corridors" — addresses fragmentation; creates reconnected habitat

Part D: Extended Response (10 marks)

Rubric (all options):

  • States the ecosystem type and disturbance cause: 2 marks
  • Identifies at least 2 food web effects: 3 marks
  • Addresses reversibility: 2 marks
  • Makes a specific recommendation: 2 marks
  • Uses correct ecological vocabulary throughout: 1 mark

Option A sample: Pesticide kills insects in a wetland → primary consumer (insects) collapse → waterbirds (insect eaters), frogs decline → food web disruption cascades upward; decomposer insects (crane flies) also affected → nutrient cycling slows; partially reversible if pesticide degrades and insect refugia remain nearby; recommendation: stop pesticide use, restore wetland buffer zones

Option B sample: Pike as invasive top predator → no natural predators → population explodes → native fish (small trout, perch) preyed upon heavily → food web collapses from top; native fish are prey items for loons, osprey, bears → higher trophic levels affected; not reversible easily once established; recommendation: intensive removal program, public awareness to prevent spread to other lakes

Option C sample: 30 years of fire suppression allowed dead wood accumulation (fuel load) → when fire finally ignited, much larger/hotter burn than natural fires → soil sterilized in some areas; boreal species adapted to periodic light fires → less adapted to stand-replacing fire; some areas recover faster than others; fire was natural disturbance — suppression altered the ecosystem more than the fire; recommendation: reinstate managed prescribed burning to reduce fuel loads
