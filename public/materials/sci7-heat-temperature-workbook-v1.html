Q4 ★★ Extended

Cluster 3 — Particle model applied to heat · Analyze

A railroad track is laid at 20°C. Each section is 18.6 m long, and a 1 cm expansion gap is left between sections. On a July afternoon the track reaches 55°C.

Student claim to evaluate

"We don't need the gaps — the track might get a tiny bit longer but it'll just stay in place. No big deal."

(a) Using the particle model, explain what happens to the metal particles in the rail as the temperature rises from 20°C to 55°C. Be specific about vibration and spacing.

(b) Why would removing the expansion gaps cause the track to buckle? Use your particle-level explanation to explain the mechanism of buckling — what is the track trying to do, and what happens when it can't?

(c) What would you tell a new engineer about why the gap must be sized for the maximum expected summer temperature, not the installation temperature?

Q5 ★ Core

Cluster 5 — Specific heat capacity · Apply

Two identical cups sit in direct sunlight — one full of dry sand, one full of water, both starting at 18°C. After 20 minutes you measure: sand = 45°C, water = 24°C.

(a) Which material has the higher specific heat capacity? How do you know from the data?

(b) Both cups received the same amount of energy from the sun. Use specific heat capacity to explain why the sand temperature rose so much more than the water temperature.

(c) A student suggests filling a solar collector with sand instead of water: "Sand gets way hotter — it'll store more energy." Evaluate this suggestion. Is the student right? Explain what specific heat capacity actually tells us about energy storage.

Q6 ★★ Extended Alberta

Cluster 6 — Applications · Evaluate

The Red Deer River downstream of a power plant has had three consecutive summers with major fish kills. The plant draws cold river water for cooling and releases it 8°C warmer. The plant manager says: "Just wait — the river will fix itself in a few weeks."

(a) Warmer water holds less dissolved oxygen. Using the particle model, explain why warmer water has less capacity to hold dissolved gases.

(b) Using specific heat capacity, explain why the Red Deer River (a large body of water) takes a long time to cool back down after warm water is discharged — even if the plant stops releasing warm water tomorrow.

(c) Evaluate the plant manager's claim. Name at least two factors that determine whether the river ecosystem recovers, and explain whether "just waiting" is a scientifically defensible response.
