Q4 ★★ Extended

Cluster 2 — Plant systems and vascular tissue · Analyze

A Grade 7 class places a celery stalk in red food colouring dissolved in water. After 2 hours, they cut the stalk crosswise and find a ring of small red dots near the outer edge of the cut surface — not spread uniformly throughout.

(a) Which type of vascular tissue is responsible for moving the coloured water upward? Explain what this tissue does and in which direction materials travel in it.

(b) Phloem is not stained red in this experiment. Why not? What does phloem transport, and in which direction does it move materials?

(c) Evaluate this student claim:

Student claim to evaluate

"The celery drinks the red water like a straw — it goes everywhere evenly through the whole stem."

Is transport in a plant stem uniform, or organized into specific channels? What does the ring pattern of red dots tell you? What does this organization reveal about how plant transport systems evolved to meet plant needs?

Your evaluation — refer to the structure of vascular bundles and the two tissue types.

Q6 ★★ Extended Evaluate

Cluster 5 — Plants for food and fibre · Evaluate

Alberta grows approximately 40% of Canada’s canola crop. Canola is used for cooking oil, biodiesel, and livestock feed. Approximately 95% of Alberta’s canola is genetically modified for herbicide resistance.

(a) Explain what “genetically modified for herbicide resistance” means at a basic biological level. What was changed in the plant, and what practical problem does it solve for the farmer?

(b) Evaluate these two positions. For each, identify what is scientifically supported and what is overstated or unsupported:

  • Organic farmer: “GMO canola is dangerous — scientists are playing God with our food supply.”
  • Biotechnology company: “GMO canola is perfectly safe and increases yields.”
Distinguish between claims about food safety, ecological effects, and agricultural benefits. Use what the evidence actually says.

(c) Even if a GMO crop is safe to eat, what ecological concern might an ecologist raise about growing a single herbicide-resistant variety at massive industrial scale across Alberta? Explain one specific risk.

Q7 ★ Core

Cluster 2 — Plant systems and vascular tissue · Apply

A pine tree in Jasper National Park is girdled — bark is stripped in a complete ring around the trunk, cutting through the phloem but not the xylem. The tree still has needles (leaves) and intact roots when the girdling occurs.

(a) The needles initially continue to photosynthesize normally. Will the roots survive long-term? Explain, using your knowledge of what phloem transports and in which direction it moves.

(b) After several months, the needles begin to yellow and die — even though xylem is intact and water is still reaching the leaves. Explain why the leaves eventually fail when only the phloem has been cut.

HintThink about what roots need to stay alive, and where that comes from.

(c) What does the girdling scenario reveal about the interdependence of the root system and the shoot system? Explain the relationship in your own words.

Q8 ★★ Extended Evaluate

Cluster 6 — Biotechnology · Evaluate

A group of Alberta students are discussing the Three Sisters companion planting system used by many Indigenous peoples across North America — growing corn, beans, and squash together in the same plot. Two students disagree:

  • Student A: “This is just traditional practice — it has no scientific basis.”
  • Student B: “This is an example of traditional biotechnology — Indigenous peoples have been selectively breeding these plants together for thousands of years.”

(a) Evaluate Student A’s claim. Identify at least two ecological benefits of the Three Sisters system. Consider: nitrogen fixation by beans, corn as a trellis, and squash as ground cover. Is there a scientific basis for this system?

(b) Evaluate Student B’s claim. Is selective breeding a form of biotechnology? How does it differ from GMO technology at the genetic level?

(c) A modern industrial farmer proposes replacing the Three Sisters system with a monoculture of genetically modified corn. Using your knowledge of biodiversity and biotechnology, evaluate the tradeoffs — what is gained and what is lost?

Your evaluation — address yield, soil health, ecological risk, and food security.

Q10 ★★ Extended

Cluster 4 — Reproduction · Analyze

In Alberta, dandelions reproduce both sexually (producing seeds carried by wind) and asexually (through root fragmentation when roots are cut or disturbed by mowing). This makes them extremely difficult to eliminate from lawns.

(a) Explain why dandelions are so ecologically successful using both reproductive strategies. What specific advantage does each strategy provide that the other cannot?

(b) A homeowner tries to eliminate dandelions by mowing repeatedly before any flowers appear. Analyze whether this strategy will work long-term, given what you know about asexual reproduction in plants.

(c) Many people label dandelions as “weeds,” but dandelions are often the first available nectar source for bees after winter in Alberta. Using your knowledge of plant reproduction and ecosystem relationships, evaluate the tradeoff of eliminating dandelions from urban lawns at scale. Who or what benefits? Who or what is harmed?

Q11 ★ Core

Cluster 5 — Plants for food and fibre · Apply

A family in Red Deer, AB wants to grow their own food in a 10 m × 10 m plot. They are choosing between:

  • Wheat — grain crop, high yield per hectare but requires milling and processing
  • Beans — legume, edible directly, fixes nitrogen in soil
  • Potatoes — high calorie yield per m², reproduces asexually via tubers

(a) Potatoes reproduce from tubers (asexual reproduction). Explain what a tuber is and why this method gives a home gardener who wants to grow the same variety every year a specific advantage over growing from seed.

(b) The family chooses beans partly because “beans make the soil better.” Explain the biological reason for this. (Hint: think about what the roots of legumes can do with nitrogen gas from the air.)

(c) If the family grows only one crop in the same plot year after year (monoculture), what problem might they face over time? Use your knowledge of soil biology and plant health to explain.

Q12 ★★ Extended Evaluate

Cluster 6 — Biotechnology · Evaluate

A regulatory agency has approved a new genetically modified wheat variety that is drought-resistant. Many Canadian wheat farmers want to grow it, but several European countries have banned the import of GMO wheat.

(a) Explain what “drought-resistant” means at the level of plant biology. What might scientists have modified to reduce water loss or increase water uptake in dry conditions?

(b) Evaluate both positions below. For each, identify what is scientifically supported and what remains genuinely uncertain:

  • Alberta farmer: “This would let me use less water and make my farm more sustainable.”
  • European consumer: “We don’t know the long-term effects of GMO wheat on human health or ecosystems.”
Balanced evaluation — address food safety and ecological dimensions separately. Avoid dismissing either view entirely.

(c) If you were advising the Alberta government on whether to permit farmers to grow this GMO wheat for export, what two specific, science-based conditions or safeguards would you require before approving it? Explain the scientific reason for each.

S2 Clusters 4 + 6 — Evaluate

Scientists have developed “seedless” watermelons by giving the plant an extra set of chromosomes, which prevents viable seed formation. The fruit is grown by crossing these sterile plants with normal plants — so every crop requires new parent plants produced in a laboratory.

(a) Is this technique selective breeding or genetic modification? Explain the distinction between the two at the genetic level. Where does the seedless watermelon process fit?

(b) A farmer says: “I can’t save seeds from my crop, so I have to buy new seeds every season — this makes me dependent on the seed company.” Evaluate the ecological and economic implications of this dependency for Alberta agriculture.

(c) Traditional seed-saving — selecting and storing seeds from the best plants each season — has been practised by Indigenous peoples across the Americas for thousands of years. Compare this practice to the seedless watermelon model. What knowledge or agricultural capacity is at stake when seed-saving traditions are lost?

S3 Clusters 2 + 3 + 5 — Create

You are designing a vertical farm to grow leafy greens inside a Calgary office building year-round. The farm has no soil, no natural sunlight, and no outdoor air access.

(a) Using your knowledge of plant systems and photosynthesis, identify three conditions your farm must provide. For each, explain which specific plant biological process it supports, and predict what would happen to the plants if that condition were missing.

Three conditions — for each: name the condition, the biological process it supports, and what fails without it.

(b) Your investor says: “Use a nutrient solution instead of soil — it’s more efficient.” Explain what a nutrient solution replaces in the plant’s normal biology. What does soil normally provide, and what must the solution substitute?

(c) A critic says: “Vertical farming is completely unnatural and bad for people.” Evaluate this claim using science. What would you need to measure or test to compare the nutritional quality of vertically farmed greens versus field-grown greens? Name two measurable variables and explain what they would tell you.
