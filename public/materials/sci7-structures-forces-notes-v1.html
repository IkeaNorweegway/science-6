Every structure must handle multiple types of loads — forces acting on it from many sources.

Dead load: the   weight of the structure itself. Always present. Always acts   (gravity). Cannot be avoided — part of structural design is keeping dead load manageable.

Examples: weight of steel beams, concrete, glass, roof tiles.

Live load: the weight of   the structure is designed to support. Varies — it is not always at maximum. Acts   (gravity).

Examples: people, furniture, vehicles, snow on a roof.

Dynamic load:   and often   in direction. The most dangerous loads in terms of structural failure.

Source Direction Effect
Wind   (lateral) pushes on walls, lifts roofs
Earthquake   (lateral and vertical) shakes foundations, causes structural collapse
Moving traffic   and   rhythmic vibration; resonance danger
Impact (crash) any direction sudden concentrated force

Why dynamic loads fail structures:

Static loads are predictable — engineers design for the maximum expected load. Dynamic loads are   in direction and   in magnitude. They can also cause  : if a force is applied at a structure's natural frequency, it can cause it to vibrate with increasing amplitude until it fails.

Historical example:

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) — collapsed not because of extreme wind speed, but because wind matched the bridge's   frequency. The bridge started oscillating and tore itself apart.

Alberta loads to consider:

  • Wind: high winds on open prairies; chinook winds in foothills
  • Snow: dead load that accumulates — Alberta roofs must be designed to handle snow weight
  • Frost heave: freezing ground expands (dynamic), pushing up foundations

Guided Notes

Centre of gravity (CoG): the single point where the entire weight of an object can be considered to act. For a uniform object, the CoG is at the  . For an irregular object, it depends on where mass is distributed.

The stability rule:

A structure is stable when its   of gravity is   its base of support. When the CoG moves outside the base, the structure  .

Lower CoG →   stable

Wider base →   stable

MISCONCEPTION

"A heavy bottom always makes a structure more stable." More precisely: What determines stability is where the centre of gravity is relative to the base of support. A very tall structure with a wide base but all its mass near the top can still topple. A short, wide structure with mass at the bottom is highly stable. The relationship between CoG position and base area is what matters — not weight alone.

Increasing stability in design:

  • Widen the   (triangle at the base)
  • Lower the centre of   (heavy components at the bottom)
  • Use   (rigid triangular units that cannot deform)

Why triangles? A triangle is the only polygon that cannot change shape without changing the length of its sides. A square under force can become a rhombus (deform) — a triangle cannot deform. This is why   is the fundamental structural element in bridges, cranes, and towers.

Failure modes:

Failure Cause Example
Buckling   in a slender column tall, thin column under excessive compression
Toppling CoG moves   base tower crane with too much load on one side
Shear failure opposing forces tear through a joint bolt snapping in a connection
Fatigue repeated loading and unloading over time metal cracking after millions of cycles

Arches redirect   down and outward to the supports (abutments). They are highly efficient under compression — stone and concrete arches have lasted thousands of years. The weakness: arches fail catastrophically if the abutments move apart.

MISCONCEPTION

"Simple machines give you more power — you get more out than you put in." Why this is wrong: Energy is conserved. If a machine multiplies your force by 3, you must push 3× the distance. The total work (force × distance) is the same on both sides — minus friction losses. You never get more energy out than you put in.
