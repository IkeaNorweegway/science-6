Plants for Food and Fibre — Teacher Guide

Grade 7 Science — Teacher Guide

Read alongside: `_courses/grade-7-science/plants-food-fibre/_context.md` and the student notes package.

Unit Overview

Suggested length: 5–6 weeks (15–18 classes) Tier: 1 — full depth, all misconceptions addressed Unit colour: Green

What this unit is really about: The most powerful misconception in all of K–12 biology is the soil-food misconception — the belief that plants get their food from the ground. This unit's primary job is to upend that belief cleanly and permanently. Everything else (cell theory, vascular tissue, reproduction) supports a coherent picture of how plants work. The van Helmont thought experiment is the single most effective teaching moment in this unit.

Lesson Sequence

Week 1 — Cell Theory and Plant Cells
Lesson 1 — Cell theory
Show Ask students: "What do a mushroom, a whale, and a blade of grass have in common?" Take answers. Then reveal: all three are made of cells, and every one of their cells came from a pre-existing cell.
Say "Cell theory is one of the most powerful unifying ideas in all of science. Before 1839, biology was a collection of separate observations. Cell theory made it a single science."
Notes Section 1, cell theory tenets.
Lesson 2 — Plant cell under the microscope (lab day)
• Equipment: compound microscopes, prepared slides (onion skin with iodine, Elodea leaf)
Show Set up student microscopes before class. Have students find and sketch a cell at 100× and 400×.
Say "You're looking at the basic unit of life. Every structure you see has a job. Your job today is to find three structures and hypothesize their function before I tell you."
Cue Label diagram in notes, Section 1. Compare plant (Elodea, green chloroplasts) vs. animal (cheek cells — optional if time allows). Exit Ticket 1.
Safety: Iodine is a mild irritant. Avoid eye contact. Have paper towels available for spills.
Week 2 — Plant Systems and Vascular Tissue
Lesson 3 — Root and shoot systems
Show Bring in a whole plant with roots intact (dandelion or grass works). Trace the complete structure: root hairs → roots → stem → leaves → flowers. Ask: "What job does each part do?"
Say "Every plant is two systems working together. The root system gathers water and minerals from below. The shoot system captures light and turns it into food above. They cannot function without each other."
Notes Section 2, plant systems table.
Lesson 4 — Vascular tissue: xylem and phloem
Show Celery-in-food-colouring demonstration set up the day before — cut cross-sections, show blue dye in xylem.
Say "Xylem is like the supply pipeline going up — water and dissolved minerals from soil to leaves. Phloem is the distribution network going down — sugar made in leaves to the rest of the plant. No vascular tissue = no tall plants. Mosses stay low because they have no pipes."
Cue Exit Ticket 2.
Week 3 — Photosynthesis
Lesson 5 — The soil misconception (CRITICAL)
Show Tell van Helmont's experiment before any notes. "A willow seedling weighs 2 kg. He plants it in 90 kg of dry soil and waters it only. Five years later: the willow weighs 76 kg. The soil weighs 57 grams less. Where did the 74 kg of willow come from?"
• Let students work in pairs for 3 minutes. Hear all answers. Then: "It came from air and water — almost entirely. Plants are made mostly of CO₂ from the air and water."
Say "Most people believe plants eat soil for their entire lives and never think to question it. Van Helmont's experiment — from 1648 — was one of the first controlled biological experiments in history, and it showed that belief was wrong. Today you are correcting a 400-year-old misconception."
Important: Do not start the photosynthesis notes until after this misconception is confronted. The correction must precede the explanation.
Lesson 6 — Photosynthesis
Show Walk through the equation step by step. For each input, ask "where does this come from?" For each output, ask "where does this go?"
Say "The equation isn't chemistry to memorize — it's a description of a real process happening right now in every green leaf in the room. This plant on my desk is splitting water and fixing carbon dioxide with light."
Notes Section 3, full guided notes. Exit Ticket 3.
Lesson 7 — Photosynthesis and ecosystems
Show Go back to the food web from the Ecosystems unit (if taught first) or briefly establish one. Ask: "Where does the energy in this food web come from?"
Say "All the energy in almost every food chain traces back to one source: photosynthesis. The wolf, the moose, the beaver — ultimately powered by a plant fixing CO₂."
Week 4 — Plant Reproduction
Lesson 8 — Flower structure and sexual reproduction
Show Bring in real flowers. Dissect one per pair of students. Identify stamen, pistil, petals, sepals.
Say "Flowers didn't evolve to be beautiful for humans. They evolved to attract pollinators. The colours and shapes are advertisements targeted at specific insects and birds. What we call 'beautiful' is actually a very effective commercial."
Notes Section 4, sexual reproduction.
Lesson 9 — Pollination, seed dispersal, and asexual reproduction
Show Variety of seeds — dandelion, burdock, maple key, acorn, berry. Sort them by dispersal method.
Say "Every seed has a travel strategy. Plants can't move, so their seeds have to. The strategy evolved to match the available dispersal agents in that ecosystem."
Cue Address asexual reproduction misconception: "Cloning isn't unnatural — strawberry runners are cloning. An aspen grove is often one organism. This is millions of years older than any lab."
Notes Section 4 continued. Exit Ticket 4.
Week 5 — Agriculture and Biotechnology
Lesson 10 — Alberta crops: food and fibre
Show Map of Alberta agricultural regions. Show where canola, wheat, barley, flax, hemp are grown. Show what these crops look like at different stages.
Say "Alberta is one of the world's most productive agricultural regions. This is a direct result of the same photosynthesis we've been studying — scaled up to millions of hectares."
Notes Section 5.
Lesson 11 — Biotechnology: selective breeding vs. GMOs
Show Wild mustard vs. broccoli vs. cabbage vs. kale vs. Brussels sprouts — all the same species, different selective breeding for different traits. Show this is an ancient practice.
Say "Selective breeding and genetic modification are both biotechnology. The difference is precision and speed, not nature vs. unnatural. Wild mustard would not be recognized as 'natural' by anyone today — it has been transformed by humans for 10,000 years."
Cue Balanced discussion — "What are the specific concerns about GMOs? What are the specific benefits? Distinguish the technique from any specific application."
Notes Section 6. Exit Ticket 6.
Lesson 12 — Biotechnology debate/discussion
• Format: Socratic seminar or structured discussion. Give students one of three positions: (1) pro-GMO conventional farmer; (2) organic consumer; (3) environmental scientist. Each must argue their position using facts.
Say "This is a genuine scientific and social debate. There is no simple right answer. Your job is to understand all three positions, not just confirm the one you started with."
Week 6 — Review and Assessment
Lesson 13 — Retrieval practice
• Workbook Parts C and D (photosynthesis and reproduction) independently, then pair-check.
Lesson 14 — Synthesis
• Notes Unit Synthesis from memory; compare Pre-Knowledge Check answers.
• Collect and read before the test. The soil-food answer from the Pre-Knowledge Check is your best indicator of whether the central misconception was resolved.
Lesson 15 — Test

Misconception Management

Misconception When to address Strategy
Plants get food from soil Lesson 5, BEFORE photosynthesis notes van Helmont experiment first; let students solve the puzzle; then explain
Roots absorb food Lesson 3 + Lesson 5 Address at root function; connect directly to van Helmont
Flowers are for aesthetics Lesson 8 Dissect flowers; frame everything in terms of pollinator attraction and reproduction function
Cloning is unnatural Lesson 9 Runners, bulbs, tubers — all natural; connect to evolutionary strategy

Lab and Activity Notes

Microscopy lab (Lesson 2):

  • Set up slides before class
  • Elodea shows chloroplasts beautifully — put in bright light for 30 minutes before class for best results
  • Onion skin: red onion shows cell wall and vacuole more clearly than white onion
  • Have a pre-drawn blank cell diagram for students to fill in during observation

Celery demonstration (Lesson 4):

  • Set up 24–48 hours before class — cut celery stalks and place in coloured water
  • Blue or red food colouring works; cut the cross-section at the top so students can see the xylem dots
  • Optional: use white carnations for a more dramatic visual

Flower dissection (Lesson 8):

  • Any large flower with visible stamen and pistil works well — lilies, tulips, alstroemerias (readily available at grocery stores)
  • Gloves optional; some students are allergic to pollen
  • Have magnifying glasses available for smaller parts

Assessment Notes

Quiz (after Section 3): Covers cell theory, plant systems, photosynthesis — LO 7-2-01 to 7-2-03.

Test: All five LOs. Must include a labelling question (cell or plant structure), a photosynthesis explanation question, and a biotechnology evaluation question.

Watch for in Pre-Knowledge Check Q1: If a student's answer to "where do plants get their food" changes from "soil/fertilizer" to anything involving light/photosynthesis/air, that's the core learning outcome. Track this.
