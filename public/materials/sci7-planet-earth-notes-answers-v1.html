Section 1: Earth's Interior

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Deepest drill hole (Kola) reached ~12 km; crust up to 70 km thick
Interior knowledge comes from seismic waves
Oceanic crust:   thick,   dense thin; more dense (basalt)
Continental crust:   thick,   dense thick; less dense (granite)
Crust + upper mantle = lithosphere
Mantle is   but   over geological time solid; flows/moves
Mantle contains   currents convection
These drive   tectonic plate motion
Outer core: liquid   and   iron; nickel
Outer core generates Earth's magnetic field
Inner core:   iron and nickel solid
Inner core solid because extreme pressure keeps it solid despite high temperature

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Crust → mantle → outer core → inner core
  2. Extreme pressure at the inner core prevents atoms from moving freely despite the high temperature; pressure overcomes the thermal energy
  3. Liquid iron-nickel moving in the outer core generates Earth's magnetic field, which deflects harmful solar radiation
  4. Seismic waves travel through Earth at different speeds through different materials; they bend and reflect at layer boundaries; by analyzing the timing and paths of waves at many stations, scientists mapped the interior structure
  5. Stretch: Oceanic crust is denser (basalt) than continental crust (granite); at convergent boundaries, the denser oceanic plate subducts; as it descends, it melts back into mantle — completing part of the rock cycle; less dense continental crust cannot sink

Section 2: The Rock Cycle

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Igneous from cooled magma (underground) or lava (surface)
Intrusive cooling rate / crystal size slow; large crystals
Extrusive cooling rate / crystal size fast; small crystals or glassy
Sedimentary from compressed/cemented sediment and organic material
Clastic sedimentary examples sandstone, shale, conglomerate
Chemical sedimentary examples rock salt, gypsum
Biologic sedimentary examples coal, chalk, some limestone
Sedimentary important because contains fossils; layers record history
Metamorphic conditions heat + pressure, no melting
Limestone → marble
Shale → slate
Granite → gneiss
Foliated metamorphic visible layers/bands (slate, schist)
Non-foliated metamorphic no distinct layers (marble, quartzite)
Rock cycle: melting + cooling → igneous
Rock cycle: weathering/erosion/deposition → sedimentary
Rock cycle: heat + pressure (no melt) → metamorphic

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Intrusive: cools slowly underground → large crystals (time to grow); Extrusive: cools rapidly at surface → tiny or no visible crystals (atoms freeze before arranging into large crystals)
  2. Sediment deposited in horizontal layers over time; lower layers are older; layers reveal the sequence of environments (marine, river, desert) that existed at that location through time
  3. Conditions: heat + pressure; conditions that do NOT occur: melting (if melted → becomes magma → igneous, not metamorphic)
  4. Sedimentary rock — organisms are rapidly buried in sediment (anaerobic conditions slow decay) and hard parts are preserved; igneous rock forms from hot magma (would destroy organic material); metamorphic transforms under heat/pressure (destroys fossils)
  5. Stretch: Original limestone formed from marine organisms (biogenic sedimentary); high-grade metamorphism created marble; now eroding; weathered marble fragments transported by water → deposited in river/lake → compacted → new sedimentary rock over millions of years

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
~  major plates 15

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Mantle convection currents — hot mantle material rises, spreads, cools, sinks, creating circulation that drags plates
  2. Divergent: plates separate; mid-ocean ridges; Convergent: plates collide; mountains/trenches/volcanoes; Transform: plates slide sideways; earthquakes/no new crust
  3. Neither continental plate is dense enough to subduct; both compress and buckle upward forming mountains
  4. Earthquakes and volcanoes are not random; they cluster along plate boundaries — where plates interact; mapping their distribution reveals boundary locations
  5. Stretch: Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic divergent boundary; the two plates are pulling apart; new magma rises to fill the gap; eventually the rift will widen enough that the island splits; this is the same process forming new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges

Section 4: Earthquakes and Volcanoes

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Plates locked by friction
Stress builds until rocks break and slip along fault
Focus = underground point where rock breaks
Epicenter = surface point directly above focus
Richter scale logarithmic energy measurement; each step = 10× more energy
Mercalli scale observational effects (what people feel/see)
Shield volcano: lava viscosity low (fluid)
Shield eruption style gentle; flows slowly
Shield shape broad, low dome
Composite: lava viscosity high (thick)
Composite eruption explosive; violent
Why viscosity determines style fluid lava releases gas gradually; thick lava traps gas → pressure → explosion
Alberta seismic activity small earthquakes from fracking and wastewater injection

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Tectonic plates lock at boundaries due to friction; stress builds as plates try to move; when stress exceeds rock strength, rocks break → sudden slip → seismic waves radiate outward
  2. Focus = underground rupture point; epicenter = surface point directly above; epicenter is usually where strongest shaking felt
  3. Richter: measures energy released (instrument/seismograph); Mercalli: measures effects (what observers experience); both measure earthquake size but from different perspectives
  4. Shield volcano; low-viscosity lava allows gas to escape gradually → no explosive pressure buildup → tourists can safely observe flows
  5. Stretch: Mauna Loa: low-viscosity lava; gas escapes gradually; slow-moving flows give time for evacuation; much larger lava volume. Mt. St. Helens: high-viscosity lava traps gas; pressure builds dramatically; explosive blast sideways (Plinian eruption); suddenly lethal

Section 5: Weathering, Erosion, Deposition

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Weathering = breaking rock without   moving it
Mechanical weathering — composition   doesn't change
Freeze-thaw: water freezes → expands → widens crack → breaks rock
Root growth: roots   crack rock pry apart
Chemical weathering — composition   changes
Acid rain: CO₂ + water → carbonic acid; dissolves limestone
Oxidation: oxygen + iron → rust (iron oxide)
Erosion = transport by water, wind, ice, gravity
Deposition when erosion agent slows down

Geological change mostly slow / imperceptible / gradual

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Weathering: breaks rock in place; Erosion: transports broken material elsewhere; weathering must occur before erosion (produces the material to be transported)
  2. Mechanical: freeze-thaw (water expands in cracks); Chemical: oxidation (iron in rock rusts); difference: mechanical = shape change only; chemical = composition changes
  3. Hoodoos: hard caprock protects the softer shale beneath from rainfall/runoff; surrounding softer rock erodes faster; hard cap and pillar of protected rock remain as a column; caprock eventually erodes and hoodoo collapses
  4. Glaciers transported them during the last ice age (~20,000 years ago); glaciers covered most of Alberta; boulders frozen into ice were carried south/east from source mountains; when glaciers melted, boulders were dropped wherever the ice stopped
  5. Stretch: Prairies were covered by glaciers; as ice retreated, it deposited till (unsorted sediment of clay, sand, gravel, boulders) across the landscape; the flat surface is the glacial till plain; geological activity was extensive, even if not dramatic; the result was one of the world's most productive agricultural soils

Section 6: Fossils and Geological Time

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Fossil = any   of past life preserved evidence
Replacement: minerals   original material replace atom by atom
Mould: organism  , leaving impression dissolves
Cast: mould fills with minerals → 3D replica
Compression: organism   under sediment flattened
Trace fossil: preserved   activity/behaviour (footprints, burrows)
Most fossils are microfossils / small organisms
Fossil formation conditions rapid burial; in sediment; mineral-rich water; hard parts
Law of superposition: older layers are   deeper / lower
Index fossils: abundant, widespread,   time period short
Alberta fossils in Cretaceous sedimentary rock; exposed by river erosion
Geological time scale: Mesozoic = "Age of Dinosaurs"; 252–66 million years ago

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Conditions: rapid burial before decomposition; in sediment; mineral-rich groundwater to replace organic material; fossils are rare because: most organisms decay before burial; soft tissue rarely preserved; specific sedimentary environment required
  2. Law of superposition: in undisturbed rock layers, lower layers are older and upper layers are younger; used to determine the relative sequence of geological events without knowing actual dates
  3. Alberta's Cretaceous (75 million years ago) sedimentary layers are exposed at the surface in the Badlands by river erosion; the Western Interior Seaway covered Alberta then — perfect conditions for marine and nearshore dinosaur fossil preservation
  4. (1) Mould: organism decays, leaves hollow impression in rock; (2) Trace fossil: preserved activity (footprint, burrow) rather than the organism itself
  5. Stretch: Rocks are the same age (same index fossil = same time period, same environment); to determine actual age in years, need absolute dating (radiometric decay measurement — e.g., uranium-lead, potassium-argon); law of superposition gives sequence but not calendar dates
