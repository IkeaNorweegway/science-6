Two conceptual moves. First: Earth is dynamic — everything we see is a result of slow, ongoing processes, not a fixed backdrop. Second: we can read the past from the present — rock layers, fossils, and seismic data are all recordings of geological history. Alberta is one of the best places on Earth to teach this unit because the evidence is local: Rockies, Badlands, oil sands, glacial erratics. This unit should feel grounded in place, not abstract.

Materials per Lesson

  • Every lesson: Student notes packages, whiteboard / projector
  • Lesson 1: No materials needed — pre-knowledge check is a drawing exercise
  • Lesson 3: Rock samples: granite, basalt, sandstone, shale, marble, quartzite (rock kits from science supply companies; or self-collected)
  • Lesson 4: Photos of Alberta rock types: oil sands, Rockies limestone, Badlands shale
  • Lesson 5: Outline map of the continents (political borders and names removed); scissors; fossil distribution map
  • Lesson 6: Animated plate boundary diagram or board-drawn step-by-step
  • Lesson 7: Richter scale reference; logarithmic scale diagram
  • Lesson 8: Shield vs. composite volcano images; thick honey and thin water in tilted containers (viscosity demo)
  • Lesson 9: Images of Alberta Badlands, Royal Tyrrell Museum, hoodoos
  • Lesson 10: Rock column diagram with multiple layers (drawn or printed)

Alberta Context — Must Appear in This Unit

At minimum, use these Alberta-specific examples in class:

  1. Rocky Mountains — Section 3 (plate tectonics) and Section 4 (earthquakes: Alberta is quiet today, but Rockies show tectonic past)
  2. Badlands (Drumheller) — Sections 2, 6 (sedimentary rock, Cretaceous fossils, river erosion)
  3. Oil sands — Section 2 (sedimentary origin) and Section 6 (Cretaceous inland sea)
  4. Glacial erratics — Section 5 (glaciation, deposition)
  5. Athabasca Sand Dunes — Section 5 (wind erosion, aeolian deposition)

Section 1 — Earth's Interior

Lessons 1–2 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — What's Inside Earth?

Show

Board: Pre-knowledge check question — "Draw what you think Earth's interior looks like." Students sketch quickly. You'll see everything from solid all the way through, to hollow Earth, to fiery core with no structure. Collect visual observations without correcting.

Say

"We have never drilled to the mantle. The deepest hole ever drilled (Kola Superdeep, Russia) reached 12 km after 24 years. The mantle begins at 30–70 km down. Everything we know about Earth's interior comes from seismic waves — the 'echoes' of earthquakes."

Cue — ✎ Draw

Students complete pre-knowledge sketch. Section 1 notes: Earth's layers (crust, mantle, outer core, inner core).

Beat 1.2 — Layers and Their Properties

Show

Board: Cross-section diagram of Earth with labeled layers. Ask students to predict: "Why is the inner core solid but the outer core is liquid?" Let them reason from temperature and pressure before explaining.

Say

"The inner core is hotter than the outer core, but it's under so much pressure that the atoms can't escape into a liquid state. Pressure keeps it solid even at extreme temperatures. This is also why diamonds form deep underground."

"How does the liquid outer core produce the magnetic field?" — brief, no equations: moving liquid metal conducts electricity; moving electricity generates magnetism.

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students label Earth's cross-section diagram: crust, mantle, outer core, inner core — with state (solid/liquid) and key property for each. Exit Ticket 1.

Misconception Flag — Alberta Has No Geological History

Address from the first lesson. Alberta is one of the richest geological records on Earth: Rockies (plate tectonics), Badlands (Cretaceous inland sea), oil sands (compressed marine organisms), glacial erratics (last ice age). Use Alberta examples throughout every section — this is not a nice-to-have, it is an outcome (LO 7-5-05 and 7-5-06).

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Layer order: Crust → mantle → outer core → inner core.
  2. Why inner core is solid: Extreme pressure prevents atoms from moving freely despite the high temperature; pressure overcomes the thermal energy.
  3. Magnetic field: Liquid iron-nickel moving in the outer core generates Earth's magnetic field, which deflects harmful solar radiation.
  4. How we know the interior: Seismic waves travel through Earth at different speeds through different materials; they bend and reflect at layer boundaries; by analyzing the timing and paths of waves at many stations, scientists mapped the interior structure.
  5. Stretch: Oceanic crust is denser (basalt) than continental crust (granite); at convergent boundaries, the denser oceanic plate subducts; as it descends, it melts back into mantle — completing part of the rock cycle; less dense continental crust cannot sink.

Section 2 — The Rock Cycle

Lessons 3–4 2 beats 0 flags

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — Three Families of Rock

Show

Board: Rock samples — granite, basalt, sandstone, shale, marble, quartzite. Pass around. Ask students to classify by texture and appearance before they know the names. What patterns do they see?

Say

"Every rock on Earth is one of three types. And every rock can become any other type given the right conditions. There is no permanent rock — just rock in different stages of a continuous cycle."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students fill Section 2: rock family table (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic) with formation process and examples. Draw the rock cycle diagram. Connect each sample to an Alberta location if possible.

Beat 2.2 — The Rock Cycle and Alberta's Rocks

Show

Board: Photos of Alberta's rock types: oil sands (sedimentary), Rockies limestone and quartzite, Badlands shale. Ask: "What does each tell us about what Alberta looked like when it formed?"

Say

"Alberta's Badlands are Cretaceous sedimentary rock — formed at the bottom of an inland sea 75 million years ago. The sediments that make up the Rockies were once seafloor. Alberta's oil sands are the compressed remains of marine organisms. Alberta's rocks are a deep-time record of this place."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 2 continued: Alberta rock types and what each reveals about geological history. Exit Ticket 2.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Intrusive vs. extrusive: Intrusive: cools slowly underground → large crystals (time to grow); Extrusive: cools rapidly at surface → tiny or no visible crystals.
  2. Why sedimentary layers matter: Sediment deposited in horizontal layers over time; lower layers are older; layers reveal the sequence of environments (marine, river, desert) that existed at that location through time.
  3. Metamorphic conditions: Heat + pressure; conditions that do NOT occur: melting (if melted → becomes magma → igneous, not metamorphic).
  4. Why fossils in sedimentary only: Organisms rapidly buried in sediment (anaerobic conditions slow decay) and hard parts preserved; igneous = hot magma (destroys organic material); metamorphic = heat/pressure (destroys fossils).
  5. Stretch: Original limestone formed from marine organisms (biogenic sedimentary); high-grade metamorphism created marble; now eroding; weathered fragments transported → deposited → compacted → new sedimentary rock over millions of years.

Section 3 — Plate Tectonics

Lessons 5–6 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 5–6 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — The Evidence for Moving Plates

Show

Board: Outline map of the continents (names removed). Have students cut them out (or trace and rearrange) to see how South America and Africa fit together.
Show the fossil distribution map: same species on continents now separated by thousands of km of ocean.

Say

"Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912 and was ridiculed. Scientists asked: 'What force could move continents?' The answer came 50 years later: mantle convection. The evidence was right. The mechanism was missing."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 3: lines of evidence for plate tectonics (continental fit, fossil distribution, seafloor spreading, paleomagnetic striping). Extension: show paleomagnetic striping image alongside the puzzle.

Beat 3.2 — Three Boundary Types

Show

Board: Animated plate boundary diagram (or draw step-by-step). At each boundary ask: "What happens when plates move this way?"
Divergent (rifting, new crust) · Convergent (subduction or collision) · Transform (shearing, earthquakes)

Say

"The Rockies formed when an oceanic plate pushed under North America. The plates are moving today — at about the same rate your fingernails grow. In 50 million years, Alberta will look very different."

"Alberta is far from active plate boundaries today. But its rocks record a convergent collision. When?" — build toward Cretaceous context.

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students fill Section 3 continued: three boundary type diagrams with labels and Alberta connection. Exit Ticket 3.

Misconception Flag — Plates Are the Same as Continents

Address in Lesson 6. Draw a world map showing plate boundaries — most are underwater; the Pacific Plate is almost entirely ocean. Tectonic plates include both oceanic and continental crust; they do not align with continents. Show the map before notes so students can see this directly.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Driving force: Mantle convection currents — hot mantle material rises, spreads, cools, sinks, creating circulation that drags plates.
  2. Three boundary types: Divergent: plates separate; mid-ocean ridges. Convergent: plates collide; mountains/trenches/volcanoes. Transform: plates slide sideways; earthquakes/no new crust.
  3. Continent-continent collision: Neither plate is dense enough to subduct; both compress and buckle upward forming mountains.
  4. Earthquake/volcano distribution: Not random; they cluster along plate boundaries — where plates interact; mapping their distribution reveals boundary locations.
  5. Stretch: Iceland sits on the Mid-Atlantic divergent boundary; the two plates are pulling apart; new magma rises to fill the gap; this is the same process forming new oceanic crust at mid-ocean ridges.

Section 4 — Earthquakes and Volcanoes

Lessons 7–8 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 7–8 · Student notes: Section 4

Beat 4.1 — Earthquakes

Show

Board: Richter scale — logarithmic. Ask: "How many times more energy does a magnitude 7 release than a magnitude 6?" (10×) "Than a 5?" (100×).
Note: "The largest earthquakes release more energy than all the world's nuclear weapons combined."

Say

"Earthquakes happen when rock that has been locked — held in place by friction while plates try to move — suddenly slips. The energy stored over decades or centuries releases in seconds. This is why the ground can be perfectly still for 50 years and then shake violently."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4: earthquake mechanism, Richter scale (logarithmic), seismic waves types.

Beat 4.2 — Volcanoes and Surface Processes

Show

Board: Shield vs. composite volcano comparison images.
Viscosity demonstration: thick honey vs. thin water in a tilted container — which flows faster?
Ask: "What does viscosity predict about how a volcano will erupt?"

Say

"Lava viscosity determines whether a volcano is gentle or explosive. Fluid lava releases gas as it goes — gradual flow. Thick lava traps gas until the pressure overcomes the resistance — explosion."

Alberta weathering connection: "What has been happening to Alberta's surface since the glaciers left?"

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Sections 4 and 5: shield vs. composite volcano comparison, viscosity-eruption type connection, Alberta weathering examples. Exit Tickets 4 and 5.

Misconception Flag — Geological Change Is Always Fast or Catastrophic

Address in Lessons 5 and 8. Calculate plate movement rate: 3 cm/year × 100 million years = 3,000 km. The Rockies formed over tens of millions of years. Contrast with earthquake (seconds) and volcanic eruption (days to weeks). Geological change operates on vastly different timescales — most is slow and continuous, occasionally punctuated by rapid events.

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Earthquake mechanism: Tectonic plates lock at boundaries due to friction; stress builds as plates try to move; when stress exceeds rock strength, rocks break → sudden slip → seismic waves radiate outward.
  2. Focus vs. epicenter: Focus = underground rupture point; epicenter = surface point directly above; epicenter is usually where strongest shaking is felt.
  3. Richter vs. Mercalli: Richter: measures energy released (instrument/seismograph); Mercalli: measures effects (what observers experience); both measure earthquake size from different perspectives.
  4. Hawaii volcano type: Shield volcano; low-viscosity lava allows gas to escape gradually → no explosive pressure buildup → tourists can safely observe flows.
  5. Stretch: Mauna Loa: low-viscosity lava; gas escapes gradually; slow-moving flows; much larger lava volume. Mt. St. Helens: high-viscosity lava traps gas; pressure builds dramatically; explosive blast; suddenly lethal.

Section 5 — Surface Processes

Lesson 8 (continued) 1 beat 0 flags

See Beat 4.2 above — Sections 4 and 5 are addressed together in Lesson 8.

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Weathering vs. erosion: Weathering breaks rock in place; erosion transports broken material elsewhere; weathering must occur before erosion (produces the material to be transported).
  2. Mechanical vs. chemical: Mechanical: freeze-thaw (water expands in cracks); Chemical: oxidation (iron in rock rusts); difference: mechanical = shape change only; chemical = composition changes.
  3. Hoodoo formation: Hard caprock protects softer shale beneath from rainfall/runoff; surrounding softer rock erodes faster; hard cap and pillar of protected rock remain as a column; caprock eventually erodes and hoodoo collapses.
  4. Glacial erratics: Glaciers transported them during the last ice age (~20,000 years ago); boulders frozen into ice were carried south/east from source mountains; when glaciers melted, boulders were dropped wherever the ice stopped.
  5. Stretch: Prairies were covered by glaciers; as ice retreated, it deposited till (unsorted sediment) across the landscape; the flat surface is the glacial till plain; the result was one of the world's most productive agricultural soils.

Section 6 — Fossils and Geological Time

Lessons 9–10 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 9–10 · Student notes: Section 6

Beat 6.1 — Fossils and Geological Time

Show

Board: Images of Alberta Badlands, Royal Tyrrell Museum, hoodoos. Ask: "What does this landscape tell us about Alberta's past?"
Fossil variety: marine shells, fish scales, plant imprints, insect in amber, microfossils — not just dinosaur bones.

Say

"The Badlands expose 75-million-year-old Cretaceous sedimentary rock. Alberta was covered by a shallow inland sea. The marine and dinosaur fossils found there are the remains of organisms that lived in and around that sea. When you walk through the Badlands, you're walking through 75 million years of geological time, exposed by 10,000 years of river erosion."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 6: fossil formation process, geological time scale, Cretaceous inland sea connection to Alberta Badlands.

Misconception Flag — Fossils Are Mostly Dinosaur Bones

Show fossil variety in Lesson 9: marine shells, fish scales, plant imprints, insect in amber, microfossils. Most fossils are of marine organisms, not dinosaurs. The Badlands contain both because the inland sea and its margins were dinosaur habitat. Alberta's Cambrian and Cretaceous records are both important.

Beat 6.2 — Reading Rock Layers

Show

Board: Rock column diagram with multiple layers. Have students use law of superposition to interpret the history from bottom (oldest) to top (youngest).
Extension: add an angular unconformity (tilted layer) for challenge.

Say

"Rock layers are like pages in a history book — the oldest pages are at the bottom. Geologists can read Alberta's entire geological history from the layers exposed in the Badlands, the Rocky Mountains, and in well cores from oil and gas exploration."

Cue — ★ Write

Students interpret the rock column using law of superposition. Write one sentence describing each layer's geological event in order. Exit Ticket 6. Extension rock column: include one unconformity, one igneous intrusion, one fold. Use as challenge only — most Grade 7 students find this extension level.

Section 6 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Fossil formation conditions: Rapid burial before decomposition; in sediment; mineral-rich groundwater to replace organic material; fossils are rare because most organisms decay before burial; soft tissue rarely preserved.
  2. Law of superposition: In undisturbed rock layers, lower layers are older and upper layers are younger; used to determine the relative sequence of geological events without knowing actual dates.
  3. Alberta Badlands: Cretaceous (75 million years ago) sedimentary layers exposed at the surface by river erosion; the Western Interior Seaway covered Alberta — perfect conditions for marine and dinosaur fossil preservation.
  4. Fossil types: (1) Mould: organism decays, leaves hollow impression in rock; (2) Trace fossil: preserved activity (footprint, burrow) rather than the organism itself.
  5. Stretch: Same index fossil = same time period; to determine actual age in years, need absolute dating (radiometric decay measurement); law of superposition gives sequence but not calendar dates.
