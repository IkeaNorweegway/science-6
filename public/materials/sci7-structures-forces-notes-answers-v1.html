Section 1: Types of Structures

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Structure definition supports a load or withstands forces without changing shape
Solid — made of   material continuous / uniform
Frame — rigid   of connected members skeleton / network
Shell —   curved surface thin
Combination structures combine two or more types; most real structures
Shape vs. material shape/form matters as much as material; tube > flat sheet

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Frame: rigid skeleton with open spaces; Shell: thin curved outer surface that carries load through its shape
  2. (a) egg = shell (b) bicycle = frame (c) concrete dam = solid
  3. Example: a house (frame skeleton + shell exterior + solid foundation); reasons match the function of each part
  4. The curved tube shape distributes force around the circumference; the flat sheet buckles under direct compression

Section 2: Loads

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Dead load = weight of structure itself; always present; always vertical
Live load = weight of people/objects structure supports; varies; vertical
Dynamic load = variable, unpredictable force; often lateral
Wind direction lateral (horizontal)
Earthquake direction lateral and vertical
Dynamic loads most dangerous because unpredictable direction; can cause resonance

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Dead = structure's own weight (steel of a bridge); Live = loads the structure is designed to carry (cars, people)
  2. Dynamic loads can come from any direction, be unpredictable in magnitude, and cause resonance oscillations that grow over time
  3. Snow accumulation = live load; no wind or seismic = dynamic loads ruled out; accumulated snow = excessive live load
  4. (a) dead (b) dynamic (c) dynamic (d) live

Section 3: Internal Forces

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Compression = forces   material together squeezing
Compression direction inward / pushing in
Resists compression well concrete, stone, brick
Tension = forces   material apart pulling
Tension direction outward / pulling apart
Resists tension well steel cables, ropes
Shear = forces on   planes in   directions parallel; opposing
Torsion =   force twisting
Beam: compression on   surface top
Beam: tension on   surface bottom
Reinforced concrete = concrete (compression) + steel rebar (tension)

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Compression pushes material together (inward); tension pulls apart (outward); key difference: direction of force
  2. Plain concrete resists compression well but cracks under tension; steel rebar handles the tension that concrete can't
  3. (a) tension (b) compression (c) shear
  4. Diagram: beam supported at both ends, load in middle; C marked on top surface; T marked on bottom surface
  5. Stretch: I-beam under torsion: would twist and deform at the flanges, possible failure at welds; concrete under tension: brittle fracture — cracks and fails suddenly with little deformation warning

Section 4: Stability

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Centre of gravity = point where all gravitational force can be considered to act
CoG for uniform object at geometric centre
Stable when CoG is   base of support within
When CoG moves outside base structure topples
Lower CoG → more stable
Wider base → more stable
Triangle strongest because cannot deform without changing side lengths
Buckling — cause compressive force in slender column
Toppling — cause CoG moves outside base of support

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Structure topples when its centre of gravity moves outside its base of support
  2. Triangle cannot change shape (deform) without changing its side lengths; a square can become a rhombus without any length change → triangles resist lateral deformation that squares cannot
  3. Low body = low centre of gravity; same wheelbase (base of support); lower CoG relative to base = more stable
  4. Widen the base; lower the centre of gravity (place heavy components lower in the structure)
  5. Stretch diagram: Small load: CoG within base, crane stable; large load at far end: CoG shifts toward load end; if CoG passes beyond edge of base → toppling. Base design: wide outriggers/counterweights on opposite side

Section 5: Simple Machines

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Simple machine changes direction or magnitude of a force
MA = output/load force ÷ input/effort force
MA > 1 multiplies force; effort over greater distance
MA = 1 changes direction only
MA < 1 multiplies speed/distance; effort force is greater
1st class lever examples seesaw, crowbar, scissors
2nd class lever MA always > 1
3rd class lever MA always < 1
Wheel and axle MA radius of wheel ÷ radius of axle
Single fixed pulley MA 1 (direction change only)
Single movable pulley MA 2
Compound MA = number of rope segments
Inclined plane MA length ÷ height
Screw = inclined plane   wrapped around a cylinder

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. MA = output force ÷ input force; MA of 3 means output force is 3× input force (applied over 3× the distance)
  2. (a) inclined plane (b) 3rd class lever (c) single fixed pulley (d) wheel and axle (jar lid = screw)
  3. MA = 200 ÷ 50 = 4
  4. No — work is conserved; MA of 4 means 1/4 the force but 4× the distance; total work (force × distance) is the same; machines don't create energy
  5. Stretch: MA = 600 ÷ 150 = 4 rope segments; diagram shows 4 ropes supporting the lower movable pulley

Section 6: Materials and Design

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer
Strength = resists breaking under force
Hardness = resists scratching/denting
Flexibility/ductility = bends without breaking
Elasticity = returns to original shape after deformation
Brittleness = fractures with little deformation
Column under compression — good material concrete or steel
Suspension bridge cable — needs tensile strength; steel
Earthquake building frame — needs flexibility; steel
Cutting edge — needs hardness

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Strength: resists breaking under force; Hardness: resists surface damage (scratching/denting) — a material can be hard but brittle
  2. Steel is flexible; deforms rather than shattering under sudden lateral seismic forces; concrete is brittle — cracks catastrophically
  3. (a) tensile strength (b) compressive strength (c) flexibility/impact resistance
  4. Stretch: Wood advantages: locally available (Alberta forests), renewable, good strength-to-weight ratio, workable; limitations: susceptible to fire, rot, insects; requires treatment for moisture; multi-story: modern mass timber (CLT) technology addresses many limitations; generally viable for up to ~18 storeys with current technology
