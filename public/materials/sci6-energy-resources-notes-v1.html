Misconception — "Renewable = Clean"

Renewable means the resource is   naturally and will not run out. It does NOT mean the resource is environmentally harmless. Large hydroelectric dams flood entire river ecosystems. Wind turbines require land and affect bird migration. Solar panel manufacturing uses toxic materials. Renewable and clean are separate ideas — a resource can be one without being the other.

Notes

Some energy resources can be used   in their natural form — before any processing or conversion happens.

Some energy resources must be   into another form first — after processing — before we can use the energy in them.

This does NOT map cleanly onto renewable versus non-renewable. A resource can be renewable AND used before processing, or renewable AND used after processing. The two categories are  .

Wind is a renewable resource that is used both before and after processing:

Misconception — "Before processing = renewable, after processing = non-renewable"

This is not true. The before/after processing categories and the renewable/non-renewable categories are completely independent. Look at the examples:

  • Wind (  — renewable) can be used before processing (windmill) or after (turbine)
  • Wood (can be renewable) can be used before processing (burning logs) or after (charcoal)
  • Crude oil (non-renewable) is only used after processing — but that is not because it is non-renewable. It is because oil in its raw form is not useful as a fuel.

Do not mix these two classification systems. They answer different questions.

Notes

Choosing which energy resource to use is not simple. Many factors must be considered. Scientists, engineers, governments, and communities all weigh these   when making energy decisions.

There are five selection factors:

Factor 1: Availability

Is the resource present in   quantity at the location?

  • A region with consistent   can use wind power effectively.
  • A region with little sunlight in winter may not get enough energy from   panels year-round.
  • A community without a nearby   cannot use hydroelectric power.

Availability is about geography and   — does the resource exist in usable amounts, where and when it is needed?

Factor 2: Societal Impact

How does using this resource affect people and communities? Societal impact includes:

  •   — does extracting or generating this energy create employment?
  •   effects — does the resource benefit or harm nearby communities?
  • Public   — do people support or oppose this energy project in their community?
  •   — can this energy source deliver power consistently, or does it only work when the sun shines or wind blows?

Indigenous land stewardship principles align closely with this factor. First Nations and Métis communities in Alberta understand resources as being held   for future generations — not simply extracted for present-day benefit. Many First Nations and Métis communities have been significantly affected by oil sands development on or near their territories. Today, many of the same communities are leaders in   energy development on their lands, bringing both economic benefit and stewardship values to the work.

Factor 3: Economic Impact

What does it cost? Economic impact includes the cost to:

  •   the system (building a wind farm, installing solar panels, drilling for natural gas)
  •   the system (ongoing upkeep and repairs)
  •   the system day-to-day (running costs)

A resource that is cheap to extract may be expensive to process. A resource that is expensive to install (e.g., solar) may cost almost nothing to operate once built. Economic decisions look at the   cost over the full life of the project.

Factor 4: Environmental Impact

How does using this resource affect the environment? Environmental impact includes:

  •   — releasing greenhouse gases or particulates into the air
  •   use — how much land is disturbed or taken up?
  •   disruption — does extraction or infrastructure harm wildlife or ecosystems?
  • Water   — does the extraction process affect rivers, groundwater, or lakes?
Connection to Computer Science

Technology is designed to solve problems, but it always has unintended consequences. Smart grids, electric vehicles, and renewable energy infrastructure are computational artifacts — they are designed systems, built to reduce emissions and improve energy efficiency. But they have their own unintended consequences: mining rare earth metals for batteries and solar panels disturbs ecosystems; rapid expansion of electric vehicles creates e-waste; connecting more renewable sources to the electrical grid can cause   instability when supply and demand are mismatched. Every technological solution creates new trade-offs that must be monitored and managed.

Factor 5: Responsible Management

How do we use the resource wisely over the long term? Responsible management includes:

  •   — using less energy overall, reducing waste
  • Long-term   — will this resource still be available and affordable in 50 years?
  •   monitoring — using data to track the impacts of energy use and adjust as needed
  • Leaving options open for   generations — not locking in decisions that cannot be reversed

This factor connects directly to scientific methods: responsible management requires   collection (how much energy is being used?), evidence-based decisions (what do the data show about environmental impact?), and a willingness to   decisions when evidence changes.

This factor also aligns with Indigenous land stewardship — the idea that the land, water, and resources we use today are held in trust, not owned. What we take now affects what is available to those who come after us.
