Q5 ★ Core Alberta

Cluster 5 — Environmental change · trophic cascade · Apply (LO 7-1-04)

In the 1950s, wolves were removed from Banff National Park. The following changes were observed over the next 30 years:

  • Elk populations tripled
  • Willows, aspens, and sedges along riverbanks were heavily grazed down
  • Stream banks eroded significantly
  • Beaver populations declined sharply
  • Songbird species in riparian zones declined

(a) Which trophic level was directly affected first by wolf removal? Name it and explain what specific interaction was disrupted.

(b) Trace the cascade from wolf removal to beaver decline using at least four linked steps. Include both biotic and abiotic changes in your chain.

Wolf removal → elk populations _______________________ → riparian plants _______________________ → stream banks _______________________ → beavers _______________________

(c) This chain of effects — a top predator removal triggering changes through multiple trophic levels and into the physical environment — is called a trophic cascade. Does this evidence suggest ecosystems are stable or fragile? Take a position and defend it with specific evidence from the Banff example.

Is Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) consistent with scientific understanding of ecosystem health? Explain your reasoning with specific evidence.

Q8 ★★ Extended Alberta

Cluster 1 — Biotic-abiotic interactions · Analyze (LO 7-1-01, 7-1-04)

A pulp mill begins discharging warm water into the North Saskatchewan River near Edmonton, raising average water temperature by 3°C year-round. Scientists monitoring the river record these changes over five years:

  • Dissolved oxygen drops by 25%
  • Bull trout (a cold-water species) populations decline sharply
  • Common carp (warm-water tolerant) begin appearing in large numbers
  • Aquatic insect diversity drops by 40%
  • Algae blooms increase dramatically each summer

(a) The mill is altering an abiotic factor. Trace the cause-and-effect chain from temperature increase to bull trout decline. Use at least three linked steps and be specific about which abiotic change harms the trout and why.

(b) Carp are generalists that tolerate wide temperature ranges. Bull trout require cold, well-oxygenated water. What does the replacement of bull trout by carp tell us about what has happened to biodiversity in this section of the river?

(c) The mill manager states: "Fish populations are recovering — we can see lots of carp now." Evaluate this claim. Is species count alone a reliable measure of ecosystem health? What has actually happened to biodiversity, and what is the manager getting wrong?

Q9 ★ Core Alberta

Cluster 4 — Biodiversity · keystone species · Apply (LO 7-1-03)

Grizzly bears in Alberta's mountain ecosystems are considered a keystone species — a species whose impact on the ecosystem is disproportionately large relative to its population size. Grizzlies eat berries, ground squirrels, carrion, and salmon (in BC). When they drag salmon carcasses inland, they deposit marine nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) in forest soil, fertilising trees up to 500 m from streams.

(a) Explain what "keystone species" means in your own words. The term comes from architecture — a keystone is the central stone in an arch that holds all others in place. Explain how this analogy applies to a keystone species in an ecosystem.

(b) Grizzly bears are listed as threatened in Alberta. Predict two specific ecosystem effects if grizzly bears were extirpated from their remaining Alberta mountain habitat. For each: name the effect, identify the organisms most affected, and explain the mechanism.

(c) A landowner says: "Grizzlies are dangerous and rare — only a few hundred left. Getting rid of the last ones wouldn't really affect the ecosystem." Using the definition of keystone species, explain precisely why this reasoning is flawed.

Q11 ★ Core Alberta

Cluster 6 — Sustainability · invasive species · ecosystem services · Apply (LO 7-1-04)

Purple loosestrife is an invasive plant from Europe, first recorded in Alberta in the mid-1900s. It grows aggressively in wetlands and along riverbanks, forming dense mats that crowd out native plants like cattails, bulrushes, and sedges. By the early 2000s it had colonised thousands of hectares of Alberta wetland.

(a) Invasive species are a major threat to biodiversity. Using purple loosestrife as your example, explain specifically how one invasive plant can reduce biodiversity — even if it doesn't directly kill any animals.

(b) Wetlands provide ecosystem services — benefits ecosystems provide to humans and other species. Name two specific ecosystem services that Alberta wetlands provide. For each, explain how purple loosestrife invasion would reduce that service.

(c) Alberta uses a biocontrol program — releasing Galerucella beetles that eat loosestrife in its native European range — to manage the plant. What is one ecological risk of introducing a second non-native species to control the first? How would scientists evaluate whether this tradeoff is worth taking?

Q12 ★★ Extended Alberta

Cluster 5 — Environmental change · cumulative effects · habitat fragmentation · Evaluate (LO 7-1-04)

In the Peace River region of northern Alberta, a 200 km2 area of boreal forest is being converted to agriculture over 15 years. The forest contains woodland caribou, black bears, wolves, moose, snowshoe hares, and hundreds of bird and plant species. Each year, additional sections are cleared.

Developer claim to evaluate

"We're only clearing 5% of the forest this year — that's a small impact. The ecosystem will recover between clearing phases. Nature always bounces back."

(a) The developer's claim ignores the concept of cumulative effects. Define cumulative effects and explain why clearing 5% per year for 15 years is ecologically different from a single 75% disturbance followed by full recovery time.

(b) Woodland caribou require large, continuous areas of undisturbed old-growth forest. Clearing creates "edges" — boundaries between forest and open land — where wolves hunt more effectively because prey are more visible. Explain how forest fragmentation can harm caribou even if the total amount of forest cleared seems small.

(c) Is the claim that "nature always bounces back" scientifically defensible? Identify two conditions that determine whether an ecosystem can recover from disturbance, and explain whether those conditions are likely to be met in this particular scenario.

S1 Stretch

Clusters 3 + 4 — Energy pyramid · Biodiversity · Create / Evaluate

You are asked to design a self-sustaining 1 km2 nature reserve in central Alberta. You must choose organisms at each trophic level so the ecosystem functions independently for 20 years with no human intervention.

(a) Using the 10% rule and energy pyramid, explain why you cannot stock equal numbers of producers and top predators. What ratio would you expect between trophic levels, and why does the math force this?

(b) You can choose one keystone species to include. Choose an Alberta species, explain its ecological role, and justify why including it is more valuable to reserve stability than simply adding more producers.

(c) You propose to include decomposers (bacteria, fungi, invertebrates). A planning committee member says: "That's a waste of space — decomposers don't add anything useful." Write a formal response explaining precisely what would happen to the reserve without decomposers, using nutrient cycling and energy flow as your evidence.

S2 Stretch Alberta

Clusters 5 + 6 — Environmental change · Sustainability · Evaluate

Alberta's boreal forest is warming at roughly twice the global average rate. Scientists predict that by 2080, the southern boundary of boreal forest will shift 200–300 km northward — meaning areas that are currently boreal will become parkland or grassland.

(a) Is this a natural disturbance or a human-caused disturbance? Justify your classification — trace the cause from its origin to the effect on Alberta's boreal forest.

(b) Woodland caribou can only survive in old-growth boreal forest. If the forest boundary shifts 200 km northward over 80 years, what would caribou need in order to survive the shift? Consider both biological requirements and habitat requirements in your answer.

(c) Cree, Dene, and Métis communities of the boreal forest have observed changes in animal migration patterns, plant timing, and water levels for generations. A scientist says: "We need satellite data and peer-reviewed studies — Traditional Ecological Knowledge alone is not sufficient." Evaluate this position. What does TEK offer that satellite data cannot, and vice versa? How could both approaches work together more effectively?

S3 Stretch Alberta

Clusters 1 + 2 + 4 — Ecosystems · Energy flow · Biodiversity · Evaluate / Create

Three changes happen simultaneously to a southern Alberta wetland:

  1. A drought reduces water levels by 60% (abiotic change)
  2. Purple loosestrife invades, displacing 70% of native aquatic plants (biotic change)
  3. A nearby road is built, increasing road-salt runoff and raising salinity (abiotic change)

(a) For each of the three changes above, predict one specific consequence for food web structure — which organism is most immediately affected and through which pathway?

(b) These three stressors are simultaneous. Explain why three simultaneous moderate stressors can be more damaging than a single severe stressor of the same total magnitude. Use the concept of ecosystem resilience in your explanation.

(c) You are an ecosystem manager with resources to address only one of the three stressors. Which would you address first, and why? Your answer must draw on food web structure, the energy pyramid, and biodiversity concepts from this unit — not just common sense.
