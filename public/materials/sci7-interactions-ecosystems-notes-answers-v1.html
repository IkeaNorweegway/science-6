Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Biotic = living (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria, decomposers); Abiotic = non-living (water, temp, sunlight, soil, wind)
  2. Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem
  3. Any two valid differences (e.g., more moisture in boreal; higher winter temperatures in boreal)
  4. Ecosystems include both biotic AND abiotic factors, plus their interactions
  5. Stretch: Biotic effects could include: reduced plant diversity from drought stress; shift in species composition; possible loss of species that can't adapt; new species invasions; abiotic effects cascade into biotic changes

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer Annotation
Photosynthesis photosynthesis Some students say "sunlight" — accept; photosynthesis is the process

Primary consumer also called herbivore

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Producer = makes own food via photosynthesis; essential because they are the energy source for all consumers
  2. Wolf = tertiary consumer (3rd trophic level above producers)
  3. True — decomposers consume dead organisms (a form of consuming)
  4. Arrows represent energy flow — from food source to the organism that eats it
  5. Stretch: Beaver removal → dams collapse → wetlands drain → loss of willow/aspen → fewer snowshoe hares → fewer lynx; loss of open water affects waterfowl, muskrat, fish populations

Section 3: Energy Pyramids

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer Annotation
10% rule — energy transferred ~10% Range 5–15% acceptable
Lost as heat Used for movement, digestion, body maintenance
Energy pyramid base producers Widest, most energy
Each level above narrower / has less energy  
Pyramid labels Producers / P.C. / S.C. / T.C. with ~10,000/1,000/100/10  
Top predators energy-poor yes; they receive only ~0.1% of original solar energy This surprises students consistently
Keystone species example grizzly bear (or wolf, beaver) Any appropriate Alberta species with explanation

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. The 10% rule: only about 10% of energy at one trophic level is available to the level above; the rest is lost as heat
  2. Primary consumers: 5,000 units; Secondary consumers: 500 units
  3. Pyramid: base (grass) 10,000 → rabbits/ground squirrels 1,000 → hawks/foxes 100 → coyotes/eagles 10
  4. Large territories needed because so little energy reaches top predator level; must range widely to find enough prey
  5. Stretch: Meadowlarks decline (less food); coyotes that eat only meadowlarks decline most; generalist coyotes that eat voles/rabbits less affected; indirect effect on grass (less grazing by grasshoppers = more grass temporarily)

Section 4: Biodiversity

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer Annotation
Biodiversity variety variety of species, genetic diversity, ecosystem types All three components for full mark
More biodiverse = more   resilient resilient / stable  
If one species disappears others can fill its role Core resilience argument
Beaver — keystone role creates wetland habitat with dams; food for wolves; affects water levels Multiple cascading effects
Wolf — keystone role regulates prey populations; creates "landscape of fear" affecting vegetation Wolf reintroduction narrative
Ecological niche includes where it lives + what it eats + when active + how it reproduces Habitat is just location; niche is the whole role
Competing for same niche one will eventually outcompete Competitive exclusion principle

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Biodiversity = variety of living species and ecosystems in an area; measured by number of species and their abundance
  2. More species = more redundancy; if one species declines, others fill its role; ecosystem resists collapse
  3. Keystone species has disproportionately large effect; examples: grizzly bear (Rockies), beaver (wetlands), wolf (boreal/mountain)
  4. Habitat = where an organism lives (location); niche = everything about how it lives (food, timing, behaviour, reproduction)
  5. Stretch: Wolf removal → more elk → more grazing of riverbank plants → fewer beavers (less willow/aspen) → eroding riverbanks → less songbird habitat. Wolf = keystone; its behavioural effects (elk avoiding riverbanks) ripple through entire ecosystem ("trophic cascade")

Section 5: Environmental Change

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer Annotation
Natural disturbances — forest fire effect burns existing vegetation; releases nutrients; opens canopy for new growth  
Succession gradual replacement of simpler communities by more complex ones Pioneer species → climax community
Human changes are more   severe / permanent / difficult to reverse  
Habitat loss mechanism destroys or fragments living space

Tipping points callout threshold beyond which recovery doesn't happen Examples: coral bleaching, permafrost loss, grassland conversion

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Natural disturbances: fire (boreal forest regenerates in 5–10 years; fire-adapted species return); drought (most grassland species adapted to periodic drought; recover when rain returns)
  2. Invasive species lack natural predators/parasites in new ecosystem; can grow unchecked; outcompete native species
  3. Fragmented habitat breaks up migration corridors; isolates populations; reduces gene flow; increases edge habitat (often lower quality)
  4. Any at-risk species with specific threats and conservation needs (see unit context for current list)
  5. Stretch: Boreal forest is large but not infinite; habitat loss has cumulative effects; individual clearcuts create fragmentation that affects caribou even if total forest area unchanged; some species (woodland caribou) require continuous old-growth, not fragments; tipping point = "minimum viable habitat" below which species cannot persist

Section 6: Sustainability and Stewardship

Blank / Prompt Expected Answer Annotation
Provisioning services food, water, timber, medicine Any 2 from list
Cultural services recreation, spiritual, educational, aesthetic Any 2 from list
Humans are   responsible responsible / stewards Not owners — a key TEK concept
TEK built from generations of careful observation  
TEK and Western science are complementary Not competing; both valid
Sustainability definition meeting current needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs Brundtland definition

Retrieval Check answers:

  1. Clean water (replacement: water treatment plant, $50–200M); carbon sequestration (replacement: industrial carbon capture, enormously expensive); pollination (replacement: hand pollination, economically impossible at scale)
  2. TEK = knowledge built from generations of careful observation in specific ecosystems; valuable because it captures long-term patterns, species relationships, and changes over time that Western science hasn't studied yet in those specific places
  3. Stewardship = responsibility to maintain the health and function of the land/ecosystem for present and future generations; not just using resources, but actively protecting them
  4. Stretch: Policy should reference: ecosystem services at risk; biodiversity principle (maintaining species diversity for resilience); Indigenous stewardship practices (e.g., prescribed burning, traditional harvesting practices that maintain balance)
