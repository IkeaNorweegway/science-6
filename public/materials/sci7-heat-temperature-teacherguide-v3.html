This unit has the densest misconception load in Grade 7 Science. Students bring strong but wrong intuitions about heat — cold is a substance, metal is colder than wood, heat and temperature are the same. The first four lessons are largely misconception correction before building the correct framework. Do not rush past Sections 1 and 2.

Section 1 — Temperature and Measurement

Lessons 1–2 4 beats 0 flags

Beat 1.1 — Pre-Knowledge Check: What Do You Think You Know?

Show

Board: Write the heading "Pre-Knowledge Check" and the 4 questions:
1. What is the difference between heat and temperature?
2. Touch a metal desk and a wooden desk. Which feels colder? Are they actually at different temperatures?
3. How does heat move from place to place?
4. Why does Alberta have cold winters — where does the "cold" come from?

Say

"Before we start anything — no notes, no Google. Just write what you honestly think right now. There are no right or wrong answers. I want to see what you already believe."

"We are going to come back and read these answers at the very end of the unit. I want you to see your own thinking change."

Expect most students to say heat and temperature are the same, and that cold "comes in" from outside. Do not correct yet — you will address both in Sections 1 and 2. These misconceptions are extremely common and were believed by scientists for centuries. Let them sit.

Cue

★ Write — Pre-knowledge check page: 4 questions, honest answers only. 5–6 minutes. Tell students: "Keep this page. Do not throw it away. We are coming back to it."

Beat 1.2 — What Is Temperature? (Particle Level)

Show

Board: Two boxes side by side.
Left box: "LOW TEMPERATURE" — small, tightly packed dots, tiny arrows showing slow movement.
Right box: "HIGH TEMPERATURE" — same dots spread slightly, large arrows showing fast movement.
Label: "faster particles = higher temperature"

Say

"Temperature is a measure of the [average] kinetic energy of particles in a substance."

"What does kinetic mean? It comes from Greek — it means movement. Kinetic energy is the energy of [motion]."

"So temperature is literally a speed measurement at the particle level. When particles move faster, temperature [rises/increases]. When particles move slower, temperature falls. That is all temperature is — a gauge of particle speed."

"Look at these two boxes. Same material. Different particle speed. Different temperature."

"Now — the vocabulary. Fill in your vocab table for 'Temperature' only right now. We will do the rest of the Section 1 terms by the end of Lesson 2."

★ Write — Guided notes, top of Section 1:
Students fill: average · motion · rises.

Beat 1.3 — The Celsius Scale: Five Landmarks

Show

Board: Vertical Celsius thermometer with 5 labeled marks:
100°C — water boils
37°C — body temperature
20°C — room temperature
0°C — water freezes
−20°C — typical Alberta January

Say

"The Celsius scale was designed around water. Two anchors: the freezing and boiling points of water."

"Water freezes at [0]°C. Water boils at [100]°C. Those are the two fixed points that define the scale."

"Normal body temperature is [37]°C. Room temperature is approximately [20–22]°C. A typical Alberta January day is approximately [−20]°C — you want these memorized. They will come up constantly."

"These are not random numbers. The scale is built around water specifically because water is everywhere, easily obtainable, and has reproducible phase-change points."

Cue

★ Write — Celsius bullet list (5 blanks):
Students fill: 0 · 100 · 37 · 20 (or 21–22) · −20.

Beat 1.4 — The Kelvin Scale, Absolute Zero, and Thermometers

Show

Board: Two thermometers side by side — Celsius (left) and Kelvin (right).
Mark: 0°C = 273 K. 100°C = 373 K. −273°C = 0 K (absolute zero).
Write conversion formula: K = °C + 273
Draw conversion table (4 rows) from student notes, leaving Kelvin column blank to fill live.

Say

"Scientists use a different temperature scale called Kelvin. It starts at absolute zero — the temperature at which particles have [no/zero] kinetic energy. Theoretically, no movement at all."

"Absolute zero = [−273]°C. There is [no] negative temperature on the Kelvin scale, because you cannot have particles moving less than not at all."

"Conversion is simple: K = °C + 273. Let's run the table: 0°C = [273] K. 100°C = [373] K. −40°C = [233] K. 20°C = [293] K. And body temperature, 37°C = 310 K, which means the Celsius must be [37]°C — show that one working backwards."

"Why use Kelvin? Because negative temperatures cause problems in equations. 0°C is arbitrary — just water's freezing point. 0 K is meaningful — zero energy. Scientific equations need a meaningful zero."

"How thermometers work: liquids [expand] when heated and [contract] when cooled. The liquid rises up the tube as it expands. The scale was calibrated by defining two reference points: [the freezing point of water] and [the boiling point of water]."

Cue

★ Write — Kelvin bullet list (4 blanks): scientific · no/zero · −273 · no.
Conversion table (5 rows): 273 K · 373 K · 233 K · 293 K · 37°C.
Thermometer blanks: expand · contract · freezing point of water · boiling point of water.
Vocabulary table — complete remaining rows: Kelvin scale, Celsius scale, Absolute zero, Thermometer.

Section 1 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Temperature measures the average kinetic energy (average speed of motion) of particles in a substance.
  2. 25°C = 298 K. −10°C = 263 K. (K = °C + 273)
  3. Absolute zero is 0 K = −273°C — the temperature at which particles have zero kinetic energy (no movement). You cannot go lower because there is no movement left to remove.
  4. Liquid thermometers use liquid that expands when heated and contracts when cooled. As it expands, the liquid rises up the narrow tube. Scale calibrated against water's freezing and boiling points.
  5. Stretch: 0°C is an arbitrary reference point (water's freezing point). A temperature of −5°C in a physics equation could produce a negative result that has no physical meaning. 0 K = true zero energy — the equation's answer scales correctly from zero.

Section 2 — Heat vs. Temperature

Lessons 3–4 4 beats 2 misconception flags

Misconception — Address at start of Lesson 3

"Heat and temperature are the same thing." This is the unit's most critical misconception. Do not dismiss it quickly. Students have used these words interchangeably for years. Beat 2.1 uses a concrete physical comparison (cup vs. bathtub) to drive the distinction. Return to it in Beat 2.3. The distinction should be explicit before any other Section 2 content.

Beat 2.1 — The Core Distinction: Heat Is Not Temperature

Show

Board: Two boxes side by side.
Left: "CUP of water — 90°C"   Right: "BATHTUB of water — 90°C"
Under each, draw a blank row: "Temperature = ?" and "Total thermal energy = ?"
Ask students to predict which one would burn them more if they fell into it.

Say

"Same temperature. Which one hurts more if you fall in it? The bathtub — obviously. But they're the same temperature. How can that be?"

"Temperature tells you how fast particles are moving on average. It doesn't tell you how many particles there are."

"The cup and the bathtub have the same [temperature]. But the bathtub has far more [thermal energy] — more particles, each moving at the same average speed, so total kinetic energy is enormous compared to the cup."

"Heat is energy [transferred] from a hotter object to a cooler one. Heat is not stored inside the bathtub — it is what we call the [transfer/flow] of energy between objects of different temperatures."

"Temperature: how fast particles are moving on average. Heat: how much total energy is being transferred. One is a speed gauge. The other is a flow of energy."

Cue

★ Write — Misconception card blanks: temperature · thermal energy.
Heat definition blanks: transferred · transfer/flow.
Vocabulary table: Heat, Thermal energy, Heat transfer.

Beat 2.2 — Direction of Heat Flow and Thermal Equilibrium

Show

Board: Simple diagram — two boxes connected.
Left box: "HOT (80°C)" → arrow pointing right → Right box: "COOL (20°C)"
Below: same two boxes, both now labelled "50°C" — arrow erased, replaced with: "THERMAL EQUILIBRIUM — heat transfer stops"

Say

"Heat always flows from [hot/warm] to [cold/cool]. Never the other direction — not unless a machine forces it."

"Your fridge is an example of a machine forcing heat in the 'wrong' direction. It uses electricity to push heat from cold food to warm room air. That takes work. Left alone, heat will always flow hot to cold."

"When two objects in contact reach the [same] temperature, heat stops transferring. They are in thermal equilibrium."

"Example: a metal spoon in hot soup. Soup is 90°C, spoon is 20°C. Heat flows from soup into spoon. Eventually both reach the same temperature. Heat flow stops. Thermal equilibrium."

Cue

★ Write — Direction blanks: hot/warm · cold/cool.
Equilibrium blank: same.
Vocabulary table: Thermal equilibrium.

Beat 2.3 — The Lake and the Cup

Show

Board: Draw the comparison table from student notes.
Columns: [blank] / Cup of water (80°C) / Lake (80°C)
Rows: Temperature / Number of particles / Total heat energy / Which cools a burn faster?
Fill in as you talk — leave the two blanked cells for students to fill.

Say

"Same temperature. Completely different amounts of total energy. Let's fill in this table."

"Temperature: same for both — 80°C. Particle speed is identical in both."

"Number of particles: the cup has very few. The lake has trillions of times more."

"Total heat energy: the cup has [low/small] total energy — few particles, each moving at the same speed. The lake has [enormous/vast] total energy — same speed, but trillions of times more particles."

"Which cools your burn faster if spilled on you? The [cup] — because it contains so little total energy that it dumps quickly. The lake would continuously deliver enormous energy to your skin."

"Speed gauge vs. fuel tank. The cup and the lake are running their engines at the same speed. But the lake has a vastly larger fuel tank."

Cue

★ Write — Lake-and-cup table:
Total heat energy: low/small (cup) · enormous/vast (lake).
Burns faster: cup.

Misconception — Beat 2.4

"Cold travels — cold air comes in through the window." Students will say this naturally. The correct model: cold is not a substance. Heat leaves the warm space. "Cold" is just the word for low heat energy. Beat 2.4 addresses this directly using both the window scenario and the "cold wind" scenario. This misconception recurs in Section 6 (insulation) — flag it there too.

Beat 2.4 — "Cold Travels" Is Wrong

Show

Board: Two scenarios with big X drawn through each wrong version:
✗ "Cold is coming in through the window." → ✓ "Heat is leaving through the window."
✗ "Cold wind hits you." → ✓ "You are losing heat to the moving cold air faster."
Also write: Self-check: "Your soup cools in 20 minutes. What actually happened?"

Say

"Here's a common one: 'Close the door — you're letting the cold in.' What's wrong with that statement?"

"Cold is not a substance. It doesn't move. It doesn't arrive or leave. What happens is that [heat] leaves your warm house through the window and is lost to the cold outside air."

"When a cold wind hits you, it's not cold travelling to you. It's your body [losing] heat to the moving cold air faster. The wind strips away the warm air layer your body had built up around it."

"'Cold' is just the word we use for low heat energy. Heat moves. Cold doesn't."

"How do you correct 'close the door, you're letting the cold in'? → 'Close the door — we're losing heat too fast.' Or: 'Open the window — you're letting the heat out.'"

"Self-check question: Your soup is hot. You wait 20 minutes and it's lukewarm. Is it because coldness came in from the air? What actually happened? → [Heat left the soup and transferred to the cooler air around it until they reached thermal equilibrium.]"

Cue

★ Write — Misconception blanks: heat · losing.
Self-check question: students write their own answer in the answer line provided.

Section 2 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Temperature = average kinetic energy (speed) of particles. Heat = energy transferred from hot to cold. A cup and a bathtub can have the same temperature but vastly different total heat energy — different number of particles.
  2. Heat always flows from hot to cool (high temperature to low temperature). When both objects reach the same temperature, heat transfer stops — thermal equilibrium.
  3. The swimming pool has more heat energy. It has an enormous number of particles, all at 30°C. The cup at 100°C has higher temperature but far fewer particles — far less total thermal energy.
  4. Corrected: "Open the door — you're letting the heat out." Cold doesn't travel. Heat flows from warm indoor air to cold outdoor air.
  5. Stretch: Spoon in soup: soup is hotter than spoon, heat flows from soup to spoon (hot → cool). Spoon on ceramic plate: spoon is now warmer than plate, heat flows from spoon to plate until both reach equilibrium. Both follow the same rule: heat flows from higher to lower temperature.

Section 3 — The Particle Model and Heat

Beat 3.1 — Particles in Motion: Expansion and Contraction

Show

Board: Two particle diagrams side by side.
Left: "COLD SOLID" — tightly packed dots, small arrows.
Right: "HOT SOLID" — same dots, farther apart, large arrows.
Label: "THERMAL EXPANSION" with arrow between the two states.
Below: draw the reverse (large → small arrows) labelled "THERMAL CONTRACTION".

Say

"The particle model says: all matter is made of tiny particles that are [constantly] in motion. The faster the particles move, the [higher] the temperature."

"What happens when you heat a solid? Walk through the four steps:"

"Step 1: particles absorb energy and [move/vibrate] faster. Step 2: particles vibrate [farther] apart from each other. Step 3: the material [expands] — it takes up more space. Step 4: this is called [thermal expansion]."

"And the reverse — cooling: particles slow down → move [closer] together → material [contracts/shrinks] — takes up less space. This is thermal contraction."

Cue

★ Write — Particle model blanks: constantly · higher.
Heating sequence (4 blanks): move/vibrate faster · farther · expands · thermal expansion.
Cooling blanks: closer · contracts/shrinks.
✎ Draw — particle diagrams (cold solid vs. warm solid) in retrieval check box.
Vocabulary table: Particle model, Thermal expansion, Thermal contraction.

Beat 3.2 — Real-World Applications of Thermal Expansion

Show

Board: Four simple sketches:
1. Railroad track cross-section — small gap between two sections, labelled "expansion gap".
2. Bridge expansion joint — grooved gap, labelled "expansion joint".
3. Power line between two poles — sagging in summer, taut in winter.
4. Thermometer — liquid in bulb, arrow pointing up tube.

Say

"Thermal expansion isn't just a physics concept — engineers have to design for it."

"Railroad tracks: [expansion] gaps left between sections so tracks don't buckle in summer heat. Without those gaps, the expanding metal has nowhere to go and the tracks warp."

"Bridges: [expansion] joints — those grooved gaps you drive over — allow the bridge to expand without cracking. On a long bridge, seasonal expansion can be several centimetres."

"Power lines: hung [loosely/with sag] in summer. In winter cold, they tighten. If strung tight in summer, winter contraction would snap them."

"Thermometers: [liquid] in the bulb expands up the tube when heated. Calibrated against the two fixed points we already know."

Cue

★ Write — Expansion applications (4 blanks):
expansion · expansion · loosely/with sag · liquid.

Pacing — Beat 3.3: Temperature plateau during phase change

Students often struggle to understand why temperature stays constant during melting or boiling. The intuition "I'm still adding heat so temperature should keep rising" is very natural. Do not move on until students can explain it in their own words. Budget an extra 5+ minutes. The heating curve lab reinforces this — use it.

Beat 3.3 — Changes of State and Why Temperature Plateaus

Show

Board: Heating curve — time on x-axis, temperature on y-axis.
Draw: rising line (solid heating) → flat line at 0°C (melting) → rising line (liquid heating) → flat line at 100°C (boiling) → rising line (gas heating).
Label each flat section: "temperature stays constant — all energy goes into breaking bonds."
Below or beside: draw the changes-of-state table from student notes, leaving energy-change and particle-change columns blank.

Say

"Walk through the changes of state table with me."

"Melting: solid → liquid. Heat is [absorbed]. Particles gain enough energy to overcome [the fixed bonds/attractive forces] between them — they can now slide past each other."

"Freezing: liquid → solid. Heat is [released]. Particles slow until locked in fixed positions."

"Evaporation/boiling: liquid → gas. Heat is [absorbed]. Particles gain enough energy to escape the liquid's surface entirely."

"Condensation: gas → liquid. Heat is [released]. [Gas particles slow down and rejoin the liquid surface.]"

"Now — the big question. Look at the heating curve. Why is the line flat during melting and boiling? You're still adding heat. Why doesn't the temperature rise?"

"Because during melting or boiling, added heat goes into [breaking] the bonds between particles — not into speeding them up. That's why temperature [stays constant/doesn't rise] even though you keep adding energy. All the energy is being used to change the [state], not the temperature."

"Ice water at 0°C on a hot stove: the temperature will not move until every last bit of ice is melted. Then it starts rising again."

Cue

★ Write — Changes-of-state table (energy column, 4 rows): absorbed · released · absorbed · released.
Condensation particle blank: gas particles slow down and rejoin the liquid surface.
Temperature plateau explanation (3 blanks): breaking · stays constant/doesn't rise · state.
Vocabulary table: Change of state.

Section 3 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. When heated, particles absorb energy, move faster, and vibrate farther apart — the material takes up more space (thermal expansion).
  2. Without expansion gaps, the expanding rails in summer have nowhere to go — the metal pushes against itself and buckles or warps, which can derail trains.
  3. All added heat is going into breaking the bonds between ice particles (overcoming attractive forces between water molecules). None of it is going into speeding particles up. Temperature doesn't rise until all bonds are broken and all ice has melted.
  4. Draw: Cold solid — tightly packed particles, small arrows pointing in various directions. Warm solid — same particles but with more space between them, larger arrows. Temperature difference shown by arrow size and particle spacing.
  5. Stretch: 75°C range × 2000 m × 0.000012 per °C = 1.8 m total expansion seasonally. (Accept any clear working showing 0.0012% per degree × 75° range × 2000 m.)

Section 4 — Modes of Heat Transfer

Beat 4.1 — Conduction: Particle-to-Particle Transfer

Show

Board: Metal rod — one end in flame. Particle diagram along the rod:
Left end (hot): large arrows. Middle: medium arrows. Right end (cool): small arrows.
Label: "CONDUCTION — direct contact, particle-to-particle"
List below: Good conductors (metals) vs. Poor conductors / Insulators (wood, rubber, foam, air).

Say

"There are three ways heat moves. First: conduction."

"Conduction is heat transfer through [direct/physical] contact, particle-to-particle."

"How it works: fast-moving particles in the hot region bump into [slower]-moving particles in the cooler region. Energy transfers. Heat moves through the material — no particle moves far; they pass the energy along like a chain."

"Conduction works best in [solids] — particles are packed close together and constantly colliding."

"Good conductors: [metals] — that's why pans are made of metal. Energy needs to travel efficiently from the burner to the food."

"Poor conductors (insulators): [wood, rubber, foam, air] — that's why pot handles are not metal. Wood conducts so slowly your hand doesn't lose heat fast enough to burn."

Cue

★ Write — Conduction blanks: direct/physical · slower · solids · metals · wood/rubber/foam/air.
Vocabulary table: Conduction, Insulator, Conductor.

Misconception — Beat 4.2

"Metal is colder than wood." Students will feel this and believe it. The metal and wooden desk have been in the same room all night — they are at identical temperatures. Metal feels colder because it conducts heat away from the hand faster. This is one of the most powerful demonstrations in the unit — use an IR thermometer to measure both surfaces in front of the class. Seeing "same temperature" on a device is more convincing than being told.

Beat 4.2 — "Metal Feels Colder" — The Conduction Misconception

Show

Board: Metal desk vs. wooden notebook side by side.
"Touch both. Which feels colder?"
Below: IR thermometer reading both — both showing room temperature (approx. 20°C).
Draw: arrow from HAND → METAL (large, fast) and arrow from HAND → WOOD (small, slow).
Label: "Your hand is losing heat. Metal takes it faster."

Say

"Touch your metal desk. Now touch your notebook or a wooden surface. Which one feels colder?"

"The metal. Every time. Now — if I use an IR thermometer and measure both, what do I get?" [Measure both.] "They are at [the same] temperature. They've been in this room all night."

"So why does the metal feel colder? Metal [conducts/draws] heat away from your hand much faster. Your hand is the warm object; the desk is the cooler one. Heat flows from your hand into the desk."

"Metal conducts so quickly your hand loses heat fast — your brain interprets that as 'cold.' Wood conducts so slowly your hand barely loses any heat — your brain reads that as 'warm.'"

"The metal is not colder. It just steals your heat faster. You are the source; the desk is the sink. Metal moves the energy between you and the desk much faster than wood does."

Cue

★ Write — Misconception blanks: the same · conducts/draws.

Beat 4.3 — Convection: Heat Moving Through Fluids

Show

Board: Two convection diagrams side by side.
1. Pot of water on burner — arrows showing: warm water rising from bottom, cool water sinking from top, forming a circular loop. Label: "CONVECTION CURRENT".
2. Room with a radiator — warm air rising to ceiling, cool air near windows sinking. Label: "home heating convection".
Write rule: "Convection only works in LIQUIDS and GASES — not in solids."

Say

"Second mode: convection."

"Convection is heat transfer through the movement of [fluids] — liquids or gases."

"The sequence: fluid near heat source warms → particles move [farther] apart → fluid becomes [less] dense → less dense warm fluid [rises] → cooler, denser fluid [sinks] to replace it → a [convection] current forms and keeps circulating continuously."

"Examples: boiling water — [hot water] rises from the hot bottom, [cooler water] sinks from the surface. Home heating — warm air from the radiator [rises], cool air near windows [sinks]. Sea breeze — land heats up faster than water → [warm air rises] over land; cool ocean air moves in to replace it."

"Key rule: convection only works in [liquids] and [gases]. Not solids. Solids can't circulate — particles are locked in place. No circulation, no convection current."

Cue

★ Write — Convection sequence (5 blanks): farther · less · rises · sinks · convection.
Boiling water: hot water (rises) · cooler water (sinks).
Home heating: rises · sinks. Sea breeze: warm air rises.
Only in: liquids · gases.
Vocabulary table: Convection.

Beat 4.4 — Radiation: No Medium Required + Comparison Table

Show

Board: Sun → Earth with "150 million km VACUUM" between them. Arrow labelled "electromagnetic waves — RADIATION."
Below: comparison table (from student notes):
Rows: Conduction / Convection / Radiation
Columns: Medium needed? / Works in solids? / Works in fluids? / Works in vacuum?
Fill in as you talk.

Say

"Third mode: radiation."

"Radiation is heat transfer through [electromagnetic] waves. No medium required."

"All objects above absolute zero emit electromagnetic waves that carry energy. When these waves hit a surface, they are absorbed as [heat]."

"Radiation does NOT require matter — it travels through [vacuum/empty space]. This is how the Sun heats the Earth across 150 million km of empty space. No air, no particles — the energy arrives anyway."

"Examples: [sunlight, campfire heat, infrared lamp, body heat radiating off skin] — any four count."

"Dark, matte surfaces [absorb] radiation better — they heat up faster. Light, shiny surfaces [reflect] radiation — they stay cooler. That is why a white t-shirt is cooler in summer than a black one."

"Now the comparison table: Conduction — medium needed, works in solids and fluids, NOT in vacuum. Convection — medium needed, NOT in solids, only fluids, NOT in vacuum. Radiation — no medium needed, works everywhere including vacuum."

Cue

★ Write — Radiation blanks: electromagnetic · heat · vacuum/empty space.
Examples (fill any 4): sunlight · campfire · infrared lamp · body heat.
Absorb/reflect: absorb · reflect.
Vocabulary table: Radiation.

Section 4 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Campfire 2 m away = radiation. Spoon in hot soup = conduction. Hot air from radiator = convection.
  2. Radiation is electromagnetic waves — energy travelling through oscillating electric and magnetic fields. No matter needed. A medium (particles) is only required for conduction and convection, which both depend on particle collisions or particle movement.
  3. They are at the same temperature — verified by a thermometer. Metal conducts heat away from your hand much faster than wood does. Your hand loses heat fast through metal → brain reads "cold." Wood conducts slowly → your hand barely loses heat → brain reads "warm."
  4. Convection requires fluid movement (circulation of particles). In solids, particles are locked in fixed positions — they cannot circulate, so no convection current can form.
  5. Stretch: Vacuum layer = blocks conduction and convection (no medium). Shiny inner walls = reflects radiation back (doesn't absorb it). Plastic cap = poor conductor, slows conduction through the opening.

Beat 5.2 — Water's High Specific Heat: Why It Matters

Show

Board: Three labeled diagrams:
1. Car engine → coolant loop → radiator. Label: "water as coolant."
2. Coastline map — ocean on one side, city on the other. Label: "ocean = thermal reservoir."
3. Alberta map — inland location highlighted. Label: "no moderating water body → larger temperature swings."

Say

"Water's high specific heat has three major real-world consequences."

Section 5 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Specific heat capacity = energy needed to raise 1 g by 1°C. Water has higher specific heat than metal (approximately 4–5× higher).
  2. Water can absorb large amounts of heat without its temperature rising much. In a car engine, coolant circulates through hot engine components, absorbs heat, carries it to the radiator to release, then returns. It can do this efficiently without boiling.
  3. Sand has low specific heat — it absorbs radiation quickly and reaches high temperatures. Water has high specific heat — it absorbs much more energy before its temperature rises, so it stays cooler on a hot day.
  4. Stretch: Ocean (Pacific) gives BC a high specific heat buffer — temperatures stay moderate year-round. No ocean buffer near Calgary — continental air masses cause hot summers and cold winters. Calgary also gets cold Arctic air from the north with no mountain range blocking it.

Section 6 — Retrieval Check Answer Guide
  1. Insulation slows heat transfer by blocking one or more modes: conduction (poor conductors, trapped still air), convection (small air pockets prevent circulation), radiation (shiny surfaces reflect it). A vacuum blocks conduction and convection simultaneously — no medium means no particle collisions and no fluid circulation.
  2. Alberta's temperature range (−40°C to +35°C) is far more extreme than BC's mild coastal climate. Greater temperature difference between inside and outside means more heat loss — higher R-values keep that loss manageable and reduce heating energy consumption.
  3. Thermal pollution: warm water discharged from industrial cooling raises river or lake temperature. Warmer water holds less dissolved oxygen. Lower dissolved oxygen levels harm or kill aquatic organisms, particularly cold-water species like trout and salmon.
  4. Stretch: Outer layer: shiny aluminized material (reflects radiation). Middle layer: vacuum or foam with small air cells (blocks conduction and convection). Inner layer: close-fitting, insulating foam (additional conduction barrier). Tight-sealing lid: reduces warm air infiltration (reduces convection through the opening).

Unit Synthesis — Answer Guide

6-concept table expected responses:

  • Heat vs. temperature: Temperature = average kinetic energy of particles (speed). Heat = energy transferred from hot to cold. Same temperature ≠ same heat energy.
  • Metal vs. wood: Same temperature (room temperature). Metal conducts heat from your hand much faster → feels cold. Wood conducts slowly → feels warm.
  • Three modes: Conduction (particle-to-particle, direct contact), Convection (fluid movement), Radiation (electromagnetic waves, no medium).
  • Water's high specific heat: Water requires much more energy to change temperature than most substances. It stores heat well and releases it slowly — moderates climate near oceans.
  • How insulation works: Slows heat transfer (all three modes) — poor conductors slow conduction, trapped air prevents convection, shiny surfaces reflect radiation, vacuum blocks conduction and convection.
  • Absolute zero: 0 K = −273°C — temperature at which particles have zero kinetic energy. Lowest possible temperature.

Synthesis Q2 — Hot cocoa mug: (1) Radiation — mug emits infrared radiation in all directions. (2) Conduction — heat moves through ceramic walls into the cooler air immediately in contact with the mug; also through the table surface below. (3) Convection — warm air above the mug rises, creating a convection current that continuously carries heat away.

Synthesis Q3 — Alberta home: Accept any three with correct scientific justification, e.g.: (a) High R-value roof insulation → slows conduction/convection through attic; major heat loss path in Alberta. (b) Triple-pane windows → vacuum/argon gap blocks conduction and convection; low-e coating reflects radiation. (c) Thermal mass (concrete floors in sunlit rooms) → absorbs solar heat during the day (high specific heat), releases slowly at night — reduces heating load.
