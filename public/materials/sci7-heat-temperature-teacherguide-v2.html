This unit has the densest misconception load in Grade 7 Science. Students bring strong intuitive but wrong beliefs about heat: cold is a substance, metal is colder than wood, heat and temperature are the same. The first four lessons are largely misconception correction before building the correct framework. Don't rush past this.

Materials per Lesson

  • Every lesson: Student notes packages, whiteboard / projector
  • Lesson 1: Two beakers of water at different temperatures (or described as a thought experiment)
  • Lesson 6: Crushed ice, beaker, heat source, thermometer, timer — heating curve lab
  • Lesson 7: Metal object and wooden object left overnight at room temperature, thermometer
  • Lesson 8: Red and blue food colouring, hot and cold water in clear containers; or paper spiral above heat source
  • Lesson 9: Heat lamp
  • Lesson 10: Identical containers with sand and water, heat lamp or sunlight access
  • Lesson 11: Insulation challenge materials; Alberta R-value reference sheet
  • Lesson 12: Thermal plume map; dissolved oxygen vs. temperature graph

Section 1 — Temperature and Measurement

Lessons 1–2 2 beats 0 flags

Lessons 1–2 · Student notes: Section 1

Beat 1.1 — What Is Temperature?

Show

Board: Two beakers of water — one at 20°C, one at 80°C.
Ask: "What is different about these two samples at the particle level?" List student answers. Guide toward particle speed.

Say

"Temperature is a number that tells you how fast the particles in a substance are moving on average. That's all it is. Faster particles = higher temperature."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 1: temperature definition and Celsius scale vocabulary. Notes: Section 1, temperature and Celsius scale.

Beat 1.2 — The Kelvin Scale and Absolute Zero

Show

Board: Ask "What happens if we keep cooling something? Can we cool it forever?" Draw a number line showing Celsius and Kelvin side by side. Mark 0 K = −273°C = absolute zero. Label "no negative Kelvin exists."

Say

"The Kelvin scale starts at the actual bottom. There is no negative Kelvin because there is no 'below stopped.' Celsius is convenient for daily use; Kelvin is what physics needs because it starts at zero, not at an arbitrary point."

"Absolute zero is the coldest possible temperature — where particles theoretically stop moving. Nothing in the universe has ever reached it."

Cue — ★ Write

Students complete Section 1 continued: Kelvin scale, conversion formula. Exit Ticket 1.

Section 2 — Heat vs. Temperature

Lessons 3–4 2 beats 2 misconception flags

Lessons 3–4 · Student notes: Section 2

Beat 2.1 — Heat Is Not Temperature (CRITICAL)

Show

Board: Two containers — a cup of water at 90°C and a bathtub full of water at 90°C (described scenario). Write on board: "Same temperature. Different heat?"
Ask before explaining: "If you spilled the cup on your hand versus fell into the bathtub — which causes more harm? Why, if they're both 90°C?"

Say

"Heat is not the same as temperature. Temperature tells you how fast particles are moving. Heat is the total energy transferred — which depends on how many particles there are AND how fast they're moving. The bathtub has trillions more particles. Same temperature, radically more heat."

"Let students respond first — most will say 'same temperature = same heat.' Get the wrong answer on the board first, then correct."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill in the core distinction: temperature = average speed of particles; heat = total energy transferred (depends on particle count AND speed). Section 2 notes.

Misconception Flag — Heat and Temperature Are the Same

This is the central misconception of the unit. Use the cup-and-bathtub thought experiment before any notes. Get the wrong answer on the board first, then correct it. If a student still conflates heat and temperature after instruction, the unit's central concept has not landed. Check this explicitly on Exit Ticket 2 and the unit test.

Beat 2.2 — "Cold Comes In Through the Window"

Show

Board: Ask "On a cold winter day, do you feel cold air come in through window cracks?" Take all answers (everyone says yes). Then write: "Is cold a substance that travels? Or is something else happening?"

Say

"Cold is not a substance. Nothing called 'cold' moves from outside to inside. What actually happens is that warm air from inside your house flows out through the crack — you lose heat, not gain cold. The door is bleeding heat, not inhaling cold."

Cue — ★ Write

Students rephrase "the cold is coming in" in the correct direction: "heat is going out." Fill Section 2 notes. Exit Ticket 2.

Misconception Flag — Cold Is a Substance That Travels

Ask students to describe the experience first; then reframe: heat leaves the room, cold does not enter. The door is not inhaling cold — it is bleeding heat. This is one of the most persistent everyday-language misconceptions in this unit. The correction must be made in plain language before the formal notes.

Section 3 — Particle Model and Heat

Lessons 5–6 2 beats 1 pacing flag

Lessons 5–6 · Student notes: Section 3

Beat 3.1 — Expansion and Contraction

Show

Board: Railroad track photo showing buckled track in summer heat. Ball-and-ring demonstration if available: metal ball passes through ring at room temperature, does not fit after being heated.
Label diagram: "Particles move faster → push farther apart → material takes up more space."

Say

"When you add heat to a solid, particles move faster and push farther apart. The material takes up more space. This is why every bridge, road, and railway has expansion gaps — not as an accident, but as a required design feature."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 3: thermal expansion definition, bridge/rail application example, vocabulary.

Beat 3.2 — Changes of State and the Heating Curve

Show

Board: Set up the heating curve demonstration — heat crushed ice in a beaker, record temperature every minute. Plot on board as students call out readings.
Draw the heating curve with clearly marked plateaus at 0°C and 100°C.

Say

"The temperature isn't rising during the plateau because the heat is being used to break the bonds between particles, not to speed them up. All the energy goes into the structural change, not the speed change."

"Watch the thermometer. It stays at 0°C while the ice melts — even though we're still adding heat. That energy is going into the phase change, not into making particles move faster."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students sketch the heating curve in Section 3 and label the two plateaus with explanations. Exit Ticket 3.

Pacing Flag — Heating Curve Lab Takes Time (~20 min)

Use crushed ice for a faster phase change. Record temperature every 60 seconds. The 0°C plateau typically lasts 5–8 minutes; the 100°C plateau is harder to sustain in an open beaker but should be visible. If graphing in real time is too slow, assign graphing as post-lab work. Students graph time on x-axis, temperature on y-axis.

Section 4 — Heat Transfer Modes

Lessons 7–9 3 beats 2 misconception flags

Lessons 7–9 · Student notes: Section 4

Beat 4.1 — Conduction (and the Metal Misconception)

Show

Board: Set up metal desk and wooden desk at the same temperature (measure with thermometer to confirm before students touch). Have students touch both. Record on board: "Which felt colder?"
Then: measure again with the thermometer. Both read the same temperature.

Say

"They're the same temperature. This is the most testable misconception in the unit. Touch both. Read both with a thermometer. They're the same. The metal felt colder because it conducted heat AWAY from your hand faster. Your hand is the warm object. You measured your own heat loss."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 conduction notes. The misconception callout box is in the student notes — read it aloud. Objects must have been in the room long enough to reach room temperature (ideally left out overnight). Use the student most convinced metal "must be colder" to do the touch-then-measure sequence.

Misconception Flag — Metal Is Colder Than Wood

Touch and measure demonstration — make the misconception fail in real time before any explanation. The thermometer shows equal temperature; the touch test shows different sensation. The explanation follows from the evidence. If a student's Pre-Knowledge Check Q2 still says "metal is colder" on the synthesis page, the conduction lesson did not correct the misconception — reteach before the test.

Beat 4.2 — Convection

Show

Board: Food colouring demonstration — drop red food colouring into very hot water in a clear container; drop blue into cold water. Watch the movement patterns.
Draw the convection loop: hot fluid is less dense → rises; cool fluid sinks; cycle forms and circulates.

Say

"Convection only works in fluids — liquids and gases. The fluid circulates because hot fluid is less dense and rises while cool fluid sinks. This is how your house heats — baseboard heaters warm air near the floor, which rises, which pushes cool air down to be heated again."

Cue — ★ Write + ✎ Draw

Students fill Section 4 convection notes and draw the convection loop with labels.

Beat 4.3 — Radiation

Show

Board: Hold hand near (but not touching) a hot lamp or heat lamp. Feel the warmth from a distance. No contact, no air movement needed.
Label: "Radiation — electromagnetic energy — no medium required."

Say

"Radiation requires no medium at all. It's electromagnetic energy — the same family as light. This is how the Sun heats Earth across 150 million km of vacuum. No air, no contact, no fluid. Just electromagnetic waves."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 4 radiation notes. Exit Ticket 4.

Misconception Flag — Radiation Needs a Medium

Address in Lesson 9. Ask "Then how does the sun warm us?" before explaining. Radiation is electromagnetic energy — the same family as visible light — and travels through the vacuum of space. The Sun-to-Earth example makes this concrete and memorable.

Section 5 — Specific Heat Capacity

Lesson 10 1 beat 0 flags

Lesson 10 · Student notes: Section 5

Beat 5.1 — Specific Heat Capacity

Show

Board: Two identical containers — one with water, one with sand. Expose both to the same heat source (sunlight or heat lamp) for 10 minutes. Measure temperature of both before and after. Sand heats dramatically more.
Label: "Water requires ~4× more energy to raise 1°C than most metals or sand."

Say

"Water requires about four times more energy to raise its temperature by 1 degree compared to most metals. This has enormous consequences for climate, engineering, and daily life. The ocean is a giant heat battery."

"The sand and water received the same amount of energy. The sand got much hotter. This difference is specific heat capacity — how much energy is needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1°C."

Cue — ★ Write

Students fill Section 5 notes: specific heat capacity definition and comparison table. Exit Ticket 5. Prep note: Use identical volume of sand and water in identical clear containers at consistent distance from heat source. Measure initial temperature, then after 5 and 10 minutes. Expected: sand increases ~4–6°C; water increases ~1–2°C.

Section 6 — Insulation and Applications

Lessons 11–12 2 beats 1 misconception flag

Lessons 11–12 · Student notes: Section 6

Beat 6.1 — Insulation

Show

Board: Insulation challenge setup — which material keeps ice frozen longest?
Also show: Alberta R-value requirements vs. BC R-value requirements. Ask: "Why are they different?"

Say

"Insulation doesn't make things warm. It slows heat transfer — in either direction. A cooler bag and a thermos both work on the same principle. The insulation doesn't generate cold for one and warmth for the other — it slows the flow in both cases."

Cue — ★ Write

Alberta building context: R-value comparison noted. Students fill Section 6 notes. Exit Ticket 6.

Misconception Flag — Insulation Keeps Things Warm

Address in Lesson 11 before notes. Use the cooler bag example: insulation slows heat flow in both directions. A cooler bag keeps food cold (slows heat flowing in) by the same mechanism a thermos keeps soup hot (slows heat flowing out). The insulation doesn't generate warmth or cold — it resists the flow of heat in either direction.

Beat 6.2 — Thermal Pollution

Show

Board: Map of thermal plume from a power plant near a river. Temperature data upstream vs. downstream. Dissolved oxygen vs. temperature graph.
Key finding: "Warm water holds less dissolved oxygen."

Say

"Warm water holds less oxygen than cold water. Adding heat to a river is not a neutral act — it lowers the oxygen level that fish and invertebrates need. This is a form of pollution even though no chemicals are added."

Cue — ⊡ Think

Discussion prompt: "Is it possible to pollute an ecosystem without adding any chemical substance? Explain using today's example." Written reflection, not collected.
